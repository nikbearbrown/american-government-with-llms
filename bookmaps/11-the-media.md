## Source-Level Strengths and Limitations

### Strengths

1. **Concrete examples:** Ethiopian famine, Dominican Republic, crime coverage bias, campaign soundbites—all specific and traceable.

2. **Historical breadth:** Print → Radio → TV → Cable → Internet coverage is comprehensive, allows reader to see pattern across time.

3. **Regulation coverage:** FCC, libel law, Pentagon Papers, fairness doctrine—gives legal/institutional context.

4. **Mechanism explanation:** Framing (episodic vs. thematic) and priming are well-explained in source.

5. **Contemporary relevance:** 2025 White House pool access, Meta fact-checking, COVID-19 coverage differences—timely.

6. **Primary source integration:** Amanpour interview, FDR fireside chats, Zenger trial—voices the ideas rather than just stating them.

### Limitations

1. **Scattered focus:** Moves between history, current state, regulation, effects without strong unifying principle. Reader might not see how they connect.

2. **Missing economic framework:** Mentions that print made money from ads, broadcast from ads, cable from subscriptions, internet from engagement—but does not make this central. Chapter made it central because it explains *why* different media have different effects.

3. **Underexamined gatekeeping:** Source shows examples but does not explain the *machinery* of gatekeeping (how choices compound, why gatekeeping power persists even in social media era).

4. **Regulation vs. effects:** File 04 and File 05 feel separate. Source does not ask: how do regulatory constraints affect what citizens see? Chapter integrates this.

5. **Passive voice on media effects:** Source says "media coverage affects public opinion" but does not enough examine the **mechanism** (what about media format makes it persuasive?).

6. **Fairness doctrine treatment:** Source mentions it briefly but does not use it as a **case study in unintended consequences**. Chapter made it central to trade-off argument.

7. **Missing scale insight:** Source covers individual journalist → network → conglomerate → algorithm over time, but does not show that **gatekeeping has not disappeared, just moved to larger scale and became invisible**.

## What the Chapter Added That Source Lacked

1. **Unified argument:** Three mechanisms (gatekeeping, framing, regulation) organized to show how media *construct* reality, not just report it.
