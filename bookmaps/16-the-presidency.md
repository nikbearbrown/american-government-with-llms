## Concept Coverage

**Source → Chapter Mapping:**

### Concept 1: Constitutional Settlement
- **Source:** m90454 (Constitutional Convention debates, original powers)
- **Source:** m90458 (Veto power, appointments, treaty power, commander-in-chief)
- **Chapter:** Constitutional Settlement section
- **Approach:** Rather than listing powers abstractly, the chapter opens with the *question* the framers faced (how to design a strong-but-constrained executive) and shows how they attempted to answer it. The chapter treats the Electoral College as an example of the constraint machinery (two-stage filter against demagoguery).
- **What the chapter adds:** Scene (Convention floor), mechanical question (the puzzle), and the explicit trade-off (constraint vs. decisiveness).

### Concept 2: Expansion Mechanism
- **Source:** m90454 (Washington's cabinet, Polk, Lincoln, FDR, expansion over time)
- **Source:** m90457 (Bully pulpit, fireside chats, media evolution, "going public")
- **Source:** m90458 (Executive orders, signing statements, pardon power)
- **Chapter:** Expansion Mechanism section
- **Approach:** Rather than presenting expansion as a series of historical facts, the chapter identifies three *mechanisms* by which power expanded: technology, crisis, and precedent. Each mechanism is illustrated with a case (Quasi-War for precedent, FDR for crisis, Roosevelt/radio/TV/Twitter for technology).
- **What the chapter adds:** The framework of mechanisms (absent from source). The concept that "the Constitution is rewritten every time a president acts without being stopped." The machinery of how precedent accumulates.

### Concept 3: Two Presidencies
- **Source:** m90458 (Domestic policy vs. war powers, executive action)
- **Source:** m90455, m90456 (Limited to selection/election process, less on constraints)
- **Chapter:** Two Presidencies section
- **Approach:** The source material touches on domestic vs. foreign policy division in m90458, but the chapter develops this into a full concept with asymmetry as the key insight: Congress rules domestic, president rules foreign.
- **What the chapter adds:** The case studies (Clinton health care failure for domestic, Gulf of Tonkin for foreign). The trade-off discussion (safety vs. danger in foreign policy). The War Powers Resolution and its failure.

## Concept-Level Deviations from Source

**Opening Strategy:**
- Source: Lists facts about presidency, then raises questions.
- Chapter: Opens with a scene (Convention floor debate) to make the *problem* of designing a president vivid before any facts are presented.

**Sequencing:**
- Source: Inventing → Evolving → Selecting candidates → Electing → Transition → Shaping → Powers
- Chapter: Constitution → Expansion mechanisms → Two presidencies → Integration. This groups by concept rather than chronology.

**Emphasis:**
- Source: Emphasizes facts and procedures (how elections work, what cabinet departments exist, how many executive orders each president issued).
- Chapter: Emphasizes mechanism and tension (how does power expand? what are the trade-offs? what's the asymmetry between domestic and foreign?).

**Tech Treatment:**
- Source: Lists media innovations (newsreels, radio, TV, internet) chronologically.
- Chapter: Treats technology as a *mechanism of power expansion* with a central insight: the Constitution didn't change, but the reach of the president's voice did. This transforms the president from administrator to political leader.

## Pedagogical Choices & Reasoning

1. **Why open with the Convention floor instead of starting with "The presidency is..."?**
   - Attenborough × Feynman style requires cold open in scene, not abstraction.
   - Makes the *problem* vivid before any framework is introduced.
   - Allows students to see the framers' uncertainty, not their wisdom.

2. **Why compress selection/election material?**
   - Source devotes two full modules (m90455, m90456) to primaries, caucuses, Electoral College mechanics.
   - Chapter is about *presidential power*, not *how presidents are selected*.
   - Electoral College mentioned only as example of constraint machinery.
   - Keeps chapter focused.

3. **Why frame expansion around three mechanisms (technology, crisis, precedent) rather than list presidents?**
   - Source tends to list: Washington did this, then Jefferson, then FDR, etc.
   - Chapter identifies patterns that help students *understand* how expansion happened.
   - Mechanism-first approach teaches the *method*, not facts.
   - Students can then apply the mechanisms to new situations (future crises, new technologies).

4. **Why emphasize the "Two Presidencies" asymmetry so heavily?**
   - This is the most counterintuitive and important insight: the same Constitution creates different constraints in different domains.
   - Helps students see that constitutional law is not *written*; it's *practiced*.
   - The asymmetry explains both why foreign policy is dangerous (latitude for abuse) and why domestic policy stalls (constraints prevent action).

5. **Why include the Japanese internment case?**
   - Source mentions it briefly in m90458.
   - Chapter elevates it to central case because it's the most sobering example of what emergency power means in practice.
   - Forces students to confront the human cost of unilateral action.
   - Lingers in memory better than abstract discussion of war powers.

6. **Why omit First Lady material entirely?**
   - Source has extensive coverage (Eleanor Roosevelt, Betty Ford, Hillary Clinton, Laura Bush, Michelle Obama, etc.).
   - Chapter is about presidential *power*, not about presidential *spouses*.
   - First lady role is a separate concept deserving its own chapter.
   - Inclusion would dilute focus on the three mechanisms of expansion.
