**Module 2 (m90460): Evolution, structure, and public policy**
- Origin story: No national judiciary under Articles of Confederation. Article III minimal and vague. Alexander Hamilton's *Federalist* No. 78: courts as "least dangerous branch," with "neither force nor will, but merely judgment."
- *Marbury v. Madison* (1803): Marshall claims judicial review. Ruled Congress's law unconstitutional; said Court couldn't enforce Marbury's claim because the enforcement mechanism was unconstitutional itself.
- Hierarchy: District courts (trial level, 94 total, one per state minimum). Circuit courts (appellate, 13, three-judge panels). Supreme Court (nine justices, appellate).
- *Stare decisis*: Precedent binds lower courts. System runs on consistency and stability.
- Public policy: Courts make policy through rulings. Common law tradition—judge-made law through interpretation. Code law contrast (detailed laws, less judicial discretion).
- Example cases: ACA challenges (*National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius*, *Burwell v. Hobby Lobby*, *King v. Burwell*). Abortion (*Roe v. Wade*, overturned 2022 in *Dobbs*). *Elonis v. United States* (social media and free speech). Expansions of individual rights.

*Conversion note:* This module is the heartland of the chapter. Extracted:
- Marshall's audacity and its limits (Marbury becomes centerpiece).
- The three-tier structure (became diagram opportunity).
- Judicial review as claimed-but-not-written power (mechanism section).
- ACA case as trade-off example (stability vs. Court's legitimacy).
- The precedent concept (Plessy-to-Brown as timeline).

**Module 3 (m90461): Dual court system, federalism, jurisdiction**
- Dual system: Federal and state courts, each with trial and appellate levels, both reporting to Supreme Court as final authority.
- Jurisdiction split: State courts handle ~90% of all cases. Federal courts handle federal questions, interstate matters, diversity (citizens of different states, $75K+), Native American rights, patents, maritime law, bankruptcy, cases where U.S. is party.
- Criminal vs. civil: Criminal law (prosecution by government for harm to society). Civil law (private party vs. private party, remedies for injury).
- Example: *Miranda v. Arizona* (state crime, federal constitutional question). Miranda arrested for state rape; confessed; conviction upheld. Appeal on constitutional grounds: did Arizona violate Miranda's Fifth and Sixth Amendment rights? Supreme Court agreed; ruled confession inadmissible; established Miranda warnings requirement. But: retried without confession, convicted again. The constitutional right didn't save him, but it established a principle.
- Implications: Dual system offers alternate venues, protective redundancy. But creates disparities—marijuana laws vary wildly by state and federal; courts of different states interpret law differently based on political culture.

*Conversion note:* This module's main contributions were:
- The dual court reality (90% state cases vs. federal cases). Noted but not dwelt on; the chapter focuses on federal courts.
- *Miranda v. Arizona* as a full case study. Essential because it shows judicial activism creating law from constitutional principles (the warnings weren't written anywhere; the Court inferred them).
- The federalism complexity (Marijuana example): shows that courts are constrained by a system where laws vary.

**Module 4 (m90462): Three tiers, precedent, judge selection**
- Structure detail: 94 district courts (trial courts, witnesses, evidence, one judge per court). 13 circuit courts (appellate, three-judge panels reviewing trial court decisions). Supreme Court (nine justices, appellate, decisions binding nationwide).
- Judicial Code of 1911: Congress established district courts as sole general-jurisdiction trial courts.
- History: Early circuit courts had judges ride circuits (hence the name). Now circuit courts are pure appellate.
- Precedent (*stare decisis*): Consistency and stability. Benjamin Cardozo: "Adherence to precedent must then be the rule rather than the exception if litigants are to have faith in the even-handed administration of justice in the courts."
- Flexibility: Courts can overrule. Example: *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896) to *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954). Membership changed, reasoning changed, outcome changed.
- Judge selection: President nominates, Senate confirms. Lifetime terms. Senatorial courtesy: president consults home-state senators before nominating (especially powerful if senators share party affiliation).
- Examples of nominees: Obama—Sotomayor, Kagan. Trump—Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, Barrett. Surprises: Eisenhower regretted nominating Earl Warren (who broadened civil rights). Sandra Day O'Connor (Reagan) became champion of women's rights. David Souter (George H.W. Bush) sided liberal. Anthony Kennedy (Reagan) was the swing vote.
- Diversity: Court once all-white, male, Protestant. Now more diverse. Lower courts also more diverse but still lag behind the population.

*Conversion note:* This module provided:
- The three-tier structure (visual diagram).
- The precedent concept and its flexibility (Plessy-to-Brown).
- Judge selection process and the trade-off (lifetime tenure insulates from politics but locks in ideology for decades).
- The ideology-outcome relationship (judges have views; those views influence decisions; but judges can also surprise their appointers).

**Module 5 (m90463): Supreme Court structure, case selection, procedures**
- Nine justices (one chief, eight associates). Serve lifetime terms after nomination and confirmation.
- Current Court: Six justices considered conservative (Roberts, Thomas, Alito, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, Barrett). Three liberal (Breyer, Sotomayor, Kagan).
- Law clerks: Three or four per justice. Young law school graduates. Do research, write memos, help prepare justice for oral argument, draft opinions, assist with emergency applications (often death penalty stays).
- Case selection: ~7,000-10,000 petitions per year. Court accepts fewer than 2%. Looks for conflicts between lower courts, decisions conflicting with precedent, cases with special urgency (like *Bush v. Gore*).
- *Certiorari* (cert.): Request for review. Rule of Four: four justices must vote to grant cert. for a case to be heard.
- Interest group activity: *Amicus curiae* (friend of the court) briefs attempt to influence the Court.
- Solicitor general: Lawyer representing federal government. About two-thirds of cases involve the federal government. Solicitor general's arguments get special weight; sometimes called "the tenth justice."
- Oral arguments: Each side gets 30 minutes. Justices interrupt with questions. Happens October through April.
- Conference: Private meeting. Chief justice speaks first, then others by seniority. Vote taken.
- Opinions: Majority opinion (binding). Dissenting opinion (disagrees). Concurring opinion (agrees with outcome but not reasoning).
- Traditions: Robed justices, formal entrance, ceremonial language ("Oyez! Oyez! Oyez!"). No cameras allowed (despite calls for transparency).

*Conversion note:* This module supplied:

- The solicitor general's role (noted as external influence on decision-making).
- The case selection mechanism (Rule 10—looking for conflicts).
- The opinion types (briefly mentioned).
- The ceremonial nature of proceedings.

**Module 6 (m90464): Judicial decision-making, influences, checks**
- Opinions set precedent. Majority opinion is binding. If no majority, decision of lower court stands. Tied vote is rare.
- Chief justice assigns majority opinion writer (if chief in majority). Most senior justice in majority assigns if chief is not.
- Opinions released at different times; most controversial saved for end of term.
- Influences on decision-making:
  - Legal: facts of case, Constitution, laws, precedent.
  - Personal: justices' beliefs, ideology. Judicial activism (defending individual rights, striking down laws) vs. judicial restraint (deferring to elected branches, narrow interpretation of Bill of Rights).
  - Political attitudes matter. Liberal judges tend liberal decisions; conservative judges tend conservative ones. Not 100% but real.
  - Public opinion: Court swayed by public opinion and media, though somewhat isolated. Example: Sodomy laws—in 1986 (*Bowers v. Hardwick*), Court upheld Georgia's ban. In 2003 (*Lawrence v. Texas*), Court overturned it. Between those years, most states had repealed sodomy laws, LGBTQ movement emerged. Court followed public change.
- Checks on the Court:
  - Presidential: Nominations shape Court for decades. FDR's court-packing scheme (tried to add six justices; failed).
  - Congressional: Can modify federal court structure, reject nominees, rewrite laws, pass constitutional amendments, withhold funding.
  - Most significant check: **judicial implementation**. Courts have no enforcement mechanism. President must enforce, Congress must fund. Hamilton said courts have "neither force nor will, but merely judgment."
  - Example: *Worcester v. Georgia* (1832). Court ruled Georgia couldn't seize Cherokee land. Jackson reportedly said "John Marshall has made his decision; now let him enforce it" and did nothing.
  - Counter-example: *Little Rock, 1957*. Eisenhower enforced school desegregation by nationalizing Arkansas National Guard.
  - Today: Courts generally obeyed. But dependence on other branches is real.

*Conversion note:* This module provided:
- The influences on decision-making (ideology, personal beliefs, public opinion).
- The constraints on the Court (no enforcement; dependence on executive and legislative branches).
- The Jackson example (vivid illustration of Court's dependence).
- The Eisenhower counter-example (illustration of enforcement).

## Concepts in the original, deferred or not used

1. **Federal criminal vs. civil jurisdiction detail**: The original goes into categories (patents, maritime law, bankruptcy, etc.). Chapter simplifies to "federal questions" without the granular list. These are important for specialists but not central to understanding courts' basic power.

2. **State court system variation**: The original emphasizes that states differ in structure, judge selection (elected vs. appointed), political culture. The chapter mentions federalism but doesn't dwell on state-by-state variation. Could be expanded in a state-level government chapter.

3. **Jury duty**: The original includes a sidebar on jury duty. Not used in this draft; could be an exercise or extended discussion.

4. **Specific landmarks cases from the expansion table**: The original lists *Gideon v. Wainwright* (right to attorney), *McDonald v. Chicago* (right to handgun), *Riley v. California* (cell phone privacy), etc. Chapter focuses on a few deep-dive cases rather than listing many.

5. **Senatorial courtesy detail**: Mentioned but not explored fully. Could be its own section on how Congress (through senators) shapes the judiciary.

6. **State vs. federal law conflicts in depth**: The marijuana example in the original is vivid. Chapter touches it but doesn't explore the full implications for federalism.

## Material specific to the Attenborough × Feynman conversion

**What changed:**
- Opened with Marbury scene (cold open, present tense, personal stakes) instead of abstract framing.
- Made Marshall's strategy the centerpiece of judicial review explanation (he seized power by not using it; he showed humility and principle).
- Embedded trade-offs explicitly (judicial review gives courts power but depends on legitimacy; precedent creates stability but can trap error; lifetime tenure insulates from politics but locks in ideology).
- Used fewer cases in depth rather than listing many. *Marbury*, *Miranda*, *ACA cases* get real explanation. Others are mentioned but not fully analyzed.
- Treated judges as people (they have views; that's normal) rather than as neutral interpreters (a common misconception).
- Made the Jackson example vivid (defiance showing Court's dependence) rather than just noting it.
- Escalated the scale repeatedly: Marbury's personal job → framework for government. Miranda's confession → national criminal procedure. Clothing retailer's dress code → Title VII interpretation.
- Built exercises that force reasoning about precedent, trade-offs, legitimacy, not just recall.

**What stayed:**

- The precedent concept and its flexibility.
