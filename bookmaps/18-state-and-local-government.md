## Source Coverage

The source material covers six major topics across six modules:

1. **Federal-State Power Division** (m90466) — The shift in power between national and state governments from the Articles of Confederation through the Constitution, evolution of federalism (dual → cooperative), fiscal tools (grants, unfunded mandates)

2. **State Political Cultures** (m90467) — Elazar's typology (moralistic, individualistic, traditionalistic), origins in settlement and migration patterns, critiques of the theory

3. **State Governors and Legislatures** (m90468) — Formal powers of governors (veto, appointment, budget, special sessions), functions of state legislatures (lawmaking, constituency service, oversight), composition and professionalism of legislatures

4. **Term Limits** (m90469) — History and status of term limits in states, arguments for and against, empirical evidence on effects

5. **County and City Government** (m90470) — Three forms of county government (commission, council-administrator, council-elected executive), two forms of city government (mayor-council, council-manager), responsibilities and funding

6. **Implied in all modules:** The hierarchical relationship between state and local government (Dillon's Rule), property tax as main revenue source, fiscal constraints on local governments

**Module m90466 (federal-state power):** The longest source module. Extensive discussion of constitutional history, enumerated vs. reserved powers, supremacy clause, dual federalism, cooperative federalism, grants and mandates. **Module m90470 (county and city government):** Straightforward structural information. The chapter incorporates all three county types, both city types, the discussion of special districts, and the fiscal constraints (property tax, Prop 13, Detroit). This is well-integrated.

## Conceptual Clarifications (Conversion Choices)

**Dillon's Rule as central theme:**  
The source treats Dillon's Rule as one principle among others. The conversion elevates it to the conceptual spine because it is the single most important legal principle explaining state-local relationships. Everything else (home rule, charter limitations, state mandates) flows from Dillon's Rule.

**Political culture as ONE variable among many:**  
The source (m90467) treats Elazar's culture typology as a major organizing principle. The conversion treats it as one important variable (alongside legislative professionalism, governor power, fiscal structure) explaining state variation. This reflects contemporary political science skepticism about culture-as-explanation while preserving Elazar's insights about regional patterns.

**Fiscal structure as constraint:**  
The source discusses property tax, Prop 13, and unfunded mandates. The conversion frames these as a structural fiscal squeeze: local service demands rise, property tax is capped, state mandates increase without funding, federal grants are temporary. This is implicit in the source but becomes explicit in the conversion.

**Scale oscillation:**  
The source presents the chapter in the order: federal-state, state institutions, state-local. The conversion weaves scale movement throughout: Denton shows state preempting local, Prop 13 shows voters limiting state tax authority, Detroit shows federal economic policy (globalization, suburbanization) collapsing local tax base. This illustrates federalism-as-process, not just federalism-as-structure.

## Integration with Broader Textbook Context

**Chapter 4 (American Federalism):**  
This chapter assumes readers understand federalism broadly (division of power between federal and state). Chapter 18 applies it: states are federalism's lower level, but within states, local governments are hierarchical (not federal). The transition should be clear.

**Chapter 13 (State and Local Elections) [if it exists in the textbook]:**  
This chapter assumes readers will later encounter detailed information about how states and locals conduct elections, who votes, what voter participation looks like. Chapter 18 sets up the structural context for those choices.

**Subsequent policy chapters (education, criminal justice, economic development):**  
This chapter sets up the structural constraint for all subsequent policy chapters: policy is made at multiple levels (federal, state, local) with complex incentive structures (federal funding, state mandates, local control). Those chapters can reference back to this foundation.

## Questions Raised But Not Deeply Explored

The conversion is constrained to ~6,500 words (a typical chapter). Some source material raises questions that would require expansion to answer fully:

- Why do states persist in fiscal structures (property tax caps, unfunded mandates) that weaken their own local governments?
- If Dillon's Rule is the law, why do cities have any power at all? What is the political economy of home rule?
- Why do some states professionalize legislatures and others do not? Is it a deliberate choice or historical accident?
- How has political culture changed since Elazar studied it in the 1960s? Are settlement patterns still predictive?
- Why is voter turnout so much lower in local elections when local government is most direct?

These would be good discussion questions or extensions for instructors.
