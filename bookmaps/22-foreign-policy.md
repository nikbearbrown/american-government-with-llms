## Gaps in source material

1. **Transnational threats as a distinct category.** The source mentions them but does not make clear that they represent a fundamental mismatch with the machinery designed for state-to-state relations.

2. **The military-political gap.** The source does not explicitly teach that military capability ≠ political success. This is crucial for understanding modern foreign policy failures.

3. **Democratic speed.** The source does not address the tension between democratic deliberation and the speed required in modern crises.

4. **China as a distinct challenge.** The source mentions China but does not explain why it is different from Cold War Soviet competition (not zero-sum, not purely military, involves economic entanglement).

5. **The evolution of schools of thought.** The source lists them but does not explain how each emerged in response to a specific historical failure or success.

## Ideas harvest (for future work)

- **Specification move:** Define "foreign policy success" more precisely. Is it military victory? Political stability? Economic benefit? Democratic spread? Each definition leads to different policy.
- **Analogy opportunity:** Compare foreign policy decision-making to navigating by stars (limited information, multiple possible interpretations, correction requires time).
- **Cold open candidates:** The moment a president decides not to invade (restraint); the moment a president decided to invade (overreach); the moment Congress successfully blocked a president.
- **Trade-off deepening:** Expand the speed vs. legitimacy frame to other domains (public health, technology regulation, climate).
- **Misconception deepening:** Address the idea that "better leaders" would make better foreign policy (the machinery constrains any individual).
- **Scale-shift opportunities:** Show how foreign policy problems have become globalized; trace the expansion from bilateral to multilateral to transnational.
