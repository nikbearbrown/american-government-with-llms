## A Note about AI

American government is the academic field where AI's contemporary failure modes most directly meet the textbook's content. The system the book describes is the system the model has read about, contested in real time, in language that is rarely neutral.

The model is fluent in the institutional vocabulary, the constitutional doctrine, the case law, the names of the major actors, and the canonical interpretations of contested moments. It can produce a competent paragraph on the commerce clause, the filibuster, the electoral college, or executive privilege. That fluency is a real teaching asset for vocabulary and structure. It becomes a problem when the question shifts from how American government is *designed* to how it is *behaving* — because the behaviors that matter most are the contested ones, and the model has been trained on the contestation.

Where the model genuinely helps: explaining the formal structure of the institutions (how a bill becomes law, what a writ of certiorari does, what advice-and-consent requires), tracing the historical evolution of a doctrine (incorporation, federalism, judicial review), surveying the canonical scholarship on a topic, and producing the standard arguments on multiple sides of a contested question. The model is also useful as a vocabulary scaffolder when you encounter unfamiliar institutional terms and want a fast clean explanation.

Where the model does damage: characterizing contemporary political conflicts in language that subtly carries the inflection of one side, treating contested empirical claims as settled, and producing partisan or motivated framings while presenting them as descriptive. The model has trained on enormous quantities of political commentary, much of it asymmetric, and the asymmetries leak. Ask the model what a recent Supreme Court decision means and you will get an answer; whether the answer is descriptive or argumentative is often invisible from the prose alone.

A specific failure mode worth naming: the model treats recent events as established facts when they are still contested in the literature and in the courts. The training cutoff matters. Anything that happened after the cutoff is fabricated or absent. Anything that happened near the cutoff is reported with the confidence of established fact when the historical assessment has not yet settled.

The rule that covers all three: the model is useful for the parts of American government that have stopped moving — the constitutional structure, the established doctrine, the canonical scholarship. It is unreliable on the parts that are still moving — contemporary partisan conflict, recent court decisions, the latest legislation. Use the model for the framework; verify the contemporary specifics against primary sources from named outlets with known editorial positions; and read the model's prose on contested questions assuming it carries inflection, because it does.
