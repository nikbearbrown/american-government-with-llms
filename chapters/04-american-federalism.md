# Chapter 4 — American Federalism
*The Machine Nobody Designed and Everyone Inherited.*

Here is a puzzle worth sitting with before we go any further.

Imagine you are one of the people who designed the United States government in 1787. You have a real problem in front of you — not a philosophical one, a *practical* one. The country is falling apart. The thirteen states are behaving like thirteen foreign nations. They are taxing each other's goods. They are refusing to pay war debts. The government under the Articles of Confederation has no power to make them do anything — it can pass laws, but it cannot enforce them. It is a government that governs nothing.

And yet: the same people you are trying to govern just finished fighting a war against a distant king who had too much power over them. They are not going to hand their autonomy to a new central government and trust that it behaves better than the last one.

So what do you build?

This is the actual design problem of 1787. Not a clash of ideologies — a mechanical engineering problem. How do you get the benefits of a strong national government without recreating the thing you just revolted against? The answer the framers invented was *federalism*: two governments, both real, both elected, both authorized to act directly on citizens, each with domains the other cannot legally enter.

That invention is still running. Almost every major constitutional dispute that reaches the Supreme Court today is, at some level, a dispute about where the line between those two governments falls. To understand American politics, you have to understand why the line exists, where it was drawn, and how it keeps moving.

---

## The Mechanism

Let me be precise about what federalism actually is, because it is easy to confuse with things that look similar.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: three-column comparison — unitary system (top-down hierarchy, national → local), confederation (bottom-up, states → center), federalism (dual-level, both arrows bidirectional with citizens at base). Use the structural diagram format; this is the foundational conceptual map the chapter returns to.] -->

In a *unitary system* — Britain, France, Japan — the national government is supreme. Whatever authority subnational governments have, they have because the national government gave it to them and can take it back. Local councils in England exist because Parliament allows them to exist.

In a *confederation* — the original United States under the Articles — it works the other way. The central authority depends on the subnational units. The Continental Congress could not tax citizens directly; it had to ask states to contribute money, and states could refuse.

Federalism splits this difference. Both levels are elected by the people. Both can act directly on citizens — you can be prosecuted by the federal government or by your state government; you can be taxed by both; you can vote in federal elections and state elections. Neither level gets its authority from the other. They both get it from the Constitution.

Five things make this work. First, two levels of government, both independently elected. Second, a written constitution that is hard to change — in America, you need two-thirds of Congress and three-fourths of the states, which is why the Constitution has only twenty-seven amendments in two hundred and thirty years. Third, each level has its own legislative, executive, and judicial apparatus. Fourth, national courts can referee disputes between the levels. Fifth — and this is important — the states have representation inside the national legislature. The Senate gives every state two seats regardless of population. Wyoming and California both send two senators. This means state interests do not just fight the federal government from outside; they shape it from within.

<!-- → [TABLE: five structural features of American federalism — columns: feature, what it provides, what happens without it. Rows: dual elected governments, hard-to-amend constitution, parallel apparatus at each level, national referee courts, state representation in Congress. This earns a table because removing any single feature collapses the system.] -->

These five features are not decoration. Remove any one of them and you no longer have federalism. Remove the hard-to-amend constitution and the national government can simply rewrite the rules when the states resist. Remove the courts that can referee and the two levels have no neutral arbiter. Remove state representation in Congress and states are governed by a body that has no structural reason to care about state interests.

## What Goes Where

The Constitution specifies what Congress can do. Article I, Section 8 lists eighteen powers: regulate commerce among the states and with foreign nations, coin money, raise armies, declare war, and so on. These are called *enumerated powers*. They represent what the framers were willing to hand to the national government.

Everything not on that list — the framers assumed — would remain with the states. But they wanted assurance in writing, so the Tenth Amendment says it explicitly: "The powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people." States kept education, criminal law, property law, family law, most health and safety regulation.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: Venn diagram of power distribution — left circle: exclusive federal powers (declare war, coin money, regulate interstate commerce, foreign affairs); right circle: exclusive state powers (education, criminal law, property law, family law, police power); center overlap: concurrent powers (taxation, borrowing, courts, law enforcement). Caption should note the elastic clause expands the left circle over time.] -->

Now, here is where it gets interesting. The last clause of Article I, Section 8 — the one called the *elastic clause* or the *necessary and proper clause* — says Congress can make any law "necessary and proper" to carry out its enumerated powers. That clause has stretched for two centuries. When Congress created a national bank (not mentioned in the Constitution), the Supreme Court said it was necessary and proper to Congress's powers over taxation and commerce — and added, in *McCulloch v. Maryland* (1819), that states could not tax federal institutions. That ruling established the principle of national supremacy within the federal sphere that has governed constitutional interpretation ever since. When Congress regulated manufacturing conditions, agriculture, and labor relations, the Court eventually said those activities substantially affect interstate commerce, which is enumerated, so they are fair game.

The elastic clause is what makes the enumerated powers elastic. It transforms a list of eighteen specific powers into a foundation for nearly unlimited legislation, as long as you can draw a plausible line to something on the list.

This is not a legal technicality. It is the core tension of American federalism: the national government has bounded authority, but the bounds have kept expanding because the elastic clause expands with every new way human activity can be connected to interstate commerce.

Some powers both governments exercise — these are called *concurrent powers*. You pay federal income tax and state income tax. Both governments can borrow money. Both can run courts, make laws, and enforce them. The limit is the *supremacy clause* (Article VI): when a state law conflicts with a valid federal law, the federal law wins. But the federal law has to be constitutional — it has to rest on one of those enumerated or implied powers. Where the Constitution leaves an area to the states, federal law cannot simply overrun it.

Here is a concrete example of how that plays out. Since the 1970s, more than three dozen states have legalized marijuana in some form. Federal law still lists it as a Schedule I controlled substance — a federal crime. Both things are simultaneously true. The federal government has not generally chosen to prosecute individual users in states that have legalized it. This is not because federal law is invalid. It is because federalism, in practice, works partly through law and partly through political choice about which legal authority to actually exercise. The supremacy clause exists; it does not fire automatically.

## The System Breaks and Rebuilds Itself

Here is something that textbooks often miss: the Constitution does not specify what the *relationship* between the two levels should look like in any given policy area. That gets worked out through political conflict, Supreme Court decisions, and sometimes crisis.

For about sixty years after the Civil War — roughly the 1870s through the 1930s — the dominant interpretation kept federal and state authority in relatively separate spheres. The Supreme Court drew sharp lines. In *United States v. E.C. Knight* (1895) it ruled the federal government could not regulate manufacturing, only commerce between states. In *Lochner v. New York* (1905) it struck down New York's law capping bakery work hours, saying it violated the freedom of contract between employer and employee. Think of this as a *layer cake*: federal layers and state layers, visually distinct, not mixing.

Labels beneath: "Dual federalism (pre-New Deal)" and "Cooperative federalism (post-1937)". Then the Great Depression hit, and the layer cake model stopped being sustainable.

Between 1929 and 1933, unemployment reached twenty-five percent. Industrial production fell by half. Thousands of banks failed. States had no money. Their tax bases had collapsed. People were starving in a country that had, until very recently, believed local government and private charity could handle poverty. They could not.

The federal government was the only institution with the borrowing capacity and the legal authority to act at scale. Congress created Social Security, the Securities and Exchange Commission, the Civilian Conservation Corps, federal unemployment insurance. President Roosevelt pushed programs that blurred the line between state and federal responsibility in ways that would have been unthinkable a decade earlier.

The Supreme Court initially blocked some of these programs — too much federal reach into state domains. Roosevelt proposed adding justices to the Court until he had enough votes to change the outcome. Before Congress acted, the Court shifted. In *National Labor Relations Board v. Jones & Laughlin Steel* (1937), it upheld the National Labor Relations Act, saying Congress could regulate manufacturing because manufacturing affected interstate commerce. The door between the spheres swung open and has not fully closed since.

What replaced the layer cake is something messier. Call it a *marble cake*: federal and state colors mixed together throughout. Congress funds Medicaid, but states administer it and set eligibility rules within federal minimums. Congress funds highways but sets speed limits and drinking ages as conditions of that funding. States regulate education, but federal money comes with requirements about civil rights compliance and reporting. By 2020, federal grants accounted for thirty percent of state government revenue. When a government receives thirty percent of its budget from another government that attaches conditions to the money, the question of who is really in charge becomes genuinely complicated.

This is the system that political scientists call *cooperative federalism*, and the cooperation is not always voluntary.

## The Carrot and the Stick

The federal government has two main tools to shape what states do.

The first is *grants* — money that does not have to be repaid. From 1960 to 2020, federal grant money to states grew from roughly seven billion to seven hundred billion dollars (adjusted for inflation). The biggest chunk is health care: Medicaid spending went from $43.8 billion in 1990 to $320 billion by 2014.

<!-- → [CHART: line chart showing federal grants to states 1960–2020 in inflation-adjusted dollars, with Medicaid spending as a second line beginning in 1965. Two clear trends: total grants growing 100x; Medicaid growing to dominate the total. Y-axis in billions. Should make viscerally clear how dependent states have become on federal money.] -->

But grants come in forms that give states very different amounts of freedom.

*Categorical grants* give money with conditions attached. Medicaid is a categorical grant — the federal government sets rules about who qualifies, what services are covered, and what states have to contribute. States get discretion within those rules, but not outside them. Of the 1,099 federal grants distributed in 2014, 1,078 were categorical. Twenty-one were block grants.

*Block grants* come with fewer strings. The Workforce Investment Act gives money to help workers retrain but lets states decide how. Block grants let states adapt programs to local needs. But Congress prefers categorical grants because they let individual members point to specific outcomes and claim credit.

*General revenue sharing*, which existed from 1972 to 1986, had almost no conditions — eighty-five billion dollars distributed to states and localities with minimal rules. President Reagan eliminated it.

<!-- → [TABLE: three-column comparison of grant types — rows: categorical grants, block grants, general revenue sharing; columns: federal control level, state discretion level, real-world example, current status. The 1,078-to-21 ratio of categorical to block grants belongs in the categorical row.] -->

The second tool is *unfunded mandates* — requirements imposed on states without sufficient money to pay for them. The Real ID Act of 2005 required states to verify identity on driver's licenses with standardized technology. The federal government estimated the cost at eleven billion dollars and paid a fraction of it. States had to comply or see their citizens unable to board airplanes. It worked. All fifty states complied, eventually — after years of extensions and pressure. But it worked by passing the cost to state budgets.

The Civil Rights Act, the Americans with Disabilities Act, environmental regulations, campus crime reporting requirements — all of these impose costs on states that the federal government does not fully fund. Congress can vote for a national goal without raising federal taxes; the states absorb the expense. This is sometimes called *coercive federalism*, and it has grown steadily since the 1970s.

## Three Tensions That Do Not Resolve

Understanding the machinery is not enough. What the machinery *produces* is a set of persistent tensions that are not solved by the Constitution and are not going to be solved anytime soon.

**Uniformity versus diversity.** Some things should work the same everywhere — currency, national defense, interstate trade rules. Other things arguably should vary — school curricula, criminal penalties, family law. But which category does any given policy belong in? Immigration was traditionally federal. But Arizona, concluding in 2010 that federal immigration law was not being enforced to the state's satisfaction, passed a law making it harder for undocumented immigrants to work, rent housing, or use services. The federal government sued. In *Arizona v. United States* (2012), the Supreme Court affirmed federal supremacy over immigration but let one provision stand — police could ask about immigration status during a lawful stop. The venue decided the outcome: the federal government has primacy over immigration, but states can do things at the margin.

Same-sex marriage worked differently. States defined marriage. The Defense of Marriage Act (1996) said states could choose whether to recognize same-sex marriages. In *United States v. Windsor* (2013) the Supreme Court struck down the part of the Act that prevented the federal government from recognizing such marriages. In *Obergefell v. Hodges* (2015) it made marriage equality a constitutional right, removing the issue from states entirely. Federalism did not resolve the dispute. The Court did, by declaring one answer correct for all fifty states simultaneously.

**Accountability versus efficiency.** Cooperative federalism is efficient — set a standard nationally, enforce it locally. But it fractures accountability. When a state fails to meet federal education standards, is that the federal government's failure or the state's? When federal Medicaid funding runs short and a state cuts benefits, who is responsible — the Congress that capped the funding or the governor who made the cut?

The Temporary Assistance to Needy Families program gives states a fixed block grant — $16.5 billion annually, a cap that has not been adjusted for inflation since 1996. States decide eligibility and benefit levels. If a state runs out of money mid-year, it cuts benefits. The federal government is not obligated to pay more. But the federal government declared the goal: help poor families. When poor families are not helped, the accountability is distributed so widely that no one bears it cleanly.

**Venue shopping.** This one is not a flaw — it is structural. Multiple levels of government mean multiple points of access. A group that loses at one level can try another.

Mothers Against Drunk Driving spent the early 1980s pushing states to raise the drinking age from eighteen to twenty-one. State legislators resisted — lower drinking ages brought in young adults from neighboring states. So MADD went to Congress. In 1984 Congress passed a crosscutting mandate: raise the drinking age to twenty-one or lose ten percent of federal highway funding. All fifty states complied by 1988. MADD won by changing venues.

Anti-abortion groups moved in the opposite direction. After *Roe v. Wade* (1973) established a federal constitutional right to abortion, federal legislation for a total ban was not available — Congress would not pass it. So the focus shifted to state legislatures, where incremental restrictions accumulated over decades: parental involvement requirements, waiting periods, prohibitions on public funding. The *Dobbs* decision in 2022 returned the question to states entirely, completing the venue shift the movement had been working toward for fifty years.

The same policy, the same interest group, a different venue — and a different outcome. Federalism makes this possible. The multiple access points mean defeat is rarely permanent and victory is rarely absolute.

## What the Machine Actually Produces

Take all of this together. You have a system with two levels of government, both real, both elected, competing and cooperating simultaneously across dozens of policy domains. The federal government funds thirty percent of state budgets and attaches conditions to the money. States pass laws the federal government dislikes, and the federal government sues. Interest groups try one level, then the other, until they find an answer they like or run out of levels. Courts referee constantly, and the outcomes are sometimes hard to reconcile with each other.

Is this efficient? No, not particularly. Is it stable? Somewhat — it has been running for two hundred and thirty years. Is it fair? That depends entirely on what you think uniformity and diversity each owe to citizens.

What it definitely is: the structure within which everything else in American politics happens. The fights over civil rights, elections, environmental law, health care, immigration — all of them play out in federalism's arena. Different parties have different preferences about which level should win on which issue, and those preferences shift as the parties' coalitions change. A party that controls Congress tends to favor federal solutions. The same party, when it loses Congress and holds state houses, tends to discover the virtues of state autonomy.

The framers did not design this because they thought it was theoretically optimal. They designed it because it was the only solution to a real problem that had a chance of being ratified. The system they built has the strengths and weaknesses of things designed to solve practical problems under political pressure: it works, it is inelegant, it creates new problems while solving old ones, and it proves very hard to replace.

## What Would Change My Mind

Evidence that the federal government had actually returned meaningful authority to states — not just in rhetoric but in measurable spending and rule-setting — would require rethinking whether contemporary federalism is genuinely evolving or merely decorated with devolution language while federal control persists through funding conditions and mandates.

## Still Puzzling

Why the Supreme Court sometimes enforces federalism's boundaries — striking down a federal gun-free school zones law in *United States v. Lopez* (1995) as exceeding the commerce clause — and sometimes ignores them — permitting federal regulation of a farmer's wheat grown entirely for his own consumption in *Wickard v. Filburn* (1942) — remains genuinely unclear to me. The cases seem inconsistent on what counts as "substantially affecting" interstate commerce. I suspect the Court is making political judgments under the cover of legal doctrine, but I cannot prove it.
