# American Federalism

**TL;DR:** Federalism divides power between the national and state governments not as a philosophical choice but as a practical solution to a real problem — how to preserve state autonomy while building national capacity. That tension between unity and diversity has reshaped itself three times in American history, and today it drives most of the constitutional disputes that reach the Supreme Court.

---

## 1. The Constitutional Puzzle: Why Two Governments?

In 1787, the framers faced an urgent design problem. The Articles of Confederation, which had bound the thirteen states since 1781, had failed to bind them at all. States refused to pay war debts. They blocked trade across their borders. They raised tariffs against each other's goods as if they were foreign nations. The national government lacked the power to enforce anything — it could not defend the country, regulate interstate commerce, or even print stable currency.

Yet the states that had just fought off a distant king were not ready to hand all power to a new central authority. They had fought for *local* self-determination, not to replace London's control with Philadelphia's.

The solution was federalism: divide power explicitly. The Constitution named certain powers as federal — Congress could regulate interstate commerce, raise armies, declare war, coin money. Everything else was reserved to the states. Neither level was subordinate to the other; each had direct authority over citizens within its domain. You answered to both governments at once.

This was not philosophy. It was mechanical problem-solving.

What did the framers actually create? Let's be precise: *Federalism is an institutional arrangement that creates two relatively autonomous levels of government, each possessing the capacity to act directly on behalf of the people with authority granted by the national constitution.* That definition matters because it rules out things that look similar but work differently. In a unitary system (Britain until the 1990s, France today, Japan), the national government is supreme and subnational governments depend on it. In a confederation (the original United States under the Articles), the central authority depends on the subnational units for power. Federalism splits the difference: both levels are elected by the people, both can act directly, both have domains the other cannot legally enter.

Five structural features make a federal system work:

1. **Two levels of government, both elected and autonomous.** The national government handles matters affecting the whole country — defense, foreign trade, currency. State governments handle local matters — schools, public safety, property law. Citizens elect leaders at each level.

2. **A written constitution that cannot be changed without broad consent.** In America, amendments require two-thirds of both houses of Congress plus three-fourths of the states. This prevents a sudden power grab. It also means the system can calcify — the Equal Rights Amendment failed because only thirty-eight states ratified it, one short of the thirty-eight needed, after decades of waiting.

3. **Constitutional division of legislative, executive, and judicial power between levels.** Congress legislates at the national level; state legislatures at the state level. The president enforces federal law; governors enforce state law. Federal courts interpret the Constitution; state courts interpret state law. But — and this is critical — they interact constantly. Federal law enforcement depends on state police. State highway funding depends on federal money.

4. **National courts arbitrate disputes between levels.** When a state challenges federal authority or a state law conflicts with federal law, the Supreme Court decides. In recent decades, the Court struck down state bans on same-sex marriage and mostly rejected Arizona's immigration law, but also limited federal commerce clause power in some cases.

5. **Subnational representation in the national legislature.** The Senate gives each state two seats regardless of population. This means Wyoming's 577,000 people have the same Senate voice as California's 39 million. It also means state interests shape national law from inside Congress itself.

## 2. Division of Powers: The Work of Specification

How power actually divides — this is not settled by abstract principle. It is settled by reading the document precisely and watching how courts interpret it when disputes arise.

**Enumerated powers** — the ones Congress explicitly received under Article I, Section 8 — are the federal government's core domain: taxing, borrowing, regulating interstate and foreign commerce, coining money, raising armies, declaring war. The list runs to eighteen specific items. It is detailed but not exhaustive.

The gap was filled by the **elastic clause** (also called the necessary and proper clause), the last clause of Article I, Section 8. Congress can make any law "necessary and proper" to carry out its enumerated powers. That clause looks modest. It is not. It has permitted Congress to create a national bank, regulate manufacturing, enforce labor standards, and build a highway system — none of which the Constitution names explicitly.

**Reserved powers** — the ones kept by the states — were never listed in the original Constitution. The framers assumed states would retain everything the federal government was not given. But states and ratifiers wanted assurance. The Tenth Amendment provided it: "The powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people." States retained education, criminal law, property law, most health and safety regulation, and family law (marriage, divorce, inheritance).

But — and this is the tension that shapes federalism today — boundaries blur. The federal government can now affect nearly any area through taxation, through grants that come with conditions, through interstate commerce regulation. States still regulate education, but the federal government funds part of it and sets conditions (Title VI of the Civil Rights Act, the Clery Act requiring campus crime reporting). States regulate criminal law, but the federal government prosecutes federal crimes, uses federal grants to condition local policing, and sets minimum standards for rights.

**Concurrent powers** are powers both levels exercise: taxing, borrowing, making and enforcing laws, running court systems. You pay federal income tax and state income tax (in most states). Both governments can sue. Both can regulate the same activity to some degree, as long as state law does not conflict with federal law.

**Federal restrictions** on its own power prevent Congress from suspending habeas corpus, passing bills of attainder (convicting someone without trial), or enacting ex post facto laws (punishing retroactively). The Bill of Rights further restrains federal power over speech, religion, guns, and other liberties.

**State restrictions** are equally clear: states cannot enter treaties, coin money, tax imports, or suspend habeas corpus. The Fourteenth Amendment (1868) prevents states from denying due process or equal protection. Three civil rights amendments prevent both the federal government and states from restricting voting based on race (15th), sex (19th), or age (26th).

The **supremacy clause** (Article VI) says the Constitution and federal law are the supreme law of the land. If a state law conflicts with a valid federal law, federal law prevails. But federal law must be constitutional — it must rest on an enumerated power or one implied from them. State law in areas the Constitution leaves to the states is not overridden. A state can license professions, set school curricula, regulate intrastate commerce — and its law stands unless the Constitution or a valid federal law says otherwise.

Watch this principle at work in practice. Since the 1970s, thirty-six states have legalized medical marijuana, some its recreational use. The federal government lists marijuana as a Schedule I controlled substance, a federal crime. Why have the states done this? Because the Constitution reserves drug regulation to the states in areas not involving interstate commerce. The federal government *could* enforce federal drug law aggressively. It largely has not. This is not because federal law is invalid. It is because both levels have chosen not to push the supremacy clause. This illustrates something real about federalism: it works partly through law, partly through forbearance — by not using every power you technically have.

## 3. Evolution from Dual to Cooperative Federalism: The Shift in Who Pays and Who Controls

The machinery of federalism does not stay the same. The Constitution sketches a framework, but it does not prescribe how state and federal governments relate in specific policy areas. That gets worked out through Supreme Court decisions, congressional acts, and political conflict. Over two centuries, the relationship has passed through distinct phases.

**Dual federalism (1870s–1930s)** treated the layers like a layer cake — each level exercised power in clearly defined, non-overlapping spheres. States controlled education, criminal law, property, local commerce. The federal government controlled currency, foreign trade, and military affairs. Supreme Court rulings reinforced these boundaries. In *United States v. E.C. Knight* (1895), the Court ruled the federal government could not regulate manufacturing — only commerce among states. In *Lochner v. New York* (1905), the Court struck down New York's law capping bakery work hours, saying that interfered with the liberty of contract between employer and employee — a state concern immune from both state and federal interference.

This system crumbled in the 1930s.

The Great Depression shattered the assumption that state and local governments could handle their own affairs. Between 1929 and 1933, unemployment hit 25 percent, industrial production dropped by half, thousands of banks failed, and the GDP shrank a quarter. States had no money. The tax base collapsed. People starved. The federal government was the only institution with resources and borrowing capacity. Congress created the Securities and Exchange Commission, Social Security, the National Labor Relations Board, the Civilian Conservation Corps. President Roosevelt proposed programs that blurred the line between state and federal responsibility.

The Supreme Court initially blocked them — the Court struck down the National Industrial Recovery Act and the Agricultural Adjustment Act, saying they invaded state authority. Furious, Roosevelt proposed a court-packing plan: add judges until he had enough votes to change the outcome.

Before Congress acted, the Court blinked. In *National Labor Relations Board v. Jones & Laughlin Steel* (1937), it reversed course. Congress could regulate manufacturing because manufacturing affected interstate commerce, the Court said. The commerce clause was no longer a boundary. It was an open door.

**Cooperative federalism (1930s–1970s)** was born of necessity and became habitual. Both levels of government coordinated to solve national problems. The image changed: from a layer cake to a *marble cake*, with colors mixed throughout. 

The New Deal created Social Security. The federal government set up the program and funded part of it; states and the federal government jointly administered it. States set eligibility rules and benefit levels — with federal minimum standards. Unemployment insurance worked the same way: federal law set the framework; states taxed employers and determined benefit levels. Medicaid (1965) was constructed the same way: federal and state funds, federal minimums, state flexibility.

This arrangement made sense. Richer states could spend more; poorer states got federal help ensuring some baseline. It also meant states closer to citizens could customize programs to local needs. The cost: by 2020, federal grant money had become 30 percent of state revenue, and federal grants often came with conditions. The federal government paid for the highways but set speed limits and drinking-age laws. It funded education but required civil rights compliance and campus crime reporting.

One landmark case illustrates how far the power had shifted. In *McCulloch v. Maryland* (1819), the Supreme Court ruled Congress could charter a national bank even though the Constitution did not say so explicitly — because it was "necessary and proper" to fulfill Congress's powers over taxation and commerce. The Court also said states could not tax federal property (Maryland tried to tax the Baltimore branch of the Second Bank). The principle was national supremacy: *the federal government is supreme within its sphere of action.*

This principle, stated in 1819, took two centuries to fully play out. But by the 1960s it was clear: the federal government could reach into nearly every area — education, health, welfare, environment, labor — by tying federal money and regulations to state and local action.

## 4. The Carrot and the Stick: How the Federal Government Shapes State Action Today

The federal government has two tools to make states cooperate. One incentivizes. One compels.

**Grants** are money transfers from the federal government to state and local governments that do not have to be repaid. The national government has used them since the 1780s, when it gave states land to build roads and canals. By the 1900s, states had shifted to wanting cash, not land.

There are three types of grants, arranged by how much flexibility they allow.

*Categorical grants* give money but restrict how it is spent. Medicaid and the food stamp program are categorical grants. The federal government sets detailed rules: who qualifies, what services are covered, what overhead can be charged. States get discretion within those rules, but not outside them. In 2014, the federal government distributed 1,099 grants — 1,078 were categorical. Only 21 were block grants.

Why does the federal government prefer categorical grants? Control. When a congressman can point to clean air, better-funded schools, or healthier children, the congressman takes credit. If a state messes up a block grant program, the state is blamed, not the federal grant-maker. Federal agencies also have career incentives to preserve the programs they manage. Once a categorical grant exists, federal bureaucrats fight to keep it.

*Block grants* come with fewer strings. The Workforce Investment Act gives money to help workers retrain but lets states and local agencies decide how. Block grants let states innovate, and they cost less to administer. But they are less popular in Congress because credit for outcomes is harder to claim.

*General revenue sharing* (1972–1986) had almost no conditions. The federal government distributed $85 billion to states, cities, and towns with minimal rules about how to spend it. President Reagan killed it, preferring categories or nothing.

Grants have grown dramatically. From 1960 to 2020, federal grant money to states increased from roughly $7 billion to $700 billion (adjusted for inflation). The biggest jump was in health care: Medicaid spending went from $43.8 billion in 1990 to $320 billion in 2014. By 2020, federal grants accounted for 30 percent of state government revenue.

**Unfunded mandates** are the stick. These are federal laws that require states to do something without paying for it, or paying only part of the cost. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 says states cannot discriminate in any program receiving federal money — a reasonable condition attached to federal dollars. But some mandates arrive with no money.

The Real ID Act of 2005 required states to verify identity on driver's licenses using standardized technology and data. The federal government estimated the cost at $11 billion but reimbursed only a fraction. States had to comply or see citizens unable to board airplanes. The mandate worked — by 2020, all fifty states had complied — but only after federal government kept granting extensions.

The Unfunded Mandates Reform Act (1995) tried to restrain this practice by requiring Congress to consider the cost to states before imposing mandates. It has not worked. Federal mandates on environment, civil rights, education, and homeland security have only increased. The reason: a mandate lets the federal government accomplish a national goal while passing the cost to states. A congressman can vote for strong air-quality standards without raising federal taxes; the cost lands on state budgets.

This is coercive federalism — using money and mandates to bend state behavior to federal goals. It works. But it is often unpopular with states, which see their discretion slipping away even as they still hold legal authority.

## 5. Integration: Federalism Under Stress

Three contemporary pressures expose federalism's deepest tensions.

**First, uniformity versus diversity.** The federal system assumes some policies should be uniform (national defense, currency, interstate commerce rules) and others should vary by state (education, criminal law, family law). But which policies belong in which category?

Immigration was traditionally a federal matter. States did not make immigration policy. But since the late 1990s, states have asserted a role. Arizona passed Senate Bill 1070 in 2010, which attempted to deter undocumented immigration by making it harder for undocumented people to work, rent housing, or use services. The federal government sued. In *Arizona v. United States* (2012), the Supreme Court affirmed federal supremacy over immigration policy but let one provision stand: police could ask about immigration status during a lawful stop (though the Court warned against racial profiling).

Why did Arizona act? Because federal immigration law was not being enforced to Arizona's satisfaction. The state saw undocumented immigration as a problem affecting its economy and services. So it took action. This is competitive federalism — states and the federal government competing for authority over an issue.

The same pattern appeared with same-sex marriage. Before 2013, the federal government had not said marriage was a federal issue; states defined it. The Defense of Marriage Act (1996) codified this — states could choose whether to recognize same-sex marriage. But in *United States v. Windsor* (2013), the Supreme Court ruled that the federal government could not define marriage or deny benefits to same-sex married couples. That shifted the playing field. In *Obergefell v. Hodges* (2015), the Court made marriage equality a constitutional right, taking the issue away from states entirely. Federalism did not resolve the dispute; the Supreme Court did.

**Second, accountability versus efficiency.** Cooperative federalism is efficient — the federal government can set a standard, and states enforce it. But who is accountable? When a state fails to meet a federal standard, is that the federal government's failure or the state's? When federal money runs out and a state has to choose between a program and a bridge, who is responsible?

This matters for welfare policy. Under the Temporary Assistance to Needy Families (TANF) program, the federal government gives states a capped lump sum ($16.5 billion annually). States decide eligibility and benefit levels. If a state runs out of money mid-year, it cuts benefits or stops enrolling people. The federal government is not obligated to pay more. But the federal government set the goal (help poor families). Who is accountable when poor families are not helped?

**Third, venue shopping.** Interest groups know they can go federal or state to get what they want. Mothers Against Drunk Driving (MADD) started in 1980 pushing states to raise the drinking age from eighteen to twenty-one. State legislators would not budge — lower drinking ages attracted youth from neighboring states, a small economic advantage. So MADD went to Congress. In 1984, Congress passed the National Minimum Drinking Age Act, a crosscutting mandate: states had to raise the drinking age to twenty-one or lose federal highway funding. All states complied by 1988. MADD succeeded by venue shopping — redirecting from a resistant venue (state legislatures) to a receptive one (Congress).

Anti-abortion groups did the opposite. After *Roe v. Wade* (1973) made abortion a federal constitutional right, they lobbied Congress for a federal ban. Congress would not pass one. So they shifted focus to state legislatures, where they have been more successful. By 2015, thirty-eight states required parental involvement in minors' abortions, forty-six allowed doctors to refuse to participate, and thirty-two prohibited public funding. The 2022 *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization* decision returned abortion to the states entirely, which anti-abortion groups had sought. The venue still matters.

This is not a flaw in federalism. It is a feature. Multiple access points mean that groups defeated at one level can try another. But it also means that power oscillates. The federal government pushes; states push back. Courts referee.

**Warm-up:** 

Divide a piece of paper into three columns. Label them "National Government," "State Governments," and "Both." List ten policy areas (education, criminal law, environmental protection, marriage, immigration, etc.). Place each in the column that best describes where authority primarily rests today. Then compare your list with a classmate's. Where did you disagree? What constitutional provisions or court rulings explain the difference?

**Application:**

In 2020, California issued stay-at-home orders for COVID-19 before the federal government did. In 2021, Florida prohibited vaccine mandates, while California required them. Arizona passed a strict immigration law; California passed a law providing COVID aid to undocumented immigrants. 

Pick one of these examples. Research what the Constitution says about federal versus state authority in that area. Find any relevant Supreme Court cases. Write a one-page memo to your state legislator explaining whether your state's action was constitutionally sound and whether you think it was good policy.

**Synthesis:**

The Affordable Care Act (2010) gives states the option to expand Medicaid (the federal government pays 90 percent of the cost). As of 2021, twelve states had not expanded Medicaid. Some states said the cost was still too high; others said federal funding might be cut in the future; some opposed the law ideologically.

From a federalism perspective, what are the merits and drawbacks of giving states this choice? What would happen if the federal government simply required all states to expand? What if it let each state design its own health insurance system from scratch? Which approach do you think best serves citizens, and why?

## 7. Summary

Federalism divides power between two levels of government, both elected and both authorized by the Constitution to act directly on citizens. The framers adopted federalism to solve a real problem: how to preserve state autonomy while building a strong national government.

The Constitution names federal powers explicitly (enumerated powers) and reserves the rest to states (reserved powers). Some powers are concurrent — both levels exercise them. The supremacy clause says federal law prevails when it conflicts with state law, but federal law must be constitutional.

Federalism has evolved through distinct phases. Dual federalism (1870s–1930s) kept state and federal spheres separate. Cooperative federalism (1930s–1970s) blended them, with the federal government funding programs jointly with states and attaching conditions to federal money. Today, the federal government uses categorical grants (money with strings) and unfunded mandates (requirements without payment) to shape state action. States push back through litigation, venue shopping, and political pressure.

Contemporary federalism stresses three tensions: uniformity versus diversity (should immigration and marriage be national or state issues?), accountability versus efficiency (who is responsible when federal-state programs fail?), and venue shopping (can interest groups keep trying until they get the answer they want?). The Supreme Court often has the final say, but the Constitution leaves room for argument, and that argument is rarely settled.

It is the playing field on which American politics happens.

## What Would Change My Mind

Evidence that the federal government had actually returned meaningful authority to states — not just in rhetoric but in measurable spending and rule-setting — would require rethinking whether contemporary federalism is genuinely "new" or merely decorated with devolution language while federal control persists through funding conditions and mandates.

## Still Puzzling

Why the Supreme Court sometimes enforces federalism boundaries (striking down federal mandates under the commerce clause in *United States v. Lopez*) and sometimes ignores them (permitting extensive federal regulation of agriculture in *Wickard v. Filburn*) remains unclear to me — the cases seem inconsistent on the question of what counts as substantially affecting interstate commerce, and I suspect the Court is making political judgments under the cover of legal doctrine.
