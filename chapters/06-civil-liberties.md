# Chapter 6 — Civil Liberties: When Rights Collide
*The Architecture of Freedom*

---

Here is what a civil liberty actually is.

It is not a positive grant. The government does not give you freedom of speech the way it might give you a tax credit or a driver's license. A civil liberty is a prohibition — a fence around something the government cannot do to you. The First Amendment does not say "citizens may speak." It says Congress shall make *no law* abridging speech. The grammar is negative. The constraint is on government, not a gift to you.

This distinction matters because it determines where the argument lives. When someone claims their civil liberties were violated, they are not asking "did the government help me?" They are asking "did the government stay in its lane?" The two questions have completely different answers.

Hold that frame. We'll need it.

---

## The Problem That Had to Be Solved First

For the first 130 years of American constitutional history, the Bill of Rights had a significant limitation that most people today find surprising: it only applied to the federal government.

The First Amendment says "Congress shall make no law..." It says nothing about state legislatures. The Fourth Amendment protects against unreasonable searches by federal officers. It said nothing, originally, about state police. The Sixth Amendment guarantees the right to counsel in federal prosecutions. A state court could run trials without lawyers if it wanted to. The text did not prohibit it.

This was not an accident. The framers were building constraints on the government they were creating — the federal one. The states already had their own constitutions with their own rights guarantees. The framers assumed those would handle things at the state level.

The assumption collapsed after the Civil War. The Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery. The Fourteenth Amendment, ratified in 1868, said something broader: no state shall deprive any person of "life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." The word "liberty" was not defined. It was not attached to a list. It was left open — capacious, ambiguous, a container waiting to be filled.

What filled it, slowly, over several decades, was the Bill of Rights.

The mechanism is called the incorporation doctrine, and it is worth understanding precisely because it is not automatic or obvious. The Court did not simply declare, at some point, that the Fourteenth Amendment swallowed the Bill of Rights. Instead, the Court took one freedom at a time and asked a pair of questions: Is this freedom so fundamental that the concept of liberty would be hollow without it? Is it deeply rooted in the nation's history and tradition? If the answer to both was yes, that freedom was "incorporated" — absorbed into the Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee, now binding on the states.

The process was slow. The First Amendment's speech protections were incorporated in 1925. The right against self-incrimination in 1964. The right to counsel in 1963. The Second Amendment's right to keep and bear arms was not incorporated until 2010 — more than 140 years after the Fourteenth Amendment. The Third Amendment (quartering soldiers) and the Seventh Amendment (civil jury trials) have never been incorporated. The machine is still running.

Here is what this means in practice. Before 1963, a state could put you on trial for murder without providing you a lawyer if you could not afford one. Clarence Gideon found this out in Florida. He was arrested for breaking and entering, could not afford a lawyer, asked the court for one, was refused, represented himself, lost, and went to prison. From his cell, he wrote a petition by hand asking the Supreme Court whether Florida had the power to do this. The Court said no. The Sixth Amendment's right to counsel, it held, was so fundamental to a fair trial that it had to apply everywhere — every state, every trial.

The right now existed uniformly. Florida had to provide a lawyer. What the ruling did not — could not — specify was whether that lawyer had to be any good, whether the state had to fund the public defender's office adequately, whether a lawyer handling five hundred cases simultaneously counted as "counsel" in any meaningful sense. The right was incorporated. Its implementation was left to the states, the legislatures, the budgets.

This is the recurring pattern: incorporation names the right and sets the floor. Everything above the floor is a political and resource question.

## What the First Amendment Actually Protects

The First Amendment is forty-five words covering four distinct freedoms: religion (two clauses), speech, press, and assembly. Courts have since treated speech and press as a single category — expression — because the distinction between a newspaper and a pamphlet and a podcast is less meaningful than what all three have in common: they communicate ideas that the government might want to suppress.

<!-- → INFOGRAPHIC: The First Amendment broken into its five components (establishment, free exercise, speech, press, assembly/petition) with one real case illustrating each component and its current status — student should see that "the First Amendment" is actually five distinct protections with different histories and doctrines -->

The religion clauses are interesting because they point in opposite directions simultaneously. The Establishment Clause says the government cannot establish religion — cannot sponsor it, endorse it, make it official doctrine, or fund it as religion. The Free Exercise Clause says the government cannot prohibit religious practice. These two clauses can collide.

A public school cannot lead students in prayer — that would be establishment. A student can pray in school — that is free exercise. The government cannot build a monument to one religion on public land — establishment. A religious organization can apply for a government permit to hold an outdoor gathering — free exercise. In 2022, the Supreme Court added a wrinkle: Maine had a program that reimbursed families for private school tuition, but excluded religious schools. The Court said this violated Free Exercise. Once the state opens a subsidy program, it cannot exclude participants simply because they are religious. Neutrality toward religion requires including it when others are included, not fencing it out.

The boundary between these clauses is not fixed. It moves as courts reinterpret what "establishment" and "free exercise" require in contexts the 1791 framers never imagined.

Speech gets more elaborate treatment, because the government's interest in restricting speech is more direct. The framework the Court has developed over a century distinguishes three categories.

Some speech receives no protection at all. Speech that is a direct incitement to imminent lawless action — "rush this building now" to a crowd already at the doors — is not protected. True threats, where a reasonable listener would understand the speaker as genuinely threatening violence, are not protected. Defamation — knowingly false statements that damage someone's reputation — is not protected. The Court has held, at various times and with varying consistency, that obscenity is not protected, and that "fighting words" — speech that would provoke an immediate violent response from an ordinary person — is not protected.

The important word is "imminent." The history of American free speech law is, in significant part, the history of courts learning to distinguish between speech that creates immediate danger and speech that is merely dangerous in the sense that bad ideas are dangerous. In 1919, a man named Jacob Abrams threw pamphlets from a roof opposing U.S. intervention in Russia. He was convicted under the Espionage Act and sentenced to twenty years. Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes dissented: the only speech government could suppress, he argued, was speech creating a "clear and present danger" of imminent lawless action. Abrams's pamphlets posed no such danger. They were political opposition. It took forty more years for Holmes's dissent to become doctrine.

The second category is speech restrictions that are content-neutral — regulations that control *how* speech is delivered without targeting *what* is said. A city can ban amplified music in public parks at night; the rule applies to all amplified sound, regardless of message. A city cannot ban amplified speech criticizing the mayor; that targets the message. Content-neutral restrictions survive if they serve a substantial government interest and leave open alternative channels for the speech. They are reviewed, but not with hostility.

The third category is the hostile one. Content-based restrictions — laws that regulate speech because of its subject matter or viewpoint — must survive strict scrutiny. The government must show a compelling interest, must use means narrowly tailored to that interest, and must show there is no less restrictive way to achieve the same end. Strict scrutiny is not a polite test. It almost always means the restriction fails.

The reasoning behind this asymmetry is worth stating plainly: democratic governments have an inherent incentive to suppress speech that threatens them, embarrasses them, or undermines their authority. If courts were deferential to those restrictions, the First Amendment would protect speech only when it did not need protecting. The hostile scrutiny is the machinery that makes the protection real.

In 1971, Daniel Ellsberg photocopied a classified Pentagon history of U.S. involvement in Vietnam and gave it to *The New York Times*. The government sued to stop publication. The Supreme Court sided with the newspaper. The government's national security interest was real; the Court did not deny it. But the government had to prove that publishing these specific documents would cause imminent, grave harm. Predicting harm was not enough. The burden was on the government, and it was heavy, and the government did not meet it.

One misconception is worth clearing up directly: the First Amendment protects speech from government punishment. It does not protect speech from all consequences. A private employer can fire you for what you say. A private social media platform can remove your post. A university that is a private institution can discipline you for what you say. The First Amendment constrains the government — not your employer, not your family, not a private platform. The confusion arises because we often think of "freedom of speech" as a social norm. The First Amendment is a legal rule, and it runs against the government, not against society.

## What Happens After Arrest

Rights of the accused are different in character from First Amendment rights. The First Amendment is a constraint the government faces before it acts against you. Rights of the accused are constraints on the *process* the government must follow once it has already decided to act — once you have been arrested and charged.

The Fifth, Sixth, and Eighth Amendments construct this process.

<!-- → TABLE: The Fifth, Sixth, and Eighth Amendment protections relevant to criminal procedure — columns: amendment, specific right, what it prohibits the government from doing, the key case that defined its scope — student should see these as a system of interlocking constraints on how the government processes an accusation, not as isolated rules -->

The Fifth Amendment says you cannot be compelled to testify against yourself in a criminal case. This is the right against self-incrimination — often invoked with the phrase "taking the Fifth." The government must prove your guilt. It cannot force *you* to provide the evidence. The right exists because the alternative — a system in which interrogators can compel confession — produces both injustice and unreliable information. Coerced confessions are not reliable. People say what they think the interrogator wants to hear.

The practical question is how to make this right real in a system where police interrogation is inherently coercive. In 1966, the Supreme Court faced this in *Miranda v. Arizona*. The case involved a man named Ernesto Miranda who had been interrogated for hours, confessed, and whose confession was used to convict him — all without him being told he had a right to remain silent or consult a lawyer. The Court ruled that before any custodial interrogation, police must warn suspects of four things: you have the right to remain silent; anything you say can be used against you; you have the right to a lawyer; if you cannot afford one, one will be provided.

These warnings are not in the Constitution. They are a rule the Court invented to give the right against self-incrimination practical meaning. The Court's logic was direct: once you are in custody, the pressure to speak is inherent. The physical setting, the authority of the officers, the isolation — these create coercion without any officer having to threaten anything. The warnings are an attempt to counteract that pressure by making the suspect aware of his options.

The Sixth Amendment provides the right to counsel — *Gideon*, again — and also the right to a speedy and public trial, to confront witnesses, to know the charges against you. These are the machinery of adversarial justice: a system that assumes the government and the accused are opponents, and that fairness requires equipping both sides rather than simply trusting government to get it right.

The Eighth Amendment forbids cruel and unusual punishment. In 1791, this clearly meant torture and barbaric execution methods — drawing and quartering, the rack. The harder question is what it means in 2025. Courts have held that capital punishment itself is not inherently cruel or unusual, but have been asked in recent years whether specific execution methods — including, in 2024, nitrogen gas — are unconstitutional because they are experimental and their effects cannot be predicted. The language has not changed. Its application in contexts no one imagined in 1791 is a live question.

Here is the tension worth sitting with: every one of these protections makes it harder to convict people. That includes guilty people. The right to remain silent means some murderers will not confess. The right to counsel means some guilty defendants will have competent representation that creates reasonable doubt. Speedy trial requirements mean some cases get dismissed for delay. The framers understood this. The system accepts this. The argument for accepting it is that the alternative — a system in which conviction rates are high because due process is weak — also convicts innocent people, and convicts them efficiently, without reliable mechanisms for distinguishing the innocent from the guilty. Process is the check.

The honest acknowledgment is that the check is imperfect. Public defenders in most states carry enormous caseloads. Miranda warnings can be waived. Evidence can be obtained in ways that technically comply with the rules while violating their spirit. The rights are real. Their enforcement is contingent on resources, political will, and courts that take them seriously.

## The Argument Underneath All of This

Civil liberties share a common structure: they protect the individual at some cost to collective interests.

Protecting speech means protecting bad speech. There is no way to guarantee freedom for the speech you agree with while suppressing the speech you find dangerous, because the government that decides which speech is dangerous will inevitably be tempted to classify its critics' speech as dangerous. The protection has to be broader than the cases you want to protect, or it does not protect anything.

Protecting the accused means protecting some who are guilty. There is no procedure for due process that is simultaneously perfect at convicting the guilty and perfect at exonerating the innocent. Any process that makes conviction harder protects both the falsely accused and some who actually did it.

Protecting religious practice means protecting religions the majority dislikes. A Free Exercise Clause that only protects popular religions is not a Free Exercise Clause. The protection has to extend to minority religions — unpopular ones, strange ones, ones whose practices make the majority uncomfortable — or the majority's religion is established by default.

This is not a design flaw. It is the price of constitutional government. Democratic societies accept these costs because the alternative — a government that can suppress what it dislikes, convict without process, and establish the dominant religion as official — has a worse track record than the imperfect system we have.

The question is always whether the costs are calibrated correctly. The line between protected political speech and incitement to imminent violence is genuinely difficult to draw. The line between effective assistance of counsel and a technically present but practically useless lawyer is genuinely contested. The line between religious exemptions from generally applicable laws and a wholesale override of those laws by religious preference is genuinely unclear. Courts draw these lines case by case, with judgment, in specific contexts, and they sometimes get it wrong in ways that only become visible later.

This is the Lectures-on-Physics version of civil liberties: not a set of timeless principles that the Founders handed down complete, but a set of mechanisms that courts, legislatures, and citizens have been building and arguing about for two hundred years, and that still do not have final answers.

## Connections Forward

Civil liberties are constraints on what the government may do to you. What comes next — civil rights — addresses a different question: whether the government uses its power equally. A government can respect all the civil liberties in the Bill of Rights and still systematically provide worse schools, less police protection, and weaker enforcement of contracts to people of a particular race. Civil liberties say nothing about this. That requires a different framework, built on the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment — the same amendment that made incorporation possible. The machinery overlaps. The questions are distinct.

## What Would Change My Mind

If evidence showed that states with fewer due process protections had lower crime rates and higher public safety outcomes, controlling for population and other variables, I would reconsider whether some of these procedural protections are calibrated too heavily toward the accused. The argument for strong due process is partly principled and partly empirical — that weak process produces unreliable convictions and undermines public trust in the legal system. That empirical claim is falsifiable.

---

## Still Puzzling

I do not yet fully understand why courts have moved so slowly on what counts as cruel and unusual punishment. The Eighth Amendment's language has not changed since 1791, but its application expands slowly and inconsistently. What makes courts confident enough to call a punishment "unusual" when the punishment is not unprecedented, just newly contested? The standard appears to track public opinion — the Court has cited "evolving standards of decency" as the test — but that raises its own problem: if the Eighth Amendment tracks public opinion, what work is it doing that majority rule could not do on its own?
