# Chapter: Civil Liberties — When Rights Collide

## Title Options

1. Civil Liberties — When Rights Collide
2. The Architecture of Freedom — How Constraints Protect Liberty
3. What Rights Actually Cost — The Trade-offs Written into the Bill of Rights

## TL;DR

Civil liberties are limitations on what government *can* do to individuals—but they collide constantly with legitimate public interests. The mechanism that makes them enforceable against states, not just the federal government, is the incorporation doctrine, which treats the Bill of Rights as a moving target, rewritten by courts one case at a time.

---

## Chapter Opening: A Specific Collision

**Present tense. Mid-scene.**

It is January 2017. Border agents at El Paso are holding a three-year-old in one room and her mother in another. The child was taken because her mother had crossed into the United States without authorization. No court order. No finding of guilt. The government had a policy—separate families, hold them in different detention facilities, reunite them later. The question that would travel through the courts for years was straightforward in principle but devastatingly unclear in practice: Did the government's power to enforce immigration law permit this? Or did the Fifth Amendment right not to be deprived of "liberty" without due process protect the bond between parent and child?

This is not an abstract legal puzzle. It is what civil liberties *are*—not timeless principles, but collisions between what government wants to do and what the Constitution says government cannot do to individuals. The collision is where the law lives.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

- Understand the incorporation doctrine and how it made the Bill of Rights apply to state governments

## Concept 1: The Machinery of Incorporation — How Rights Got Applied to States

**Cold open: specific case.**

The year was 1931. A man named Ed Brown had been convicted of murder in Powell, a town in Alabama. The trial lasted three days. Brown had no lawyer. Three men had been executed. Brown's trial *seemed* to happen, but the defense had been a formality. The question the Supreme Court faced was not whether Brown was guilty—courts assume they got that right—but whether Alabama had the power to run trials this way. Did the Sixth Amendment right to counsel apply to state trials, or only federal ones?

The Constitution's first ten amendments—the Bill of Rights—were written in 1789 with one audience in mind: Congress and the federal government. The First Amendment says "Congress shall make no law..." It does not address what states could do. For more than a century, the Supreme Court treated it that way. The Bill of Rights was a cage around *federal* power only. States were free to regulate speech, religion, press, and searches as they saw fit—or not regulate them at all.

This created a strange constitutional landscape: a citizen in Massachusetts had federal protection against federal censorship, but Massachusetts itself could censor freely. A citizen in Alabama could be tried without a lawyer, without a jury, or under laws the state passed *after* the alleged crime was committed. The federal government could not do these things. The states could.

The machinery that changed this was the Fourteenth Amendment, ratified in 1868 after the Civil War. It said the states could not deprive any *person* of "life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." It was written to prevent the South from returning to antebellum conditions. But the wording was capacious: it used the word "liberty"—not defined, not limited to particular freedoms. For sixty years, the Supreme Court acted as though that word meant almost nothing. Then, beginning in the 1920s, the Court began asking a radical question: *Could "liberty" in the Fourteenth Amendment mean the same liberties protected in the Bill of Rights?*

The answer came slowly, case by case, amendment by amendment. This process is called the **incorporation doctrine**—the absorption of Bill of Rights protections into the Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee of liberty, making them binding on the states.

### How incorporation actually works

It is not automatic. The Court does not simply say, "The Fourteenth Amendment incorporates the whole Bill of Rights." Instead, the Court takes one freedom at a time and asks two questions:

1. **Is this freedom "fundamental" to ordered liberty?** Would the concept of liberty itself become hollow without it?
2. **Is it "deeply rooted in this Nation's history and tradition"?**

If yes to both, that freedom becomes incorporated. States must respect it. Violations can be appealed to federal courts.

By contrast, the Second Amendment—the right to keep and bear arms—stayed un-incorporated until 2010, more than 140 years after the Fourteenth Amendment. The Court held in *McDonald v. Chicago* that the right was "fundamental" and historically rooted, but the path was contentious: four justices thought the right *should* be incorporated; four thought it should not; one thought it could be incorporated but did not have to be. The tie-breaking justice wrote separately. The machinery of incorporation is not a conveyor belt. It is a case-by-case conversation about what "liberty" means to a changing nation.

### The trade-off within incorporation

Here is the tension: incorporation *protects* individual freedoms by binding states to federal standards. But incorporation *removes* power from states to experiment, to set different rules, to move at different speeds. A state cannot decide that its citizens need more protection for speech, or religion, or due process—incorporation sets a constitutional *floor*, not a ceiling. All states must meet it; most states provide it; some provide more. But once the Supreme Court incorporates a right, a state cannot provide *less*.

This trade-off becomes concrete when you watch it happen. Before incorporation, some states protected married couples' right to contraception; others did not. Once the Supreme Court incorporated privacy (a right the Court inferred from the "liberty" language), all states had to permit it. The question of whether a state could regulate contraception for its own citizens was no longer available.

### Case study: The right to counsel

In *Gideon v. Wainwright* (1963), Clarence Gideon was arrested for breaking and entering in Florida. He could not afford a lawyer and asked the court for one. The judge refused. Gideon represented himself, lost, and went to prison. He then wrote a petition—by hand, from his prison cell—asking the Supreme Court whether Florida could force him to trial without a lawyer.

The Court said no. The Sixth Amendment's guarantee of counsel, the Court ruled, was so fundamental to a fair trial that it had to apply to every state. A poor person in Florida got the same right as a rich person in a federal court. The ruling did not say Florida had to provide *good* lawyers or lawyers paid well enough to take time for investigation and motions. It said Florida had to provide *a* lawyer.

This is how incorporation works in practice: the Court names a freedom, marks it as fundamental, and then watches states scramble to implement it—some quickly, some reluctantly, some with the bare minimum that passes constitutional inspection. The right exists. Its scope, its enforcement, its adequacy is a different question entirely.

### Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: Incorporation means the Bill of Rights is fully applied to states.** False. The Court has incorporated most—not all—of the Bill of Rights. The Second Amendment came late. The Third Amendment (the quartering of soldiers) has never been incorporated. The Seventh Amendment (jury trial in civil cases) is not incorporated. The language of the Bill of Rights can change meaning when it crosses the incorporation boundary.

**Misconception 2: Once incorporated, a right cannot be limited.** False. Incorporation sets a constitutional baseline, not a rule against any regulation. States can regulate speech that is true if regulation serves a compelling interest. The point is that states cannot erase the right entirely.

**Misconception 3: States always resist incorporation.** Not true. Many states incorporated protections *before* the Supreme Court required it. Some states have constitutional language that goes further than federal minimums. Incorporation is often a floor that some states voluntarily rise above.

## Concept 2: The First Amendment and the Test-Driven Nature of Freedom

**Cold open: specific collision.**

November 1919. The United States has just emerged from World War I. A young man named Jacob Abrams and four others printed pamphlets opposing U.S. military intervention in Russia. They threw the pamphlets from a roof onto the streets below. One pamphlet landed in a factory and someone reported it. Abrams was arrested under the Espionage Act and convicted—not of sabotaging the war effort, but of advocating that people refuse to help the war effort. He was sentenced to twenty years.

The Supreme Court upheld the conviction. But one justice—Oliver Wendell Holmes, a Civil War veteran and the Court's most respected voice—dissented alone. He wrote that the only speech the government could suppress was speech that created a "clear and present danger" of "imminent lawless action." Abrams's pamphlets, Holmes argued, posed no such danger. They were political opposition. They deserved protection.

That phrase—"clear and present danger"—became a test. Not the law yet. Not even the majority view. Just one justice's argument that freedom of speech required *something* more than "people don't like what you're saying." It took forty more years for that test to become doctrine.

This is how First Amendment doctrine works: the Court sets tests, applies them inconsistently, narrows or expands them over decades, and in the process, the meaning of "freedom of speech" changes.

### The structure of the First Amendment

The amendment is forty-five words:

> Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof; or abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the Government for a redress of grievances.

Four freedoms wear one phrase: religion (two clauses), speech, press, assembly, petition. Courts have since collapsed speech and press into one category—expression—because the distinction matters less than it did in 1791. A podcast is not quite speech and not quite press; it is both and neither.

### The two faces of religious freedom

The First Amendment protects religion in two directions: the **Establishment Clause** (government cannot establish religion) and the **Free Exercise Clause** (government cannot restrict religious practice).

These clauses sometimes collide. A school cannot sponsor prayer—Establishment Clause. But a student can pray in school—Free Exercise Clause. A government cannot subsidize churches—Establishment Clause. But if it subsidizes secular services (day care, schools, hospitals), can it exclude religious institutions that offer the same services without government money?

In 2022, in *Carson v. Makin*, the Supreme Court said it could not. Maine had a program that reimbursed families for private school tuition *unless* the school was religious. The Court held this violated Free Exercise: once the state opens the subsidy, it cannot exclude religious providers just because they are religious. The Establishment Clause does not require the state to avoid funding religion; it only requires the state not to establish religion—not to sponsor it, or privilege it, or make it official doctrine.

The boundary between establishment and free exercise is mobile. Courts have allowed religious exemptions from generally applicable laws (vaccinations in schools, for instance, if the person has sincere religious objection—though whether sincere belief is required is itself contested). Courts have also disallowed religious exemptions when the burden on the state is severe (a religious group cannot claim exemption from paying taxes). The machinery is not a formula. It is a case-by-case weighing of government interest against the burden on religious practice.

### The tests for speech

Speech gets three categories of analysis, and the test applied depends on the category:

**1. Unprotected speech.** Some speech receives no First Amendment protection. This includes:
- **Incitement to imminent lawless action** — Speech that directly urges people to commit crimes immediately, and is likely to produce that result. The line between protected political speech and unprotected incitement is fact-specific. A speaker at a rally saying "we need to change the system" is protected. A speaker saying "rush the building now" to a crowd at the door is not.
- **True threats** — Speech that a reasonable listener would understand as a threat of violence. A joke about killing the President is protected; a credible threat is not.
- **Fighting words** — Speech so inflammatory that it is likely to provoke immediate physical confrontation. Courts have rarely applied this test in modern times because its scope is narrow.
- **Defamation** — False statements that harm someone's reputation. The speaker is liable if the statements are knowably false or made with reckless disregard for truth.
- **Obscenity** — Sexually explicit material that lacks serious social, political, or artistic value. The test is notoriously vague and shifts over time and across communities.

**2. Content-neutral speech restrictions.** Government can regulate speech based on *how it is delivered*, not *what is said*—if the regulation serves a substantial government interest (like traffic flow or preventing noise) and does not target the message. A city can ban amplified music in public parks at night (content-neutral: applies to all amplified sound). It cannot ban amplified speech criticizing the mayor (content-based: targets the message). The test asks: would the regulation apply equally to all speakers with the same method, regardless of what they say?

**3. Content-based restrictions.** Government cannot regulate speech *because of its message* unless the regulation survives "strict scrutiny"—meaning the government must show the restriction:
- Serves a compelling government interest (not just a legitimate one)
- Is narrowly tailored (not broader than necessary)
- Is the least restrictive way to achieve the interest

Strict scrutiny almost never passes. In practice, it means the government cannot ban speech because of its message unless the government is fighting fire—literally preventing imminent violence or national catastrophe.

### Case study: The Pentagon Papers

In 1971, Daniel Ellsberg photocopied a classified Department of Defense study on the history of U.S. involvement in Vietnam and gave it to *The New York Times*. The government sued to stop publication, claiming national security. The Supreme Court sided with the newspaper. The Court did not say the government has no secrets to keep, or no security interests. It said the government did not meet the burden of proving that publishing *these specific documents* would cause imminent, grave harm. The burden was the government's to carry, and it was heavy. This became the operating principle: the government can suppress speech in the name of security, but only if it can prove the necessity—not predict it, prove it.

The decision did not stop newspapers from worrying about national security. It did prevent the government from asserting security *per se* to suppress criticism.

### Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: The First Amendment protects speech from *all* consequences.** False. It protects you from government punishment. Private consequences—being fired, losing customers, losing friendships—are not First Amendment violations. If you work for a private employer and get fired for your speech, the First Amendment does not apply. If you post on a private social media platform and it removes your post, the First Amendment does not apply. The amendment constrains *government*, not society.

**Misconception 2: Hate speech is unprotected in the United States.** False. The U.S. protects hateful speech more broadly than almost any other democracy. There is no "hate speech" exception to the First Amendment. There are narrow exceptions (incitement to imminent violence, true threats), but a person can speak hatefully about a race, religion, or group and remain protected—unless the speech meets one of those narrow exceptions.

**Misconception 3: You can say anything on your own property or in your own home.** Somewhat true, but with limits. You have robust First Amendment protection in your home. You can speak against government; the government cannot punish you. But if you use your property to incite violence, or to harass others, or to make true threats, you lose protection. The protection is against government restriction, not against all social or legal consequences.

## Concept 3: Rights of the Accused — When Due Process Becomes the Question

**Cold open: specific moment.**

A man named Danny Harris is sitting in an interrogation room. He has been arrested on suspicion of robbery. He has not been told he has a right to a lawyer. He is not rich; he would not hire one anyway. The police ask questions for hours. No recording. No notes beyond what the officer writes himself. Harris eventually says something that sounds like a confession. He is convicted.

The question the courts would face: Did Harris waive his right to counsel? Did he confess voluntarily? Did he understand what he was doing when he confessed? There is no recording to check. There is only the officer's word that Harris understood, and the statement itself.

This is the machinery of "rights of the accused"—a set of constitutional protections that kicks in once someone has been arrested and charged. They are not about innocence or guilt. They are about the *process* by which guilt is determined.

### The architecture of the Fifth, Sixth, and Eighth Amendments

**The Fifth Amendment** contains several clauses:
- **Self-incrimination protection** — "nor shall be compelled in any criminal case to be a witness against himself." You cannot be forced to testify that you committed a crime. The government must prove your guilt; it cannot compel *you* to provide the evidence.
- **Double jeopardy protection** — You cannot be tried twice for the same crime or punished twice for the same offense.
- **Due process clause** — You cannot be deprived of liberty without due process of law.
- **Grand jury clause** — The federal government must use a grand jury before prosecuting felonies.

**The Sixth Amendment** guarantees:
- Right to a speedy and public trial
- Right to an impartial jury
- Right to be informed of charges
- Right to confront witnesses
- Right to compulsory process for obtaining witnesses
- Right to counsel

**The Eighth Amendment** forbids "cruel and unusual punishments."

These are not abstract rights. Each one is a constraint on how police and prosecutors work.

### The machinery: Miranda warnings

In 1966, in *Miranda v. Arizona*, the Supreme Court ruled that police could not use statements obtained during custodial interrogation unless they first warned the suspect of his rights. This did not mean police could not interrogate. It meant police had to tell suspects:
1. You have the right to remain silent.
2. If you say something, it can be used against you in court.
3. You have the right to a lawyer.
4. If you cannot afford one, one will be provided.

These warnings are known as "Miranda rights." They are not in the Constitution. They are a rule the Court invented because the Court believed that once a person was in custody and being questioned, the pressure was inherent. Without warnings, any statement was likely coerced, even if no physical force was used. The warnings were a way of making it possible for the suspect to actually exercise the right to remain silent or consult a lawyer.

Here is the trade-off: Miranda rights protect suspects from coercion. They also make it harder for police to solve crimes, because some suspects invoke silence or ask for a lawyer and stop talking. Police argue that solved crimes protect the public. Suspects argue that the ability to refuse questioning protects innocent people from being pressured into confessions they did not make. Courts have sided with the suspects on the constitutional question while acknowledging the cost.

### The Eighth Amendment and the problem of "cruel and unusual"

What counts as cruel and unusual punishment? In 1791, it meant torture. It meant drawing and quartering, or the rack, or slow execution. By the 1950s, it meant prolonged confinement in brutal conditions. By the 2000s, courts began asking whether "extended solitary confinement"—keeping a person in a cell alone for years—constituted cruel punishment. In 2023, Alabama executed a man by nitrogen gas—a method never before used. Courts had to ask whether a novel execution method, untested on humans, was unconstitutional.

The language is old. The meaning is a conversation between courts and society about what punishment exceeds what government may do, even in response to real crimes.

### Case study: Gideon revisited—What counsel *means*

After *Gideon v. Wainwright*, states had to provide lawyers for the poor. But what kind of lawyer? A part-time public defender carrying five hundred cases? A novice fresh from law school? A lawyer who spends ten hours on a case that should take forty?

In 2011, in *Padilla v. Kentucky*, the Supreme Court ruled that a lawyer's failure to advise a client that a guilty plea would result in deportation was "ineffective assistance of counsel." The right to counsel was not just the presence of a body in the courtroom. It was competent representation. But "competent" is vague. Most public defenders' offices are under-resourced. Many could not meet the *Padilla* standard even if they wanted to. The right exists on paper. Its enforcement is a resource question.

This is the recurring tension in criminal procedure: the Constitution names a right, courts interpret it, and implementation depends on resources that legislatures may or may not appropriate.

### Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: If police do not read you your Miranda rights, you must be released.** False. If you are not read your rights, the statement you make cannot be used against you in court—but you are not automatically released. The police can still charge you; the prosecutor just cannot use your unwarned statement as evidence. If there is other evidence, you can still be convicted.

**Misconception 2: The right to counsel means the government must provide a good lawyer.** Technically true; practically complicated. The Constitution requires effective assistance. Courts have found violations when lawyers sleep during trial or provide virtually no defense. But proving a lawyer was so bad that the conviction should be overturned is a high bar. You must show not just that the lawyer was ineffective, but that the ineffectiveness probably changed the outcome.

**Misconception 3: The Eighth Amendment prevents all capital punishment.** False. The Eighth Amendment prevents *cruel* punishment. The Supreme Court has held that capital punishment itself is not inherently cruel, though courts continue to debate whether particular methods are.

## Integration: How the Pieces Fit

Incorporation makes the Bill of Rights apply to the states. Once applied, rights are subject to tests and limitations. The First Amendment protects expression, but not incitement or true threats. The rights of the accused protect the process, but not the outcome—courts acknowledge that innocent people are sometimes convicted, and guilty people sometimes go free. The system aims at fairness in process, not certainty of result.

The through-line is this: civil liberties are *constraints on government power*. They do not guarantee any particular outcome. They constrain the *methods* government can use. You cannot be punished for your speech—but you can face social consequences. You cannot be tortured—but you can be held in prison. You have a right to counsel—but the counsel might be overworked. The rights exist. Their implementation is always imperfect.

The tension that matters most: every civil liberty protects the individual at some cost to the collective. Protecting speech means protecting bad speech. Protecting the accused means protecting some who are guilty. Protecting religion means protecting religions the majority dislikes. There is no way to have civil liberties without accepting these costs. Democratic societies constantly debate whether the costs are worth it.

## Chapter Summary

Civil liberties are limitations on what government can do to individuals. The machinery that enforces these limits against the states is the incorporation doctrine—the Supreme Court's case-by-case process of recognizing that certain rights are "fundamental" and applying them to states through the Fourteenth Amendment's "liberty" clause.

The First Amendment protects four categories of freedom: religion (establishment and free exercise), speech, press, and assembly. Courts apply different tests depending on whether speech is protected, content-neutral, or content-based. The tests are not formulas; they are frameworks courts apply with judgment, and their application changes over time.

Rights of the accused—the Fifth, Sixth, and Eighth Amendments—protect the *process* of criminal justice, not the outcome. They require counsel, forbid coercion and self-incrimination, and limit punishment. But the constraints are imperfect. Under-resourced defense attorneys, difficult-to-define terms like "cruel and unusual," and the fact that process requirements do not guarantee fair outcomes all mean the rights exist on paper more confidently than they work in practice.

Every civil liberty protects the individual at some cost to collective interests. This is not a bug in the system. It is the price of constitutional government.
