# Chapter 7 — Civil Rights: How America Learned to Keep Its Promises
*The Machinery of Equal Treatment*

---

Here is the puzzle.

The Fourteenth Amendment was ratified in 1868. It says, plainly, that no state shall deny any person the equal protection of the laws. Those words have been in the Constitution for more than 150 years. And yet in 1896, the Supreme Court of the United States ruled that Louisiana could require Black and white passengers to ride in separate train cars — and that this did not violate equal protection.

In 1954, the same Supreme Court ruled that segregated schools did violate equal protection. Same amendment. Same words. Fifty-eight years apart.

Something changed. What changed was not the law. What changed was the specification of what those words mean in practice. And that specification — how courts decide when unequal treatment is acceptable and when it is not — is the machinery this chapter is about.

---

## Civil Liberties and Civil Rights Are Not the Same Thing

Before the machinery makes sense, one distinction has to be precise.

Civil liberties are limitations on government power. The First Amendment says the government cannot restrict your speech. The Eighth Amendment says the government cannot inflict cruel and unusual punishment. These are absolutes, or close to it. There is no clause that says "cruel and unusual punishment is acceptable if the government has a compelling reason." It just says no.

Civil rights are different. Civil rights are guarantees of equal treatment — particularly for groups that have historically been denied the same treatment as others. When the Fourteenth Amendment guarantees equal protection, it is asking a calibrated question: Is this unequal treatment justified? Does the government have a good enough reason for treating these people differently?

That question — good enough for what? — is where the machinery lives.

---

## The Three Gears

Courts have built a three-tier framework for evaluating discrimination. The tier that applies depends on what kind of discrimination is being challenged.

**The first tier is rational basis.** For most discrimination — age limits, licensing requirements, resource allocation — the challenger has to prove the law is irrational. The government only needs to show it has some legitimate purpose and the law is reasonably related to it. This is a low bar. It says: democratic majorities get to make policy. Courts will not second-guess them unless the law makes no sense at all.

Rational basis is why you cannot vote at twelve even if you are extraordinarily well-informed. The government has an obvious interest in an informed electorate, and setting an age floor is a reasonable way to approximate that, even if it is imperfect. The fact that it excludes some qualified people does not make it irrational.

Under rational basis, the government almost always wins.

**The second tier is intermediate scrutiny.** When discrimination is based on gender or sex, the burden of proof shifts. Now the government has to show that treating men and women differently is substantially related to an important governmental objective. The government has to explain itself.

The clearest way to see this is through a specific case. In 1976, Oklahoma had a law that let women buy beer at eighteen but required men to wait until twenty-one. The state argued this made sense — traffic data showed young men caused more drunk driving arrests. The Supreme Court applied intermediate scrutiny and struck the law down. Statistical patterns about a group are not sufficient justification for treating every member of that group differently. There might be less discriminatory ways to address drunk driving. The state had to do better.

Under intermediate scrutiny, the government can sometimes win — but it has to actually argue, with evidence, why the distinction is necessary.

**The third tier is strict scrutiny.** When discrimination is based on race, ethnicity, religion, or national origin, the standard reaches its highest level. The government must show a compelling interest in discriminating — not a reasonable interest, not an important one, but a compelling one — and must show that the discrimination is narrowly tailored, meaning it is the least restrictive means available to achieve the goal.

In practice, strict scrutiny is nearly impossible to survive. One legal scholar called it "strict in theory, fatal in fact."

There is one terrible exception to this pattern, and it deserves attention. In 1944, the Supreme Court upheld the internment of 120,000 Japanese Americans — most of them citizens — under strict scrutiny. The majority said the threat of wartime espionage constituted a compelling interest. They were wrong. *Korematsu v. United States* is now regarded as one of the Court's worst decisions, a monument to what happens when courts abandon their responsibility to scrutinize government claims carefully. Strict scrutiny is a tool. A court that applies the standard while accepting the government's assertions without real scrutiny has not used the tool. It has held it while nodding along.

## What *Plessy* Got Wrong and Why It Took Fifty-Eight Years to Fix

In 1896, the Supreme Court in *Plessy v. Ferguson* said segregation was not a violation of equal protection because the law did not say one race was inferior — it merely required them to be separate. The cars were, nominally, equal. No harm was done.

This reasoning has a seductive internal logic. If you take the words "equal protection" and read them to mean only "formally identical treatment," then separate but equal satisfies the requirement. Two groups, two sets of facilities, same quality. What is the inequality?

The problem is that this reading ignores what segregation actually meant and did. Segregation in the Jim Crow South was not a neutral administrative arrangement. It was a system of domination. It was designed to mark Black people as inferior, to restrict their movement, to humiliate them daily, to communicate that their presence alongside white people was intolerable. The "separate" was the message. The "equal" was the lie.

*Plessy* treated the word "equal" without specifying what equal means in the context of a society built on racial hierarchy. It looked at the form of the law — two cars, same size — and ignored its function.

*Brown v. Board of Education* (1954) named the function. Chief Justice Earl Warren, writing for a unanimous Court, said segregation in schools was unconstitutional not because the resources were necessarily unequal — though they often were — but because segregation itself caused harm. It told Black children they were inferior. It damaged their psychological development. It made equal education impossible not by denying books or teachers but by marking the children as unworthy.

The crucial move in *Brown* was specifying what equal means. It does not mean formally identical. It means equivalent in outcome and effect, including the psychological and social meaning of being separated. And separation in the context of American racial history could not mean anything other than inferiority.

*Brown* did not overturn *Plessy* by finding a new principle. It applied the same principle — equal protection — with the honesty to look at what segregation actually did rather than what it claimed to do.

## The Gap Between Law and Reality

*Brown* was decided in 1954. Ten years later, most Southern schools were still segregated. This is not because the states were defying a clear legal order. It is because *Brown* issued a legal ruling that had no immediate enforcement mechanism.

The Court's follow-up decision, *Brown II* (1955), asked for desegregation to proceed "with all deliberate speed." This phrase — which sounds like urgency — was in practice an invitation to delay. States understood it to mean: someday, eventually, when you get around to it. Virginia closed its public schools entirely rather than desegregate them. Arkansas deployed the National Guard to prevent Black students from entering Central High School in Little Rock. President Eisenhower eventually sent the 101st Airborne to force compliance.

What the Court could not do was register voters, staff schools with federal monitors, or prevent private citizens from terrorizing families that tried to enroll their children in integrated schools. Courts write opinions. Enforcement requires executive action, legislative appropriation, and sustained political will.

This is the gap that defines the history of civil rights in America. The law said one thing. The reality said another. Closing that gap required something beyond lawsuits.

## How Disenfranchisement Worked

The Fifteenth Amendment, ratified in 1870, said voting rights could not be denied based on race. Southern states immediately set about circumventing it, and they were ingenious.

The tools were interlocking. Literacy and understanding tests required voters to read and explain passages of text. The tests were administered by local registrars with complete discretion about difficulty. White applicants read "The sun is bright." Black applicants were asked to recite and explain provisions of the state constitution. The tests were not literacy tests. They were barriers with a literacy-shaped hole.

Grandfather clauses exempted voters from literacy tests if their grandfathers had voted before the Civil War. Since no Black person's grandfather had been permitted to vote, the exemption was whites-only, hidden behind a facially neutral criterion.

Poll taxes required a yearly payment to vote. In 1900, a dollar or two was not trivial for people who had emerged from slavery without land, capital, or accumulated wealth. And taxes from previous years compounded — miss one year, owe two the next.

The white primary declared that the Democratic Party was a private organization and could restrict its membership to white voters. Since the Democratic nominee ran unopposed or nearly so in the general election, exclusion from the primary was exclusion from politics altogether.

These four tools worked together as a system. Literacy tests and grandfather clauses blocked registration. Poll taxes blocked the remainder. And the white primary made any successful registration irrelevant. By the 1940s, fewer than 3 percent of eligible Black voters in the South were registered.

<!-- → INFOGRAPHIC: Flowchart showing the four disenfranchisement tools as interlocking gates on a path to voting — a Black voter attempting to register encounters literacy test (gate 1, administered with discretion), grandfather clause exemption (applies only to white voters, bypasses gate 1), poll tax (gate 2, blocks those who pass gate 1), white primary (gate 3, renders vote meaningless even if gates 1 and 2 are cleared); student should see these not as separate laws but as a system designed so that clearing one gate does not help if the next gate is still locked -->

Every single tool was facially race-neutral. No law said "Black people cannot vote." Each law used a criterion — literacy, ancestry, ability to pay, party membership — that appeared to apply to everyone. The racism was encoded in the administration, the context, and the combined effect.

This is the critical lesson of disenfranchisement. Discrimination does not require explicitly racial language. It requires criteria that produce racial effects in a context where racial inequality already exists. Identifying that form of discrimination is harder than identifying a law that says "whites only." It requires looking at effects, not just text.

## From Courts to Congress to the Streets

The NAACP understood from early in the twentieth century that Black Americans were blocked from the political system. If you cannot vote, you cannot elect representatives who will pass laws that protect you. If the legislature will not act, the courts are the only arena where power can shift without electoral victory.

For decades, the NAACP pursued a deliberate legal strategy targeting segregated education. The theory was that the "equal" in separate but equal could be attacked through specifics. In 1938, the Supreme Court ruled that if Missouri excluded Black students from its law school, it had to either admit them or build an equivalent institution. Missouri built a separate law school. The theory was still intact, but it was now more expensive.

By the 1950s, the NAACP was ready to attack the theory directly. The cases consolidated into *Brown* came from five different states and involved primary schools, where the inequality in resources was often stark but where the psychological argument — that segregation itself was the harm — was most powerful. The Court was ready for that argument. The decision was unanimous.

But *Brown* produced no desegregation. And when desegregation finally did occur — slowly, agonizingly, over a decade — it was the result of organized, deliberate pressure applied outside the courts.

Rosa Parks did not decide spontaneously in December 1955 to refuse to give up her seat on a Montgomery bus. She was a trained organizer, an NAACP secretary, a woman who had spent years working in the movement. She made a deliberate choice in a deliberate moment. The boycott that followed was not spontaneous either. E. D. Nixon organized it. Black women organized it. They walked miles to work for more than a year rather than ride segregated buses. The boycott worked because it was sustained. It cost the bus company money. Money changed behavior.

The sit-ins at Greensboro lunch counters in 1960. The Freedom Rides on interstate buses in 1961. The march across the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma in March 1965, when state troopers attacked peaceful marchers on national television. Each of these was a planned action, part of a strategic campaign, designed to make the gap between American principle and American practice visible and intolerable.

Congress passed the Civil Rights Act of 1964 after Kennedy's assassination made inaction seem unconscionable, and after Johnson pushed it through with the moral weight of the movement behind him. The law reached private businesses — hotels, restaurants, employers — through the commerce power. The Fourteenth Amendment bound only state governments; private businesses had to be reached differently.

A year later, after Selma, the Voting Rights Act of 1965 banned literacy tests outright, deployed federal examiners to register voters where state officials refused, and required states with a history of discrimination to get federal approval before changing any voting law. This last provision — preclearance — was the enforcement mechanism that courts had never provided. States could not enact new voting restrictions without federal review.

The effect was immediate. Black voter registration in Mississippi rose from 6.7 percent in 1964 to over 59 percent by 1969. In Alabama, from 19 percent to 51 percent. The constitutional right had existed since 1870. The enforcement mechanism arrived in 1965. That is the gap.

## What the Machinery Cannot Do

In 2013, the Supreme Court ruled in *Shelby County v. Holder* that the preclearance requirement was no longer constitutional. The formula used to identify which states needed federal oversight was outdated, the Court said. Voting discrimination had ended. The oversight was no longer necessary.

Within days, states that had been under preclearance passed new voting restrictions. Stricter voter ID requirements. Reduced early voting. Fewer polling locations in minority-heavy precincts. Tighter voter registration deadlines. Every restriction was facially race-neutral. All of them had disparate effects on Black voters. All of them were passed immediately after the enforcement mechanism was removed.

The pattern here is not coincidence. It is the pattern of the whole history. The Fifteenth Amendment existed in 1900 and produced essentially no Black voter registration in the South. It existed in 1965 and produced registration rates of 50 to 60 percent within a few years. The difference was enforcement.

The Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection clause has been law since 1868. Schools were segregated until the 1970s and are more segregated now, by some measures, than they were then. The law did not change. What changed was the presence or absence of enforcement mechanisms — federal courts pressing desegregation orders, busing requirements, active Justice Department involvement — and then the gradual withdrawal of those mechanisms as political will shifted.

The three-tier scrutiny framework is a genuine achievement. It takes the vague promise of equal protection and makes it workable. It says: here is how we decide what kind of inequality is acceptable, and here is how much the government has to justify itself. That is real machinery, and it does real work.

But the machinery requires someone to run it. Laws do not enforce themselves. Court decisions do not implement themselves. The gap between what the law says and what people actually experience depends on enforcement budgets, judicial appointments, executive priorities, and the continued pressure of people who refuse to let the gap persist.

Rights in a democracy are not permanently secured. They are maintained — by courts that apply scrutiny seriously, by legislatures that fund enforcement, and by people who organize, march, sue, register voters, sit at lunch counters, and refuse to accept the gap as permanent.

The machinery works. It has always required someone willing to run it.

## Still Puzzling

The preclearance mechanism of the Voting Rights Act worked remarkably well. But I am not entirely sure why. Is it that federal employees are more neutral than state officials? Is it that the mere existence of oversight changes behavior, regardless of whether any particular action is blocked? Is it the threat of embarrassment? The question matters because understanding why enforcement worked helps design enforcement for the future. I do not have a satisfying answer.

## LLM Exercises

**1. Scrutiny in practice.**
A state law requires truck drivers to obtain a commercial license with annual renewal, but waives the renewal fee for drivers over sixty-five. A forty-year-old driver challenges the waiver as age discrimination. What level of scrutiny applies? What would the state have to show to survive? Now change the hypothetical: the waiver applies only to white drivers. What changes, and why?

**2. Separate but equal, specified.**
*Plessy v. Ferguson* said separation without explicit inferiority is not a constitutional violation. *Brown v. Board of Education* said segregation is inherently unequal. Write the argument that connects these two decisions — not as contradiction but as specification. What did *Brown* specify that *Plessy* left vague? What evidence, what reasoning, made that specification possible in 1954 but not in 1896?

**3. The enforcement gap.**
The Fifteenth Amendment was ratified in 1870. The Voting Rights Act was passed in 1965. That is ninety-five years during which the constitutional right to vote existed and was effectively nullified. Identify the specific legal mechanisms that made nullification possible. For each mechanism, explain why it survived constitutional challenge for as long as it did. What changed — legally, politically, socially — that made the Voting Rights Act possible when it was?

**4. Facially neutral, racially disparate.**
After *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013), several states passed voter ID laws. The laws apply to all voters equally. But data shows Black voters are statistically more likely to lack qualifying ID than white voters, in part because of historical and economic inequalities. A civil rights organization challenges the law under the Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection clause. The state says: the law is race-neutral; rational basis applies; the law survives. The organization says: the effect is racial; strict scrutiny applies. Who is right? What would the courts need to find to apply strict scrutiny to a facially neutral law? What is the difference between discriminatory purpose and discriminatory effect?

**5. The machinery and its limits.**
The chapter argues that the three-tier scrutiny framework works but requires enforcement to produce real change. Identify one domain where the legal framework has been applied rigorously and produced substantial real-world change, and one domain where the legal framework exists but the real-world gap persists. What explains the difference? Is it enforcement resources? Political will? The nature of the discrimination? Something else?
