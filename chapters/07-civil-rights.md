# Chapter 7 — Civil Rights: How America Learned to Keep Its Promises

*The Machinery of Equal Treatment*

---

Here is the puzzle.

The Fourteenth Amendment was ratified in 1868. It says, plainly, that no state shall deny any person the equal protection of the laws. Those words have been in the Constitution for over 150 years.

In 1896, the Supreme Court ruled that Louisiana could require Black and white passengers to ride in separate train cars — and that this did not violate equal protection.

In 1954, the same Court ruled that segregated schools did violate equal protection. Same amendment. Same words. Fifty-eight years apart.

Something changed. What changed was not the law. What changed was the specification of what those words mean in practice. That specification — how courts decide when unequal treatment is acceptable and when it is not — is the machinery this chapter is about.

---

## Two Different Things: Liberties and Rights

One distinction has to be precise before anything else makes sense.

Civil liberties are limits on government power. The First Amendment says the government cannot restrict your speech. The Eighth Amendment says no cruel and unusual punishment. These are structured as prohibitions. There is no balancing test — no clause saying "cruel and unusual punishment is acceptable if the government has a sufficiently important reason." It just says no.

Civil rights are different in kind. Civil rights are guarantees of equal treatment — protections for groups that have historically been treated as less than others. The Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection clause does not say "no discrimination." It asks a calibrated question: Is this unequal treatment justified? Does the government have a good enough reason for treating these people differently than those people?

That question — good enough for what? — is where the machinery lives.

---

## The Three Gears

Courts have built a three-tier framework for evaluating discrimination claims. Which tier applies depends on what kind of classification is being challenged.

**The first tier is rational basis.** For most discrimination — age requirements, licensing rules, resource allocation — the person challenging the law has to prove it is irrational. The government only needs to show it has some legitimate purpose and the law is reasonably related to it. This is a low bar, deliberately. Democratic majorities get to make policy. Courts will not second-guess them unless the law makes no sense at all.

This is why you cannot vote at twelve even if you are well-informed. The government has a legitimate interest in an informed electorate; an age floor is a reasonable approximation, even if it is imperfect. Under rational basis, the government almost always wins.

**The second tier is intermediate scrutiny.** When discrimination is based on sex or gender, the burden shifts. The government now must show its distinction is *substantially related* to an *important* governmental objective. The government has to actually explain itself.

In 1976, Oklahoma had a law letting women buy beer at eighteen but requiring men to wait until twenty-one. The state argued this tracked reality — traffic data showed young men caused more drunk driving arrests. The Supreme Court applied intermediate scrutiny and struck the law down. Statistical patterns about a group, the Court said, are not sufficient justification for treating every individual member of that group differently. The state had to do better. Under intermediate scrutiny, the government can sometimes win — but it has to argue, with evidence, why the distinction is necessary.

**The third tier is strict scrutiny.** When discrimination is based on race, ethnicity, religion, or national origin, the standard is at its most demanding. The government must show a *compelling* interest in the classification — not reasonable, not important, but compelling — and must show the law is *narrowly tailored*, meaning it is the least restrictive means available to achieve that goal.

In practice, strict scrutiny is nearly impossible to survive. One legal scholar called it "strict in theory, fatal in fact."

There is one catastrophic exception, and it deserves attention. In 1944, the Supreme Court upheld the internment of 120,000 Japanese Americans — most of them citizens — under strict scrutiny. The majority said the threat of wartime espionage constituted a compelling interest. They were wrong. *Korematsu v. United States* is now regarded as one of the Court's worst decisions, a monument to what happens when a court holds the tool of scrutiny while accepting the government's assertions without actually using it. Strict scrutiny is not a guarantee. It is a standard. A court that applies the words while abandoning the substance has done something worse than not applying the standard at all — it has given judicial cover to what should have been stopped.

## What *Plessy* Got Wrong and Why It Took Fifty-Eight Years to Fix

In 1896, the Supreme Court in *Plessy v. Ferguson* held that segregation did not violate equal protection because the law did not declare one race inferior — it merely required them to be separate. The cars were, nominally, equal. No harm done.

This reasoning has a seductive internal logic. If "equal protection" means only "formally identical treatment," then separate but equal satisfies the requirement. Two groups. Two sets of facilities. Same quality on paper. Where is the inequality?

The problem is that this reading ignores what segregation actually was and did. Segregation in the Jim Crow South was not a neutral administrative arrangement. It was a system of domination — designed to mark Black people as inferior, to restrict their movement, to humiliate them daily, to communicate that their presence alongside white people was intolerable. The *separation* was the message. The *equal* was the lie.

*Plessy* took the word "equal" at face value while ignoring the social meaning of the arrangement. It looked at the form of the law — two cars, same size — and refused to look at its function.

*Brown v. Board of Education* (1954) named the function. Chief Justice Earl Warren, writing for a unanimous Court, said segregated schools were unconstitutional not primarily because resources were unequal — though they often were — but because segregation itself caused harm. It told Black children they were inferior. It damaged their psychological development. It made equal education impossible not by withholding books but by marking the children as unworthy of sharing space with white children.

The crucial move in *Brown* was specifying what *equal* means. It does not mean formally identical. It means equivalent in effect — including the psychological and social meaning of being separated. And separation in the context of American racial history could mean only one thing: inferiority.

*Brown* did not find a new principle. It applied the same principle — equal protection — with the honesty to look at what segregation actually did, rather than what it claimed to do.

## The Gap Between Law and Reality

*Brown* was decided in 1954. Ten years later, most Southern schools were still segregated.

This is not because states were defying a clear enforcement order. It is because *Brown* issued a legal ruling with no enforcement mechanism. The Court's follow-up decision, *Brown II* (1955), asked for desegregation to proceed "with all deliberate speed." This phrase — which sounds like urgency — was in practice an invitation to delay. Virginia closed its public schools entirely rather than desegregate. Arkansas deployed the National Guard to prevent Black students from entering Central High School in Little Rock. President Eisenhower eventually sent the 101st Airborne to force compliance.

What the Court could not do was register voters, staff schools with monitors, or prevent private citizens from terrorizing families that tried to enroll their children. Courts write opinions. Enforcement requires executive action, legislative appropriation, and sustained political will.

This gap — between what the law says and what people actually experience — defines the entire history of civil rights in America.

## How Disenfranchisement Worked

The Fifteenth Amendment was ratified in 1870. It says voting rights cannot be denied based on race. Southern states immediately set about circumventing it, and they were systematic.

The tools were interlocking. Literacy and understanding tests required applicants to read and explain passages of text. The tests were administered by local registrars with complete discretion over difficulty. White applicants read "The sun is bright." Black applicants were asked to recite and explain provisions of the state constitution. These were not literacy tests. They were barriers with a literacy-shaped hole.

Grandfather clauses exempted voters from literacy requirements if their grandfathers had voted before the Civil War. Since no Black person's grandfather had been permitted to vote, the exemption was whites-only, disguised behind a facially neutral criterion.

Poll taxes required a yearly payment to vote. At the turn of the twentieth century, a dollar or two was not trivial for people who had emerged from slavery without land, capital, or accumulated wealth. And taxes from previous years compounded — miss one year, owe two the next.

The white primary declared that the Democratic Party was a private organization and could restrict its membership to white voters. Since the Democratic nominee ran essentially unopposed in the general election throughout the South, exclusion from the primary meant exclusion from any meaningful political participation.

These four tools worked together as a system. Literacy tests blocked most registration. Grandfather clauses exempted white voters from the same barrier. Poll taxes blocked whoever got through. And the white primary made any successful registration irrelevant. By the 1940s, fewer than three percent of eligible Black voters in the South were registered.

Every single tool was facially race-neutral. No law said "Black people cannot vote." Each law used a criterion — literacy, ancestry, ability to pay, party membership — that appeared to apply to everyone. The discrimination was encoded in the administration, the context, and the combined effect.

This is the central lesson of disenfranchisement, and it matters as much today as it did in 1900. Discrimination does not require explicitly racial language. It requires criteria that produce racial effects in a context where racial inequality already exists. Identifying that form of discrimination is harder than identifying a law that says "whites only." It requires looking at effects, not just text.

## From Courts to Congress to the Streets

The NAACP understood from early in the twentieth century that Black Americans were blocked from the political system. If you cannot vote, you cannot elect representatives who will pass laws protecting you. If the legislature will not act, the courts are the only arena where power can shift without electoral majority.

For decades, the NAACP pursued a deliberate legal strategy. The theory was that the "equal" in separate but equal could be attacked through specifics — force states to actually make facilities equal, and the cost would become unsustainable. In 1938, the Supreme Court ruled that if Missouri excluded Black students from its law school, it had to either admit them or build an equivalent institution. Missouri built a separate law school. The theory was still intact, but increasingly expensive.

By the early 1950s, the NAACP was ready to attack the theory directly. The cases consolidated into *Brown* came from five states and focused on primary schools, where the psychological argument — that segregation itself was the harm — was most powerful. The Court was ready for it. The decision was unanimous.

But *Brown* produced no desegregation. Closing the gap between the ruling and reality required something beyond lawsuits.

Rosa Parks did not decide spontaneously in December 1955 to refuse to give up her seat on a Montgomery bus. She was a trained organizer, an NAACP secretary, who had spent years working in the movement and had attended the Highlander Folk School to learn nonviolent tactics. She made a deliberate choice in a deliberate moment. The boycott that followed was not spontaneous either. E. D. Nixon organized it. Jo Ann Robinson drafted and distributed fifty thousand flyers the night of Parks's arrest. Black women organized and sustained it. They walked miles to work for more than a year rather than ride segregated buses. The boycott worked because it was sustained and because it cost the bus company money. Money changed behavior.

The sit-ins at Greensboro lunch counters in 1960. The Freedom Rides in 1961. The march across the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma in March 1965, when state troopers attacked peaceful marchers on national television. Each of these was a planned action — part of a strategic campaign designed to make the gap between American principle and American practice visible, undeniable, and politically intolerable.

Congress passed the Civil Rights Act of 1964 after Kennedy's assassination made inaction seem unconscionable and after Johnson drove it through with the moral force of the movement behind him. The law reached private businesses — hotels, restaurants, employers — through the commerce power, because the Fourteenth Amendment bound only state governments. Private discrimination required a different constitutional hook.

A year later, after Selma, the Voting Rights Act of 1965 banned literacy tests outright, deployed federal examiners to register voters where state officials refused, and — most critically — required states with a documented history of discrimination to obtain federal approval before changing any voting law. This preclearance provision was the enforcement mechanism that courts had never been able to provide. States could not enact new voting restrictions without federal review.

The effect was immediate. Black voter registration in Mississippi rose from 6.7 percent in 1964 to over 59 percent by 1969. In Alabama, from 19 percent to 51 percent. The constitutional right had existed since 1870. The enforcement mechanism arrived in 1965. That is the gap — ninety-five years — and then, once the enforcement existed, registration rates doubled and tripled within five years.

## What the Machinery Cannot Do

In 2013, the Supreme Court ruled in *Shelby County v. Holder* that the preclearance requirement was no longer constitutional. The coverage formula — the method of identifying which states needed federal oversight — was based on data from the 1960s and 1970s. The Court said discrimination had ended; the oversight was no longer necessary.

Within days, states that had been under preclearance passed new voting restrictions. Stricter voter ID requirements. Reduced early voting hours. Fewer polling locations in heavily minority precincts. Tighter voter registration deadlines. Each restriction was facially race-neutral. All of them had disparate effects on Black voters. All of them were passed immediately after the enforcement mechanism was removed.

The pattern is not coincidence. It is the pattern of the whole history.

The Fifteenth Amendment existed in 1900 and produced essentially no Black voter registration in the South. It existed in 1965 and produced registration rates of fifty to sixty percent within a few years. The difference was enforcement. The Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection clause has been law since 1868. Schools were segregated until the 1970s and are more segregated now, by some measures, than they were then. What changed was not the law — it was the presence or absence of enforcement mechanisms, and then the gradual withdrawal of those mechanisms as political will shifted.

The three-tier scrutiny framework is a genuine achievement. It takes the vague promise of equal protection and gives it structure. It says: here is how we decide what degree of inequality is acceptable, and here is how much the government must justify its own distinctions. That is real machinery, and it does real work.

But machinery requires someone to run it. Laws do not enforce themselves. Court decisions do not implement themselves. The gap between what the law promises and what people actually experience depends on enforcement budgets, judicial appointments, executive priorities, and the continuing pressure of people who refuse to accept the gap as permanent.

Rights in a democracy are not secured once and held forever. They are maintained — by courts that apply scrutiny seriously rather than as cover, by legislatures that fund enforcement, and by people who organize, march, sue, register voters, sit at lunch counters, and walk miles to work rather than ride a segregated bus.

The machinery works. It has always required someone willing to run it.

## What I Don't Fully Understand

The preclearance mechanism of the Voting Rights Act worked remarkably well. But I am not entirely sure why. Is it that federal employees are more neutral than local state officials? Is it that the mere existence of oversight changes behavior — that jurisdictions don't try certain restrictions when they know they'll face federal review? Is it the threat of public embarrassment? The question matters because understanding *why* enforcement worked is essential to designing enforcement that works in the future. The Court in *Shelby County* said the discrimination had ended. The states' behavior immediately after the decision suggests the discrimination had been *suppressed* rather than ended, which is a very different claim. I don't have a clean account of the difference, and the difference is not trivial.

## LLM Exercises

**1. Scrutiny in practice.**
A state law requires truck drivers to obtain a commercial license with annual renewal, but waives the renewal fee for drivers over sixty-five. A forty-year-old driver challenges the waiver as age discrimination. What level of scrutiny applies? What would the state have to show to survive? Now change the hypothetical: the waiver applies only to white drivers. What changes, and why?

**2. Separate but equal, specified.**
*Plessy v. Ferguson* said separation without explicit inferiority is not a constitutional violation. *Brown v. Board of Education* said segregation is inherently unequal. Write the argument that connects these two decisions — not as contradiction but as specification. What did *Brown* specify that *Plessy* left vague? What evidence, what reasoning, made that specification possible in 1954 but not in 1896?

**3. The enforcement gap.**
The Fifteenth Amendment was ratified in 1870. The Voting Rights Act was passed in 1965. That is ninety-five years during which the constitutional right to vote existed and was effectively nullified. Identify the specific legal mechanisms that made nullification possible. For each mechanism, explain why it survived constitutional challenge for as long as it did. What changed — legally, politically, socially — that made the Voting Rights Act possible when it was?

**4. Facially neutral, racially disparate.**
After *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013), several states passed voter ID laws. The laws apply to all voters equally. But data shows Black voters are statistically more likely to lack qualifying ID than white voters, in part because of historical and economic inequalities. A civil rights organization challenges the law under the Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection clause. The state says: the law is race-neutral; rational basis applies; the law survives. The organization says: the effect is racial; strict scrutiny applies. Who is right? What would the courts need to find to apply strict scrutiny to a facially neutral law? What is the difference between discriminatory purpose and discriminatory effect?

**5. The machinery and its limits.**
The chapter argues that the three-tier scrutiny framework works but requires enforcement to produce real change. Identify one domain where the legal framework has been applied rigorously and produced substantial real-world change, and one domain where the legal framework exists but the real-world gap persists. What explains the difference? Is it enforcement resources? Political will? The nature of the discrimination? Something else?
