# The Politics of Public Opinion

**TL;DR:** Public opinion forms early in life through socializing agents (family, school, media, peers, workplaces); polls measure it with precision but significant error; the branches of government attend to it selectively—Congress most, the president inconsistently, the Supreme Court least.

## Three Titles to Consider

1. Why Polls Matter (Even When They're Wrong)
2. The Formation of Belief: Why Your Politics Arrives Early
3. Public Opinion as Measurement Problem

## 1. Chapter Opening: The Question That Shouldn't Have an Answer

November 8, 2012. Mitt Romney's campaign had built a transition website detailing Cabinet appointments, planning the inauguration, preparing the president's acceptance speech. One problem: Romney had lost.

His pollsters had made a systematic error. They'd assumed Republican voters were unusually motivated and would turn out at high rates. When the actual returns came in, they hadn't. The campaign's own numbers—which showed Romney competitive—contradicted the nation's polls, which showed Obama ahead. Romney's team had believed the first set. They built a website for a presidency that wasn't coming.

The question haunting that November morning was not whether polls matter. It was something harder: if something is measured carefully and scientifically, with sample sizes and margins of error and statisticians watching every step, how does it still get so wrong?

This chapter answers that question in pieces. We'll see how opinion gets formed in the first place, usually before you're old enough to vote. We'll look at how pollsters actually measure what people think—and where the machinery breaks. And we'll examine what politicians do with the numbers they collect. The pattern isn't simple. Some follow polls closely. Others ignore them entirely. The relationship between what we think and what our government does is murkier than it sounds.

**Learning objectives by section:**
- Understand how beliefs and ideology form, and why they resist change
- Identify the five major agents of political socialization
- Build and evaluate a scientific poll
- Diagnose sources of polling error and bias
- Explain when and why politicians attend to public opinion
- Recognize the different relationship each branch has to the polls

## 2. Concept 1: How Opinion Forms (Political Socialization)

### The Mechanism: Beliefs Travel

You do not wake up as an adult and decide what you think about politics from first principles. Your core political beliefs arrive in childhood, often before you understand what politics is.

Begin here: a belief is a closely held idea that supports your expectations about life. Not a fact. A belief. You might believe you're entitled to equality, liberty, and privacy. You might believe that hard work leads to reward. You might believe government should protect the weak or that it should stay out of people's lives. These beliefs do not arrive by logic. They arrive by exposure—watching your parents vote, hearing a teacher explain the Constitution, sitting in a religious community where leaders speak about duty and obligation.

This process is called *political socialization*—learning the norms and practices of a political system through others and the institutions around you. It starts young. Sometimes you're socialized by direct instruction (a teacher explaining how Congress works). More often it's indirect. You watch what adults do. You notice which attitudes are rewarded and which are not. You absorb.

The earliest and most powerful agent of socialization is family. Children who watch their parents vote absorb the act as civic duty. Children who overhear political conversations at dinner learn which party aligns with values they hear praised. The evidence is consistent: children of Democrats often become Democrats. Children of Republicans become Republicans. Children in homes where parents show no strong party loyalty become independents. This isn't brainwashing. It's water. You absorb what surrounds you because you have no other reference point.

As you age, other agents take on weight. School teaches explicitly: the Pledge of Allegiance, the Constitution, the three branches, the founding story of the nation. Early lessons are idealized—George Washington as hero, not as enslaver. But deliberate. Designed to socialize you into respect for the system.

Religious institutions teach in different registers. They frame duty, right conduct, the proper limits of privacy and authority. A teenager sitting in a mosque, a church, a synagogue hears leaders connect faith to politics: *this is what we believe about abortion, military involvement, the role of government in moral questions*. These connections travel home as opinion.

Friends and peers socialize through something simpler than instruction: trust and common interest. When a friend shares political information, you often accept it because you trust them. You don't fact-check your best friend the way you fact-check a stranger. Social media automates this—you see what your networks "like," and you absorb it.

Workplace socialization is quieter but powerful. People who work in education surround themselves with others of similar education. Their concerns about policy track with the workplace: education funding, school reform, curricula. Teachers' unions teach their members about which party supports school funding. Doctors' organizations teach members about healthcare policy. You become the people you work beside.

Finally, media socialize through selection and framing. Television networks choose which stories to report and which to ignore. They choose what language to use. When some networks called Ferguson protests "riots" and others called them "demonstrations," they were framing—choosing the angle that would shape how viewers perceived the same event. This framing isn't transparent. Often the audience doesn't know it's happening.

These agents build up beliefs. Beliefs crystallize into attitudes—preferences based on your values and experiences. You have racist experiences, and your attitude toward authority shifts. You graduate, live in a city, meet people unlike yourself, and your attitude toward immigration changes slightly. Beliefs and attitudes together create ideology—the cluster of ideas that shape how you vote and what you believe government should do.

The key fact: this all happens early. Your core ideology, rooted in family and school, establishes early and does not shift easily. James Stimson's research found that ideologies formed by your early twenties tend to persist. They may shift subtly as you age, but dramatic reversals are rare. This is why politics runs in families. This is why you can predict, with decent accuracy, how many people will vote in the next election by knowing how they voted in the last one.

But not perfectly. Events can shatter ideology. After 9/11, some families became more Republican. After the 2008 financial crisis, some became more Democratic. The economic catastrophe didn't convert everyone—ideology was too entrenched. But enough voters reassessed that it shifted the political landscape.

### The Trade-off: Stability versus Responsiveness

This mechanism creates a tension. Early socialization is a feature and a bug.

The feature: A country where citizens share basic beliefs about legitimacy—about whether the government deserves respect—is stable. This is called *diffuse support*. When a nation has diffuse support, even when citizens disagree about specific policies, they agree the system is worth preserving. This stability is powerful. It allows democratic systems to survive economic downturns, wars, scandals, and policy failures. The system can weather the storm because people believe in the system itself.

The bug: Early socialization means that ordinary citizens' opinions don't shift as conditions change. You learned your political basics in childhood. Now it's a decade later. The economy has collapsed. Your job disappeared. New data on climate change arrived. And yet your fundamental beliefs—shaped in a different world—don't adjust proportionally. Collective public opinion responds to events, but slowly and partially.

Democrats and Republicans illustrate this tension. Polarization has grown sharply. In 1987, 58 percent of Democrats and 60 percent of Republicans agreed that government controlled too much. By 2012, only 47 percent of Democrats agreed, while 77 percent of Republicans agreed. The gap widened from 2 percentage points to 30. This happened partly because the parties' policy positions shifted, but also because people sorted themselves into parties that already matched their existing beliefs. You didn't just choose your party; you were socialized into it long before you could choose.

### Worked Example: Why Ideology Persists

Take a voter socialized in a conservative household in the 1980s. Her parents voted Republican. They talked about the importance of individual responsibility and skepticism toward government programs. She absorbed this. By the time she could vote, these beliefs felt natural—not taught, but native.

Now it's 2010. She's in her forties. She's experienced layoffs, a healthcare crisis in her family, and the financial crash. She sees that market failures can be catastrophic. You might expect her to shift left, to believe government needs to cushion people against these risks.

She may not. Her childhood ideology says: *I succeeded through hard work; those without work need to try harder. Government makes things worse.* That belief is now three decades old. It's woven into her identity and her social circle. Her friends share it. Her church reinforces it. Even facing evidence that contradicts it, she can rationalize. *I was lucky. But I still believe in personal responsibility.*

She might shift slightly. Many voters do, moving a few percentage points left or right. But a wholesale reversal—abandoning the belief structure she learned at age seven—almost never happens unless a trauma comparable to 9/11 or the Depression shatters everything.

### Misconception: "Opinions Can Change Easily"

A common assumption: people are persuadable. Show them evidence. Change their minds.

The evidence says something different. Yes, people change opinions on specific issues—whether to ban a local fracking operation, whether a particular candidate is trustworthy. But the underlying ideology that shapes which issues matter and how you weigh trade-offs is more durable. It was installed early. It's reinforced daily by the people around you. Changing it requires not just new information, but a social upheaval—a move to a new town, a new job, a life crisis, or an event so shocking that it cracks the lens through which you've always seen politics.

Pollsters sometimes mistake shifting responses to specific questions for shifting ideology. A poll shows that support for a war dropped from 60 to 40 percent. Journalists say "public opinion shifted." Technically true. But the underlying ideology—the belief in American military strength, or skepticism of foreign intervention—usually stayed constant. What changed was whether people thought *this specific war* served that belief.

## 3. Concept 2: How Opinion Is Measured (Polling)

### The Mechanism: The Apparatus of Measurement

A poll seems simple: call people, ask a question, report the results. The reality is machinery. Scientific polling requires solving four puzzles.

**First, who do you ask?** If you want to know what Americans think, you cannot ask everyone. You need a sample—a group small enough to interview but large enough to represent the whole.

The sample must be *random*—each person has an equal chance of being chosen, which prevents the pollster from unconsciously selecting people who already agree. Historically, pollsters drew phone numbers from telephone directories. Now they use computer random-digit-dialing: a computer generates phone numbers with the area codes you want. This introduces new problems—cell phones are portable, so the area code no longer signals location. Pollsters compensate with follow-up questions about zip code.

The sample must also be *representative*—its demographic makeup should mirror the nation's. The U.S. is about 51 percent female, so a representative sample should be about 51 percent female. If your sample is 70 percent male, it will misrepresent what the nation thinks because men and women have different political views.

**Second, how big should the sample be?** Larger samples are more accurate. But bigger samples cost more. So pollsters use a formula balancing accuracy and cost.

Gallup often uses 500 respondents. Pew Research often uses 1,000 to 1,500. Academic studies sometimes interview 2,500 or more. The bigger the population you're trying to represent, the larger your sample needs to be. Polling a state might require 500 respondents. Polling the nation requires more.

The tradeoff between sample size and accuracy follows mathematics. As you add respondents, accuracy improves, but the gains diminish. Going from 100 to 500 respondents cuts error significantly. Going from 1,500 to 2,000 improves accuracy much less. Polls stop growing when the added accuracy no longer justifies the cost.

**Third, how do you phrase the question?** A question can nudge respondents toward an answer.

Compare these two questions about presidential approval:
- "Given the high unemployment rate, do you approve of the job President Biden is doing?"
- "Do you approve of the job President Biden is doing?"

Both ask the same thing. The first *frames* the question by mentioning unemployment, which will shade responses negative. This is called a *leading question*, and scientific polls avoid it. Some organizations, however, design polls precisely to get a predetermined answer. These are *push polls*—disguised campaign ads. In 2015, an organization called MoveOn ran a push poll to test Elizabeth Warren against Hillary Clinton. The poll included ten positive statements about Warren before asking respondents who they'd vote for. The result was predictable: Warren polled higher. The poll was later criticized as "fake."

Pollsters also test whether respondents actually know what they're answering about. If you're polling about the Affordable Care Act, the pollster might first ask who the program serves and how it's funded. Respondents who can't answer correctly may be excluded. Otherwise, you're measuring confidence in ignorance, not actual opinion.

**Fourth, what do you do with the numbers?** The margin of error.

If a poll says "Candidate A will receive 45 percent of the vote with a margin of error of plus or minus 3 percent," it means the true number probably lies between 42 and 48 percent. Larger samples produce smaller margins of error. A margin of error over 5 or 6 percent is generally considered unreliable.

When two candidates are polling at 45 and 47 percent, and each has a margin of error of 4 percent, the true numbers could be anywhere from 41 to 49 and 43 to 51. The candidates' real support might be tied. A poll claiming to distinguish between them is making a false claim.

### The Trade-off: Speed versus Accuracy

Early polls were conducted in person. Interviewers walked neighborhoods, knocked on doors, interviewed neighbors. This was slow and expensive.

Now most polls are conducted by phone or online. Computer-assisted telephone interviewing (CATI) calls random numbers and connects respondents to trained interviewers. The interviewer types responses directly into a computer, which updates the data in real time.

*Robo-polls* are faster and cheaper still. A computer dials numbers and a prerecorded voice asks the question. Respondents press keypad numbers to answer. No human interaction. Robo-polls can interview thousands of people quickly.

The speedier the poll, the less accurate it tends to be. Robo-polls have higher error because respondents misunderstand questions or press wrong keys. There's no live person to clarify. Robo-polls also have lower response rates—people hang up on computers.

Online polls introduce different errors. Someone with multiple email accounts can answer the same poll multiple times. Respondents can misrepresent their age or location. Online polls cannot determine whether a person actually exists or is a bot. Yet online polls are fast and cheap, and reaching younger demographics requires online methods. Pollsters navigate this trade-off by combining methods: some phone, some online, weighted toward accuracy.

### Worked Example: The 1936 Literary Digest Disaster

In 1936, *Literary Digest* magazine predicted that Alf Landon would win the presidential election with 55 percent of the vote. The actual result: Franklin D. Roosevelt won with 62 percent. Landon got 38.

What went wrong? The pollster mailed opinion cards to people with subscriptions, phones, or car registrations—all markers of wealth. In 1936, wealthy people skewed Republican. The poor, who couldn't afford subscriptions or cars, couldn't answer. The sample was dramatically unrepresentative. It looked like a large sample (over 2 million people responded), but it was biased from the start.

The lesson: size doesn't guarantee accuracy. A biased sample, no matter how large, will give you confident garbage.

Twelve years later, Thomas Dewey led in the polls heading into the 1948 election. The famous headline: "Dewey Defeats Truman." Except Truman won. Pollsters hadn't polled close enough to Election Day. In the final weeks, enough voters changed their minds that the old numbers became obsolete. The polls were methodologically sound. They were just timed wrong.

### Misconception: "Polls Are Unreliable Because They Can Be Biased"

Some people dismiss all polling, claiming pollsters can "make" any number they want with the right framing.

There's a grain of truth here. Framing *does* matter. But the claim is exaggerated. Respondents are not mindless. They can detect obviously biased questions and object. If a question is so loaded that people think you're trying to manipulate them, they won't cooperate. Sophisticated pollsters know this. They publish their question wording publicly. If a poll is nonsense, the questions are visible to anyone who wants to scrutinize them.

The real issue isn't that bias is easy to create. It's that bias is *easy to create accidentally*. A poll conducted only on weekday mornings skews older (retirees are home). A poll that stops early for scheduling reasons might miss demographic groups. These biases aren't intentional. They're the cost of practicality. And they're real.

## 4. Concept 3: How Government Uses (and Ignores) Public Opinion

### The Mechanism: Three Different Relationships

Different branches have different relationships to the polls. This is the clearest pattern in the data.

**The House of Representatives.** Members serve two-year terms. They're always running. A vote that angers constituents can cost them the next election. So House members track public opinion closely. When the public mood shifts, House voting patterns shift within a few years. It's not that individual representatives change their minds. Rather, some lose reelection and are replaced by members whose views align with the new public mood. The institution shifts.

Studies show that when public opinion becomes more liberal, more liberal members get elected to the House. When it becomes more conservative, more conservative members arrive. The House is, on average, responsive to long-term shifts in public opinion.

**The Senate.** Senators serve six-year terms. This gives them time to act against public opinion and then repair the relationship before reelection. A senator can vote against the public mood, return home, give speeches explaining why the vote was necessary, and by the time the election arrives, constituents have moved on or forgotten.

Interestingly, Senate voting behavior does *not* shift in response to public mood the way House voting does. Individual senators don't change their votes as opinion changes. What changes is which senators get elected. When public opinion shifts, fewer incumbent senators win reelection. New senators, elected on the new tide, hold different views. The Senate changes more slowly and less directly than the House.

**The Presidency.** Presidents sometimes pay attention to polls. Sometimes they ignore them.

The pattern depends on approval ratings. When a president enters office, approval is high—averaging 66 percent. Presidents use this political capital quickly, pushing for their preferred policies before approval declines. George W. Bush used the 9/11 rally effect (his approval jumped from 51 to 86 percent in days) to pass an authorization for the use of military force.

Presidents also use the *bully pulpit*—leveraging their visibility to shape the media agenda and push Congress. When President Obama spoke about gun control after the Umpqua shooting, he wasn't passing a law. He was directing attention to an issue and calling on citizens to pressure Congress.

But presidents don't simply follow polls. Some studies find that conservative presidents like Ronald Reagan and George W. Bush pursued policies that contradicted public opinion consistently. They led rather than followed. Other presidents, particularly Bill Clinton, polled extensively and adjusted positions accordingly. Clinton became famous for polling on everything—even his dog's name.

A president's consistency in this regard matters little. What matters is that presidents have tools other branches don't. They can command media attention. They can blame Congress for failures. They can explain unpopular decisions in speeches. This freedom to lead rather than follow may explain why public opinion constrains presidents less than it does House members.

**The Supreme Court.** Justices have lifetime tenure and do not face elections. This insulates them from public opinion. In theory, they should ignore the polls entirely.

In practice, it's murkier. Studies on whether the Court follows opinion or shapes it contradict each other. Some research finds that when the public overwhelmingly agrees on an issue, the Court is more likely to decide with the majority. But the effect is weak. When a case is less visible, the Court's decisions still track public opinion slightly—perhaps because the justices, being human, absorb the culture around them.

Notably, the Court's decisions can *shape* public opinion even if public opinion doesn't shape the Court. When the Court ruled in *Obergefell v. Hodges* (2015) that same-sex marriage was constitutional, it went against the political right and parts of the Republican constituency. Yet the decision did not significantly harm the Court's public approval. Why? Because even though the ruling was controversial, public opinion on same-sex marriage had already shifted dramatically. In 1996, only 27 percent of Americans supported it. By 2015, it was 60 percent. The Court's decision affirmed what the nation was becoming.

### The Trade-off: Responsiveness versus Wisdom

This creates a fundamental tension in democratic theory.

One view: politicians should follow public opinion. Voters elect representatives to be their voice—the theory of *delegate representation*. The politician's job is to vote as the people want, not as the politician thinks best. This is democratic in a direct way. It honors the people's agency.

The opposite view: politicians should lead. They have better information. They understand consequences the public doesn't. If a popular policy would cause an economic crash, a good politician votes against it and explains why. This is *trustee representation*. The politician is a trustee of the public interest, not a delegate of the public's current preference.

The United States mixes both. Representatives are delegates—they track public opinion closely and answer to constant pressure from voters. Senators are trustees—they have room to act against current opinion. Presidents are partly delegates (high approval enables their agenda) and partly trustees (they can command attention and persuade). The Supreme Court is entirely trustee—insulated from elections so justices can rule by law rather than popularity.

This mixture is intentional. It reflects a balance: the country needs some politicians who respond to what people want now. It also needs some who can act against current opinion if wisdom requires it.

### Worked Example: Syria, 2013

In August 2013, the Syrian government used chemical weapons on its own citizens in the suburbs of Damascus. Sarin gas. President Obama said this crossed a "red line" and he was considering military strikes.

The public opposed. Forty-eight percent said they opposed airstrikes. Only 29 percent favored them. Democrats were especially opposed—they remembered Iraq.

Obama changed his mind. He allowed Russian President Putin to negotiate a deal where Syria would surrender its chemical weapons instead. No strikes.

Did Obama follow the polls? On one reading, yes. He polled the public, saw opposition, and adjusted course.

On another reading, no. Obama had already decided to consider military strikes before the polling showed opposition. If he'd genuinely polled and then adjusted, would he have announced the strikes in the first place? Or was he always looking for a diplomatic off-ramp, and the public opposition made the off-ramp more palatable politically?

This is why studies contradict each other. Presidents do sometimes follow opinion. But distinguishing that from coincidence (the president wanted to do what the public wanted anyway) is nearly impossible.

### Misconception: "Politicians Either Follow Polls or They Don't"

The real pattern is more textured. Politicians are information-seekers. They want to know what the public thinks. But they use that information selectively.

A House member facing reelection in a few months pays close attention to local opinion. A senator who just won comfortably can ignore opinion for five years. A president with high approval rating moves quickly; one with low approval rating is more cautious.

Public opinion also constrains differently depending on salience. When an issue is invisible (most issues most of the time), politicians have freedom to act as they wish. When an issue becomes visible and the public cares (election years, major disasters, viral moments), politicians adjust.

The pattern isn't rule-following. It's incentive-following.

## 5. Integration: Why This Matters

Public opinion is not magic. Knowing how many people approve of the president does not tell you whether the president's policies are wise. Knowing that 68 percent of Americans support legalizing marijuana does not mean it will be legalized—interest groups with political power may block it.

But public opinion is a constraint and a resource. For politicians, it's a constraint on what they can do without paying a political price, and a resource they can use to push Congress for action.

For the public, understanding how opinion forms and is measured matters. You now know that your own political beliefs arrived in childhood and resist change—that's not a flaw in your reasoning, it's how human belief systems work. You know that polls can be scientific and still misleading, that margins of error matter, that framing shapes responses.

And you know that the relationship between what Americans think and what government does is not simple. Congress listens more than the Court. Presidents lead more than the House. The dynamics shift with elections, crises, and party control. There's no fixed rule. But there is a pattern: democratic institutions have built-in mechanisms that sometimes push them toward the public's preference and sometimes shield them from it.

The purpose is neither to follow the public perfectly nor to ignore it. It's to allow the government to be responsive to long-term shifts in what people want while protecting space for wisdom to act against temporary fashions.

## 6. Exercises: Building the Skills

**Warm-up:** Find a recent public opinion poll reported in the news. Identify the sample size, the margin of error, the polling dates, and the source. Calculate what the margin of error means: if the poll says a candidate will receive 52 percent with a margin of error of 3 percent, what's the range?

**Application:** Interview someone in your family or a neighbor about a political issue. Ask follow-up questions: Where did you learn that? Why do you believe that? Has your opinion ever changed? What would change your mind? Then predict their vote based on their family background and life experiences. How accurate was your prediction? What was it missing?

**Synthesis:** Look up the approval rating of the current president from a month ago, a year ago, and two years ago. Graph it. Identify major events (wars, recessions, scandals, achievements) that occurred during this time. How much did each event affect approval? Was the change larger for a military event or an economic event? What does your graph suggest about what shapes presidential approval?

**Challenge:** Design a push poll on an issue you care about. Write three questions designed to push respondents toward your preferred answer. Then write three neutral questions on the same topic. Show both versions to friends. Do they notice the difference in framing? Which version would they trust more? Why?

## 7. Summary

Public opinion is a product and a puzzle. It's a product of childhood socialization—family, school, religion, peers, workplace, and media all teach us what to believe about politics. These beliefs crystallize early and resist change. This stability is good for democracy (a country where citizens share a commitment to the system is stable), but it also means that public opinion lags behind facts.

Measuring opinion is harder than it sounds. Scientific polls require a representative sample, careful question-wording, the right sample size, and honest interpretation of margins of error. The most famous polling failures—Literary Digest 1936, Dewey in 1948—teach that size alone doesn't guarantee accuracy and that timing matters. Contemporary polling faces new challenges: cell phones are harder to locate geographically; younger people don't answer landlines; online polls can be answered by the same person multiple times; robo-polls have high error rates.

Different branches of government relate to polls differently. The House tracks public opinion most closely because members face elections every two years. The Senate responds less directly, over longer time scales. Presidents use approval ratings as political capital but aren't slaves to polls. The Supreme Court is most insulated, though not completely immune.

The fundamental tension is real: should politicians be delegates, voting the people's preference, or trustees, exercising judgment against current opinion? American democracy answers: both, distributed across different institutions and time horizons.

## What Would Change This Chapter's Reading

If large-scale studies found that political ideology does change substantially after early adulthood (contrary to current evidence), the mechanism section would need rewriting. The claim that beliefs crystallize early is central to the chapter.

If polling methodologies achieved dramatically higher accuracy (through new sampling or response technologies), the section on polling error would require updating, though the trade-offs between speed, cost, and accuracy would likely persist.

## Still Puzzling

Why public opinion on some issues shifts fast (same-sex marriage acceptance nearly doubled in fifteen years) while others (gun rights, welfare support) barely move over decades. The mechanism is incomplete here. It's not simply that beliefs crystallize early—something about the salience of issues, the social networks that discuss them, and the events that challenge them matters differently than we've measured so far.
