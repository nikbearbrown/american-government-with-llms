# Chapter 8 — The Politics of Public Opinion
*What the country thinks, how we measure it, and why the answer is always murkier than it sounds.*

---

Here is a puzzle I find genuinely strange.

On the morning of November 7, 2012, Mitt Romney's campaign had a transition website ready. Cabinet appointments were drafted. Inauguration logistics were being worked through. The acceptance speech was written. One small problem: Romney had already lost. The election had been called the night before for Barack Obama.

His campaign's own internal polling had shown him competitive — close, winnable, a real race. The national polls had shown something different: Obama consistently ahead. Romney's team had believed their own numbers. They'd built a website for a presidency that wasn't coming.

What I want to know is not why political campaigns make mistakes. I want to know something harder. If polling is a scientific enterprise — with random samples and margins of error and statisticians watching every step — how does something this basic go wrong? Why does the machinery of measuring opinion produce confident wrong answers? And more importantly: what *does* it actually measure when it works?

Those are the questions this chapter is really about.

We're going to go through three things, in this order. First: where opinion comes from — not "what do people think" but *why* they think it, and why it's mostly not their fault. Second: what polling actually is mechanically, and where the machinery breaks. Third: what politicians do with the numbers — which turns out to depend entirely on which branch of government you're looking at.

---

## Where Belief Comes From

Let me start with a fact that took me a while to sit with: you didn't choose your core political beliefs.

I don't mean this as criticism. I mean it precisely. The cluster of values that shapes how you see government — whether you think markets or regulations protect people better, whether collective security or individual liberty matters more, which threats seem real and which seem exaggerated — most of that was installed before you were old enough to vote. The technical term for the process is *political socialization*, but the term makes it sound more deliberate than it is. It's not instruction. It's water. You absorb what surrounds you because you have no other reference point.

The strongest single agent is the family you grew up in. The evidence on this is consistent enough to be called a finding: children of Democrats tend to become Democrats. Children of Republicans tend to become Republicans. Children in homes with no strong party identification tend to become independents. This isn't indoctrination. It's that children watch what adults do, hear which attitudes get praised and which get criticized, and conclude — without being told — what the normal orientation toward politics looks like. They're building a model of the world, and their parents are the primary data.

As you grow, other agents take on weight. School teaches explicitly — the Pledge of Allegiance, the three branches, the founding story, the Constitution. Early lessons are idealized (Washington as hero, not as enslaver), but they are deliberate. Designed to build what political scientists call *diffuse support*: the belief that the system as a whole deserves respect, regardless of whether you like the current policies. Religious institutions connect faith to political obligation — they tell you what your values imply about abortion, military force, the scope of government. Peers socialize through trust: you absorb political information from your friends at much lower skepticism than you'd apply to a stranger, because trust and political agreement travel together. Workplaces do it quietly — teachers surround themselves with other teachers and develop policy concerns that track the profession; union members learn from their union which party has historically supported their interests.

And then there's media. Not just consuming media — being shaped by how it frames events. When one network described Ferguson protesters as "rioters" and another described them as "demonstrators," they were making choices about language that would shape how viewers understood the same event. The framing isn't always visible. Often you absorb it without knowing it's happening.

These layers accumulate into something that solidifies early. Research by James Stimson and others has found that ideological orientations formed by early adulthood tend to persist — sometimes with modest shifts, but wholesale reversals are rare. This is why you can predict voting behavior better by knowing the past than by knowing the present economic conditions. It's why politics runs in families. It's why the most sophisticated election forecasters weight prior voting heavily: past behavior is a better signal than current opinion, because current opinion is mostly past opinion held slightly longer.

The stability has a name and a function. *Diffuse support* — the shared belief that the system is worth preserving even when you disagree with its current occupants — is what allows democracies to survive. Countries where citizens' core beliefs shift quickly with every new crisis don't have stable governments; they have recurring constitutional crises. The stickiness of ideology is a feature.

But it's also a problem. The same mechanism that stabilizes democracy means that ordinary people's beliefs don't track reality as fast as reality changes. You learned your basic political framework in a different decade. The economy has changed. The environment has changed. The evidence on multiple contested questions has moved. And your fundamental orientation — shaped at age seven by watching your parents, reinforced for thirty years by your social network and your church and the media you chose — doesn't adjust proportionally.

What adjusts, instead, are positions on specific issues. Support for a specific war can drop from 60 to 40 percent in a year. Support for a specific candidate collapses after a scandal. Pollsters sometimes call this "shifting public opinion." It's more precise to say: specific positions shifted while the underlying ideological framework that generates positions held firm. The framework is durable. The applications of the framework are negotiable.

This is important for understanding everything that follows — because when we measure "public opinion," we're usually measuring specific positions. We are not measuring the ideology underneath them. The ideology is too old, too deep, and too thoroughly woven into identity to come out in a phone survey.

## What Polling Is

A poll looks simple. Call people, ask a question, report the results. The reality is an engineering problem — and like most engineering problems, it has multiple places where the design can fail.

**The first problem is who you ask.**

You can't ask everyone. You need a sample — a group small enough to interview but large enough to represent the whole. The sample must be *random*: every person in the population has an equal chance of being selected. Without randomness, you'll unconsciously select people who already agree with the answer you expect. And the sample must be *representative*: its demographic proportions should mirror the population's. A sample that's 70 percent male, in a country that's 51 percent female, will skew every answer, because men and women have systematically different political views.

Historically, pollsters drew from telephone directories. Now they use computer random-digit-dialing — a computer generates phone numbers with the area codes they want. This creates a new problem: cell phones are portable. Your area code no longer tells me where you live. Pollsters compensate with follow-up questions about zip code. The machinery is always playing catch-up with the world it's trying to measure.

**The second problem is size.**

Larger samples are more accurate. Larger samples also cost more. The relationship between the two follows a mathematical diminishing return: going from 100 to 500 respondents cuts your error substantially. Going from 1,500 to 2,000 barely moves it. Gallup often uses 500. Pew Research often uses 1,000 to 1,500. Academic studies sometimes use 2,500. The poll stops growing when additional accuracy no longer justifies the additional cost.

The result is the margin of error — the range within which the true number probably lies. A poll reporting 45 percent support with a margin of error of ±3 percent means the actual support is probably somewhere between 42 and 48 percent. When two candidates poll at 45 and 47 percent with a margin of error of 4 percent each, their true support could be anywhere from 41–49 and 43–51. The race may be tied. A poll claiming to distinguish between those numbers is making a false claim.

**The third problem is how you ask.**

A question can nudge you toward an answer without your noticing. Consider two formulations of the same thing:

- "Given the current unemployment rate, do you approve of the job the president is doing?"
- "Do you approve of the job the president is doing?"

Both ask the same question. The first frames it by mentioning unemployment, which shades the response negative. This is called a *leading question*, and legitimate polls avoid it. But some organizations design polls precisely to get a predetermined result. These are called *push polls* — disguised campaign advertising. In 2015, an organization ran a poll that included ten positive statements about Elizabeth Warren before asking respondents who they'd vote for. The result was predictable: Warren polled higher. The poll was later criticized as fabricated research. The tell is always in the question wording, which reputable pollsters publish openly.

Legitimate pollsters also test whether respondents actually know what they're being asked about. If you're polling on the Affordable Care Act, you might first ask who it serves and how it's funded. Respondents who can't answer correctly may be excluded — because otherwise you're measuring confident ignorance, not actual opinion.

**The fourth problem is time.**

In 1948, the polls showed Thomas Dewey defeating Harry Truman. The Chicago Tribune ran its famous headline: "Dewey Defeats Truman." Except Truman won. What happened? The pollsters stopped polling too early. In the final weeks before Election Day, enough voters changed their minds that the old numbers became obsolete. The methodology was sound. The timing was wrong.

Twelve years earlier, *Literary Digest* magazine predicted that Alf Landon would defeat Franklin Roosevelt in 1936 with 55 percent of the vote. Roosevelt won with 62 percent. The Digest had mailed opinion cards to people with magazine subscriptions, telephones, and car registrations — all markers of wealth. In 1936, wealthy people skewed Republican. The poor, who had neither subscriptions nor cars, couldn't respond. The Digest had over two million responses. It had confident garbage. Size doesn't fix a biased sample. It just makes the bias more expensive to produce.

Now think about Romney's pollsters in 2012. They didn't get the sample wrong. They didn't ask leading questions. What they got wrong was the turnout assumption — they believed Republican voters were unusually motivated and would turn out at high rates. When those voters didn't, the numbers that had seemed accurate became systematically wrong. The methodology was fine. The model of who would vote was wrong.

This is the deepest problem in polling: a poll doesn't measure what will happen. It measures what people say they think *now*, adjusted by assumptions about which people will actually show up. Change the assumptions and the number changes — without a single respondent lying, without a single bad question, without any fraud. The number is real. The inference from the number to the election outcome was wrong.

## What Politicians Do With the Numbers

Different branches have different relationships to polls, and understanding why explains a lot about how American government works.

The **House of Representatives** is the most responsive institution. Members serve two-year terms. They are always running. A vote that angers the district in year one is still on the record in year two, when they're explaining themselves to voters. Studies show that when public opinion moves in a consistent direction over several years, House voting patterns shift accordingly — not because individual members change their minds, but because some lose reelection and are replaced by members whose views match the new mood. The institution tracks the public, if slowly.

The **Senate** is different. Senators serve six-year terms. This buys them time to vote against public opinion, go home, give speeches explaining why it was necessary, and let the issue recede before they face voters again. Senate voting patterns don't shift in response to public mood the way House patterns do. What shifts is which senators get elected. When opinion moves, incumbent senators lose more often; new senators, elected on the new tide, bring different views. The Senate changes more slowly, more indirectly, and with greater lag than the House.

**Presidents** are the most complicated case. When a president enters office, approval tends to be high — averaging around 66 percent. They use that capital quickly, pushing priority legislation before approval declines. George W. Bush's approval jumped from 51 to 86 percent in the days after September 11. He used that surge to secure authorization for military force. The capital was real; he spent it.

Presidents also have a tool no other official has: the bully pulpit. When a president speaks, cameras appear. When Obama gave a speech on gun control after a mass shooting, he wasn't passing legislation. He was directing the nation's attention, calling on citizens to pressure Congress, framing the issue on his terms. This ability to set the agenda is itself a form of power — separate from whether the polls favor the president.

But presidents don't uniformly follow polls. Ronald Reagan and George W. Bush consistently pursued policies that contradicted public opinion and didn't substantially change course. Bill Clinton polled extensively on everything — reportedly including his dog's name. The variation isn't ideology; it's temperament and strategy. Some presidents lead. Some follow. The same president sometimes does both in the same term.

The **Supreme Court** is the outlier. Justices have lifetime tenure. They don't face elections. In theory, they're insulated from opinion entirely, which is the design — it allows them to rule on law rather than popularity.

In practice, it's messier. Some research finds that when public opinion is overwhelming and consistent, the Court is modestly more likely to rule with the majority. But the effect is weak and disputed. What's clearer is the reverse: the Court's decisions can *shape* public opinion. When the Court ruled in *Obergefell v. Hodges* (2015) that same-sex marriage was constitutional, the decision went against the political right. Yet it didn't substantially damage the Court's approval ratings. Why? Because public opinion on same-sex marriage had already shifted dramatically — from 27 percent support in 1996 to 60 percent by 2015. The Court was ruling in the direction the country was already moving.

This points to something important about the relationship between opinion and governance. The relationship doesn't run in only one direction. Public opinion constrains government. But government also shapes opinion — through decisions, speeches, agenda-setting, and the legitimacy that comes from an authoritative institution declaring something settled.

## The Tension in the Design

There's a philosophical disagreement buried in all of this.

One theory says elected officials should be *delegates* — they were sent to represent the people's preferences, and their job is to vote the way the people want. If the district supports a bill, the representative supports the bill. End of analysis. This is the most directly democratic view.

The opposite theory says officials should be *trustees* — they were chosen for their judgment, they have access to information voters don't have, and their job is to act in the public interest even when it contradicts current public preferences. If the popular policy would cause an economic collapse, vote against it and explain why. This is the view Edmund Burke articulated in 1774 when he told his Bristol constituents that he owed them his judgment, not his obedience.

The American system embeds both, distributed across institutions and time. House members are closest to delegates — short terms, direct accountability, constant pressure to track local opinion. Supreme Court justices are pure trustees — insulated from elections entirely, ruling by law rather than polls. Senators and presidents fall in between, with more latitude than House members and less than justices.

This distribution isn't accidental. The framers were trying to solve a problem: democratic legitimacy requires government to be responsive to the people; democratic stability requires that government not be *too* responsive — that it can resist temporary passion, popular error, and manufactured opinion long enough for wisdom to operate. So they built different response rates into different institutions. Fast response at the House. Slower at the Senate. Uncoupled at the Court.

The result is a system that moves in the direction of public opinion over long time horizons, while retaining space to resist it in the short term.

## What This Means

Your political beliefs arrived early, in a household and a school and a community before you had the tools to evaluate them. They're not wrong because of that — formation through socialization is how all cultures transmit values, and a society where no one shares any foundational commitments is not a society. But knowing this means you can hold your beliefs with slightly more curiosity: *where did this come from? What would it take to change it?*

Polls measure something real, with real error, in real time. They are not magic. A poll with a 4-point margin of error cannot distinguish between candidates polling at 45 and 47 percent. A poll conducted with a biased sample will be confidently wrong regardless of size. A poll timed wrong will capture opinion that has already moved.

And the branches of government attend to opinion at different rates, by design. Not because politicians are variously principled or corrupt, but because the system was built to have different response times for different institutions. The House tracks. The Senate lags. Presidents lead and follow selectively. The Court rules on law and absorbs the culture slowly, like everyone else.

The question that Romney's website raised on the morning of November 7, 2012 wasn't really about polling error. It was about the gap between what we measure and what we think we're measuring — between the number on a screen and the actual state of a country. That gap doesn't close with better methodology. It closes with better judgment about what the number can and cannot tell you.
