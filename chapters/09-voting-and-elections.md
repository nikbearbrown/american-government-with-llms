# Voting and Elections: Who Decides, and How

## Three opening sentences to choose from

1. In Kentucky in 2019, a Democrat won in a state that hadn't elected one in a generation—by fewer than 5,000 votes.
2. You do not have to vote. You know this. But what you may not know is how narrow the machinery is that lets your vote count, or fail to count.
3. It takes 623,212 signatures to put a law on California's ballot. Or 997,139 to change the Constitution. Either way, it starts with people walking neighborhoods with clipboards.

**TL;DR:** Elections are the moment democracy becomes mechanical. Who votes, when they vote, and how their votes translate to power depend on registration rules, turnout drivers, campaign strategy, and whether the system was designed to let their voice through.

## Chapter Opening: The Machinery of Participation

A courtroom in Louisville, 2015. Attorney General Andy Beshear, a Democrat working in a deep red state, is suing the governor—his boss's boss—for overreaching executive authority. Nobody gives him much chance. The state is Republican. The governor is Republican. But Beshear files case after case: on pension changes, on university boards, on anything he can reach. He wins some. He loses some. What he does win is recognition. By 2019, when Beshear runs for governor against that same Matt Bevin, the polls say Bevin is the least popular governor in America. Beshear wins the election by 5,300 votes. In a state that had not elected a Democrat governor since 1995.

If Bevin had gotten 2,651 more votes, he would have remained in office. The entire shape of Kentucky governance would be different. This is not a metaphor. This is the actual weight of a single vote, compressed into one close race, made visible.

Elections are where the idea of self-government meets the reality of counting. They are not about choice alone. They are about *translation*—how a preference held in a voter's mind becomes a vote, how votes become delegates, how delegates become nominees, how all of it becomes policy. Each translation step has rules. Rules can help the translation or block it. Rules can be written to make voting easy or to make it hard. Rules written to count everyone equally or to count some people more. This chapter traces how that machinery works: who gets to vote, what gets them to the polls, how campaigns reach them, and how their votes finally become government.

**By the end of this section, you will understand:**

- The registration rules that determine who can vote, and how those rules shift who actually does
- What drives some citizens to the polls and keeps others home, and why those patterns are measurable and predictable
- How campaigns target different voters with different intensity, and why money matters more early
- How direct democracy—ballot measures, recalls, referendums—gives citizens power that bypasses the legislature entirely, and what happens when they use it

**You'll need:** Basic knowledge of how state and federal law get made. A sense of what a primary election is. The idea that rules that seem neutral might not be.

## Concept 1: Registration and Who Can Vote

**The Cold Open: Two Doors**

A young man in 1964 Mississippi walks to register to vote. He is Black. He knows he can do this only because federal law, passed just one year earlier, says he can. Before that law, the state used "literacy tests"—exams with questions crafted so that no Black person could pass them, no matter how educated. The state also used poll taxes: a fee to vote, designed to price out the poor. And when those tactics weren't enough, the state ignored violence and intimidation at the ballot box.

That machinery of exclusion was deliberate and worked. In Mississippi in 1965, just 6.7 percent of eligible Black people were registered to vote.

Compare that to Texas in 2021. A woman sits at home filling out her driver's license renewal online. The form offers a checkbox: *Also register to vote?* She checks it. The system matches her data to the state's records. She is registered. It took thirty seconds.

Same country. Same activity. Opposite machinery.

**The Mechanism: What Registration Actually Does**

A voter registration system does one job: it creates a list of who is allowed to vote, from which addresses, in which precincts. That sounds simple until you ask: allowed according to what rules? And enforced how?

The United States Constitution sets minimal requirements. You must be a citizen. You must be eighteen years old. Beyond that, states decide. A state can require you to have lived there for thirty days. Or it can require no residency at all (North Dakota does this). A state can bar people with felony convictions forever (Kentucky does). Or it can restore voting rights after people serve their sentences (Florida voters approved this in 2018). A state can require you to have a government ID to register (Texas does). Or it can accept utility bills or sworn statements (Oregon does). A state can require registration thirty days before an election (Michigan does). Or it can let you register the day you vote (Maine, Colorado, and fifteen other states do).

Each of these rules seems like a small choice. Together, they create a system that makes voting either easy or hard. And the evidence is clear: easy registration gets more people voting. The National Commission on Voting Rights found in 2015 that citizens in states with simple registration rules were much more likely to be registered. Registration systems that are cumbersome—forms with many questions, required documents, short windows to register—correlate strongly with lower turnout.

Here is the thing about registration rules: they do not affect all groups equally. When Oregon implemented automatic voter registration in 2015—the state now registers anyone eighteen or older to vote when they renew their license—it added voters. But it added different *kinds* of voters unevenly. Young people who move frequently are less likely to be on the voter rolls. Poor people who cannot afford a state ID are less likely to be able to verify their residency. People who have been incarcerated—disproportionately Black and Latino men—are barred from voting in many states. The rules stack.

Consider the history. After the Civil War, southern states passed literacy tests, grandfather clauses (which exempted people if their fathers or grandfathers could vote before a certain date—a way to grandfather in White voters while excluding recently freed enslaved people), and poll taxes. The Supreme Court struck down grandfather clauses in 1915 (*Guinn v. United States*). Congress ended poll taxes in 1964 with the Twenty-Fourth Amendment. But the Voting Rights Act of 1965 did the real work. It let the federal government send examiners into states with a history of discrimination. Those examiners could refuse to let states implement new voting rules unless the states could prove they would not discriminate. This is called "preclearance." It worked. In Mississippi, Black registration shot from 6.7 percent in 1965 to nearly 60 percent by 1967. In Alabama, from 19.3 percent to 51.6 percent.

Then in 2013, *Shelby County v. Holder*, a 5-4 Supreme Court decision, threw out preclearance. The Court said the formula for determining which states needed federal oversight was outdated. Congress could rewrite it if it wanted to. Congress did not. Within months, states that had been under preclearance—Texas, Alabama, Georgia, North Carolina, Virginia—began changing their voting rules. Texas passed a strict photo ID requirement. North Carolina changed which polling places were open where. Georgia, Virginia, and Alabama did the same. Some provided free IDs for voting. Some did not. The evidence on whether strict ID requirements reduce turnout is split—some studies find they do, especially among minority voters; others find no effect. But the pattern afterward was consistent: these states changed the rules, and the demographic composition of who voted shifted.

**The Trade-Off**

States argue that strict registration and voting rules prevent fraud. Supporters of photo ID requirements say that if you allow anyone to vote who claims to be eligible, some people will vote twice, or people will vote in the wrong precinct, or non-citizens will vote. This is a reasonable concern.

The opposing argument is that documented election fraud is extremely rare. A Reuters analysis found that 4 percent of households with income under $25,000 said they did not have an ID that would qualify for voting. Young people eighteen to twenty-four were significantly more likely to lack the right ID. Hispanics were overrepresented. People without college degrees were overrepresented. If you make voting harder, you make it harder for some people more than others.

The United States Supreme Court, in *Crawford v. Marion County Election Board* (2008), decided that Indiana's strict photo ID law was constitutional. The Court acknowledged the law imposed a burden on some voters but said the state's interest in election integrity justified it. The decision left open the possibility that a case could be brought in the future that met a higher burden of proof to overturn it. No such case has succeeded.

Here is the core trade-off: Do you prioritize access to voting, making it easy for anyone eligible to vote? Or do you prioritize certainty that the person voting is actually eligible? You cannot maximize both. Every rule that makes voting more certain makes it harder for some people to vote. Every rule that makes voting easier creates some theoretical risk of fraud. Different states choose different balances. And because the United States is a federal system, that means voters in different states face different machinery.

**Case Study: Motor Voter and Why Easy Registration Doesn't Always Mean Higher Turnout**

In 1993, Congress passed the National Voter Registration Act, nicknamed "Motor Voter" because it required states to let people register to vote when they got or renewed a driver's license. The idea was straightforward: remove one barrier (you have to go somewhere special to register), and more people will register, and more will vote.

What happened: registration went up 7 percent between 1992 and 2012. But voter turnout did not. In fact, for two years after the law passed, turnout went down slightly. One study found that those who used Motor Voter to register were mostly people who were already intending to vote. The law did not reach the people who were never planning to go to the polls. The law was easier, but ease alone was not the answer. Something else was keeping those other people home.

Contrast that with Oregon's automatic registration in 2015. When Oregon started automatically registering people who turned eighteen, the state did not ask them to do anything. The registration happened at the DMV when they renewed their license. Was turnout higher? Yes—Oregon saw increased turnout in 2020, despite the pandemic. The key difference: Oregon made registration something that happened unless you said no. Motor Voter made people take an extra step, even if it was small. Automatic registration removes the step entirely.

The lesson is subtle. The machinery of registration matters. But the *visibility* of the step matters too. The easier something seems, the more likely someone will do it.

**Common Misconception: "If you're not registered, you just weren't motivated to vote."**

This is sometimes true. But registration happens at a single moment in time. If you move, you have to update. If you're a student, you move every year. If you're a seasonal worker, you might not know where you'll be living in October. If you're homeless, you may not have an address to put on the form. If English is not your first language, a form with complicated instructions is an extra barrier. College students are an instructive case. They move. They often have temporary addresses. Many choose not to register near campus because they think they're only there temporarily. Studies find that students who live more than two hours from home are much less likely to vote than those within thirty minutes. The reason is not motivation. The reason is logistics. The machinery of registration puts the burden on them to solve a problem the state created.

## Concept 2: Turnout and What Gets People to the Polls

**The Cold Open: A Puzzle in the Numbers**

The 2024 presidential election had record turnout. Media announced it everywhere. And it's true—64 percent of the voting-eligible population voted. But wait. In 2020, 67 percent of the voting-eligible population voted. So 2024 had *lower* turnout, not higher. How can both headlines be true?

The answer is in the denominator. Eighty-four percent of *registered voters* voted in 2024. Only 77 percent of registered voters voted in 2020. But the number of registered voters fell. If your denominator shrinks and your percentage rises, your total can still go down. In 2024, the total number of votes cast was 3.4 million fewer than in 2020. So turnout went up and down at the same time, depending on which population you measure.

This matters because it shows something important about voting behavior: it is measurable and predictable. Different groups vote at different rates. Political scientists and campaign managers know who is likely to show up. They know so well that they build strategy around it.

**The Mechanism: Why Turnout Varies—The Predictions That Work**

Start with age. People between eighteen and twenty-five vote at the lowest rates. People between sixty-five and seventy-four vote at the highest rates. In 2016, only 39 percent of eligible people eighteen to twenty-four voted. Meanwhile, 68 percent of people sixty-five to seventy-four did.

Why? The simple answer: older people depend on government. If you get Social Security, you notice when policy changes. If you're on Medicare, health policy matters. If you use transportation assistance or senior centers, government services are concrete to you. Younger people often are not yet receiving these things. They're still in school or working entry-level jobs. Government feels abstract. They're also more likely to move. When you move, you have to re-register or update your address. Campaigns don't know where you'll be on Election Day, so they don't invest in reaching you. When you're neglected by campaigns, you're less likely to become engaged. The cycle feeds itself.

What about education and income? College graduates voted at 80 percent in 2020. Non-college graduates at about 60 percent. People earning over $150,000 a year voted at rates over 80 percent. People earning under $25,000 voted at about 55 percent. Education and income are strongly connected to each other, and both are strongly connected to voting. Why? Partly material: higher income means you can take time off work to vote. Higher education often means you're more aware of how government affects you. It also means you're more likely to be in a stable home, so you're not moving as much, so you're more likely to be registered.

Race and ethnicity matter too, though the patterns are complex. In 2020, non-Hispanic White voters turned out at 71 percent. Black voters at 63 percent. Asian American voters at 59 percent. Hispanic voters at 54 percent. But these numbers vary sharply by state. In New Mexico, with a long history of Hispanic political organization, Hispanic turnout reached 59 percent in 2020. Context matters. Political culture matters. If a community has been historically active in politics, if there are organizations reaching out, if candidates are visible, turnout goes up.

Gender is relevant mostly historically. In 1920, women got the right to vote. For decades, they voted at lower rates than men. Now they vote at higher rates. In 2016, 63 percent of women voted compared to 59 percent of men. Women are now more likely to vote, on average, than men.

Here is the core insight: None of these variables is destiny. An eighteen-year-old can vote. An immigrant can vote. A person earning low wages can vote. But the probability shifts with each of these factors. Campaigns know this. They target people with high probability of voting. They target older voters. They target educated voters. They target people who voted last time. They spend far less on people unlikely to vote, even though reaching those people might shift the outcome.

**The Trade-Off: Turnout vs. Selection**

One way to increase voter turnout is simple: make voting compulsory. Belgium and Turkey require citizens to vote or pay a fine. Turnout in those countries is in the mid-80s. The United States? About 60-67 percent depending on the election and the population measured. Chile moved from compulsory to voluntary voting and saw turnout drop from 87 percent to 46 percent.

But there is a trade-off. Compulsory voting increases participation of the *entire* population, including people who know little about the candidates, who care little about the election, who are voting only because it's required. Optional voting means the people who show up tend to be more invested. They are "likely voters"—people who care enough to work around the barriers (registration, time, location, work schedules).

Is higher turnout better? Or should voting require some minimum level of interest and information? There is no right answer. A healthy democracy probably wants both some participation from most people and concentrated energy from those who care most. The United States currently selects for the second over the first.

**Case Study: Early Voting and Mail-in Voting—the Logistics Question**

One simple fact: if you make voting harder logistically, fewer people vote. Polling places open on one day, usually a weekday, usually during work hours. This is hard for people juggling jobs, childcare, school.

Some states tried early voting. Texas opened early voting in 1988. The state saw an initial bump in turnout, but the effect faded over time. Why might that be? One theory: when voting is spread over weeks, the social pressure to vote decreases. Election Day becomes an event. Early voting makes it a convenience, which is good, but it also removes the collective moment.

Other states moved to mail-in voting. Colorado, Hawaii, Oregon, Utah, and Washington all shifted to all-mail systems. The results? Higher turnout. Colorado went from 1.8 million votes cast in 2010 to 2.6 million in 2018—an increase of nearly 50 percent. These systems remove logistical barriers. You vote at home, on your time, with the ballot in front of you so you can research as you go.

There is one cost: if you vote early or by mail, you cannot change your mind when breaking news emerges. In 2016, two major stories broke in the final days: the Access Hollywood tape of Donald Trump on October 7, and FBI Director James Comey's letter reopening the Hillary Clinton email investigation on October 28. Did these change how people voted? We can't know exactly. But candidates running in mail-in states cannot count on those last-minute persuasions.

**Common Misconception: "People who don't vote just don't care enough."**

Some don't. But many others face real barriers. Census data consistently show that people cite "too busy" as the top reason they didn't vote (19 percent). But 5.5 percent cite restrictive registration laws. 3.3 percent cite transportation problems. These are material barriers, not character flaws. A mother working two part-time jobs, without reliable childcare, cannot "just find the time" to vote, no matter how much she cares. The machinery of voting was designed when most workers were male, had a single full-time job, and had a spouse at home. That changed. The machinery has not fully caught up.

## Concept 3: Campaigns, Money, and Reaching Voters

**The Cold Open: The Year Donald Trump Won Without Spending Much**

In the 2016 Republican primary, most candidates raised millions. Jeb Bush raised over $100 million. Ted Cruz raised $14 million early on. Marco Rubio, John Kasich, Chris Christie—all serious money.

Donald Trump spent much less. He raised $25 million compared to Bush's $100 million-plus. Yet Trump won. How?

The answer is not that Trump is a political genius who proved campaign money doesn't matter. The answer is that Trump got coverage. He was shocking. He said things no major candidate said. The press covered him constantly. The estimate is that Trump received roughly $2 billion in free media coverage during the primary—coverage that paid for itself many times over. Without that free coverage, he would not have won. But he had a huge advantage most candidates don't: he was already famous. He owned buildings with his name on them. He had a hit TV show called *The Apprentice*. When he spoke, people knew who he was.

This is the lesson: campaigns spend money to become known and to reach voters. But if you're already known, you can spend less and still win.

**The Mechanism: How Campaigns Reach Voters**

Campaign spending goes to several places. Early money is especially valuable. EMILY's List, a political action group, was founded on the principle that "Early Money Is Like Yeast." It makes the dough rise. Candidates who raise money early in the process can afford to advertise early, which builds name recognition, which attracts more donations, which allows more advertising. The dynamic feeds itself.

Why is early money so important? Because in a primary, there are many candidates. Voters don't know who most of them are. A governor from Arkansas or a junior senator from Illinois is not a household name. Candidates must spend to become known. Advertising is expensive. Television is expensive. Digital advertising is cheaper but still requires spending.

In a general election, the problem is different. Each party has only one candidate. Most voters know which candidate they're supporting based on party. Campaigns instead spend on three things: reaching undecided voters and independents, convincing party members to actually show up and vote, and using shadow campaigns (Super PACs and other groups not officially connected to the candidate) to run negative ads attacking the opponent.

Negative ads work. They are memorable. But they also seem to decrease turnout, which is interesting—if you convince people all politicians are corrupt, fewer people vote. Yet campaigns keep running them because the voters who do come out tend to remember them.

Technology has changed how campaigns reach people. Television is old. Digital is new. Candidates now advertise on social media, use text messages, run search engine ads. Barack Obama in 2008 used texts and tweets to reach young people. In 2004, Howard Dean raised money online by posting a video of himself eating a turkey sandwich—a "down-to-earth" gesture that raised over $200,000 online. By 2020, campaigns were doing sophisticated micro-targeting based on what people searched for online, where they lived, what they bought.

This creates a problem: campaigns can reach some voters with entirely different messages than they send to others. A candidate can tell suburban women she supports gun control while telling rural men that she defends the Second Amendment. The Internet allows this. Television did not.

**The Trade-Off: Candidate-Centered vs. Party-Centered Campaigns**

Once, campaigns centered on parties. You voted Republican or Democrat because you were Republican or Democrat. Party machinery did the work of reaching voters, organizing neighborhoods, turning out voters.

Now campaigns center on individual candidates. This is good in one sense: it lets outsider candidates challenge the party establishment. Donald Trump did this in 2016. Bernie Sanders did it in 2020. In both cases, the candidates had weaker party connections but strong individual followings.

It is bad in another sense: it requires more money. Party machinery is cheap once it exists. Candidate machinery has to be built from scratch for each race. It's also more susceptible to a candidate's personal flaws becoming disqualifying. A party can survive one weak candidate. An individual who makes a gaffe or faces a scandal can sink fast.

**Case Study: Iowa, New Hampshire, and Why Early Contests Matter Disproportionately**

The Iowa caucuses and New Hampshire primary are small states. Together, they represent about 1 percent of the U.S. population. Yet they are the first contests in presidential campaigns. Massive media attention. Campaigns spend enormous sums in these two states.

Why? Because momentum. The candidate who finishes first or second in Iowa or New Hampshire gets media coverage saying they're "on fire" or a "front-runner." They raise more money. They attract better campaign staff. Candidates who come in third or fourth drop out, even if they haven't lost yet. The narrative momentum is everything.

In 2020, Joe Biden came fourth in Iowa and fifth in New Hampshire. By the metrics of those states, he should have dropped out. Instead, he finished second in Nevada and won South Carolina decisively. That shifted the narrative. Suddenly Biden was the "comeback candidate." Other moderates dropped out and endorsed him. He swept the Super Tuesday primaries. He became the nominee.

The point: the machinery of the primary process—which states vote first, when they vote, the delegate math—matters more than the machinery of voting itself. A candidate can lose early and still win the nomination, if the narrative is favorable. Or win early and still lose the nomination, if the narrative shifts. The campaign, not the voters, drives the story.

**Common Misconception: "Money determines who wins elections."**

Money matters. Candidates with money can advertise. Candidates without money fade. But it's not deterministic. Trump spent less and won. Money is more important in congressional races, where most voters don't know who the candidates are, than in presidential races, where candidates are famous. Money is more important in primary elections, where there are many candidates, than in general elections, where there are usually only two. Money matters, but it's not fate.

## Integration: How Registration, Turnout, and Campaigns Work Together

The election machinery has three parts that reinforce each other.

First: registration rules determine *who can vote*. These rules are not neutral. They make voting easier for some people and harder for others. States justify strict rules by saying they prevent fraud. States with loose rules say they maximize access. Both are true. You have to choose which matters more.

Second: turnout patterns are predictable. Certain kinds of people are more likely to vote. Campaigns know this and target accordingly. They spend money and effort on people likely to vote. They neglect people unlikely to vote. This means the machinery selects for certain kinds of voters—older, wealthier, more educated, more stable housing—even though the machinery is written in race-neutral language.

Third: campaigns reach voters through money, advertising, and the machinery of the primary process. But they also reach voters through social networks. If someone you know votes, you're more likely to vote. If your family is politically engaged, you're more likely to be. If your community has a history of organizing, turnout goes up. Campaigns matter, but so do families, communities, and social pressure.

These three layers interact. Strict registration requirements reduce turnout most among the young, the mobile, the poor—the people least likely to vote anyway. Campaigns that target predictable voters ignore the communities where turnout is lowest, which lowers it further. The machinery feeds itself.

This is not a conspiracy. It's a system with internal logic. But the logic privileges some voters over others, even though the law says all votes are equal.

## Direct Democracy: When Citizens Vote to Make Law

**The Cold Open: A Ballot with Forty-Two Measures**

A voter in San Francisco walks into the polling place on Election Day 2016. The ballot is forty-two measures long. Seventeen are state-level propositions. Twenty-five are local ballot measures. Many require serious thought. A bond to fund transit. A change in the city charter. Rules on zoning. Property taxes. The voter is handed a thirty-page sample ballot with argument for and against each measure.

This is democracy. It is also chaos. No voter can possibly research all forty-two measures responsibly. Yet all forty-two are on the ballot. California law makes it easy to put measures on the ballot—you need only 5 percent of votes from the last gubernatorial election as signatures. That's 623,212 signatures for a law, 997,139 for a constitutional amendment. Doable with money and organization.

California voters approved more than a dozen ballot measures in 2020. One state. One election. Twelve laws decided directly by voters rather than by the legislature.

**The Mechanism: Direct Democracy, Three Forms**

Direct democracy occurs when voters decide policy questions directly instead of through representatives. It takes three forms.

*Referendum*: The government proposes something (a bond measure, a constitutional amendment, a judicial appointment) and voters say yes or no. This is the mildest form—citizens ratify, they don't initiate.

*Initiative* (or *proposition*): Citizens propose a law or constitutional amendment, gather signatures from registered voters, and if they get enough signatures, the measure goes on the ballot. If voters pass it, it becomes law, bypassing the legislature and governor entirely. This is the strongest form of direct democracy.

*Recall*: Voters decide to remove a politician from office before their term ends. This is rare and expensive. California Governor Gray Davis was recalled in 2003 when the state faced an energy crisis. Governor Scott Walker of Wisconsin faced a recall in 2012 over labor union disputes; he spent over $60 million defending his seat and won.

The process for initiatives is laborious. You write the text of the law you want. You file paperwork with the state. The state attorney general or another official reviews it to make sure it's legal and estimates its cost. You then have a limited time—often 150 days, sometimes up to two years—to collect signatures from registered voters equal to a percentage of votes from the last election. Different states have different thresholds. California's are lower (5-8 percent); Oklahoma's are higher (8-15 percent). Once you have signatures, a county elections office verifies them. If enough signatures are valid, the measure goes on the next ballot. You then campaign to get voters to approve it.

Seems straightforward. But the signature collection is expensive. In larger states, you might need hundreds of thousands of signatures. You can hire people to collect signatures, but many states regulate how much you can pay them or whether you can pay them at all. So you either have grassroots organization (volunteers) or money. Most initiatives are backed by one or the other, often money.

Once on the ballot, the problem shifts. Voters face a complex measure, often with arguments for and against, and must decide with limited information. Many voters simply skip ballot measures they don't understand. California saw this in 2008 with Proposition 8, which asked voters whether same-sex couples should have the right to marry. The title was "Eliminates Rights of Same-Sex Couples to Marry," and the measure did exactly that—a yes vote would ban same-sex marriage. But some voters, wanting to support same-sex marriage, voted yes because they thought that's what they were doing. Others voted no believing they were voting against same-sex marriage. Confusion was rampant.

**The Trade-Off: Direct Democracy vs. Representative Democracy**

Direct democracy sounds good in principle: let people decide directly, don't trust politicians. But it has costs.

It requires more of voters. A representative system asks you to pick someone you trust to learn the issues. You vote once per position per election. A direct democracy system asks you to research every ballot measure, understand complex policy questions, and vote many times. Most people don't have time for this. So they rely on ballot titles, campaign ads, or the recommendations of groups they trust. This is fine until the ballot title is misleading (as in Prop 8) or until well-funded special interests run the ads.

Direct democracy also opens the door to special interests using it to pass their own pet projects. When California Governor Gray Davis faced recall in 2003, it cost millions to gather signatures. But Representative Darrell Issa (R-CA) donated $2 million of his own money to help push the recall forward. His personal wealth decided whether the recall moved forward. Is that direct democracy by the people, or direct democracy by the wealthy?

Colorado legalized recreational marijuana through initiative (Amendment 64) in 2012. Other states followed. Now forty-two states have some form of legal marijuana. These are places where voters decided directly, voting against the federal government's Controlled Substances Act, which still classifies marijuana as a dangerous drug. The federal government has mostly chosen not to prosecute medical users, but recreational use is still illegal at the federal level. So you can legally buy marijuana in your state and still be breaking federal law. Direct democracy gave citizens power, but that power created a conflict—state law vs. federal law—that has never been fully resolved.

**Case Study: Why Ballot Measures Get Fewer Votes Than Top-of-Ticket Races**

In Colorado in 2012, 70 percent of registered voters cast a ballot for the presidential race. But only 54 percent voted on a measure asking whether to retain Supreme Court Justice Nathan Coats. This is ballot fatigue—voters come to vote for president, vote for senator, and then stop, fatigued by the long ballot.

Some states tried to solve this with straight-ticket voting, which lets you select one box at the top and all candidates from that party are selected automatically. This reduces fatigue. But it also reduces choice—if you want to vote Republican for president but Democratic for a local judicial race (which are often nonpartisan), straight-ticket voting prevents you. In Michigan in 2010, a group that included actors from the TV show *The West Wing* created an ad reminding voters to also vote for state supreme court, not just the top of the ballot. The ad worked. The candidate won.

The lesson: the structure of the ballot itself affects the outcome. If a measure is at the top, more people vote on it. If it's at the bottom, fewer do. Candidates and interest groups know this and jockey for position. In some states, you can time your signature collection so your initiative appears on a presidential election ballot (high turnout) instead of a midterm ballot (low turnout), and the timing affects whether you pass.

**Common Misconception: "Direct democracy is how democracy is supposed to work—the will of the people."**

Direct democracy *can* reflect the will of the people. But it can also reflect the will of whoever can afford to collect signatures and run ads. And it can reflect the will of a portion of the people who happened to vote that year, not the broader public. When only 54 percent of voters say yes or no to a Supreme Court retention, is that "the people deciding," or is that a small subset of people who happened to care about that particular race?

## Exercises: How to Think Like a Campaign Manager

### Warm-Up: Know Your Voters

You're a campaign manager for a state senate race in a working-class district. Your candidate is a former school teacher running against an incumbent business owner. Early polls show you're down 8 points. You have $200,000 to spend before the election. What do you spend it on?

First, ask: Who votes in this district? Pull data. If the district is older, voters are more likely to be home during the day and to watch television. Television ads might work. If the district is younger, voters are more likely to be on phones and social media. Digital ads might work better. If the district has high turnout, even unlikely voters might show up, so you might as well spend on everyone. If the district has low turnout, spend on likely voters only.

Second, ask: Who do I need to persuade? You're down 8 points. You need either to persuade some of the incumbent's voters to switch, or to mobilize some of your voters who are unlikely to vote. Persuasion usually costs more than mobilization. Which can your budget do?

Third, ask: What message works? Your candidate is a teacher. That resonates with union voters and people with school-age kids. It doesn't resonate as well with retirees or business owners. Your candidate probably doesn't win the business owner vote. So don't spend money trying. Spend money on your actual constituency.

**Your answer might be:** Spend $80,000 on digital ads targeting voters ages 25-50, especially union members and parents. Spend $60,000 on direct mail to similar voters. Spend $30,000 on a phone banking operation to identify your voters and remind them to vote. Spend $30,000 on a candidate tour of high-turnout neighborhoods. You're using your limited money to reach the people most likely to support you and to turn them out.

### Application: Registration Rules and Turnout

Five states, five different registration rules. Using what you know about what drives turnout, predict which state will have highest turnout and which will have lowest.

**State A**: Online voter registration, same-day registration allowed, automatic registration for all people turning 18.

**State B**: Paper registration only, must register 30 days before election, strict photo ID required at polls, state provides free ID if you apply.

**State C**: No voter registration required (you can vote if you show up with proof of residency).

**State D**: Online registration, must register 14 days before election, student IDs not accepted at polls.

**State E**: Paper registration only, must register 45 days before election, photo ID required, but many people lack acceptable forms of ID.

**Prediction**: State A will have the highest turnout. It removes every barrier to registration. State E will have the lowest. It has multiple barriers and makes it hard to get acceptable ID.

**Why?** Because registration barriers disproportionately affect young voters, people who move frequently, and low-income people. State A embraces this and reaches out to them. State E erects barriers that discourage them. The rules determine who actually votes, even though the law says everyone can.

### Synthesis: The 2024 Election and Why It Happened

In the 2024 U.S. presidential election, several things happened simultaneously: Joe Biden, the incumbent, initially had strong support from his party's base but faced concerns about his age and capacity. Donald Trump, the Republican nominee, faced multiple indictments. Third-party candidate Robert Kennedy Jr. initially ran but then dropped out and endorsed Trump. Turnout (by voting-eligible population) was 64 percent, down from 67 percent in 2020.

What do you know about what affects election outcomes? Use the framework from this chapter to explain:

1. **Registration**: How did registration rules affect who could vote and which demographic groups were most affected?

2. **Turnout**: Why might turnout have gone down from 2020 to 2024, even though this was a competitive race with major figures?

3. **Campaigns**: How did campaigns reach voters? What messaging worked in the primaries vs. the general election?

4. **Strategic voting**: Why did Kennedy drop out of the race and endorse Trump instead of staying in as a third-party candidate?

**Sample answer for #2 (Turnout)**:

Several factors could explain lower 2024 turnout despite competitive race:
- **Age fatigue**: Both major-party nominees were elderly (Biden 81, Trump 78 when elected). This might mobilize younger voters but could depress others who worried about age issues for both.
- **COVID rebound**: 2020 saw massive mail-in voting and pandemic-driven turnout. By 2024, people returned to normal voting patterns, and some of that 2020 surge didn't repeat.
- **Registration changes**: Voter rolls may have been cleaned up between elections. Some 2020 voters may not have re-registered.
- **Apathy**: If voters felt both candidates were equally flawed, some might have stayed home. This is pocketbook voting and apathy combined.
- **Structural factors**: If some early-voting or same-day registration options were reduced in swing states (possible after recent elections), logistical barriers could have reduced turnout.

### Challenge: Design Your Own Ballot Measure

You want to pass a state law that funds free community college tuition. You must design an initiative. Answer these questions:

1. **The text**: What exactly does your measure do? Does it fund all states? Just in-state students? All degrees or just two-year degrees? Be specific. Vagueness kills ballot measures.

2. **The title**: How do you title this on the ballot? Make it clear, accurate, and persuasive. (Remember Prop 8—a confusing title kills you.)

3. **The funding**: How do you pay for it? Income tax? Sales tax? Corporate tax? Tax the wealthy? Do you say in the measure, or leave it to the legislature? (Leaving it to the legislature looks like you're dodging the hard question.)

4. **Signature gathering**: You need 600,000 signatures in your state. You have 150 days. Do you hire signature collectors or organize volunteers? How much will this cost? ($1-3 per signature is typical.)

5. **Campaign message**: Who is your constituency? Students? Parents? Teachers? What's your message to each group? What opposition do you expect, and how do you respond?

**Challenge answer for #1 (The text):**

"Free community college tuition: All students, regardless of income, attending public two-year community colleges in this state, may attend tuition-free, beginning Fall 2026. The cost of tuition is paid by the state from general revenue. Books and fees are not covered. Transfer to four-year universities is not covered. Existing financial aid for other purposes remains available."

Why be this specific? Because a vague measure invites court challenges. "Free community college" could mean free to everyone or means-tested, could include books or not. Be specific upfront.

## Chapter Summary

Elections are the machinery through which democracy becomes real. That machinery has three moving parts.

First is registration. Rules vary from state to state. Easy registration rules (automatic registration, same-day registration, simple online systems) correlate with higher turnout. Difficult rules (strict photo ID, long registration windows, complex forms) correlate with lower turnout. The rules are written in race-neutral language, but they do not have race-neutral effects. Historically, strict rules were designed to reduce Black voting. After the Voting Rights Act of 1965, preclearance (requiring federal approval for voting rule changes in states with a history of discrimination) increased Black registration dramatically. When the Supreme Court struck down preclearance in *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013), states began tightening rules again. Registration rules matter. They determine who actually votes.

Second is turnout. Not everyone who can vote does. Turnout is predictable. Older voters, wealthier voters, more educated voters, voters in more stable housing, people who have voted before—all vote at higher rates. Younger voters, poorer voters, less educated voters, people who move frequently, people new to voting—all vote at lower rates. Campaigns know this. They spend money on people likely to vote. They spend less on people unlikely to vote. This means the electoral machinery selects for certain kinds of voters even though all voters theoretically have equal power. Mail-in voting and early voting can increase turnout by removing logistical barriers. Compulsory voting increases turnout in countries that have it. But increasing turnout requires either removing barriers or changing incentives, and both have trade-offs.

Third is campaigns. Candidates need money to become known, especially in primaries with many candidates. Early money is particularly valuable because it builds name recognition, which attracts more money, which attracts better campaign staff and media. Campaigns reach voters through television, digital advertising, social media, direct mail, and door-to-door canvassing. Modern campaigns can micro-target voters, sending different messages to different groups. Negative ads work but may depress turnout. Campaign spending matters most in elections where voters don't know the candidates. In presidential elections, candidates are famous, so the effect of spending is weaker. In congressional elections, candidates are often unknown, so spending is crucial.

Beyond these three is direct democracy: ballot initiatives, referendums, and recalls. These give citizens the power to bypass the legislature entirely and make law directly. The process requires collecting signatures and then winning a vote. Direct democracy sounds like pure democracy, but it requires either grassroots organization or money. Most major initiatives are backed by one or the other. Voters often skip ballot measures they don't understand, especially at the bottom of long ballots. Ballot fatigue is real. And sometimes ballot measures create conflicts: Colorado voters legalized marijuana through initiative, but it remains illegal under federal law. Direct democracy gave voters power but did not resolve the tensions that power creates.

All of these—registration, turnout, campaigns, direct democracy—are machinery. They seem neutral. But none of them are. Each creates certain patterns of who votes, when, for whom, and with what effect. Understanding the machinery is understanding why elections come out the way they do.

## Connections Forward

How do elections translate into government? That happens through the structure of representation. Some elections pick a president who wins all of a state's electoral votes, even if the margin was narrow. Some elections pick representatives from gerrymandered districts. Some elections pick senators representing very different populations and getting different per-capita resources. The machinery of *converting votes to power* is as important as the machinery of voting itself. That comes next.

Also forward: campaign finance is a recurring tension. The Supreme Court's *Citizens United* decision (2010) allowed corporations and unions to spend unlimited money on political advertising. This increased the role of money in elections significantly. How much money is too much? When does money become speech? When does speech become corruption? These questions animate electoral politics. And the answers differ depending on your theory of democracy: direct rule by the people, or representative rule by elites? Those theories shape everything—voting rules, campaign rules, redistricting rules.
