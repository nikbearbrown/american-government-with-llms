# Chapter 9 — Voting And Elections: Who Decides, And How
*The machinery of participation, and why it is never neutral.*

---

In Kentucky in 2019, a Democrat won the governorship in a state that hadn't elected one in a generation. Andy Beshear beat the sitting governor, Matt Bevin, by 5,300 votes. In a state of 3.5 million registered voters, that margin is 0.15 percent. If 2,651 people had stayed home, or voted differently, the entire shape of Kentucky governance would have changed.

This is not a metaphor about every vote counting. It is a precise fact about one election. And it raises a question that sounds simple but isn't: what determines whether those 2,651 people show up?

The answer is machinery. Not metaphorical machinery — actual rules, systems, and structures that govern who can register, who actually votes, how campaigns reach them, and how votes translate into power. That machinery is the subject of this chapter. It looks neutral. It is not. Every rule that makes voting easier or harder, every system that targets some voters and ignores others, every calendar choice about when polls open and close — these are design decisions, and design decisions have consequences.

---

## The Registration Problem

Here is a fact that should be stranger than it seems: before you can vote in the United States, you have to apply.

Most democracies automatically enroll citizens to vote when they reach adulthood. The U.S. does not. You have to find the right form, fill it out, submit it before the deadline, and make sure the information matches your records. If you move, you start over. This is a choice — not an accident of history — and the consequences are measurable.

The Constitution sets minimal requirements. You must be a citizen. You must be eighteen. Everything else is up to states. A state can require photo ID to register. Or it can accept a utility bill. It can require registration thirty days before the election. Or it can let you register on Election Day itself. It can permanently bar anyone with a felony conviction. Or it can restore voting rights after sentences are served. The variation is not small — it is the difference between a system that treats voting as the default and one that treats it as something you have to earn the right to do.

The evidence on what these choices produce is consistent. States with automatic registration, same-day registration, and simple online systems have higher turnout. States with strict photo ID requirements, long pre-registration windows, and complex forms have lower turnout. This should not be surprising. The harder something is, the fewer people do it.

But the effect is not uniform across the population. It concentrates. Young people move frequently — for college, for jobs, for apartments — which means they re-register more often and fall off voter rolls more often. Poor people are less likely to have the kind of government-issued photo ID that some states require. People who have been incarcerated — disproportionately Black and Latino men — are barred from voting entirely in many states. The rules are written in race-neutral language. They do not have race-neutral effects.

This is not new. For most of American history, voting restrictions were not subtle. In Mississippi in 1965, just 6.7 percent of eligible Black citizens were registered. The state used literacy tests — exams designed so that no Black applicant could pass them regardless of education. It used poll taxes — fees designed to price out the poor. It used violence. These were not incidental failures of administration. They were policy.

The Voting Rights Act of 1965 broke that machinery. It allowed the federal government to send examiners into states with a documented history of discrimination. Any state that wanted to change its voting rules had to prove to the federal government in advance that the change would not discriminate. This is called preclearance. It worked: Black registration in Mississippi went from 6.7 percent in 1965 to nearly 60 percent by 1967. In Alabama, from 19.3 percent to 51.6 percent.

<!-- → [CHART: bar chart of Black voter registration rates in Mississippi and Alabama — two bars per state: 1965 (pre-VRA) and 1967 (post-VRA) — student should see the magnitude of the change in two years and understand what preclearance enforcement produced when actually applied] -->

In 2013, the Supreme Court threw out preclearance in *Shelby County v. Holder*. The Court's majority held that the formula determining which states needed federal oversight was based on outdated data. Congress could update the formula if it chose to. Congress did not. Within months, states that had been under preclearance — Texas, Alabama, Georgia, North Carolina — began changing their voting rules. Strict photo ID requirements. Changes to which polling places were open and where. Reductions in early voting hours. Whether these changes meaningfully reduced turnout, and for whom, is empirically contested — some studies find effects, others find none. What is not contested is that the changes happened immediately and consistently, as soon as the legal constraint was removed.

The underlying mechanism is the one worth understanding: registration rules determine who is on the list of people allowed to vote. Who is on that list determines who campaigns bother reaching. Who campaigns bother reaching determines who thinks their vote matters. Who thinks their vote matters determines who shows up. The effect of a registration rule is not just the people it directly excludes — it is the cascade of signals and decisions that follows.

## Who Shows Up and Why

Not everyone who can vote does. In 2020, about 67 percent of the voting-eligible population voted — a record high for a presidential election. In 2024, that fell to 64 percent. In a typical midterm election, turnout is closer to 40 percent. In local elections, it can be 15 to 20 percent.

This is not random. Turnout patterns are stable enough that political scientists can predict them with reasonable accuracy, and campaign managers build entire strategies around them. The patterns are worth knowing because they explain why the electorate — the people who actually decide elections — is not the same as the population it supposedly represents.

Age is the strongest predictor. In 2016, 39 percent of eligible voters aged eighteen to twenty-four voted. Among voters aged sixty-five to seventy-four, the figure was 68 percent. The gap has persisted for decades. Why? One answer: older voters depend concretely on government. If you receive Social Security and Medicare, policy changes have immediate, visible effects on your life. Government is not abstract. If you are twenty-two, working an entry-level job, and renting an apartment, the connection between your vote and your daily life is harder to see. This is not apathy — it is a rational response to a system that returns less visible value to you.

Income and education reinforce the age pattern. College graduates voted at roughly 80 percent in 2020. Non-college graduates at about 60 percent. People earning over $150,000 voted at over 80 percent. People earning under $25,000 at about 55 percent. Higher income means more time flexibility — you can take an hour off work to vote. Higher education correlates with more stable housing and more information about when and where to vote. The barriers are not psychological. They are logistical and material.

Race and ethnicity also correlate with turnout, though the patterns are complicated by the fact that race correlates with income and education, and by the fact that political mobilization varies enormously by community. In 2020, non-Hispanic White voters turned out at 71 percent, Black voters at 63 percent, Asian American voters at 59 percent, Hispanic voters at 54 percent. But in New Mexico, with a long history of Hispanic political organization, Hispanic turnout was significantly higher than the national average. The political culture of a community — whether organizations are reaching people, whether candidates are visible and relevant, whether voting is treated as a normal expectation — matters as much as demographic factors.

Campaigns know all of this. They buy data. They model likely voters. They identify which precincts have high concentrations of their supporters, which voters have voted in the last three elections, which registered voters have never voted at all. Then they make a calculation: spend money on people likely to vote, spend less on people unlikely to vote. This is rational from the campaign's perspective. Its consequence is that the people least likely to vote get the least outreach, which makes them less likely to vote, which confirms the model that they're not worth reaching. The pattern self-reinforces.

One intervention consistently increases turnout: removing the logistics. Mail-in voting — used statewide in Colorado, Hawaii, Oregon, Utah, and Washington — lets voters fill out their ballot at home, on their own schedule, with the ballot in front of them. Colorado went from 1.8 million votes cast in 2010 to 2.6 million in 2018, an increase of nearly 50 percent. The mechanism is simple: voting by mail eliminates the need to take time off work, find transportation, locate a polling place, stand in line. It removes the friction that makes voting hard for people who are already managing multiple demands.

Same-day registration has a similar effect. If the biggest barrier to voting is that you moved and forgot to re-register, or that you turned eighteen after the registration deadline, or that you didn't know the deadline existed, then allowing registration at the polls eliminates that barrier entirely.

The pattern suggests a principle: the barriers to voting are not random obstacles. They are friction that falls most heavily on the people with the least slack — least time, least stability, least prior experience with the system. Removing friction raises turnout. Adding friction lowers it. This is not a surprising finding. It is a predictable consequence of design.

## Campaigns, Money, and Who Gets Reached

In the 2016 Republican primary, Jeb Bush raised over $100 million. Donald Trump raised roughly $25 million. Trump won.

The easy interpretation is that money doesn't matter. That is wrong. The correct interpretation is that Trump had something money usually buys: he was already famous. He owned buildings with his name on them. He had a hit television show. When he said something, networks covered it. The estimate is that Trump received approximately $2 billion in free media coverage during the primary — coverage that would have cost an ordinary candidate hundreds of millions of dollars to match. He didn't need to buy attention because he already had it. Most candidates do not have this advantage. Most candidates need money to exist in voters' minds at all.

Why is early money particularly valuable? Because in a primary with many candidates, voters don't know who most of them are. A governor from a small state, a junior senator, a former cabinet official — these are not household names. Candidates must buy recognition. The candidate who raises money early can advertise early. Early advertising builds name recognition. Name recognition attracts more donations. More donations allow more advertising. The cycle feeds itself. EMILY's List, a political action group, was built on the explicit principle that "Early Money Is Like Yeast" — it makes the whole enterprise rise. The candidates who fail to establish early momentum tend to drop out before most voters have engaged with them at all.

In a general election, the calculus shifts. Each party has one candidate. Most voters already know who they're supporting based on party identification alone. The marginal voter — the person who genuinely hasn't decided, or who might not show up — is a much smaller pool. Campaigns in general elections spend on three things: persuading that small pool of undecided voters, mobilizing their own supporters who might stay home, and running negative advertising through Super PACs and other outside groups not officially connected to the candidate.

Negative ads work. They are memorable in ways that positive ads often are not. But they appear to decrease turnout overall, because they convince some voters that all politicians are corrupt and not worth voting for. Campaigns run them anyway, because the voters who remain — those who come out despite the negativity — tend to favor the side that ran the ads. The math works even if the aggregate effect on democracy does not.

Technology has changed the texture of all of this. Campaigns now micro-target voters with digital advertising — sending different messages to different people based on what they have searched for, what they have purchased, where they live, what they have previously clicked on. A candidate can tell suburban women she supports gun safety measures and tell rural men she defends the Second Amendment. Television didn't allow this — the same ad went to everyone watching the same broadcast. The Internet does. The consequence is that voters in the same election may encounter entirely different versions of the same candidate, and have no way of knowing that other voters are seeing something different.

## The Iowa Problem

The Iowa caucuses and New Hampshire primary together represent about 1 percent of the U.S. population. They are the first contests of every presidential primary. They receive enormous media attention. Campaigns spend disproportionate resources in both states for months before the contests.

Why? Momentum. The candidate who finishes first or second in Iowa or New Hampshire gets covered as a front-runner. Coverage attracts donations. Donations attract better staff and more advertising. Candidates who finish poorly lose donors, lose staff, lose the narrative, and often drop out — even though Iowa and New Hampshire have selected exactly zero delegates that would be needed to win the nomination.

The 2020 Democratic primary illustrates both the power and the limits of this mechanism. Joe Biden finished fourth in Iowa and fifth in New Hampshire. By those metrics, he was finished. Instead, he finished second in Nevada and won South Carolina decisively. That shifted the narrative — suddenly Biden was the comeback candidate. Other moderate candidates dropped out and endorsed him. He swept Super Tuesday. He became the nominee.

<!-- → [CHART: timeline of the 2020 Democratic primary — horizontal axis showing contest dates from Iowa through Super Tuesday — each candidate shown as a line, with dropouts marked — Biden's trajectory highlighted to show the bottom-then-surge pattern — student should see how the delegate math (nearly zero from Iowa/NH) contrasts with the narrative momentum those contests generate] -->

The mechanism here is not purely about voter preferences. It is about the interaction between voter preferences, media narrative, and campaign resource dynamics. A candidate who loses momentum loses money loses staff loses coverage loses voters who conclude there is no point voting for someone unlikely to win. The primary system does not simply aggregate voter preferences. It filters them through a sequence of contests whose order and structure were chosen arbitrarily and whose effects are outsized relative to what they would be if all states voted simultaneously.

## Direct Democracy and Its Costs

Twenty-six states allow citizens to place laws and constitutional amendments on the ballot directly, bypassing the legislature. In California, getting a law on the ballot requires 623,212 valid signatures from registered voters. A constitutional amendment requires 997,139. These are large numbers, but they are achievable — with money, organization, or both.

California voters approved more than a dozen ballot measures in 2020. Some of the most significant policy changes in recent American history came through direct ballot initiative: California's Proposition 13 in 1978, which capped property taxes and fundamentally restructured the state's finances. Colorado's Amendment 64 in 2012, which legalized recreational marijuana — the first state in the country to do so, in direct conflict with federal law that still classifies marijuana as a dangerous controlled substance. The federal government has mostly declined to prosecute recreational users in legal states, but the legal contradiction has never been resolved. Voters gave Colorado a right the federal government does not recognize.

The process is laborious by design. You write the text of the law you want. You get it reviewed by the state attorney general. You have a limited window — often 150 days — to collect signatures. Then you campaign for passage. Seems democratic. The friction is in who can actually run this process at scale. In large states, signature collection is expensive — typically $1 to $3 per valid signature, which means a California initiative might cost $1 to $3 million just to qualify for the ballot. You either have deep grassroots organization or you have money. Most major initiatives have one or the other, often money.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: ballot initiative process flowchart — stages: draft measure text → attorney general review → signature collection window (150 days) → signature verification → ballot placement → campaign → vote — annotated with cost estimates at each stage where money is required, and the signature threshold numbers for California — student should see that "citizen democracy" has significant resource requirements before a single vote is cast] -->

There are also comprehension problems that direct democracy creates that representative democracy does not. A legislature considering a bill has staff members who read it carefully, hearings where experts testify, debates where objections get raised and recorded. A voter faced with a ballot measure has the text, a summary written by a state official, and paid arguments for and against from whoever chose to file them. California voters in 2008 faced Proposition 8, which asked whether same-sex couples should have the right to marry. The measure *eliminated* that right — a yes vote was a vote to ban same-sex marriage. Some voters who wanted to support same-sex marriage voted yes because they thought that's what "yes" meant. The confusion was measurable in exit polls. Ballot measures are not self-explanatory, and the structure of the question shapes the answer in ways that do not necessarily reflect voter preferences accurately.

The deepest tension in direct democracy is this: it gives citizens power that bypasses representative institutions — but using that power requires the same resources (money, organization, information) that determine who has power in those institutions in the first place. It is democracy, but it is not democracy that automatically levels the playing field.

## The Machine as a Whole

Registration determines who is on the list. Turnout patterns determine who on that list actually shows up. Campaigns determine who gets reached and with what message. Primary structures determine which candidates survive to the general election. Direct democracy determines whether some questions bypass the legislature entirely.

Each layer seems separate. They are not. Strict registration rules reduce turnout most among young, mobile, and low-income voters — the same people campaigns are least likely to target because they are least likely to vote — which confirms the model and deepens the pattern. A voter who is never registered is never on a targeting list. A voter who is never targeted never hears from campaigns. A voter who never hears from campaigns is less likely to feel that elections are relevant to them.

The machinery does not produce this outcome because anyone planned it. It produces it because systems have internal logic, and the internal logic of this one is that each component selects for the same population: older, wealthier, more stable, more educated. The electorate that results is systematically different from the eligible population. And the policies that result from elections where that electorate decides are systematically shaped by who showed up.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: two overlapping population diagrams — left circle: voting-eligible population (demographic breakdown by age, income, education, race) — right circle: actual 2020 electorate (same breakdown) — the gap between the circles shows who is eligible but systematically underrepresented — student should see the electorate as a filtered subset of the population, not a representative sample of it] -->

Understanding elections means understanding this gap — between the population and the electorate — and understanding that the gap is not natural or inevitable. It is the product of rules. Rules can be written differently. Some states already have. The question is whether different rules would produce a more representative electorate, and whether a more representative electorate would produce different policy. The evidence suggests the answer to both is yes.

## What Would Change My Mind

The argument in this chapter assumes that registration barriers reduce turnout in ways that are demographically skewed — that strict rules affect young, poor, and minority voters more than older, wealthier, and White voters. Some research disputes this; some studies find no measurable effect of strict ID requirements on turnout or demographic composition. If the preponderance of evidence shifted — if we had consistent, well-controlled studies showing that registration rules had no differential demographic effects — the central argument about machinery selecting voters would need significant revision.

## Still Puzzling

The evidence on negative advertising is genuinely confusing. Campaigns run negative ads because they work — the candidate who runs them tends to win. But they appear to depress overall turnout, which should hurt both candidates. If negative ads lower turnout and turnout is lower for the demographics that support your opponent more than the demographics that support you, running negative ads is a rational strategy even if it makes the overall electorate smaller and less representative. I do not have a clean model of how campaigns calculate this trade-off, or whether they do consciously at all.
