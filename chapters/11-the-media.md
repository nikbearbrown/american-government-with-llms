# Chapter 11 — The Media: How Information Becomes Reality
*What Gets Covered, Who Decides, and Why It Matters*

---

Here is a fact worth sitting with.

In the spring of 2015, the Dominican Republic was preparing to deport roughly 250,000 Haitian nationals and undocumented residents. The situation had been developing for weeks. People's lives were being upended. And for most of that time, it was not news in the United States. ABC did not cover it. NBC did not cover it. The *New York Times* did not cover it.

Then Al Jazeera ran a piece framing it as a humanitarian crisis. Within days, the American networks followed. Petitions circulated. The U.S. government faced pressure to respond.

The situation on the ground had not changed between the day Al Jazeera published and the day ABC aired its first story. What changed was that it was now *news*. And once it was news, it was real — to millions of Americans who had no other way to know it was happening.

This is the machinery the chapter is about. Not bias, exactly. Not propaganda. Something more fundamental: the media's power to decide what counts as real.

---

## The Choosing

Walter Lippmann noticed this in the 1920s, which is one reason he is still worth reading. He observed that most citizens have no direct experience with government, foreign policy, or the wider world. What they know comes *through* media — which means it comes through choices. A camera points here, not there. A reporter asks this question, not that one. An editor runs this story today and holds the other one. These choices compound: the story that runs gets amplified, gets shared, gets discussed. The story that does not run disappears. Over time, citizens come to believe that what the media covered is what happened, and what the media ignored never existed.

Lippmann called this the construction of a "pseudo-environment." You are not responding to the world. You are responding to the picture of the world in your head, which is assembled from what you have been shown.

This is agenda-setting: the media's power to decide which issues deserve attention. It is not mind control. Citizens are not empty vessels. But it is structural. If you do not have sources inside government, if you do not read obscure policy journals, if you depend on mainstream outlets for your picture of politics — and almost everyone does — then you learn about issues when the media decides to tell you. The media do not tell you what to think. They tell you what to think about. And that is almost more powerful.

Once an issue reaches the media, a second choice reshapes how you see it.

A city is dealing with rising homelessness. A journalist decides to cover it. But how? Episodic framing focuses on the immediate case: a person sleeping in a doorway, the shelter's budget shortfall this year, the count from last month. You see the human suffering. You may feel sympathy. Thematic framing steps back: it traces the policy decisions over decades, compares what different cities have tried, connects individual situations to structural causes. You see systems. You may feel something closer to understanding.

Neither frame is false. Both are selections. And the selection changes what the reader concludes — including what they think should be done about it. A viewer who has seen twenty episodic stories about homelessness will tend to believe that homeless individuals made bad choices. A viewer who has seen twenty thematic stories will tend to believe that policies failed. Same facts, different interpretation, because the frame was different.

This is priming. Once you have been shown a frame repeatedly, you become predisposed to interpret new information through it. You do not experience it as a frame. You experience it as reality. That is the nature of it: it works by being invisible.

One more piece of this machinery deserves attention because it is easy to dismiss as coincidence and is not. Studies of local news coverage found that when stories about crime ran with images of perpetrators, they showed Black suspects at rates significantly higher than the actual crime statistics justified. Viewers who watched local news consistently believed, incorrectly, that most criminals were Black. The news had not lied. It had selected which images to show. The cumulative effect of those selections rewired what viewers thought was real.

## The Economics of Attention

You cannot understand how the media shapes reality without understanding how it makes money. Every major shift in what information does to politics has followed a shift in the economics of information.

**Print** built American political culture for roughly two centuries. Newspapers made money from subscriptions and advertisements. Printing and distribution were expensive. The capital requirements meant outlets were few. This made them *local* — a family's political views were shaped by the editor of the *Boston Gazette* or the *New York Sun*, whoever happened to publish in their town. Geographic location determined which paper you read, and which paper you read shaped what you believed.

**Broadcast** changed the economics entirely. A radio signal costs the same to transmit whether one person hears it or one million. Television works the same way. This meant national networks could exist. It meant millions of Americans could watch the same news at the same time. In the 1970s, if you watched the evening news, you watched Walter Cronkite. Three networks, two or three anchors, one broadcast at one time. The gatekeeping was narrow. But it was also unified. Americans, broadly, were reading from the same script.

Broadcast made money from advertising — companies paying for access to large audiences. This meant that what succeeded was what attracted the largest possible audience without driving any of them away. News was serious but not too challenging. Coverage was designed to offend nobody. Not because the journalists were cowards, but because the economics required it: you cannot sell beer ads against a newscast that alienates beer drinkers.

This created a strange side effect that we now miss: a baseline of shared political reality. You and your neighbor, regardless of party, had probably seen the same news. You disagreed about what it meant. But you had seen the same events. Argument was possible because you shared premises.

**Cable** destroyed that. Cable networks could target narrow audiences. You did not need fifty million viewers. You needed three million loyal subscribers who would pay for a worldview. The old constraint — offend nobody — vanished. FOX News launched in 1996 explicitly for right-leaning viewers. MSNBC tilted left. The economics now rewarded consistency and loyalty, not breadth.

Consider what this means concretely. Same State of the Union address. A viewer on FOX sees a president failing on the economy, soft on foreign threats, speaking to a liberal base. A viewer on MSNBC sees a president making reasonable arguments that Republicans are blocking out of obstruction. A viewer on CNN sees theater and conflict. These are not the same event. They are three different events, each constructed through the editorial choices of a network whose survival depends on keeping its particular audience satisfied.

Politicians learned this. Ronald Reagan's 1984 campaign figured out that if you give reporters exactly one quotable moment per day, in a controlled setting, that one moment becomes the entire story. You did not have to persuade journalists. You had to manage the frame. By 2016, Donald Trump had discovered the opposite approach: be so unpredictable, so provocative, so impossible to ignore that the media covers you constantly, on your terms, because your behavior generates the engagement that their economics demand. He was not captured by the media. He hacked the machinery.

**The internet** introduced a fourth economics: free publication with algorithmic distribution.

Before the internet, starting a news outlet required capital. Presses. Broadcast equipment. Distribution infrastructure. This meant outlets were few and owned by entities with resources to run them. The internet removed the entry cost. A newsletter costs nothing. A YouTube channel is free. Anyone can publish.

But free publication is not the same as free reach. Reach is controlled by algorithms. YouTube decides what video appears next. TikTok's algorithm determines which posts get shown to which users. Twitter's system decides what trends. These are not neutral pipes. They are editorial systems, optimizing for engagement — shares, comments, outrage, argument. Nuance does not generate engagement. Speed does. Certainty does. Provocation does. An accurate, carefully hedged explanation of a policy trade-off gets far fewer clicks than an outraged claim that one side is lying.

The result is not that gatekeeping ended. It is that the gates multiplied and became invisible. The old gatekeeping was at least visible: a few newspapers, a few networks, editors you could name. The new gatekeeping is opaque code, written by private companies, optimizing for metrics that have nothing to do with democratic information.

The collapse of local news is the least glamorous part of this shift and possibly the most consequential. One-fifth of small-town papers have closed in the last decade. Local newspapers covered city council meetings, school board decisions, contracts, corruption. No other medium did this with the same rigor. When the paper closes, that coverage does not move to TikTok. It disappears. Corruption that would have been exposed goes undetected. Citizens govern their communities with less information. This is a loss of democratic capacity, not just a business story.

## What the Government Can and Cannot Do

The First Amendment says Congress shall make no law abridging the freedom of speech or the press. It is nearly absolute in its language. It has never been completely absolute in practice.

Some categories of speech can be restricted: incitement to imminent violence, fraud, defamation. The question is always where the line falls. That line is not fixed. It has been argued, litigated, and moved continuously since 1791.

Defamation law — libel and slander — creates a permanent tension. A journalist cannot knowingly publish false information that harms someone's reputation. But when is a journalist *certain* something is true? The law resolves this with a distinction that is doing a lot of work. A *private figure* who is defamed needs only to show the media outlet was negligent — that it did not take reasonable care. A *public figure* — a politician, a celebrity, anyone who has voluntarily entered public life — must show the outlet acted with "actual malice": it either knew the statement was false or showed reckless disregard for whether it was true. This comes from *New York Times v. Sullivan* (1964), when a police commissioner sued over an inaccurate political advertisement. The Court decided that open debate about public figures matters so much that it needs extra protection.

The asymmetry is intentional. Politicians can be written about more roughly than private citizens. The law privileges political speech over individual reputation.

Classified information creates a different tension. The government can seek a court injunction to stop publication of secrets — this is called prior restraint. But the Supreme Court set a very high bar in the Pentagon Papers case (1971), when the government tried to stop the *New York Times* from publishing a classified study of the Vietnam War. The Court ruled that prior restraint is only justified when publication poses a grave and imminent threat, not merely when it is embarrassing or inconvenient. Publication proceeded.

This means the government can ask news outlets to withhold information. Journalists, historically, have voluntarily withheld information when they concluded publication would cost lives — during World War II, during certain intelligence operations. But the decision is theirs, not the government's. That negotiation — between what the government wants suppressed and what journalists decide the public needs to know — is permanent and ongoing.

Broadcast media face a different regulatory structure entirely, and the reason is worth understanding. Radio and television use the electromagnetic spectrum, which is a public resource. Because there are only so many frequencies, Congress decided in 1934 that the public — through government — could require broadcasters to serve the public interest as a condition of their license. The Federal Communications Commission enforces this. Licensed broadcasters must limit advertising, serve local communities, and give political candidates equal access: if you sell airtime to one candidate, you must offer the same terms to all others.

The crucial distinction is scarcity. Broadcast frequencies are scarce. Cable channels and websites are not. So cable and the internet are not regulated the same way. This has produced a strange result: broadcast television, which reaches fewer people than cable, is more heavily regulated than cable, which reaches more people and has more political influence. The legal logic made sense in 1934. Whether it makes sense now is an open question.

The Fairness Doctrine illustrates how regulation can produce unintended outcomes. From 1949 to 1987, the FCC required broadcasters to cover controversial issues with balance. If you gave airtime to one side, you had to give time to the other. This sounds like it promotes fairness. What it actually produced was avoidance: stations that did not want to spend airtime on the opposing view simply did not cover controversial topics. When the FCC repealed it in 1987, partisan cable news became possible. FOX News launched nine years later. The regulation had suppressed the partisan speech. Deregulation enabled it. Whether the result was better or worse for democracy is not a simple question — both options are costly.

The newest regulatory puzzle has no clear answer. Private platforms — Facebook, YouTube, TikTok, Twitter — now function as the primary public forums for political speech. They are not governments. The First Amendment does not apply to them. They can remove content, ban users, and suppress topics without any constitutional constraint. At the same time, their editorial decisions — what gets amplified, what gets suppressed — have more effect on American political reality than any newspaper editor in history. A private company, making editorial choices without democratic accountability, now controls the information environment of hundreds of millions of citizens. The legal framework built around broadcast scarcity and print freedom was not designed for this. We have not yet designed anything that is.

## What the Machinery Produces

Put these three mechanisms together — gatekeeping, framing, and regulation — and you get something that is neither conspiracy nor neutral information flow. You get an institution that shapes political reality as a structural byproduct of making editorial decisions.

An event happens. The media decide whether to cover it. If they cover it, they choose a frame. The frame is constrained by the economics of the outlet — what will attract and keep their particular audience. The outlet's ability to cover certain topics is constrained by regulation, libel exposure, and access. The citizen who consumes the coverage develops a picture of what happened.

That picture is constructed. It is not random. It is not deliberately false. But it is shaped by choices made under economic constraints, by people who are trying to do good work inside systems that reward certain kinds of stories over others.

This is not a reason for cynicism. It is a reason for precision. When you read a news story, the useful questions are not "is this true?" — usually, the facts are accurate — but "why this story today?" and "what frame is organizing these facts?" and "what would the story look like if a different outlet, with different economics and a different audience, had covered the same event?"

The media are not neutral conduits. They never were. They are institutions with economics, constraints, and gatekeeping power. Understanding that machinery does not mean you cannot trust information. It means you know how to read the machinery — what it is optimized for, where it tends to fail, and what it tends not to show you.

## What Would Change My Mind

Evidence that media coverage does not affect political outcomes — that citizens form their views entirely independently of framing and gatekeeping, that the agenda-setting effect is negligible — would require substantial revision here. The current evidence points the other way. But media effects research is genuinely contested, and the effects are probably smaller and more conditional than the strongest claims suggest.

## Still Puzzling

How much of media bias is intentional versus structural? If a news organization's economics reward outrage and an editor selects for outrage, is that ideology or incentive? The distinction matters because the remedies are different. Individual ethical commitments cannot fix a structural incentive. But structural incentives are not destiny either — outlets operating under the same economics sometimes make very different choices. I do not have a clean answer to this.

## LLM Exercises

**1. Agenda-setting in practice.**
Choose any news event from the past month that received significant media attention. Now identify something that was happening at the same time that received little or no coverage. What explains the difference? Consider: which story is more dramatic, more visual, more local, more connected to existing narratives the media were already covering? What does this comparison tell you about the criteria that shape gatekeeping decisions?

**2. Frame analysis.**
Find two news stories covering the same event — one from a left-leaning outlet and one from a right-leaning outlet. Identify the specific framing choices each makes: which facts are emphasized, which are omitted, what language is used to describe the actors, what context is provided, what the implied cause of the problem is. Then write a paragraph explaining what a citizen who read only one story would believe, and what they would believe if they read only the other.

**3. The economics of a news outlet.**
Pick one news outlet — the *New York Times*, FOX News, a local newspaper, or a major podcast. Research how it makes money. What does its revenue model reward: large audiences, loyal subscribers, advertising clicks, sponsorships? Now argue how those economic incentives shape what it covers and how it covers it. Use specific examples from the outlet's recent coverage to support your argument.

**4. Regulation and its limits.**
The FCC requires broadcast stations to offer equal time to political candidates. A television station sells thirty-second ads to a Republican candidate for Senate. Under the equal-time rule, what are its obligations to other candidates? Now: a social media platform runs paid political advertising for the same candidate. Is the platform subject to the same rule? Why or why not? What is the democratic argument for extending equal-time requirements to platforms? What is the argument against?

**5. The platform problem.**
A private social media company removes a post by a sitting U.S. senator, citing its rules against health misinformation. The senator argues this is a violation of his First Amendment rights. Is it? Explain carefully, using the distinction between government action and private action that underlies First Amendment doctrine. Then argue whether the First Amendment *should* be extended to cover large private platforms, and what the practical consequences of that extension would be.
