# THREE TITLE OPTIONS

- Coalitions and Voting Rules: How American Parties Stay at Two
- Why Parties Exist and Refuse to Multiply
- The Party Machine: From Winning Elections to Governing Together

---

## TL;DR

Parties solve a collective action problem — turning diffuse voter preferences into actual government power. The U.S. two-party system persists not because it's inevitable, but because simple voting rules (one seat per district, highest vote wins) make it mathematically lethal for third parties to compete. As the parties have grown more polarized over the past thirty years, they have become more distinct, not more cooperative — which some celebrate as clarity and others fear as gridlock.

---

## Chapter Opening: The Restaurant Problem

Imagine you're at a gathering of twelve friends trying to choose where to eat. Three want Thai. Four want Mexican. Five have no preference but will go along with whatever wins. Under a show of hands, the five could go to any restaurant. Under a secret ballot where everyone gets one vote and the highest count wins, the Thai group and the Mexican group each get exactly their supporters, leaving the five uncommitted. No outcome satisfies more than four people.

Now scale that moment to three hundred million Americans voting on how to spend trillions of dollars, what laws to enforce, which judges should sit, who goes to war. The same problem blooms. Millions of distinct preferences cannot simply coexist. *Someone has to choose.* And once someone starts choosing, others realize they need to organize in response.

That is what political parties do. They take the restaurant problem and solve it through aggregation: gathering loose coalitions of voters and interests into large enough blocs that the bloc's choices become government policy. They do this by fielding candidates, raising money, mobilizing voters, and enforcing internal discipline once they hold office. The machinery is elegant. The mathematics is brutal. And once you see how it works, nearly everything about American politics — why we have two parties instead of seven, why they seem to get more hostile instead of friendlier, why the party leadership can punish a defector in Congress — becomes transparent.

This chapter walks through three mechanisms: how parties aggregate preferences and coordinate voters (and why other groups don't do the same); why the electoral system itself locks in two parties; and how the internal structure of parties changes as they transition from campaign machines into governing bodies. Each mechanism has a real cost. Each makes certain kinds of politics possible and others nearly impossible.

## CONCEPT 1: What Parties Do (and What Distinguishes Them)

### The Cold Open

January 2025. The House of Representatives needs a speaker. No single candidate has the votes. The Republican conference fragments into factions — the Freedom Caucus, the moderates, the Trump loyalists — each one willing to withhold support unless concessions are made. For weeks, the chamber cannot function. Bills cannot be moved. The presiding officer is empty. The mechanism that turns loose majorities into law is jammed.

Outside the chamber, interest groups work the same halls. The gun lobby wants a certain outcome. The environmental groups want another. They testify, they lobby, they spend money on advertisements. But they do not control who sits in that chair. They cannot nominate a candidate and enforce party discipline on their members. They cannot credibly promise that if you vote with them today, they will fund your reelection next cycle.

The difference is not enthusiasm or legitimacy. It is *binding coordination*. Interest groups influence. Parties govern. And governing requires that large numbers of people agree to move together, even when they disagree privately.

### The Mechanism: Aggregation and Binding Coordination

A political party in the United States is a binding coalition that performs three functions. 

**First, it aggregates.** Millions of Americans hold distinct preferences about the role of government. Some want low taxes and minimal regulation. Others want expansive public services. Some prioritize military strength; others prioritize environmental protection. These preferences do not align neatly. A voter might want gun rights and climate action simultaneously — a pairing that neither party offers completely. A party steps in and says: here is a bundle of positions. We are offering this slate of candidates, this vision, this tradeoff. If you vote for us, these are the policies you get. Not all of them. A bundle.

**Second, it nominates and runs.** The party identifies candidates, provides training, supplies money, organizes campaign infrastructure, and coordinates messaging. A individual politician running alone against a party-backed incumbent has the resources of a single person. A party candidate gets the machinery of the organization. This advantage is enormous. It means that even a mediocre Republican in a Republican district has a real chance of winning reelection. The party has already framed the battleground, allocated resources, and defined the opposition. Third-party candidates, by contrast, start from nothing. They have no access to donor networks, no voter lists, no advertising budget.

**Third, it enforces.** Once candidates win office, the party leadership has leverage to keep them in line. Committee assignments go to loyal members. Leadership positions and campaign money flow to those who vote the party line. A member of Congress who defects faces primary challenges from within their own party — the party apparatus funds a challenger who will take their seat. This enforcement mechanism is crude but effective. It is why a president of one party often cannot poach more than a handful of votes from the other party's House members, even when the issue cuts across party lines. The party has already made clear: vote with us or lose your job next election.

Interest groups cannot do any of this. They can influence what parties do. They cannot bind thousands of people into a unified electoral machine. They cannot enforce voting discipline. They cannot make binding deals that stretch across time and geography. This is why parties, not interest groups, are the primary vehicles for turning preferences into power.

### Trade-off: Breadth Versus Coherence

The aggregation function creates an immediate tension. A party that wants to win needs as many votes as possible. So it tries to take broad positions that appeal to voters across the ideological spectrum. The downside: the party's own members start to disagree with each other. The Republican Party includes both libertarian tech entrepreneurs who want minimal government and evangelical Christians who want government to enforce traditional values. The Democratic Party includes both progressive activists who want to defund police and centrist prosecutors who want to police more effectively.

When a party holds both factions, it is broad and wins elections. When it narrows to one faction, it loses elections but gains coherence. Every party leadership faces this choice: sacrifice breadth for internal consistency, or sacrifice consistency for breadth. Until about 1990, American parties chose breadth. A moderate Republican from the Northeast could coexist with a conservative Republican from the South. Both wanted lower taxes and a strong military, even if they disagreed on civil rights. A liberal Northern Democrat could coexist with a segregationist Southern Democrat — both defended the New Deal, even though they fought over race.

This trade-off collapses the moment voters begin to sort by ideology. If liberal and conservative are no longer scattered randomly but instead cluster geographically and demographically, then a party that tries to hold both ends finds its members constantly at war internally. The party leadership then faces an ugly choice: **hold the coalition and lose legislative power through internal deadlock, or let the coalition fracture and gain coherence but lose votes.** By the 1990s, both parties chose to let the coalition fracture. The result: parties became more ideologically distinct and less able to compromise with each other. Voters got clearer choices. Governing became harder.

### Case Study: The Freedom Caucus and the Speakership

In January 2023, Kevin McCarthy won the speakership after a four-day floor fight, only by agreeing to a historic list of concessions to the Freedom Caucus — a group of roughly thirty far-right Republicans who threatened to withhold support. They secured the removal of members from committees, the power to call a motion to vacate him, and procedural changes that weakened leadership's ability to move legislation. McCarthy accepted these terms because he had no choice. His party had such a narrow margin that even thirty defectors could block the speakership. He could not aggregate a majority without them. But those concessions made him unable to govern. He could not move the appropriations bills or major legislation without their consent. By September 2023, they triggered the motion to vacate, and McCarthy was ousted. He was the first speaker in U.S. history to lose the gavel this way.

The mechanics are simple: once a party loses the ability to enforce unity internally — because a faction is large enough that dropping them costs you the majority — that faction can demand concessions that prevent the party from governing effectively. The party chose coherence over breadth and lost the ability to aggregate enough votes to do the actual work. This is what happens when the trade-off between breadth and coherence breaks down entirely.

### Common Misconception: Parties Are Obstacles to Democracy

One persistent critique runs like this: parties are obstacles to democracy because they block pure majoritarian will. If 60% of Americans want policy X, why doesn't the government do policy X? Why does the party system allow for delay, compromise, faction, gridlock?

The reason is that 60% of Americans never want the same policy X, especially on issues that matter for elections. When you ask people in isolation, you get broad support for nice-sounding goals: "Do you want clean air?" "Do you support helping children?" When you ask people to choose between clean air and lower electricity bills, the answer fragments. Aggregation always involves tradeoff. The party does not block the will of the people. It *constitutes* the people as a political actor capable of generating a will in the first place. Without aggregation, you have thirty-seven different policy preferences scattered across three hundred million voters. You have a restaurant with no mechanism for choosing where to eat.

## CONCEPT 2: The Electoral System and the Two-Party Trap

### The Cold Open

In March 1854, the Whig Party dissolved. It had won the presidency twice in twenty years, held Senate majorities, shaped policy. Within a decade it was gone — not through a clean transition, but through a collapse so sudden that historians still debate whether it was inevitable. The Republican Party rose to replace it. A century later, in the 1950s, political scientists spoke confidently about the stability of the two-party system. It seemed hard-wired into American democracy. And it was. But not by constitutional design — by electoral arithmetic.

Third-party candidates run regularly in the United States. Green Party, Libertarian Party, various insurgent movements. Most get 1-3% of the vote. A few crack 5%. Yet in 2024, with a deeply unpopular incumbent, a fracturing opposition, and a third-party candidate drawing attention across the political spectrum, the third party could not dislodge either major party from the two seats that mattered: the presidency and Senate control. The mathematics of the system held.

### The Mechanism: Plurality Voting in Single-Member Districts

The U.S. voting system is simple: **the candidate with the most votes wins, regardless of whether they hit 50%**. This is called plurality voting or first-past-the-post. It is different from majority voting (requiring 50% + 1 vote) and radically different from proportional representation (where each party wins seats in proportion to its share of votes nationally).

Under plurality voting in single-member districts, this logic unfolds:

1. Voter A prefers a Green Party candidate who shares their environmental values. But the Green candidate will get 5% of the vote in a three-way race. The Republican and Democratic candidates will each get 47% and 48%. Voter A knows this math. If they vote Green out of principle, they are splitting the vote that could have gone to the Democrat, helping the Republican win. This is called a "wasted vote." Knowing this, Voter A votes Democrat despite preferring the Green.

2. The Green candidate loses — again. The next election cycle, fewer people even volunteer to run Green. Donors ask why they should fund a candidacy that cannot win. Local Green chapters shrink from lack of oxygen. The third party becomes self-evidently not viable.

3. Meanwhile, each of the two major parties has perverse incentives to move toward the median voter, but also to keep their own faction mobilized. The party that wins is the one that can hold its base AND pick off swing voters. Over time, as we saw, the two parties begin to sort ideologically, each claiming a different half of the political spectrum. Third parties cannot emerge because voters refuse to waste their votes on them — and voters refuse because third parties cannot win.

This is not a bug in the system. It is a feature. Political scientists call this Duverger's Law: **plurality voting in single-member districts mechanically produces a two-party system**. The law is not ironclad (multi-party systems can exist under plurality if regional preferences are sharply divided), but it holds for most democracies that use first-past-the-post.

Why not just use proportional representation? Germany, the Netherlands, Israel, and many other democracies do. Under proportional representation, if a party wins 10% of votes nationally, it wins 10% of seats. A Green Party that polls at 5% gets 5% of the seats, not zero. This changes the incentive structure completely. Suddenly third parties become viable. Voters do not need to worry about wasting their vote. The tradeoff is that the legislature becomes fragmented — you need coalitions to govern, and those coalitions are often unstable.

The U.S. chose differently: single-member districts with plurality voting. The Founders did not explicitly choose this. It emerged from early practice and the structure of state legislatures. But once it was in place, it locked in. Changing the system would require constitutional amendment (for House and Senate) or a massive effort to coordinate state legislatures (for local offices). Neither will happen. So the two-party system persists, not because Americans prefer it, but because the electoral rules make any alternative non-viable.

### Trade-off: Stability Versus Representation

Plurality voting and single-member districts create stable, durable parties. The same two parties have dominated American politics for 170 years (the current versions since 1932). This is an advantage: voters know what they are voting for. Parties can invest in long-term infrastructure, recruit and train candidates, build organization. Governments can be formed quickly — the plurality winner has the power. There is clarity.

The cost is representation. Millions of voters hold preferences that do not map neatly onto either party. A voter who is socially conservative but economically progressive has no natural home. A voter who wants open immigration and strong gun rights has to choose which value to compromise. Proportional representation would let these voters find a party that represents them precisely. But it fragments the legislature. A proportional U.S. Congress would likely have six or seven parties instead of two, and coalition-building would be constant and fragile. Governments might fall repeatedly over budget disputes.

Each system makes different things possible. Plurality voting stabilizes power and enables decisive government. Proportional representation distributes power widely and respects minority preferences. The U.S. chose stability. The cost is precision.

### Case Study: The Rise and Fall of the Whig Party

The Whigs were a major party for nearly two decades — from roughly 1834 to 1854. They won multiple presidential elections, controlled Congress, shaped national policy on the Bank of the United States, public works, and tariffs. Yet they dissolved rapidly when the slavery question fractured the party beyond repair.

The Whig collapse looks mystifying in a multi-party system. In Germany or the Netherlands, a party fracture like this would split into two or three new parties, which would then form a coalition government. The system would absorb the fracture. In the U.S., the fracture instead triggered a reformation: the anti-slavery Whigs merged with the Free Soil Party (which opposed slavery in the territories), the radical Republicans, and anti-slavery Democrats to form the Republican Party. The pro-slavery Whigs either left politics or joined the Democratic Party.

The Republican Party that emerged was explicitly a single-issue coalition: opposition to slavery's expansion. This was ideologically tight enough to hold, at least long enough to win the 1860 election. Once in office, the Republican Party broadened to include economic interests (industrialists), geographic interests (the North, the Midwest), and other factions. It became a durable party.

The key point: the plurality-voting system does not merely discourage third parties. It actively forces coalitions to consolidate. When the Whigs could not hold their coalition because the slavery issue was too divisive, they did not splinter into three new parties that could later coalesce through negotiation. Instead, their factions had to find new homes in existing parties or form a new single coalition that could win a plurality. The system forced rapid consolidation.

### Common Misconception: We Could Easily Fix This with Ranked-Choice Voting

Some advocates push ranked-choice voting (RCV) as a solution to plurality voting's flaws. Under RCV, voters rank candidates in order of preference. If no candidate wins 50% on the first round, the last-place candidate is eliminated, and their votes are redistributed according to voters' second preferences. This repeats until someone hits 50%. RCV does reduce the "wasted vote" problem and can help third parties gain traction.

But RCV does not change the underlying structural incentive. If you are a left-leaning voter in a safe Republican district, RCV lets you vote Green first and Democrat second without fear of wasting your vote. But the Green candidate still loses. The Green Party still cannot build infrastructure. The two major parties still have overwhelming advantages in fundraising and name recognition. RCV is not nothing — Maine and Alaska have adopted it — but it does not overturn Duverger's Law. For that, you would need proportional representation or some other system that awards seats based on vote share, not geographic wins.

The deeper point: any voting system is a set of incentives embedded in a political culture. Change the system without changing the culture, and the culture often reverts the system's effects. Change the culture without changing the system, and the system's logic will grind away until it reasserts itself.

## CONCEPT 3: Party Structure and the Transition from Campaign to Governing

### The Cold Open

October 2024. A Senate Republican who has publicly criticized his party's presidential nominee meets privately with staff from a Democratic challenger. The Democrat's campaign manager shows him internal polling suggesting their candidate could win the state. The Republican offers nothing explicit. But the message is understood: if the Democrat wins this race, the Republican will have real power. The Republican Senate that emerges might not give the new Democratic president enough votes to pass legislation. The Republican might become indispensable for any coalition. As opposed to serving under a president his party nominated, whom he despises.

This is not corruption or disloyalty in the conventional sense. It is a rational response to the structure of the modern party: by the time a candidate is nominated and running for office, the party organization has largely done its work. The party machinery got the candidate nominated, trained them, allocated resources, and now runs the campaign. But once the candidate is in office and the campaign ends, the relationship inverts. The officeholder becomes the party. And the officeholder's personal political survival may be more urgent than the party's platform.

### The Mechanism: The Three Components of a Party

Political scientist V.O. Key identified three components of any political party: the party-in-the-electorate (voters), the party organization (staff and operatives), and the party-in-government (elected officials). Each operates on a different logic and timeline, and they often conflict.

**The party-in-the-electorate** is the set of voters who identify with the party and prefer its candidates. In the U.S., the party does not require formal membership. You become part of it by considering yourself a Republican or Democrat, and most voters who do so are casual about the affiliation. You get no membership card. You have no say in party decisions. You can hold positions the party platform opposes and still identify with the party. This has made American parties "loose" compared to parties in many other democracies, which require membership dues and active participation. The looseness is an advantage for parties trying to aggregate as many votes as possible, and a disadvantage for maintaining ideological coherence.

**The party organization** is the formal structure: the precinct captains, the county party chairs, the state party offices, the Democratic National Committee or Republican National Committee. This is where the real staff work happens — organizing voters, raising money, training candidates, coordinating campaigns. The organization is deeply hierarchical. Power flows from the national committee down to the states and precincts, though in practice the reverse also happens. State parties sometimes resist national party direction, especially on controversial issues. But the organization is the machine that turns money and labor into electoral outcomes.

**The party-in-government** is the elected officials who hold office under the party label. They are responsible for implementing the party platform once in power. But they also have personal incentives: reelection, power within the legislature, influence over specific policies that matter to their district. These incentives sometimes align with the party platform and sometimes conflict sharply. A senator from a swing state might refuse to vote for legislation the party leadership demands, because the vote would tank their reelection chances in their state.

These three components make decisions on different timescales. The party-in-the-electorate thinks in presidential cycles — you vote every four years. The party organization thinks in election cycles — 2024, then 2026, then 2028. But the party-in-government thinks in legislative terms — next week's vote, next committee assignment, next reelection fight. And the incentives can misalign dramatically.

### Trade-off: Campaign Discipline Versus Governing Flexibility

When a party is campaigning, discipline is essential. If twenty Democrats in purple districts start saying "I won't support the party's healthcare plan because my constituents hate it," the campaign unravels. The electorate gets a confused message. The party loses the coherence that drew people to it in the first place. So the party organization imposes discipline: conform to the platform, support the nominee, stay on message. Violators face primary challenges and loss of organizational support.

But once the party wins and moves into governing, discipline becomes a problem. The party now holds power, and power is distributed across multiple offices and chambers. A president cannot order a senator to vote a certain way — the senator is independently elected and has their own base. If the president is from one party and the Senate has a slim majority, every senator of that party becomes pivotal. They can demand concessions. They can block the president. If the senator's state is trending away from the president's party, the senator's personal reelection chances may depend on distancing from the president's agenda.

This dynamic played out throughout 2021-2024. The Democratic Party held the presidency and Congress, but with a narrow majority in the Senate. Two Democratic senators — one from a conservative state (West Virginia) and one from a swing state (Arizona) — refused to support major Biden initiatives, most notably voting rights legislation and a sustained push on climate. The party organization could not threaten them effectively, because primary challenges to sitting senators are rare and dangerous (they can split the vote and help the Republican win). So the party had to negotiate with them, make concessions, and in some cases let the legislation die. The trade-off: while campaigning, you want rigid discipline. While governing, you need flexibility, because power is dispersed and no single authority can enforce compliance.

### Case Study: The 2024 Harris Campaign and Coalition Fracture

In the 2024 presidential campaign, the Democratic party-in-government (sitting officials, donors, and institutional figures) moved quickly to unify behind Kamala Harris after Joe Biden's withdrawal. The party organization mobilized resources. The party-in-the-electorate, however, split. Some voters wanted Harris to run further left, on issues from Gaza to healthcare. Others wanted her to move right, to court college-educated suburban voters and moderate Republicans. Harris tried to hold a broad coalition, moved toward the center, and in doing so lost some enthusiasm on the left, especially on foreign policy.

This fractured the trade-off. During the campaign, the party had tried to impose discipline — stay on message, support the nominee. But voters who identify as Democratic but disagree with Harris on Gaza or economic policy refused to be disciplined. The party organization tried to message its way out of the fracture. It did not work, because the fracture was not a messaging problem. It was a genuine disagreement about what the party should stand for.

The deeper lesson: as the party-in-the-electorate becomes more ideologically sorted (liberals in one party, conservatives in the other), the easier it becomes for voters to choose not to show up or to vote third party when the party does not align with their values. Loose party identification — the traditional American strength — becomes a weakness. The party has no binding mechanism to keep voters in line.

### Common Misconception: Party Leaders Have Total Control

Many voters and even some journalists describe party leadership as if they have dictatorial control: "The Democrats decided to..." or "The Republicans are unified behind..." In fact, party leadership has power only insofar as it can credibly distribute rewards and punishments. A president can do this for some legislators by offering earmarks, committee positions, or campaign help. Party organizations can primary a defector. But these tools weaken the more decentralized power becomes.

Consider the House Speaker fight of 2023. The Speaker has significant power within the House: committee assignments, legislative scheduling, campaign resources. Yet Kevin McCarthy lost the speakership because thirty Republicans could withhold support and he had no way to make up the votes elsewhere. The party organization could not magically produce twenty Democratic votes for McCarthy — Democrats would never support a Republican speaker. So McCarthy had to negotiate with the thirty rebels, and the negotiations bent the House's ability to function. Power was not centralized. It was distributed among factions, and the only way to govern was through continuous internal negotiation.

This is structural, not a failure of leadership. In a system where any faction of a party can hold the majority hostage by threatening to withhold votes, power flows from the center to the factions. The only solution is either (a) build a coalition large enough that defectors do not matter, or (b) accept that you will govern in coalition with the other party. The U.S. has been moving toward (a) and away from (b) for three decades.

## Integration: From Coalitions to Governing

The three mechanisms work together to shape American politics into a specific form.

First, parties solve the aggregation problem by binding millions of voters and donors into a coalition large enough to field candidates and govern. This is necessary work. Without it, government could not form. But it creates an inherent tension: the broader the coalition, the less coherent its ideology. The more coherent the ideology, the fewer votes it can win.

Second, the electoral system locks in two parties by making any third-party vote mathematically likely to be wasted. This is not a flaw that needs fixing. It is a design feature that produces stable governments, at the cost of excluding views that do not map neatly onto the two-party spectrum.

Third, the party's three components — voters, organization, officeholders — operate on different timescales and sometimes conflicting logics. During campaigns, discipline is enforced and the party presents a unified front. During governing, that discipline breaks down because the officeholders are independently elected and can survive without party support in many cases. Power becomes distributed. Gridlock becomes possible.

What has changed since mid-century is the *sorting* of the party-in-the-electorate by ideology. For decades, the Democratic Party held liberal Northerners and conservative Southerners; the Republican Party held both progressive Midwesterners and conservative traditionalists. Cross-party voting was common. Regional interests cut across ideological lines. By the 1990s, this had sorted itself out. Liberals moved into the Democratic Party. Conservatives moved into the Republican Party. The geographic distribution changed: conservatives clustered in the South, the Midwest, and the Mountain West; liberals clustered in coastal cities and the Northeast.

This sorting has two effects. First, it clarifies the choice for voters. The parties now offer genuinely different visions. Second, it reduces the party organization's ability to maintain discipline among the party-in-government, because politicians from safe districts do not depend on party resources and can defect without losing reelection. A Republican from a deep-red district faces a primary challenge from the right if they break ranks with Trump. A Democrat from a deep-blue district faces a primary challenge from the left if they align with corporate donors. The threat that matters to them is internal to the party, not from the other party. This pushes both parties further toward their ideological edges.

The result is a paradox: the two parties have become clearer in their positions, more ideologically consistent, more distinct from each other. By the standard the 1950 APSA commission wanted, the parties are now healthier — they offer clear choices. But the cost is that internal cooperation has become rarer. Compromise within a party requires facing a primary challenge. Compromise across parties means losing to your own primary. The incentive structure now rewards ideological purity more than electoral victory, at least for politicians from safe districts.

Whether this is democracy thriving (clear choices, distinct parties, competitive elections) or democracy failing (paralysis, inability to compromise, polarization) depends on which outcome you think matters more.

## Graduated Exercises

**Warm-up: Aggregation and the Restaurant Problem**

Think of a decision-making body you belong to — a club, a family, a sports team, a class project. Describe a recent disagreement where the group needed to reach a single decision. Who aggregated the preferences? Did they succeed in keeping the coalition together? If one faction had refused to accept the outcome, how would that have changed the dynamics?

**Application: Duverger's Law in Practice**

Look up the results of the 2016 and 2020 presidential elections. Find the vote totals for the Green Party, Libertarian Party, and any other candidates outside the major two parties. Calculate what would have happened if proportional representation were in effect: if those votes translated into House seats. How many Green or Libertarian seats would exist? How would this change the composition of Congress?

Then consider: would you want to live in that Congress? Would legislation be easier or harder to pass? Would the coalition negotiations be more or less transparent?

**Synthesis: Party-in-the-Electorate Versus Party-in-Government**

Identify a recent vote by a senator or representative that went against their party majority. Interview the reasoning (find statements, press releases, interviews). Which logic was driving the vote: party-in-the-electorate logic (their constituents demanded it), or party-in-government logic (they needed leverage, a committee seat, campaign money)? How do you tell the difference? What evidence would you need to be confident?

**Challenge: The Sorting Effect**

Geography has changed party affiliation dramatically. In 1960, Democrats won Senate seats in Texas, Oklahoma, Georgia, and Arkansas. Republicans won Senate seats in Massachusetts, Connecticut, and Vermont. By 2024, this had nearly reversed. Using county-level election data (available through MIT Election Lab or similar sources), trace one state or region through the sorting. At what point did the parties switch their geographic strongholds? What issues were driving it? Was it immigration, economics, race, religion, something else?

## Chapter Summary

Parties solve a fundamental problem in mass politics: turning millions of individual preferences into a government capable of acting. They do this through aggregation (bundling preferences into platforms), binding (forcing politicians to support the platform or face discipline), and coordination (moving resources to where they matter most electorally). This machinery is necessary. Without it, government could not form.

But the machinery creates two structural constraints. The first is electoral: plurality voting in single-member districts makes third parties non-viable. This locks in a two-party system, which has persisted for over 170 years. The second is organizational: as the party-in-the-electorate has sorted by ideology, the party-in-government has lost the ability to enforce discipline. Politicians from safe districts can afford to defect. This has made each party more ideologically pure and also more prone to internal conflict.

The result is the paradox of modern American politics: the parties have become more distinct (which some call health) and less able to compromise (which others call crisis). Whether this represents democracy functioning as intended depends on whether you prioritize clear electoral choices or effective governance. The system provides the first at the cost of the second.

## Connections Forward

Understanding party structure illuminates everything downstream: how voting rights, ballot access, campaign finance, and primary elections shape who gets nominated and why. It explains the apparent contradiction between "the people want bipartisanship" (true, polling shows majority preference for compromise) and "the parties cannot compromise" (true, the incentive structure punishes it). It shows why campaign promises are often broken once in office — the party-in-government operates under different constraints than the party that campaigned. And it makes clear that fixing any of these problems requires changing not just the rules, but the incentive structure embedded in those rules.

Or evidence that ranked-choice voting, once adopted, genuinely reduces sorting and increases cross-party cooperation (suggesting that voting system design can overcome incentive structures).
