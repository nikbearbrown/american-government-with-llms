# Interest Groups and Lobbying

## Suggested Titles

1. The Puzzle of Organized Interests: Why People Band Together When It Doesn't Pay
2. Who Gets Heard? How 50,000 Groups Compete for Power in American Government
3. The Machinery of Access: Inside Lobbying, Outside Pressure, and the Question of Whose Voice Counts

---

## TL;DR

Interest groups form when people overcome a basic disincentive: your individual action rarely decides the outcome. Lobbyists operate through two channels—direct access to lawmakers (inside lobbying) and mobilizing the public to pressure them (outside lobbying)—yet representation tilts sharply toward those with money. Citizens United opened the floodgates for corporate and union spending; the result is a system where some interests command the room and others are barely heard.

---

## 1. Chapter Opening

It is 2009, and a young mother from Ohio is calling her representative in Congress about the Affordable Care Act. She has three minutes to say why the law matters to her family. Down the hallway, in an office decorated with photographs of hunting trips and legislative victories, a lobbyist from the insurance industry is walking toward the same office. He has spent the last six months with this representative—lunches, briefings, draft language. He knows the congressman's chief of staff by first name. He knows which committee members hold the votes that matter.

Both are trying to influence the same law. Neither expects to change the other's mind, but their chances of being heard—truly heard—are not equal.

This is the paradox at the heart of American interest group politics. We say government is of the people, by the people, and for the people. And it is true that ordinary people form groups, show up, make calls, and push back when they care about something. But the machinery that moves government is not neutral. It advantages some and disadvantages others. It speaks in the language of money and access. People with time but no money show up in the streets. People with money show up in offices. Government tends to listen to whoever is in the room.

This chapter asks: How do interest groups form in the first place when most people have little reason to join? How do they actually move government—through which channels, using which methods? And who gets represented? The answer to the third question is not "everyone equally," and understanding why requires understanding the machinery.

**Learning Objectives**

By the end of this chapter, you will understand:

- Why collective action problems exist and how groups overcome them (Mancur Olson's insight)
- How inside lobbying (direct access, information, drafting) differs from outside lobbying (public pressure, media, mobilization)
- Why representation is systematically biased toward wealthy interests and what Citizens United changed
- The specific mechanisms through which money translates into political influence
- How to recognize when an interest group's victory says more about its resources than about what citizens want

**Prerequisites**

You should be familiar with the three branches of government (legislative, executive, judicial), the committee system in Congress, and basic campaign finance concepts.

## 2. The Problem: Why Groups Form When People Have No Reason to Join

A construction worker benefits when the carpenters' union negotiates a wage increase. Everyone who works in construction gets higher pay. Yet the worker knows that one more person joining the union—or staying in it—will not determine whether that wage increase happens. The negotiating power comes from the collective. His individual contribution is noise. So he has a rational incentive to free ride: receive the benefit without paying dues, attending meetings, or doing the work.

This is **Mancur Olson's central insight** from his 1965 book *The Logic of Collective Action*. People have a disincentive to participate in groups that seek collective goods—goods that benefit everyone in a group regardless of whether they helped pay for them. Clean air helps you whether you joined the Sierra Club or not. Gun rights exist for all gun owners whether you paid membership dues or showed up to the rally. This creates what economists call the **free rider problem**: why pay if you can get the benefit free?

And yet: the United States has somewhere between 50,000 and 200,000 registered interest groups. Millions of people belong to them. They show up. They volunteer. They pay dues even when they could defect without losing the benefit. Why?

Olson identified several mechanisms that overcome the free rider problem, and they are worth understanding because they explain which groups exist, which ones survive, and which voices get amplified in American politics.

**Material incentives** work straightforwardly. AARP (the American Association of Retired Persons) offers hotel discounts, insurance rates, prescription drug savings. The membership dues are low. A person joining to save money on hotels might also be exposed to the organization's political campaigns. That person was not primarily seeking a collective good; they wanted a private good—a discount—and the group happens to also pursue political influence.

**Solidary incentives** appeal to the human need to belong. You join because you want to be with people like you, who share your concern. The NAACP puts you in a room with others who have dealt with racism, who understand the issue in their bones. The American Civil Liberties Union puts together people committed to civil liberties—even people who dislike each other's conclusions, like free-speech advocates who defend Nazi speech in the name of principle. The solidarity is not about being comfortable; it is about being understood.

**Purposive incentives** cut deepest. You join because you care about the cause. Not because you get a discount. Not because you want friends. Because the issue matters. An environmentalist joins the Audubon Society because the organization fights for migratory bird habitat. That person might be the only Audubon member in their county. They will never meet another member. But they believe the work needs doing, and they are willing to pay into it.

These three mechanisms—material, solidary, purposive—are what Olson called the carrots. But there is also a stick: **mandatory membership**. To practice law, you may be required to join the bar association. In some professions and some places, unions could once demand membership as a condition of employment. Mandatory membership solves the free rider problem by eliminating the choice.

But something else also drives group formation, and it does not appear in Olson's original framework: **disturbance**. When something happens—a crisis, an outrage, a threat—people mobilize. Rachel Carson published *Silent Spring* in 1962, documenting the dangers of pesticides. People read it. They were disturbed. Environmental groups proliferated. When police killed Michael Brown in Ferguson, Missouri, and then were not indicted, protests erupted. The Black Lives Matter movement grew. When Donald Trump ran for president and made immigration central to his campaign, both anti-immigration and pro-immigration groups mobilized in response. External shock creates a opening for group formation that money and incentives alone cannot explain.

**Key Trade-off**: Groups that are small and well-organized are better able to overcome the free rider problem than large, diffuse groups. A carpenters' union of 50,000 people can more easily enforce membership and discipline defectors than a movement representing "all Americans who care about the environment." Small groups create social pressure; large groups allow anonymity. Yet small groups also have less political power per capita. The deep tension in American interest group politics is this: the groups most effective at overcoming collective action are the smallest and most specialized, while the groups most able to represent broad constituencies struggle to mobilize members who benefit without contributing.

**Case Study: The AARP Difference**

AARP has nearly 38 million members. It is one of the largest organizations in the country. How did it overcome the free rider problem at such scale?

The answer is material incentives layered with purposive ones. AARP started as a way for a retired teacher to sell group health insurance to people over 50. It was purely transactional. But as it grew, AARP became an advocate for Social Security, Medicare, prescription drug coverage, and policies affecting older Americans. Members joined for the insurance discounts and stayed for the political power. Today, members are often unaware of AARP's legislative agenda. They joined for the card in their wallet. But AARP lobbies Congress aggressively on issues ranging from healthcare to long-term care. The organization leverages its member base—millions of voters—to amplify its political voice.

This is the business model that makes sense at scale: give people a reason to join that is not about politics (a discount, a magazine subscription, a community event), and then use the resulting organization as a political force. It is not deceptive. Members know AARP advocates for their interests. But the political power comes partly from the fact that the organization exists for reasons beyond politics.

**Common Misconceptions**

*Misconception 1: Larger groups are always more powerful than smaller ones.*

Wrong. A large, diffuse group with weak internal organization is often less influential than a small, tightly coordinated one. The NRA has about 6 million members. Gun owners in the United States number roughly 40 million. Yet the NRA punches above its weight because membership means real engagement: members receive ratings of politicians, are trained on advocacy, and are mobilized on specific votes. Compare this to a hypothetical "Americans Who Like Nice Parks" organization with 30 million members who never interact and have no idea what the organization lobbies for. The smaller, activated group wins.

*Misconception 2: Interest groups exist mainly to elect candidates.*

They do not. Electoral support matters, but it is secondary. The primary work of interest groups is policy influence—getting legislative language drafted, getting regulations written a certain way, getting budgets allocated toward favored programs. Elections matter to interest groups mainly because of what elected officials can do once in office, not because of the electoral competition itself.

*Misconception 3: The free rider problem affects all groups equally.*

It does not. Wealthy interests—corporations, trade associations, professional societies—can hire staff to lobby and can fund the group through dues they can easily afford. They face a free rider problem, but it is manageable. Poor people, even if they want to form a group, face a harder problem: they cannot afford to pay someone to lobby on their behalf. The problem of collective action is not evenly distributed. It is worse for the poor.

## 3. The Machinery: Inside Lobbying and Outside Lobbying

Lobbyists work through two channels, and they are fundamentally different operations.

**Inside lobbying** means taking the message directly to government officials. A lobbyist walks into a congressional office, meets with a staffer, presents data, discusses language for a bill, answers technical questions. Inside lobbying is information and access. A lawmaker does not have time to become an expert on tax policy, environmental regulation, healthcare administration, and criminal justice reform all at once. They cannot. Congress has 535 members and thousands of bills. Members rely on staff. And staff relies on lobbyists for the technical detail, the precedent, the numbers, the arguments. A lobbyist for an environmental group explains what a particular regulation would cost and what it would accomplish. A lobbyist for a business argues that the same regulation is unworkable and counterproductive. Both provide information. Neither expects the lawmaker to simply believe them. The power of inside lobbying is not persuasion; it is **access and specificity**. You have the ear of the decision-maker. You can say something detailed enough to matter.

Inside lobbying also includes drafting legislation. This happens more often than most citizens realize. A 2019 investigation by *USA Today* and the *Arizona Republic* found that entire sections of bills submitted to Congress were written word-for-word by lobbyists representing corporate clients. This is not necessarily scandalous. Legislators do not have the capacity to write every bill from scratch. They rely on lobbyists, advocacy organizations, and think tanks to generate language. The issue arises when a single interest—say, a tobacco company—writes the language in a way that advantages itself. But the practice itself is routine.

Inside lobbying works best with **sympathetic lawmakers**. An environmental lobbyist does not spend time trying to convince a coal-industry congressman that climate regulations are necessary. That lawmaker already opposes such regulations, and one conversation will not change his vote. Instead, the environmental lobbyist targets lawmakers who are already inclined to support environmental protection or are persuadable—votes that are uncertain. They also target **committee members** because committees shape bills before they reach the floor. If you want a healthcare bill written a certain way, you lobby the Health Committee members who will draft it. And they target the **conference committee** at the end—when the House and Senate versions have to be reconciled. A 1 percent difference in a tax rate might mean millions of dollars in revenue, and that difference gets negotiated in conference. Lobbying at that point is highly targeted and high-stakes.

The vast majority of lobbyists rely on inside strategies. Surveys show nearly all report that they contact lawmakers, testify before committees, help draft bills, and meet with agency officials. Inside lobbying is the bread and butter of the profession.

**Outside lobbying** operates in a different register. The goal is to get the public to care and then to translate that care into political pressure. An environmental group issues a press release about a threatened wetland. A reporter picks it up. It becomes a news story. Members of the public see the story and call their representatives. The representatives receive calls from constituents and understand that their vote on wetland protection will be noticed. The outside campaign created the impression of public engagement. The representative votes with that in mind.

Outside lobbying tactics include press releases, media campaigns, organizing rallies and protests, hashtag activism, letter-writing drives, and mobilizing members to contact lawmakers. The goal is to demonstrate **public salience**—to show that the issue matters to ordinary people, not just to the interest group. A lawmaker who is hearing from 100 constituents about an issue will pay more attention than one receiving letters from 5. The outside campaign tries to make that multiplier happen.

**Trade-off**: Inside lobbying is more efficient for changing specific language in bills, but it requires ongoing relationships with lawmakers and staff. Outside lobbying is noisier and less precise, but it can create political pressure that no inside conversation can match. Most sophisticated interest groups use both. An environmental organization might meet with a committee member to discuss specific bill language (inside) while simultaneously mobilizing members to call their representatives about the broader issue (outside). The inside track sets the details. The outside pressure sets the stakes.

The Sierra Club, for instance, will issue a press release about threatened public lands and encourage members to contact Congress. It will simultaneously testify before a committee about specific acreage and proposed protections. Both happen. The outside campaign makes the issue visible. The inside work makes the protection real and specific.

**Case Study: The NRA's Two-Track Strategy**

The National Rifle Association operates on both channels simultaneously, but in a distinctive way. On the inside track, the NRA maintains continuous contact with lawmakers sympathetic to gun rights. It provides technical briefings on firearms regulation, on Second Amendment interpretation, on the hunting practices that different gun types enable. It drafts language for bills that protect gun rights. On the outside track, the NRA runs ratings of politicians—grades of A+ through F based on voting records—that are published widely. It uses those ratings as a cudgel. A politician who votes for gun restrictions receives a poor rating, and that rating is circulated to NRA members and the public. It becomes part of the politician's electoral vulnerability. In elections, the NRA mobilizes voters to oppose politicians with poor ratings.

This combination—direct access plus public mobilization—is powerful because it operates on both dimensions: the legislator understands that there is a technical consequence to voting for a gun restriction (the NRA will draft alternatives that the legislator can propose) and a political consequence (the NRA will campaign against the legislator in their district). Few interests have both capacities at the NRA's scale.

**Common Misconceptions**

*Misconception 1: Lobbyists are primarily salespeople who change lawmakers' minds.*

They are not. Lobbyists mainly support lawmakers who already agree with them and provide those lawmakers with technical ammunition to defend their existing positions. Change happens at the margins—a lawmaker who is undecided might be swayed by new information. But the core work is servicing allies, not converting opponents.

*Misconception 2: Outside lobbying is always more democratic than inside lobbying.*

Not necessarily. Outside lobbying requires public attention, which requires media coverage. Media coverage requires that an issue be "newsworthy"—sensational, involving conflict, involving relatable human stakes. Some issues are inherently more telegenic than others. Gun rights and gun control are visible. Budget amendments for IRS funding are not. A group can run a sophisticated outside campaign on a visible issue and still lose to an inside campaign on an invisible one.

*Misconception 3: Money spent on lobbying directly translates into policy change.*

It does not. Lobbyists spend money on salaries, on office space, on research, and on outreach. But the relationship between lobbying spending and policy outcomes is loose. Some expensive campaigns fail. Some cheap campaigns succeed. The reason is that lobbying works through two mechanisms—information and political pressure—and money enables both but does not guarantee them. If public opinion is against you, money will not move a lawmaker's vote. If public opinion is with you, you might not need much money at all.

## 4. Money, Access, and Whose Voice Counts

The question of representation sits at the center of democratic theory. Who gets heard? Whose interests are reflected in policy? And are those the interests of "the people" or of particular factions?

Madison thought factions were inevitable and should compete with each other. The logic was Darwinian: the most legitimate interests would survive competition, and government would reflect the equilibrium. This is called **pluralism**—the idea that competition among many groups produces a rough balance.

But the empirical question is whether that balance actually exists. A 2014 study by Martin Gilens and Benjamin Page analyzed nearly 1,800 policy decisions made over a twenty-year period. They asked: when wealthy voters prefer a policy, how often does it get adopted? When poor and middle-class voters prefer a policy, how often does it get adopted? What they found was stark. When wealthy voters supported a policy, it passed more than 50 percent of the time. When poor voters supported a policy (and wealthy voters opposed it), it almost never passed. The influence gap was roughly 15 to 1. Wealthy people's policy preferences mattered. Other people's did not.

The research also found that business interest groups had roughly twice the influence of public interest groups. A lobbying campaign by the Chamber of Commerce was more likely to succeed than a campaign by a public interest group with the same nominal resources.

Why? Several mechanisms converge.

**Scale and sophistication**. Wealthy interests can afford to hire contract lobbying firms with dozens of staff. They can afford to maintain permanent offices near legislatures. They can conduct polling and focus groups to craft messages. They can buy media. A grassroots organization of low-income people can do none of these things at scale. They have passion. They have moral clarity. They have fewer resources.

**Access through money**. Campaign contributions open doors. A lobbyist for a pharmaceutical company can donate to a politician's campaign or arrange for wealthy donors to do so. That creates access—a meeting, a hearing, a chance to make the case. A person without money cannot offer that. They can vote, which is powerful in theory. In practice, one person's vote matters only at the margin. A contribution matters more directly: it finances the campaign infrastructure that makes reelection possible.

**The iron triangle**. A persistent pattern in American politics is the relationship among three actors: a congressional committee, an executive agency, and the interests that deal with both. The committee controls the budget and passes legislation. The agency implements the law. The interest group has a stake in both. Each has something the others need. The committee needs information from the interest group (about technical matters, about the group's preferences) and political support (the interest group can mobilize voters in the committee member's district). The interest group needs the committee to pass favorable legislation and needs the agency to implement it in a way the group prefers. The agency needs the committee for budget authority and needs the interest group for information and political support. The result is a tight triangle. It is hard to break in from the outside.

This structure is not secret. It is built into the system. Interests that operate inside the triangle—pharmaceutical companies, defense contractors, agricultural interests—have structural advantage over interests that operate outside. Some groups spend years trying to break in.

**Regulation and the revolving door**. One mechanism that amplifies wealth's advantage is the revolving door—the movement of people between government and the private sector. A former senator or a cabinet secretary can become a lobbyist. They already have relationships with current officials. They know how the system works. They have prestige. They can command high fees. This creates a perk for powerful people: a lucrative career path after government service. But it also creates a barrier for everyone else. An ordinary citizen cannot move seamlessly from activism to Capitol Hill and back. They lack the relationships and the market value.

Federal law requires a "cooling off" period: House members cannot lobby for a year after leaving office; senators must wait two years; cabinet secretaries must wait two years before lobbying the department they led. But these waiting periods are relatively short, and after they expire, the revolving door turns easily. Many former lawmakers become lobbyists, including former Senate majority leader Trent Lott and former House minority leader Richard Gephardt.

**Citizens United and the money floodgates**. In 2010, the Supreme Court decided *Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission*. The case centered on a question: Is political spending a form of speech protected by the First Amendment? The Court said yes. It ruled that corporations and unions—like individuals—have free speech rights, and that those rights include spending money on elections. The decision overturned a ban on corporate and union spending that had been in place since 2002 (the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act).

The result was unleashed spending. Corporations created "super PACs"—political action committees that can accept unlimited contributions and spend unlimited amounts on behalf of candidates. A super PAC cannot donate directly to a candidate, but it can run its own ads, mailings, and campaigns in support of a candidate the super PAC favors. Because donations to super PACs are not limited, wealthy individuals and corporations can write checks of any size.

In 2014, a follow-up decision, *McCutcheon v. Federal Election Commission*, struck down aggregate contribution limits—caps on the total amount an individual could give to all federal candidates and parties combined in a single election cycle. The effect was to make it even easier for wealthy donors to distribute money across many races simultaneously.

The debate about *Citizens United* revolves around whether the Court was right that spending is speech. Some argue it is: if I cannot spend money to amplify my message, my speech is constrained. Others argue it is not: spending money is conduct, not speech, and the government has legitimate reason to regulate conduct in order to prevent the corruption that comes from wealthy donors having outsized access. But empirically, the decision has meant one thing: a massive increase in the amount of money spent on elections by corporate and wealthy interests.

**Dark money**. One mechanism that amplified the Citizens United effect is the rise of "dark money"—spending by groups that do not have to disclose their donors. A nonprofit can spend unlimited amounts on politics without revealing where the money came from. These organizations are not required to disclose donors to the IRS, so the public does not know who is funding them. A group called "Americans for a Better Future" might spend millions on a campaign without the public knowing if that money came from a tobacco company, a foreign government, a hedge fund, or a concerned citizen group.

Dark money is not new, but it has exploded since *Citizens United*. In the 2012 election, dark money groups spent about $300 million. In 2016, it was over $800 million. By 2020, groups were spending billions, and the source of much of it remained hidden.

The effect is to amplify inequality in the political system. Not all rich people can spend unlimited money on politics (that requires organizing a super PAC and attracting donors), but those who can have enormous power. Meanwhile, the identities of some major donors remain invisible, meaning accountability is impossible. If you know that the Chamber of Commerce funded an ad, you can judge the message through that lens. If you do not know who funded the ad, you cannot.

**Trade-off**: Campaign finance regulation involves a genuine tension. One side emphasizes the need for transparency and the prevention of corruption—money in politics is corrosive, creates the appearance of bribery, and tilts policy toward wealthy interests. The other side emphasizes free speech—restrictions on political spending are restrictions on political expression, and the remedy for bad speech is more speech, not censorship. The Supreme Court has sided with the free speech argument. The result is less regulation and more money in politics.

The cost is paid by representation. A person or group without the ability to spend money on politics finds their voice amplified less. Government still hears them—elections still matter, public opinion still has weight. But on the margins, where policy is actually written, the voices with money are louder.

**Case Study: Healthcare Lobbying and the Affordable Care Act**

When Congress debated the Affordable Care Act in 2009, every major healthcare interest was in the room. Insurance companies. Pharmaceutical companies. Hospital associations. Doctors' groups. Employers. Patient advocates. The stakes were enormous—the law would reshape how tens of millions of people got healthcare.

The insurance industry spent more than $100 million lobbying the ACA. So did pharmaceutical companies. Hospital associations spent tens of millions. All these interests hired top contract lobbying firms. All had direct access to key committee members. Some provided language for the law. Some negotiated side deals (the pharmaceutical industry agreed to certain price controls in exchange for not running ads opposing the law).

Patient advocates also mobilized. Grassroots groups of sick people, people without insurance, and people with chronic illnesses organized rallies and letter-writing campaigns. They testified before Congress about their experiences. Their voices mattered—some provisions in the ACA reflected their priorities, like protections for people with pre-existing conditions.

But the conversation was not evenly balanced. The industry voices had money, existing relationships, and expertise. The patient voices had moral authority and electoral risk if ignored (no politician wants to face an ad saying "she voted against healthcare for sick children"). The result was legislation that reflected both influences but was written largely in the back rooms where money and access determined the language.

This is not conspiracy. This is how the system works. The interests with resources hire lobbyists. The lobbyists have access. They participate in writing the law. The interests without resources participate less and influence language less. Some of their priorities are included because they matter to the public and to politicians worried about reelection. But the details—the technical rules, the implementation timelines, the definitional edges—are often shaped by whoever had the lobbyist in the room.

**Common Misconceptions**

*Misconception 1: Money directly buys votes.*

It mostly does not. Lobbyists support politicians who already agree with them. A voting record shows whether a politician supports an interest's priorities. The interest then contributes to that politician's campaign. The causation is: the politician's vote causes the interest's support, not the other way around. Money does not buy converts; it buys incumbent advantage. A politician who opposes gun rights will receive little support from the NRA, no matter how much money the NRA has.

*Misconception 2: The wealthy always win.*

They do not. Public opinion matters. If the public cares about an issue, politicians must account for it. A proposal to eliminate Social Security, which is wildly popular, would fail even if opposed by well-funded interests. Conversely, proposals that are invisible to the public—like highly technical tax provisions—can pass even if opposed by large constituencies, because the public is not paying attention.

*Misconception 3: Citizens United changed politics more than anything else.*

It was significant, but campaign finance was already tilted toward the wealthy before 2010. Wealthy people and corporations could give to campaigns (just with lower limits). They could fund "issue advocacy" outside of campaigns. They could hire lobbyists. Citizens United amplified existing advantages but did not create them. The fundamental problem—that money translates into political access—predates the decision.

## 5. Integration and Synthesis

Take a step back. You have now seen three distinct puzzles in interest group politics.

The first is the puzzle of formation: why groups exist at all when individual participation creates a free rider incentive. The answer is that groups solve this through incentives (material, solidary, purposive), through mandatory membership in some cases, and through response to external disturbance. But this answer has a sharp inequality built in: it is easier to overcome free riding if you have money. You can offer material incentives. You can hire staff. You can sustain the group through funding sources outside of membership dues. The poorest interests struggle most with collective action problems.

The second is the puzzle of influence: how groups move government. The answer is through two channels—inside lobbying (access, information, drafting) and outside lobbying (public mobilization, media, pressure). But again, there is an inequality. Inside lobbying requires ongoing relationships, which money helps build. Outside lobbying requires public attention, which some issues naturally attract and others do not. The gun rights debate is visible. Budget amendments are not.

The third is the puzzle of representation: whose interests get reflected in policy. The answer is complex. Pluralists point out that competition among groups does happen and that no single interest dominates every issue. Others point out that wealthy interests win disproportionately, and that the poor and unorganized are systematically underrepresented. Both are true. The system is neither perfectly competitive nor entirely captured. It is biased toward money and access while still responsive to public opinion and elections.

These three puzzles are connected by a common theme: **inequality amplifies at each stage**. Wealthy interests more easily form groups. Wealthy interests have better access to the inside lobbying channels. Wealthy interests' policy preferences are more likely to be reflected. The system is not rigged in the sense of being entirely determined. But it is tilted. And the tilt is systematic: toward the organized over the disorganized, the wealthy over the poor, the concentrated interest over the diffuse one.

Understanding this does not require cynicism. It requires seeing the machinery. Interest groups are not villains. They are ordinary people and institutions trying to influence policy in ways that serve their interests. The problem is that "ordinary people" are not evenly distributed. Some have resources. Some do not. The machinery of influence reflects that inequality.

## 7. Summary

Interest groups form when people overcome the rational incentive not to participate in collective action. They do this through material, solidary, and purposive incentives; through mandatory membership; and through response to disturbance. But the ability to overcome these barriers is unequally distributed—wealthy interests can more easily form and sustain groups.

Interest groups influence government through two channels: inside lobbying (direct access, information, technical expertise) and outside lobbying (public mobilization, media campaigns, mobilizing members). Most lobbyists rely heavily on inside channels. But sophisticated interests use both.

Representation in the American interest group system is not equal. Research shows that wealthy interests have disproportionate influence on policy. Citizens United has amplified this by removing limits on corporate and union spending. Dark money—spending by groups with hidden donors—further clouds the picture.

Yet the system is not entirely captured by wealthy interests. Public opinion still matters. Elections still create incentives for politicians to listen to broad constituencies. And ordinary people do organize, mobilize, and make themselves heard. The machinery of influence is tilted toward money and access, but it is not impervious to pressure from below.

## 8. Connections Forward

The next chapter examines political parties, which are distinct from interest groups but interact with them constantly. Parties are broader coalitions seeking to elect people under a party label; interest groups are narrower and more issue-specific. But both are trying to influence government, and both must overcome collective action problems. Understanding how parties mobilize voters (a different puzzle from how interest groups mobilize members) will deepen your understanding of why representation in America is structured the way it is.

You will also encounter interest groups again when studying elections and campaign finance, the judicial branch (interest groups file amicus briefs trying to influence court decisions), and regulatory agencies (interest groups lobby agencies to interpret rules in ways they prefer).

The underlying tension—between competition among many interests (which could produce balance) and inequality in resources (which tilts the balance)—runs through all of American politics. You will see it everywhere: in elections, in the judiciary, in states and cities. The machinery of representation is never neutral. *What would change my mind*: Evidence showing that legislative outcomes are significantly more responsive to public opinion than to interest group preferences would substantially revise the chapter's emphasis on inequality in representation.

*Still puzzling*: The precise mechanisms through which continuous access translates into legislative outcomes on the margin remain incompletely specified in the empirical literature, and this chapter simplifies that relationship perhaps more than is fully warranted.
