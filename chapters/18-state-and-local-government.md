# Chapter 18 — State And Local Government
*The sovereignty nobody thinks about, and the machinery that runs most of your life.*

---

In 2015, the citizens of Denton, Texas voted to ban hydraulic fracturing within city limits. Denton is a university town. Residents had been watching drilling rigs appear near playgrounds and schools for years. They organized, gathered signatures, put the measure on the ballot, and won. Democracy in action.

Six months later, the Texas state legislature passed a law prohibiting cities from banning oil and gas operations. The ban was voided. The drilling resumed.

Denton's residents had done everything right. They had organized. They had voted. They had won. And it didn't matter, because they had exercised power they did not actually possess. The city of Denton, it turns out, does not have the authority to tell the state of Texas what to do with mineral rights. No Texas city does. The state gave cities the power to govern local matters. Energy regulation is not a local matter. The state said so, and the state gets the last word.

This is the puzzle that governs everything in this chapter: who actually has power in American government, below the federal level? The answer is more complicated — and more consequential for daily life — than most people realize.

---

## The Constitutional Silence and What It Produced

The Constitution names the federal government. It names the states. It says nothing about cities, counties, townships, school districts, water boards, or any of the other local bodies that actually provide most of the government services Americans encounter.

That silence was not an oversight. It was a choice. And it meant that states got to decide whether local governments existed at all, what they could do, and what authority to grant them. In the 1860s, an Iowa Supreme Court justice named John Dillon formalized this into a legal principle that still governs American local government: local governments are "creatures of the state." They can exercise only powers the state explicitly grants. A city is not sovereign. It exists at the pleasure of its state. The state can dissolve it, change its boundaries, take over its operations, or restrict its powers by statute. Denton discovered this the hard way.

Dillon's Rule is distinct from federalism. In a federal system, the Constitution protects both levels of government from each other — the federal government cannot simply abolish states, and states cannot simply ignore federal law. But Dillon's Rule is pure hierarchy. States and cities are not co-sovereigns. The state is the sovereign. The city is a subordinate body with delegated authority.

In practice, states recognized that governing cities from the state capital was impractical. People needed services where they lived, and providing them required local authority. So states created home rule — charters that gave local governments some autonomy over local matters. Most states now permit home rule. But home rule is a grant from the state, not an inherent right. The state gives it. The state can take it back. North Carolina did exactly this in 2016, when the state legislature preempted local ordinances on wages and working conditions, overriding several cities that had passed their own policies. Dillon's Rule, deployed.

## States as Sovereigns

A state government is not a local branch of the federal government. It is a sovereign government with its own constitution, legislature, governor, courts, and police power. The federal Constitution sets floors — states cannot violate the Bill of Rights or the Fourteenth Amendment — but states can build higher. California has a constitutional right to privacy that exceeds federal protection. Many states protect voting rights beyond federal minimums. States can grant rights the federal government does not.

State legislatures are the engine of state government. They pass laws, control budgets, set the structure of courts and agencies, and decide what authority to delegate downward to local governments. But here is something the federal analogy obscures: state legislatures vary enormously in what they are.

California's legislature is a professional, full-time body. Members have substantial salaries, large staffs, and meet nearly year-round. They can compete with the governor on equal footing. New Hampshire's legislature is the opposite: 400 members, meeting four months every other year, earning $100 annually. It cannot develop independent policy capacity. The result is not just a difference in size — it is a difference in what the institution can do. California's legislature initiates significant policy. New Hampshire's legislature mostly responds to the governor.

This variation in institutional capacity matters for policy outcomes in ways that are easy to miss if you assume all state governments work like the federal one. A state with a weak legislature and a strong governor operates differently from a state where the legislature can genuinely compete. A state whose judges are elected by voters has different judicial dynamics than one where judges are appointed and insulated from electoral pressure. These are not minor variations. They determine what kinds of laws get passed, how courts interpret them, and how the executive branch is held accountable.

State governors have formal powers most people underestimate. Many governors have a line-item veto — the ability to strike a single line from a budget bill and sign the rest. The president cannot do this. Some governors have an amendatory veto: return a bill to the legislature with specific changes requested. Some have a reduction veto: lower the dollar amount in an appropriation without killing the whole bill. These tools give governors leverage over budgets that the federal president simply lacks.

But informal power often matters more than formal power. A governor with 70 percent public approval can reshape an entire state agenda by proposing it. A governor with 35 percent approval cannot move the legislature even with formal veto authority intact. The currency of state executive power is partly legal authority and partly political capital, and the two do not always move together.

## The Patchwork Below

Below states sits the actual machinery most Americans encounter. The United States has roughly 3,000 counties, 19,500 municipalities, 16,500 township governments, and 38,000 special districts. These numbers matter because they reveal something: American local government is not a system. It is a patchwork of overlapping jurisdictions, each with its own elected officials, its own taxing authority, its own budget, and its own relationship with the state that created it.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: nested diagram showing the layers of American government — federal at the top, then states, then within a single representative county: the county government, the municipalities within it, and overlapping special districts (school district, water district, fire district, transit authority) — student should see that a single home address can sit within six or more independent governing jurisdictions simultaneously, each with its own elections and tax authority] -->

Counties are the largest local unit, covering multi-city regions. They exist to perform functions the state wants distributed geographically: administering courts, running jails, recording property deeds, assessing property for taxes, conducting elections, providing emergency services. A county might have a population of 10,000 or 10 million. Los Angeles County has more residents than most states.

County governments take several structural forms. Under a commission system, an elected board of commissioners serves as both legislature and executive — the same body that passes policy and runs the bureaucracy. This concentrates power without checks. Under a council-administrator system, voters elect a council that appoints a professional administrator to run day-to-day operations. Policy is separated from administration: the council sets direction, the administrator executes it. Under a council-elected executive system, voters elect both the council and a county executive, who can veto council decisions and propose budgets. Each form reflects a different theory about how accountability and professionalism should be balanced.

Cities provide the services most closely associated with daily life: garbage collection, water and sewer systems, parks, streets, zoning, police and fire protection, public transportation. The two dominant structures are the mayor-council model, where voters elect both a mayor and a council (with the distribution of power between them varying significantly by charter), and the council-manager model, where voters elect a council that appoints a professional city manager. The city manager model was explicitly designed to take cities out of the hands of political machines and put them in the hands of professionally trained administrators. It is common in mid-sized cities.

Special districts are the least visible piece of the patchwork and arguably the most important. A school district. A fire protection district. A water board. A transit authority. A hospital district. A pest control district. The United States has 38,000 of them, more than any other type of local government. Special districts exist because states created them to address specific functions that cross city and county lines. A water district might serve multiple cities. A transit authority might cover an entire metropolitan region. Each has its own elected or appointed board, its own budget, and its own taxing authority.

The fragmentation this produces is genuinely disorienting. A homeowner in a suburban area might be subject to a city government, a county government, a school district, a water district, a fire district, and a transit authority — all simultaneously, all making independent decisions, all sending tax bills. Voter turnout in local elections is typically 15 to 20 percent. In special district elections, it can be lower still. The governments that have the most direct effect on daily life — the ones that determine whether your school is funded, whether your water is safe, whether your neighborhood floods — are the ones that most people never vote in.

## The Fiscal Squeeze

Local governments do not set their own rules about what they can tax. States decide that. And the dominant source of local revenue — property tax — has been under political assault for decades.

Property tax is visible in a way income tax is not. You receive a bill. You see the number. It is easy to organize politically against a tax you can see. In 1978, California voters passed Proposition 13, limiting property tax to 1 percent of assessed value and allowing reassessment only when a property changed hands. The result was an immediate and permanent reduction in local government revenue. It was also a structural distortion: two identical houses on the same block could have property taxes differing by 300 percent, depending on when each last sold. Other states followed California's lead. The pattern is consistent: property tax caps constrain local revenue precisely when populations grow and service demands increase.

The federal government compounds the problem with categorical grants — money for specific purposes, with specific conditions attached. Federal grants built the modern infrastructure of local social services: mental health programs, housing assistance, job training, community health centers. When federal priorities shift and grants end, local governments must either absorb the cost from property tax revenue (which is capped) or eliminate the service. Many communities built programs around federal money that disappeared in the 2000s. The service evaporated. Local government could not replace it.

States add a third layer of fiscal pressure: unfunded mandates. A state legislature can require a city to meet environmental standards, fund certain services, or comply with new regulations without providing money to do so. Local governments must then choose whether to raise taxes (where they have authority to do so), cut other services, or accept noncompliance. This fiscal squeeze — rising requirements, capped revenues, and unpredictable federal funding — is the permanent condition of American local government. Detroit's 2013 bankruptcy was the most visible result of this dynamic pushed to its extreme: decades of declining tax base, pension obligations accumulated during better times, state aid that shrank, and federal grants that ended. The city owed more than it could pay and had fewer residents every year to pay it.

## Political Culture and What It Produces

Law explains what governments can do. Political culture explains what they actually choose to do.

The political scientist Daniel Elazar identified three distinct political cultures embedded in American states, shaped by the migration patterns of different European settler groups and reinforced over generations. Moralistic states — the upper Midwest, New England, the Pacific Northwest — see government as a tool for improving society collectively. Citizens expect officials to be honest and public-spirited. They tolerate higher taxes for collective goods. Voter participation is high. Educational and welfare spending tends to be generous.

Individualistic states — parts of the Mid-Atlantic, parts of the Midwest — see government as a marketplace, useful for specific services but otherwise intrusive. Citizens want low taxes and minimal interference. They are more tolerant of corruption if services are delivered. Welfare spending is modest. Economic development incentives — tax breaks for businesses — are popular.

Traditionalistic states — the South, parts of the Southwest — see government primarily as a mechanism for maintaining existing social order. Participation is narrower. Government protects property and status but does not redistribute wealth or expand opportunity broadly. Educational and welfare spending tends to be low. Poverty rates tend to be high.

<!-- → [IMAGE: map of the United States shaded by Elazar's three political culture types — moralistic, individualistic, traditionalistic — with transitional zones shown — student should see the geographic clustering and be able to connect it to the migration patterns that produced it; caption should note that the map reflects Elazar's original classification and that states have shifted with demographic change] -->

These are generalizations, and they shift as states change demographically and politically. But they correlate with measurable outcomes: voter turnout, educational attainment, health outcomes, social mobility. The correlation is not coincidental. Political culture shapes what citizens demand of government, what politicians believe they can propose, and what bureaucracies understand their job to be. A state's political culture is the water its institutions swim in.

## The Vertical Tension

The picture that emerges from all of this is not a clean federal hierarchy — federal on top, states in the middle, cities and counties at the bottom, each level minding its own domain. It is a tangle of competing authorities, overlapping jurisdictions, and contested powers.

States constrain cities through Dillon's Rule and preemption. The federal government constrains states through the Supremacy Clause and federal grants with conditions. Cities sometimes push back against states through litigation, ballot initiatives, and political organizing. States sometimes push back against the federal government the same way. The tensions run vertically in both directions simultaneously.

What makes this arrangement consequential rather than merely confusing is the proximity. A state representative represents roughly 30,000 people. A U.S. representative represents roughly 760,000. Local officials — city council members, school board trustees, county commissioners — represent far fewer. The physical and political distance between a citizen and their local government is shorter than any other level. That proximity makes local government more responsive to organized constituent pressure, more accountable to people who actually show up to meetings, and more vulnerable to capture by small organized interests when turnout is low.

The services that local governments provide — schools, water, roads, zoning, police, fire — are more immediately felt in daily life than most federal programs. Whether your school is well-funded, whether your neighborhood is zoned to allow a factory next door, whether your city has the fiscal capacity to maintain its roads — these are local decisions, made under state constraints, funded through property tax, and shaped by the political culture of the community. The federal government passes laws that dominate national headlines. State and local governments run most of the machinery that actually determines what living in America is like.

Understanding this machinery — who created it, who controls it, how it is funded, and why it varies so much from place to place — is understanding where the real levers of governance are. They are closer than most people think, and more accessible than most people use.

## What Would Change My Mind

The argument here assumes that state preemption of local authority is routine and judicially unchallenged — that Dillon's Rule operates as a real constraint on local power, not just a rhetorical one. Systematic evidence that courts were actively striking down state preemption statutes as unconstitutional encroachments on local authority would suggest that home rule has more legal protection than I am giving it credit for. I have not seen such evidence, but the pattern of judicial deference to state preemption is not something I have examined comprehensively.

## Still Puzzling

Why states rationally impose unfunded mandates on local governments rather than funding the requirements they create remains genuinely unclear to me. The short-term logic is obvious: the state gets political credit for requiring a service without paying for it. But the long-term cost — local governments that cannot meet mandates, eroding service quality, occasional fiscal collapse — falls partly back on the state in the form of bailouts, takeovers, and political embarrassment. That long-term cost seems systematically underweighted. Whether that is a failure of political time horizons, a genuine belief that local governments can absorb the cost, or something else entirely, I am not sure.
