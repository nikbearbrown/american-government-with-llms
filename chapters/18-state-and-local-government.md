# State and Local Government

**TL;DR:** States and cities are not branches of a national system—they are sovereigns in their own right, constrained first by state constitutions and then by federal law. The real machinery of American government happens closest to home: who paves your road, whether you can drill on your land, whether your city can ban fracking. That happens not in Washington but in statehouses and city halls, under rules that vary wildly and purposefully between states.

---

## 1. The Constitutional Puzzle: Why Are Cities Creatures of States, Not Co-sovereigns?

In 1787, the framers made an awkward choice. The Constitution named the federal government. It named the states. It was silent on cities, counties, towns, special districts—the local jurisdictions where most Americans encounter law most directly.

That silence was not accidental. It meant states got to decide whether local governments existed at all, what they could do, and what authority to grant them. A city is not sovereign. It exists at the pleasure of its state. The state can dissolve it, take it over, change its boundaries, or restrict its powers by statute. This principle is called *Dillon's Rule*, named for John F. Dillon, an Iowa Supreme Court justice who formalized it in the 1860s. Dillon's Rule says local governments are "creatures of the state"—subordinate bodies that can exercise only powers the state explicitly grants.

This is different from federalism. In a federal system, the Constitution grants power to both levels and protects them. Dillon's Rule is pure hierarchy: states decide, locals obey.

Yet in practice, states recognized they could not govern cities from the state capital the way a king governed towns from a palace. People needed services where they lived. So states wrote constitutions and laws that granted local governments *home rule*—some degree of autonomy over local matters. A home rule charter is like a state constitution: it spells out the structure, powers, and limits of local government. Most states now permit home rule. But it is a grant from the state, revocable by the state.

Watch this principle collide with reality. In 2015, the city of Denton, Texas, voted to ban hydraulic fracturing (fracking) within city limits. Denton is home to the University of North Texas; residents voted for the ban for health and safety reasons. The Texas state legislature, dominated by oil and gas interests, overrode the ban. Under Texas law, the state controls mineral rights and energy regulation. Denton's home rule did not extend to telling the state it could not use the state's resources. Denton wanted sovereignty it did not have.

This vertical tension—states versus cities, state constitutions versus city charters, state legislatures versus city councils—shapes what local governments can actually do.

## 2. The State as Sovereign: State Constitutions and Powers

Every state is itself a sovereign government with its own constitution, legislature, governor, courts, and police power. The federal Constitution sets floors (states cannot violate the Bill of Rights or the Fourteenth Amendment), but states can set ceilings higher. A state can grant rights the federal Constitution does not. California has protected right-to-privacy in its state constitution beyond what the U.S. Constitution protects. Many states have strengthened voting rights beyond federal minimums.

State legislatures are the engine. They pass laws, control budgets, set the structure of courts and agencies, and decide what authority to delegate to local governments. State governors are chief executives: they sign or veto bills, propose budgets, command the National Guard, appoint judges and cabinet officials, and make policy announcements that carry weight beyond their state.

But state institutions vary wildly. Some legislatures are full-time, professionalized bodies with large staffs, meeting year-round. Others are part-time citizen legislatures that meet for a few weeks every other year. Some governors have strong line-item veto power (the ability to strike out individual lines from a budget and sign the rest). Others have weak veto authority. Some state judges are elected by voters; others are appointed by governors or legislatures. These differences matter for policy.

Consider two examples. California's legislature is professional: members have salaries, staffs, and meet nearly year-round. The legislature competes with the governor on equal footing. New Hampshire's legislature is the largest state house in the nation (400 members) but meets for just four months every other year, and members make \$100 annually. They cannot compete with the governor for legislative dominance. The result is different state capacity and different state cultures: California legislates heavily; New Hampshire relies more on the executive to set direction.

State political cultures also shape what governments do. In what Daniel Elazar called moralistic states (upper Midwest, West Coast), citizens expect government to solve public problems and expand the welfare state. They tolerate higher taxes and more regulation for collective benefit. In individualistic states (mid-Atlantic, parts of Midwest), government is seen as a marketplace—provide the basics, stay out of the way, let people pursue private goals. In traditionalistic states (South, some Southwest), government is seen as maintaining existing social order and serving elites. These cultures shift with migration and time, but they still influence political attitudes within state legislatures and statehouses.

The formal powers of governors include budgeting, veto power, appointment of judges and cabinet officials, and command of the National Guard. But informal power—relationships with legislators, party leadership, persuasion, public approval ratings—often matters more. A governor with 70 percent approval can reshape an entire state agenda. A governor with 35 percent approval cannot.

State legislatures perform three primary functions: lawmaking, constituency service (helping individual constituents deal with bureaucracy), and oversight of the executive branch. Most state legislatures meet in a single legislative session each year (or every two years). The number of bills introduced varies enormously—California's legislature considers thousands; smaller state legislatures consider hundreds. Most bills die in committee. Those that pass must pass both the state house and state senate in identical form before reaching the governor's desk.

But here is the difference from Congress: state legislatures are much closer to their constituents. A state representative represents roughly 30,000 people. A U.S. representative represents roughly 760,000. State legislators are more likely to be known personally by constituents, to attend town halls and local events, and to be affected directly by state budget cuts or local economic problems. The distance between voter and legislator is shorter. That proximity shapes accountability.

## 3. The Machinery of State Government: Power Divided and Shared

State governments divide power between branches the way the federal government does. But the balance looks different.

**Governors versus legislatures.** Most governors have more power relative to their state legislatures than the U.S. president has relative to Congress. Why? Because state legislatures often meet only part-time, have smaller staffs, and depend on the governor for information about the bureaucracy. A full-time governor with a professional staff can dominate a part-time legislature. When the legislature is not in session, the governor makes budget cuts unilaterally. This power asymmetry is not universal—in California and New York, the legislature is powerful enough to compete with the governor—but it is common.

Governors have specific veto powers most presidents lack. Many governors can use a *line-item veto*: strike out a single line in a budget bill and sign the rest into law. The president cannot. About 30 percent of governors have an *amendatory veto*: send a bill back to the legislature asking for a specific amendment. A small number have a *reduction veto*: lower the dollar amount in a budget appropriation. Wisconsin once allowed governors to create entirely new words from bill language (the "Vanna White" veto) and reshape bill intent by removing words (the "Frankenstein" veto). Voters banned both after a governor used them repeatedly.

Governors can call special legislative sessions. They propose budgets that state legislatures must address. They deliver a "State of the State" address that frames the legislative agenda. They grant pardons and commute sentences for inmates. They command the National Guard in emergencies.

But state legislatures have oversight powers too. They confirm gubernatorial appointments (in most states). They override gubernatorial vetoes with a supermajority (typically two-thirds). They investigate executive agencies and can defund them. State legislatures also perform a function Congress rarely does: they pass constitutions and constitutional amendments. State constitutions are rewritten much more often than the U.S. Constitution is amended. Louisiana has written seventeen state constitutions since 1812. This gives state legislatures more opportunity to reshape the fundamental law.

**State courts.** Every state has a court system independent of federal courts. State courts handle the vast majority of litigation in America—95 percent of all lawsuits start in state court. They interpret state constitutions and state law. Federal courts interpret the U.S. Constitution and federal law. There is overlap—when a state court case involves federal constitutional questions, it can be appealed to the U.S. Supreme Court—but the separation is significant. State supreme courts are sovereigns in interpreting their own constitutions and laws.

State judges reach the bench in different ways. Some are elected by voters, some appointed by governors, some chosen by legislatures. Elected judges must raise money and campaign, which can create accountability to voters but also financial dependence on interest groups. Appointed judges may be insulated from political pressure but also less accountable. Merit-selection states have created commissions to screen judicial candidates and recommend them to governors, trying to balance independence and accountability.

## 4. The Local Patchwork: Counties, Cities, and Special Districts

Below states sits a bewildering array of local governments. The United States has roughly 3,000 counties, 19,500 municipalities (cities and towns), 16,500 township governments, and 38,000 special districts (school districts, fire districts, water boards, transit authorities). These are not subsidiaries of states. They are independent sovereigns granted power by the state, governing different geographic areas, with different purposes and different funding sources.

**County governments.** Counties are the largest local unit, typically covering a multi-city region. A county might have a population of 10,000 or 10 million. Counties exist to perform functions the state wants spread geographically: administer courts, run jails, record property deeds, assess property for taxes, conduct elections, provide emergency services. They usually take one of three forms.

Under a *commission system*, an elected board of commissioners serves as both legislature and executive for the county. Commissioners adopt budgets, pass resolutions, hire and fire staff. Power is concentrated. The system is common and straightforward but gives no one authority a check on the other.

Under a *council-administrator system*, voters elect council members, and the council appoints a professional administrator (a county manager) to run day-to-day operations. This divides policymaking (council) from administration (manager) the way a city council-manager system does. The administrator serves at the council's pleasure and can be fired. The model works when council and administrator agree; it breaks down when they clash over budget cuts or administrative decisions.

Under a *council-elected executive system*, voters elect both the council and a county executive (similar to a governor). The executive can veto council decisions, draft a budget, and propose policies. Power is still concentrated, but it is checked by separate elections.

Counties rely on property taxes, sales tax, state grants, and federal grants. Property tax is the main source. But property values fluctuate. When the housing market crashed in 2008, property values plummeted. Tax revenue fell 20-30 percent in many counties. Counties had to cut budgets, layoff staff, and close services in the middle of a recession, when services were most needed. Detroit, Michigan, is the most famous example: it filed for bankruptcy in 2013 after decades of declining tax base, pension obligations it could not fund, and declining state aid. The bankruptcy lasted four years, during which the city negotiated with creditors and restructured its debt. It has since recovered somewhat, but the scars remain.

**City governments.** Municipal governments serve cities and towns. They provide services states do not: garbage collection, water and sewer systems, parks, streets and sidewalks, zoning and building permits, police and fire protection, public transportation. Most cities use one of two models.

A *mayor-council system* has voters elect a mayor and a city council. The mayor is the executive; the council is the legislature. But the distribution of power varies. In a *strong mayor* system, the mayor can veto council decisions, appoint and fire department heads, and draft the budget. In a *weak mayor* system, the mayor is largely ceremonial—the council holds most power.

A *council-manager system* has voters elect a council (usually with a mayor chosen from among council members), and the council appoints a professional city manager to run administration. This mimics the county administrator model. The city manager handles hiring, firing, day-to-day operations, and service delivery. The council sets policy and budget. The system works well when there is political agreement; it breaks down when a council fires a popular manager or a manager makes controversial decisions without council authority.

Cities depend on property taxes, business taxes, user fees (for water, parking, trash), and grants from state and federal governments. Like counties, cities are vulnerable to property tax fluctuations. During recessions, sales tax revenue also drops.

The relationship between city and state is hierarchical. States can force cities to do things—comply with environmental standards, provide certain services, meet education mandates—without funding them. States can also take authority away. In 2016, North Carolina restricted local governments' ability to regulate wages and working conditions. The state legislature declared that local ordinances conflicted with state law. Dillon's Rule in action.

**Special districts.** Beyond counties and cities is a patchwork of special districts: school districts, fire protection districts, water boards, transit authorities, hospital districts, pest control districts. The United States has roughly 38,000 special districts, most for education (13,500 school districts) and smaller ones for specific services.

Special districts exist because states created them to address specific needs. A city might handle streets and parks, but a separate water district handles water quality and supply. A transit district handles public transportation across municipal boundaries. A school district handles education (sometimes coterminous with a city, sometimes spanning multiple cities).

Special districts have their own elected boards, budgets, and taxing authority (usually property tax). They are subject to state law but otherwise independent. They allow governments to serve areas that cross city boundaries. They also fragment local government—a person might vote in a city election, a county election, a school district election, a water district election, a fire district election, and a transit district election. Voter turnout in local elections is typically much lower than in state or federal elections, and turnout in special district elections is the lowest of all.

## 5. The Fiscal Tension: Federal and State Money, Local Responsibility

Local governments are constrained by what they can tax and what they must spend.

Most local revenue comes from property tax. Property tax is visible—a tax bill you receive once or twice a year. It is unpopular. In response, many states have capped property tax growth. California's Proposition 13 (1978) limited property tax to 1 percent of assessed value and allowed reassessment only when property changed hands. This lowered taxes but also created massive horizontal inequity: two identical houses on the same block could have property tax differing by 300 percent because one sold recently and one did not. Other states followed California's lead. The result is that property tax growth cannot keep pace with property value growth, starving local government of revenue exactly when they need to expand services for a growing population.

States also impose unfunded mandates on local governments. A state legislature can require a city to meet environmental standards, fund certain services, or comply with regulations without providing the money to do so. Local governments must then choose: raise taxes, cut other services, or attempt to sue the state (unlikely to succeed). This creates a fiscal squeeze: state requirements grow, property tax bases weaken, but local revenue options are limited.

Federal grants also complicate local finance. The federal government gives money to states and local governments through categorical grants (money with strings) and block grants (money with fewer conditions). Categorical grants let the federal government set national standards. But they also create bureaucratic burden and reduce local flexibility. When federal grant money ends or is cut, local governments must either absorb the cost or cut the program. Many communities built social services, mental health programs, and housing assistance around federal grants that ended in the 2000s. The service evaporated because local property tax could not replace the federal money.

## 6. Political Culture and State/Local Capacity

States differ not just in law but in political culture—the deep attitudes citizens and officials hold about government's role.

Moralistic states (Vermont, Minnesota, the Pacific Northwest) see government as a tool for improving society. Citizens expect elected officials to be honest, competent, and public-spirited. They tolerate taxes and spending on collective goods. Voter participation is high. They support full-time legislatures and professional public servants. Education and welfare programs tend to be generous.

Individualistic states (Pennsylvania, New Jersey, parts of Midwest) see government as a marketplace—useful for specific services but otherwise intrusive. Citizens want low taxes, limited government, and rewards for individual initiative. They are more tolerant of corruption if it does not affect service delivery. They prefer part-time legislatures and minimal bureaucracy. Welfare and social spending are modest. Economic development incentives (tax breaks for businesses) are popular.

Traditionalistic states (South, parts of Southwest) see government as maintaining existing social order. Only elites are expected to participate in politics. Government protects property and status but does not redistribute wealth or expand opportunity. Voter participation is lower. Welfare and educational spending are limited. States with traditionalistic cultures tend to have lower tax bases, higher poverty, and lower educational attainment.

These are generalizations. But they correlate with measurable outcomes: voter turnout, tax levels, spending patterns, social mobility, health outcomes. Moralistic states typically have higher voter turnout and higher educational spending. Traditionalistic states typically have lower both. This suggests that political culture, inherited from settlement patterns and migration history, shapes what local governments can actually accomplish.

## 7. Exercises

**Warm-up:**

Choose a local government service: trash collection, water supply, police, zoning, parks, public transportation, schools. Look it up. Which unit of government provides it in your area (city, county, special district, state)? How is it funded? Can you find your local government's budget online? What percentage goes to that service? Compare with a classmate in a different city. What differences do you notice?

**Application:**

In 2023, San Francisco's school district faced a $750 million budget deficit over five years. The causes were complex: declining enrollment, expensive teacher pensions, one-time federal COVID relief running out, and property tax revenues not growing fast enough. The district could raise taxes (city voters must approve), cut programs, renegotiate union contracts, or some combination.

Research what San Francisco actually did. Then research a school district near you. What fiscal pressures does it face? How much revenue comes from property tax, state aid, and federal aid? If it faced a deficit, what options would it have? Which options are politically feasible? Write a memo proposing a fiscal solution and defending why it would work.

**Synthesis:**

Imagine you are on a state legislature's local government committee. Cities have asked for home rule authority to set their own minimum wage. Business groups have asked the state to preempt local wage laws and set one statewide maximum. Unions want cities to be free to set high wages. Rural legislators worry cities will drain workers from rural areas by paying more.

How would you vote, and why? What federalism principles support your choice? What practical consequences would each option have? What is the relationship between local democracy and state sovereignty here?

## 8. Summary

States are sovereigns with their own constitutions, legislatures, governors, and courts. Local governments—counties, cities, special districts—are creatures of the state, granted power through charters and laws that the state can revoke. The principle is Dillon's Rule: local power exists only where the state explicitly grants it.

States vary widely in structure and capacity. Some have full-time professional legislatures; others are part-time citizen legislatures. Some governors have strong veto power; others have weak veto authority. State political cultures, shaped by settlement history, influence how much government citizens expect and what services they fund.

Local governments provide essential services: police, fire, water, schools, roads, parks. They depend mainly on property tax, which is unpopular and does not grow with needs. States impose unfunded mandates that limit local flexibility. Federal grants come with conditions. The result is a fiscal squeeze: rising service demands, limited revenue authority, and state requirements without state funding.

The patchwork of local governments—thousands of counties, cities, and special districts—creates fragmentation. It also creates opportunity for democracy: citizens can influence local government more directly than state or federal government. But it comes at a cost: unequal capacity, unequal services, and complexity that confuses voters about who is responsible for what.

The next chapters examine specific policy domains—education, criminal justice, economic development—to show how state and local governments compete and cooperate within the federal framework. You will see federalism not as an abstract constitutional principle but as the playing field on which real governance happens: states pushing back against federal mandates, cities suing states over authority, citizens voting with their feet by moving to jurisdictions that match their preferences.

You will also see how choices made in statehouses and city halls—about what to tax, what to regulate, what services to provide—have consequences for individual lives far more immediate than federal law.

## What Would Change My Mind

Evidence that the Supreme Court was meaningfully enforcing Dillon's Rule—striking down local ordinances that conflicted with state law—would suggest that state preemption of local authority is actively policed rather than merely invoked rhetorically. I suspect states routinely override local decisions with little judicial resistance, but systematic data on that pattern would clarify whether local autonomy is real or theater.

## Still Puzzling

Why states rationally choose to weaken their own local governments through unfunded mandates rather than investing in local capacity puzzles me. If a state wants environmental standards or services, it seems short-sighted to mandate them without funding. The answer likely involves short-term budget pressure and the political advantage of imposing costs on others, but the long-term damage to state capacity seems underestimated.
