# The Bureaucracy

**TL;DR:** Bureaucracies are complex organizations designed to insulate government from politics through competence and rules rather than loyalty. The shift from spoils system to merit system transformed hiring but created a new problem: nonelected officials who answer to no one the public voted for. Control mechanisms remain incomplete and contested.

---

## Chapter Opening: The Machine That Runs the Country

It is May 2025, and a thunderstorm approaches the central plains of Oklahoma. The National Weather Service—buried in the Department of Commerce, housed in NOAA's field office in Norman, staffed by meteorologists, forecasters, and data technicians—makes its prediction available to anyone who asks. A farmer checks the forecast at 5 AM. A highway patrol officer reads the severe weather outlook at the morning briefing. A television meteorologist pulls the model data into tomorrow's broadcast. The forecast is not made by an elected official. No congressman wrote it. No cabinet secretary signed off on it. It was made by people you have never heard of, paid according to a pay schedule established by law, following procedures written by the agency itself, using expertise they earned through competitive examination.

Three weeks later, budget cuts arrive. Twenty percent of the staff is dismissed. A hiring freeze takes effect. The remaining staff cannot launch weather balloons to gather the high-altitude data that makes accurate forecasts possible. The precision of the warning system deteriorates. No storm dies because of this. But the margin narrows—the gap between a forecast that saves lives and one that doesn't. The question is not whether bureaucrats should exist. It is: who controls them when no ballot box reaches them?

Bureaucracy evokes two opposite reactions in the American mind. For some, it means inefficiency, red tape, regulations that choke enterprise. For others, it means the machinery that makes government work at all—the Social Security check that arrives, the airline safety inspection that prevents catastrophe, the weather warning that buys time before disaster. The tension between these two readings is not accidental. It reflects a genuine problem baked into democratic governance itself: how do you get the benefits of specialized, impartial administration while keeping the government accountable to the people?

**Learning objectives.** By the end of this chapter, you will understand:
- What a bureaucracy actually is and why democracies create them
- How the United States shifted from political patronage to merit-based hiring, and why that shift mattered
- The mechanisms by which elected officials, the courts, and citizens attempt to control a fourth branch of government that no one votes for

**Prerequisites.** You should be comfortable with the basic structure of U.S. government (executive, legislative, judicial branches) and have read Chapter 2 (Federalism) or Chapter 5 (The President). Concepts around representation and accountability from Chapter 1 will help.

## Concept 1: What Bureaucracy Does and How It Thinks

**Cold open: The pyramid with no head.**

Imagine you are hired by the Federal Reserve to manage currency flows. You will spend the next twenty years of your working life in the same role, protected by law from being fired for your politics. Your salary follows a chart—GS-12, Step 5, currently $75,706 to $98,422 per year. You get a paycheck regardless of which party controls Congress or which president sits in the White House. You write regulations, review applications, attend meetings, and build expertise in one narrow domain of federal authority. You are not elected. You cannot be recalled. Your name will rarely appear in public. You will influence policy that affects millions of people.

**What a bureaucracy is and why it exists.**

A bureaucracy is an administrative organization designed to carry out a defined set of functions through a hierarchical structure, written rules, and specialized roles. The people who staff it are called bureaucrats or civil servants—nonelected officials appointed or hired to administer government programs.

The word "bureaucracy" carries weight in American political language, but the thing itself is not uniquely modern. Archaeologists have found evidence of administrative systems in ancient Egypt, where scribes kept records and inventories. Tax collectors in ancient Judea were bureaucrats. What changed in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries was not the idea of bureaucracy, but its theoretical grounding and its scope.

Max Weber, the German sociologist, developed what is called the ideal-type bureaucracy—a model that captures what bureaucracies are supposed to be at their best. In Weber's description, an ideal bureaucracy has several key features:

- It is **apolitical**—staffed by people hired for their technical knowledge, not their party loyalty
- It is **hierarchical**—organized in a clear chain of command so that authority flows downward and accountability upward
- It operates by **written rules**, not personal judgment or discretion
- It uses **specialization**—each person masters one domain and contributes that expertise to the whole
- It eliminates **arbitrary power**—a person cannot fire you on a whim, and decisions are documented

The appeal of this model is clear. If you want to regulate airlines, you do not want an airline safety inspector chosen because he shook the president's hand at a rally. You want someone who has studied aeronautical engineering, passed exams, and spent years learning what makes aircraft safe. If you want to process social security benefits, you want clerks who follow a documented procedure, not bureaucrats who favor friends and punish enemies. In Weber's ideal type, competence replaces loyalty, and rules replace favor.

**The first American bureaucracy: Washington's three departments.**

The Constitution anticipates bureaucracy without naming it. Article II gives the president power to appoint officers and see that laws are "faithfully executed." Article I authorizes Congress to establish a post office, coin money, regulate commerce. But the document does not describe what the bureaucracy should look like or how large it should be. The design was left to be worked out in practice.

Under George Washington, the bureaucracy remained small because the work was small. Three cabinet departments emerged: State (foreign affairs), Treasury (finance), and War (military). Together with the growing postal service, these constituted the entire federal bureaucracy for the republic's first decades. Presidents and their secretaries spent their days managing a few hundred people. Government employment was not yet a profession. The men appointed to these posts were often wealthy, well-educated individuals from the merchant or planter classes. In the early republic, these roles carried prestige but were not career paths.

The modern problem of bureaucracy did not yet exist because the government did not yet do much.

**Trade-off: Expertise requires power, but power without accountability is dangerous.**

There is an inherent tension in bureaucratic design that cannot be fully resolved. On one side: you need expert administrators to run complex government programs. Medicine, engineering, weather prediction, financial regulation—these require years of training and accumulated knowledge. A congressperson cannot legislate at the level of detail that implementation requires. Bureaucrats must have discretion to fill in the gaps.

On the other side: bureaucrats are not elected. They cannot be recalled by voters. If a bureaucrat becomes corrupt or incompetent, the remedy is slow and uncertain. Congressional oversight exists, but Congress has limited time and attention. The president can replace cabinet secretaries but not the hundreds of thousands of civil servants below them.

This is the core of the fourth-branch problem. Bureaucracy solves the problem of governance at scale. But it creates a new problem: who watches the watchers?

**Common misconceptions.**

One persistent error is treating bureaucracy as inherently inefficient or inherently corrupt. In fact, bureaucracy is a tool. A poorly designed bureaucracy is inefficient and corrupt. A well-designed one can be remarkably effective. The U.S. Social Security Administration processes millions of claims per year with error rates in the low single digits. The National Institutes of Health funds peer-reviewed research that has produced medical breakthroughs. These are bureaucracies that work.

Another misconception is that bureaucracy is uniquely governmental. In fact, nearly all large organizations—corporations, universities, hospitals, charities—are bureaucratic in structure. They have hierarchies, specialized roles, and written procedures. The difference is accountability: a corporation answers to shareholders and markets; a government bureaucracy answers to the president, Congress, the courts, and ultimately the public. That difference matters, but it does not make bureaucracy itself pathological.

## Concept 2: From Spoils to Merit—The Pendleton Act and Its Aftermath

**Cold open: A disappointed office seeker shoots the president.**

July 2, 1881. President James Garfield is in the Baltimore and Potomac Railroad Station in Washington, D.C., waiting to catch a train to New Jersey for a vacation. A man named Charles Guiteau approaches him and fires twice. Garfield falls. Guiteau is arrested and later hanged. What drove a man to assassinate a president?

Guiteau believed he had been promised a federal job in exchange for his campaign support. When the job did not materialize—when the new administration decided not to hire him—he decided to kill the president and, in doing so, possibly make himself famous. It is the kind of crime that belongs to an older political system, one where government jobs were currency in a patronage game.

**The spoils system: government as reward.**

For much of the nineteenth century, American government operated under the spoils system. The winning party, having won an election, claimed the right to fill federal positions with its supporters. The term "spoils" comes from the saying "to the victor go the spoils." When Andrew Jackson took office in 1829, thousands of federal jobs changed hands. Men who had worked loyally for the party during the campaign could expect payment: a job, a salary, security.

The logic was straightforward. A party that won an election believed it had earned the right to control the machinery of government. If you filled key posts with people loyal to you, the government would be responsive to your agenda. Your supporters would have an incentive to work hard during elections because their livelihoods depended on the party's success. And because parties knew that the other side would turn them out when they lost, there was pressure to maintain tight discipline.

But the spoils system had corrosive effects that became impossible to ignore. Patronage appointments often went to people who had no qualifications for the work. An experienced diplomat might lose his post because the new party wanted to reward someone else. Government effectiveness depended on which party was in power, and turnover was chaotic. Scandals accumulated—corruption became routine because loyalty, not competence, was the path to advancement. By the 1870s, after the administration of President Ulysses S. Grant was rocked by multiple corruption scandals, pressure for reform grew impossible to resist.

**The Pendleton Act: merit replaces loyalty.**

In 1883, Congress passed the Civil Service Reform Act, commonly known as the Pendleton Act. It did three crucial things:

First, it made it illegal to fire federal employees solely for political reasons. A bureaucrat hired under a previous administration could not be automatically terminated when a new administration took office. This severed the connection between party loyalty and job security.

Second, it established competitive examinations for federal jobs. If you wanted a job in the civil service, you had to pass a test demonstrating your knowledge and ability in the relevant field. The exams were designed to measure competence, not party affiliation.

Third, it created the Civil Service Commission—an independent body charged with enforcing these new rules, developing the examinations, and protecting the merit system from political interference.

The impact was gradual but transformative. In 1883, only about 10 percent of the federal workforce was covered by civil service protections. But over the following decades, the coverage expanded. Political parties could no longer promise jobs to supporters with the same confidence they once had. Federal employees began to see their careers as professional tracks rather than patronage windfalls. The relationship between government workers and elected officials changed fundamentally.

**The evolution of civil service: 1883 to now.**

After the Pendleton Act, the civil service continued to evolve. In 1912, the Lloyd-La Follette Act removed penalties for federal workers who wanted to join labor unions. By the 1930s and 1940s, federal employees were organizing collectively, and labor unions became a political force within the bureaucracy.

In 1939, Congress passed the Hatch Act (the Political Activities Act), which prohibited federal employees from actively campaigning for candidates or using their official authority to influence elections. The logic was sound: if bureaucrats could organize politically and use their power to influence outcomes, the separation between administration and politics would collapse.

By 1978, the Civil Service Commission had existed for nearly a century. But Congress believed it had become ineffective. The Commission was abolished and replaced by two new agencies: the Office of Personnel Management (OPM), which handles hiring and recruitment, and the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB), which investigates wrongdoing and hears appeals. These changes were meant to modernize oversight and protect employees from managerial abuse.

Today, approximately 85 percent of federal positions are filled through merit-based examination of education, background, knowledge, skills, and abilities. Some 15 percent are still filled through presidential appointment or other means. The precise mechanism has changed over time, but the principle—that government jobs should go to the most qualified applicants, not to political friends—remains in place.

**Trade-off: Merit insulates bureaucrats from politics, but creates distance between elected officials and the bureaucracy.**

The spoils system had a problem: it politicized the bureaucracy. A merit system solves that problem but creates another: bureaucrats who are insulated from political pressure can become unresponsive to elected officials and the public.

Under spoils, a federal employee knew that the next election could end his job. He had to pay attention to what the party leadership wanted because his livelihood depended on it. Under merit, a federal employee who has passed exams and gained seniority has substantial job security. He cannot be fired for disagreeing with the president. He cannot be punished for refusing to implement a policy he thinks is wrong. This independence is by design—it protects bureaucrats from arbitrary removal. But it also means that a bureaucrat can pursue his own vision of his agency's mission even if it conflicts with what elected leaders want.

This is not necessarily corruption. It is structural tension. A career official at the EPA might believe that environmental protection is the agency's core mission, while a president might believe that economic growth should take priority. Each has a legitimate claim on the agency's work. But the official has job protections that insulate him from political consequences. How do you maintain both expert independence and democratic accountability?

**Case study: The weather service cuts of 2025.**

In May 2025, the Trump administration moved aggressively to reduce federal employment through hiring freezes and dismissals of probationary employees (those in their first two years). The National Weather Service, part of NOAA, saw severe cuts. By late spring, the agency had a 19 percent vacancy rate across its 119 field offices, with some offices seeing vacancy rates of 40 percent or higher. The result: critical weather balloons could not be launched, field forecasters were stretched thin, and the precision of severe weather warnings declined in the critical period ahead of severe thunderstorm and hurricane season.

Career meteorologists at the NWS believed these cuts endangered public safety. They continued to advocate for their positions through professional channels. But they could not campaign openly, could not organize political opposition in the way private employees might. They were trapped in a structure designed to insulate them from politics, which also prevented them from mobilizing political support for their agency's mission.

This illustrates the trade-off clearly: the merit system protected these officials from being fired for their politics, but it also limited their ability to defend their work against elected leadership. A spoils system would have allowed the incoming administration to replace them immediately with loyal allies. A merit system forced the administration to work around them—through hiring freezes and other mechanisms—rather than directly removing them. Both approaches create problems.

**Misconception: The merit system is complete.**

It is tempting to treat the merit system as a solved problem—a system in which government jobs are allocated fairly based on qualifications. But the system is partial and porous. Presidential appointees—cabinet secretaries and their deputies—still wield enormous power and are chosen by the president for loyalty as much as competence. They oversee bureaucrats below them and can shape agendas through budgets, priorities, and personnel. Moreover, approximately 15 percent of federal positions are still excepted from merit requirements. And career bureaucrats, while protected from arbitrary firing, are not immune to pressure. Budget cuts, reorganizations, and shifting priorities can constrain what they can actually accomplish.

## Concept 3: Control Mechanisms—Congress, the President, and the Public

**Cold open: The IRS targets tax-exempt groups.**

October 2013. The Internal Revenue Service admits that it has given extra scrutiny to organizations applying for tax-exempt status, and that many of these organizations had names associated with conservative or Tea Party politics. The revelation detonates across political Washington. If the IRS can weaponize tax law against groups it disfavors, what other agencies might do the same? Congress convenes hearings. The IRS commissioner is questioned at length, sometimes combatively. Congressional committees draft investigations. The House Oversight and Government Reform Committee launches a formal inquiry.

On the surface, this looks like oversight working. An agency is caught doing something questionable; elected representatives respond. But the question that lingers is deeper: how much can Congress actually control an agency that has thousands of employees, operates in technical domains that congresspeople do not fully understand, and has its own institutional interests?

**Congressional oversight: Hearings, budgets, and the Government Accountability Office.**

Congress has several tools to oversee the bureaucracy. The most visible is the hearing. When a scandal emerges or a committee wants information, it summons agency heads to testify. These hearings are sometimes calm and fact-finding. Sometimes they are tense and confrontational, with congresspeople demanding answers and agency officials defending their actions.

But hearings are theater as much as substance. Agencies prepare carefully. Officials are trained in how to answer questions without fully answering them. A hearing might produce outrage, but little change.

More consequential is Congress's power over the purse. Agencies submit annual budget requests, and Congress decides whether to approve, reduce, or increase them. If Congress wants to constrain an agency, it can starve it of funds. This is a blunt instrument—it harms the agency's mission, which often means it harms the public—but it is powerful.

A third tool is the Government Accountability Office (GAO). The GAO is an agency within the federal government, but it is controlled by Congress and reports to Congress. It conducts audits, evaluations, and investigations into how federal agencies spend money and whether they achieve their stated goals. The GAO produces roughly 900 reports per year on everything from education to homeland security to environmental management. These reports are detailed, fact-based, and often highly critical. For an agency, a harsh GAO report can be embarrassing and can prompt congressional action.

**Trade-off: Effective oversight is resource-intensive and Congress has many other priorities.**

The problem with congressional oversight is not that the mechanisms do not exist. It is that they are all labor-intensive, and Congress has limited time and attention. There are 535 members of Congress. There are hundreds of federal agencies. A member of Congress might sit on an oversight committee, but he also sits on other committees, works on legislation, travels to his home district, meets with constituents. Oversight competes with many other demands.

As a result, congressional oversight is episodic. When a scandal explodes or when an agency becomes a political target, Congress responds with intensity. But most agencies, most of the time, operate with minimal congressional scrutiny. This is partly intentional—Congress delegates authority to the bureaucracy because Congress cannot micromanage everything. But it also means that agency officials have room to pursue their own agendas.

**Presidential oversight: Appointments and the Office of Management and Budget.**

The president controls the bureaucracy more directly than Congress does. The president appoints the heads of all cabinet departments and many independent agencies. These appointees serve at the pleasure of the president—they can be fired at will. Cabinet secretaries report directly to the president. If a president wants a cabinet department to move in a particular direction, he can replace the secretary with someone who will implement that direction.

But cabinet secretaries oversee agencies with thousands of employees. A secretary can set priorities and shift resources, but he cannot control every decision made by career officials below him. Moreover, most federal employees are protected by civil service rules and cannot be fired because the president disagrees with them.

A second tool is the Office of Management and Budget (OMB). The OMB is part of the Executive Office of the President. Its primary role is to prepare the president's budget for Congress, but it has broader authority. The OMB reviews regulations proposed by agencies before they are finalized. It coordinates and vets presidential directives. It monitors agency performance. If the OMB decides that an agency's proposed regulation is too costly or conflicts with presidential priorities, it can send the regulation back for revision. This is a powerful lever.

In January 2025, President Trump used presidential authority aggressively. He directed cabinet secretaries to reduce employment through hiring freezes and dismissals of probationary employees. He ordered the OMB to review regulations and cut those he viewed as obstacles to business. These are legitimate presidential powers, but they illustrate the asymmetry: the president can impose his will on the bureaucracy fairly directly through appointments, budgets, and directives. Congress's leverage is slower and requires coordination.

**Citizen oversight: Freedom of Information and the Sunshine Act.**

Citizens cannot vote out a bureaucrat, but they have some tools to monitor what bureaucracies do. The most important is the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA), passed in 1966. Under FOIA, citizens and journalists can request records from federal agencies. Agencies are required to release the information unless it falls under one of nine exemptions—classified materials, trade secrets, personnel privacy, law enforcement information, and a few others.

FOIA has enabled journalists to uncover bureaucratic wrongdoing and has given citizens a window into government decision-making. But it also has limitations. Agencies can slow-walk FOIA requests. They can claim exemptions broadly. In fiscal year 2020, the federal government received 772,869 FOIA requests. The vast majority were handled, but processing times can stretch months or years. And agencies do not always release what is requested—they can redact (black out) information even when they do comply.

A second mechanism is the Government in Sunshine Act of 1976. It requires multi-headed federal agencies to hold meetings in public. The logic is that "sunlight is the best disinfectant"—corruption and abuse thrive in secrecy, but shrink when exposed to public view. The act does allow exceptions: classified information, proprietary data, personnel matters, criminal investigations.

A third mechanism is the right to testify at public hearings on proposed rules. When a federal agency proposes a regulation, it must publish the proposal in the Federal Register and allow a comment period. Citizens can attend hearings and voice concerns. Some agencies hold hearings in multiple locations to make participation easier.

**Trade-off: Citizen oversight is broad but thin.**

Citizens have formal rights to information and participation, but exercising these rights requires time, knowledge, and resources. Reading a technical notice of proposed rulemaking requires expertise. Attending a hearing requires travel and time off work. Submitting a detailed comment requires understanding the issue and the regulatory process. As a result, FOIA and public comment opportunities are used most effectively by organized groups—environmental organizations, industry associations, unions—that have staff dedicated to monitoring regulations. Individual citizens participate less frequently.

Moreover, even when citizens request information or comment on rules, there is no guarantee that bureaucrats will listen. Comments can be ignored if the agency has already decided on a course of action. FOIA requests can be delayed or partially denied. The mechanisms exist, but the power they convey is unequal.

**Misconception: Whistleblowers will catch everything.**

One hope is that bureaucrats themselves will expose wrongdoing. Someone working inside an agency often sees misconduct before anyone outside does. If bureaucrats have the courage to speak up, the thinking goes, internal controls can catch problems early.

This hope is understandable but partial. The Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 and subsequent legislation have created protections for federal whistleblowers—those who publicly report misconduct and face retaliation for doing so. The Merit Systems Protection Board hears whistleblower complaints. Courts have invalidated reprisals against employees who speak out.

But protections are limited. An employee who reports wrongdoing may face subtle retaliation—poor evaluations, blocked promotions, undesirable assignments—that is hard to prove in court. Institutions protect themselves; bureaucracies are no exception. A career official knows that reporting his agency's misconduct may damage his future in that agency. And even when whistleblowers do come forward, the machinery of response is slow. Edward Snowden's revelations about NSA surveillance took years to become widely understood as raising serious questions about government overreach.

## Integration: The Bureaucratic Paradox

The United States faces a genuine and unresolved paradox. Modern government is too complex for elected officials to manage directly. We need bureaucrats—people with expertise and time to understand regulatory systems, manage programs, and make detailed decisions that laws cannot specify. We need them insulated from politics enough that they can act on technical knowledge rather than party loyalty. But we also need them accountable to the public and responsive to elected leaders. These two requirements are partly in tension.

The spoils system solved the accountability problem by making bureaucrats answerable to party leadership. But it created the incompetence problem—jobs went to loyalists, not experts.

The merit system solved the competence problem by protecting jobs for people hired on qualifications. But it created the accountability problem—bureaucrats insulated from political pressure can become unresponsive to the public and its elected representatives.

No oversight mechanism fully resolves this tension. Congress oversees through budgets and hearings, but lacks the resources for continuous monitoring. The president oversees through appointments and directives, but cannot control thousands of career employees. Citizens oversee through FOIA and testimony, but that leverage is unequal and depends on organized groups. Whistleblowers expose wrongdoing, but internal pressure and career risk deter many from speaking up.

The working solution, if it can be called that, is a patchwork: multiple overlapping mechanisms, none fully adequate, that together create enough friction that bureaucracies are sometimes responsive to public concerns. It is not elegant. It is not perfectly accountable. But it works more often than not, partly because bureaucrats are human beings who generally care about doing their jobs well, and partly because elections provide a backstop—if the bureaucracy becomes egregiously unresponsive, the public can vote out the president and Congress that oversees it.

## Chapter Summary

Bureaucracies exist because modern government is too complex for elected officials to handle directly. The United States shifted from a spoils system—in which government jobs were rewards for political loyalty—to a merit system in which jobs go to the most qualified applicants. This change was necessary to improve competence and eliminate corruption, but it created a new problem: bureaucrats who are insulated from political pressure and cannot be easily controlled.

Congress, the president, and citizens have developed mechanisms to oversee the bureaucracy—budgets, hearings, appointments, FOIA requests, public comment periods, whistleblower protections. But no single mechanism is fully adequate. The working solution is a patchwork in which multiple overlapping controls create enough friction that bureaucracies respond to public concerns, even if imperfectly and unevenly.

The core tension—between the need for expert bureaucrats and the requirement of democratic accountability—remains unresolved. It may be unresolvable. The compromise we have is not perfect, but it is the one democracies have settled on.
