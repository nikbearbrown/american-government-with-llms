# Chapter 20 — The Bureaucracy
*The Machine That Runs the Country, and the Problem of Who Runs the Machine*

---

Here is something that happened on July 2, 1881.

President James Garfield was walking through a Washington train station when a man named Charles Guiteau shot him twice. Garfield died two months later. Guiteau's motive was not ideological. He was not an anarchist or a revolutionary. He was a disappointed office seeker. He believed he had been promised a federal job in exchange for his campaign work, and when the job did not materialize, he decided to kill the president.

This sounds like pathology. It was also, in a particular way, the spoils system working exactly as designed — and producing the consequences it was always going to produce. When government jobs are currency, when they are given as rewards for political loyalty, you get a government that functions as a patronage machine. You also get Charles Guiteau.

The Pendleton Act of 1883 — the first major civil service reform — was passed partly because of what Guiteau did. And the question it tried to answer is still the central question of this chapter: how do you build a government that works on merit rather than loyalty, while keeping it accountable to the people who are elected to run it?

---

## Why Bureaucracy Exists at All

Before getting to accountability, a more basic question: why have bureaucracy at all?

The answer is scale and complexity. In 1789, the entire federal government consisted of the State Department, the Treasury Department, and the Department of War, employing a few hundred people. The government did not do very much. It collected some tariffs, managed a small army, and conducted foreign policy. A president and a small cabinet could conceivably supervise most of it.

Now consider the present. The federal government employs roughly two million civilians across hundreds of agencies. The Social Security Administration processes tens of millions of benefit claims per year. The Federal Aviation Administration certifies aircraft, licenses pilots, manages air traffic control for commercial aviation across a continent. The Food and Drug Administration decides which drugs are safe enough for American patients. The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration runs weather satellites and maintains the forecasting infrastructure that the entire civilian economy depends on.

None of this can be managed by elected officials directly. A senator cannot learn enough about aeronautical engineering to certify aircraft designs. A president cannot personally evaluate drug safety data. These tasks require people who have spent years developing expertise in narrow domains, who can apply that expertise consistently, and whose work is not interrupted every four years when a new party takes office.

Max Weber, the German sociologist who developed the most influential theory of bureaucracy, described what the ideal bureaucratic organization would look like: apolitical, meaning staffed on the basis of technical knowledge rather than party loyalty; hierarchical, with clear chains of command; governed by written rules rather than personal judgment; and specialized, with each person mastering one domain and contributing that expertise to the whole.

Weber was describing a design ideal, not a description of any real organization. But the ideal captures the purpose. You want aircraft safety inspectors hired because they understand aircraft, not because they attended the right fundraiser. You want drug approval scientists who follow documented procedures, not ones whose conclusions shift with the political winds. The bureaucracy is, in theory, the part of government that does not change with elections, that maintains institutional memory, that brings expertise to problems too complex for any elected official to understand.

The problem is that this design creates its own form of unaccountability. A civil servant with job protections, hired on the basis of qualifications, cannot easily be removed by a president who disagrees with how they do their job. That independence is the feature — it prevents political pressure from overriding technical judgment. It is also the bug — it means that someone who was never elected and can never be voted out has authority over decisions that affect millions of people.

This is what political scientists sometimes call the fourth-branch problem. The Constitution creates three branches. The bureaucracy is not one of them. And yet it exercises something that looks a great deal like law-making power (writing regulations), executive power (implementing programs), and even quasi-judicial power (adjudicating disputes). Who controls it?

## The Spoils System and Why It Failed

To understand the merit system, you have to understand what it replaced.

For most of the nineteenth century, the federal government operated on what was called the spoils system. When a party won a presidential election, it claimed the right to fill federal positions with its supporters. The phrase comes from "to the victor go the spoils" — the idea that winning an election meant winning control of the machinery of government, including all the jobs.

Andrew Jackson, who took office in 1829, is most associated with this doctrine, though he did not invent it. Jackson believed that rotation in office was democratic — that no man should hold a government job long enough to treat it as property. Better to cycle through ordinary citizens than to build a permanent class of government workers. This argument had genuine appeal. It was anti-aristocratic. It said: these jobs belong to the public, not to a professional class.

But the spoils system had corrosive effects. Jobs went to people who had earned them through political work, not through relevant qualifications. An experienced consul in a foreign posting might lose his job because the incoming administration wanted to reward someone else. Departments turned over every four years, losing institutional memory. And because loyalty, not competence, was the path to advancement, corruption became endemic. The Grant administration in the 1870s was rocked by a series of scandals — the Crédit Mobilier affair, the Whiskey Ring, the Indian Ring — that flowed directly from a government filled with men whose primary qualification was party loyalty.

By the time Guiteau shot Garfield, reform was inevitable. The Pendleton Act of 1883 established three foundational rules: federal employees could not be fired solely for political reasons; competitive examinations would determine hiring for covered positions; and an independent Civil Service Commission would enforce the system and develop the exams.

The transformation was gradual. In 1883, only about 10 percent of federal positions fell under civil service protections. But coverage expanded over the following decades. By the mid-twentieth century, roughly 85 percent of federal jobs were merit-based. The career federal employee — hired for qualifications, protected from political removal, building expertise over years in one domain — had become the norm rather than the exception.

What the merit system solved: incompetence and corruption rooted in political loyalty.

What it created: a class of unelected officials with substantial job security and discretionary authority, answerable only loosely to the elected government above them.

## The Control Problem

Here is the tension in its starkest form.

A career meteorologist at the National Weather Service has been in her position for fifteen years. She has a deep understanding of atmospheric dynamics, of the specific failure modes of weather forecasting in the central plains, of how to communicate uncertainty to the public in ways that prompt appropriate response. She cannot be fired because a new administration dislikes her union affiliation or her previous public statements about climate science.

In the spring of 2025, the Trump administration imposed hiring freezes and dismissed probationary employees across federal agencies, including the National Weather Service. Some field offices saw vacancy rates of 40 percent. The practical consequence was that critical weather balloons could not be launched, forecasters were stretched thin, and the precision of severe weather warnings deteriorated entering tornado season. The career meteorologists believed this endangered public safety. They continued to work. They could not organize politically to oppose the cuts — the Hatch Act of 1939, which prohibits federal employees from engaging in political campaigns, constrained their ability to mobilize public pressure on their own behalf.

This is the structure of the problem in miniature. The merit system protected the meteorologists from being removed because of their politics or their union membership. It also prevented them from fighting back through political means. The administration had the authority to cut budgets and freeze hiring. The meteorologists had no formal recourse except to continue doing their jobs as best they could with diminished resources.

Three mechanisms exist to address the control problem, and none of them fully works.

**Congressional oversight** operates through hearings, budgets, and the Government Accountability Office. Hearings are the most visible tool — an agency head is summoned, questioned, sometimes confronted. But hearings are episodic and often more theater than substance. Agency officials are trained to answer questions without fully answering them. The GAO produces hundreds of reports per year that are detailed, critical, and largely unread by the public. The budget is Congress's sharpest tool: starve an agency of funds and it cannot pursue agendas Congress dislikes. But budget cuts are blunt. They harm the agency's mission, which often means harming the public it serves. And Congress has 535 members, hundreds of agencies to oversee, and a thousand other priorities competing for attention. Oversight is intense when scandal erupts. It is minimal when everything is quietly going wrong.

**Presidential oversight** is more direct. The president appoints the heads of all cabinet departments and many independent agencies. These appointees serve at the president's pleasure and can be removed at will. The Office of Management and Budget, which sits inside the Executive Office of the President, reviews proposed regulations before they are finalized and can send them back for revision. A president who wants an agency to move in a particular direction can install a secretary aligned with that direction and use the OMB to filter out regulations that conflict with administration priorities.

But presidential control has limits. A cabinet secretary oversees an agency with thousands of employees. She can set priorities and shift budgets, but she cannot control every decision made by career officials below her. And most federal employees are protected from being fired because the president disagrees with their work. Presidential appointees are the top layer — the direction-setters. Career officials are the institution itself, and they persist through changes of administration, carrying the institutional culture and expertise forward regardless of who is nominally in charge.

**Citizen oversight** provides a third avenue, more diffuse but potentially reaching what the other two miss. The Freedom of Information Act of 1966 allows citizens and journalists to request records from federal agencies. The Government in Sunshine Act of 1976 requires multi-headed agencies to hold meetings in public. When agencies propose regulations, they must publish the proposals and accept public comment. These mechanisms have produced real accountability — investigative journalists have used FOIA to expose agency misconduct, public comment periods have genuinely shaped the content of regulations.

But citizen oversight is unequal. Filing a useful FOIA request, parsing a notice of proposed rulemaking, submitting an informed comment — these require time, expertise, and sometimes legal knowledge. Organized groups with dedicated staff exercise these rights effectively. Individual citizens participate much less. The formal openness of the system obscures its practical inaccessibility.

## What the Machinery Produces

Put the three control mechanisms together and you get something that is neither adequate democratic accountability nor a rogue fourth branch running itself without constraint. You get a patchwork.

Congress oversees episodically and with limited attention. The president oversees through appointment and budget, but cannot control the career workforce below the appointee layer. Citizens oversee when they have the resources and expertise to do so, which is unevenly distributed. Whistleblowers can expose misconduct, but face career risk in doing so and institutional resistance from the agencies they expose.

The practical result is that most agencies, most of the time, operate in a zone of managed autonomy. They have missions established by statute, resources allocated by Congress, and leadership appointed by the president. Within those constraints, career officials exercise substantial discretion — deciding how to implement vague statutory mandates, how to allocate inspection resources, how to prioritize enforcement actions, how to write regulations that fill in the gaps legislation leaves open. This discretion is not lawless. It is constrained by statute, by judicial review, by OMB review, by oversight from the relevant congressional committees. But it is discretion nonetheless.

Whether this arrangement is good or bad depends on what you think government is for. If you believe that elected officials should be able to implement their agendas without friction from career bureaucrats who prefer the old way, the merit system looks like an obstacle. If you believe that technical expertise and institutional continuity produce better outcomes than political loyalty, the merit system looks like a necessary protection against chaos and corruption.

Both beliefs contain truth. The spoils system was genuinely corrupt and genuinely incompetent. The merit system was necessary. But it created its own accountability gap, and that gap is real. A career official at the EPA who believes that her agency's core mission is environmental protection will implement regulations with a particular kind of intensity, regardless of whether the current administration shares that priority. A career official at the Defense Department who has spent decades working on a particular weapons program will advocate for that program even when the secretary wants to cut it. Neither of these is corruption in the conventional sense. Both are expressions of institutional interest and professional identity that sit in tension with democratic control.

The ratchet runs in both directions. Crisis and changing politics can push agencies in directions their career employees resist. Career employees can push back, formally and informally, slowing implementation, raising procedural obstacles, appealing to congressional oversight committees. The result is a permanent negotiation between the elected government and the permanent government — a negotiation that has no clean resolution because neither side is entirely right.

The bureaucracy is not the enemy of democracy. It is democracy's necessary instrument for doing things too complex for elected officials to do themselves. But it is an instrument that has its own interests, its own culture, and its own resistance to direction. Understanding that is the beginning of thinking clearly about what government can and cannot do.

## What Would Change My Mind

If Congress developed an oversight mechanism that provided continuous monitoring without politicizing the bureaucracy — something beyond the episodic hearing and the blunt budget cut — and if that mechanism demonstrably improved agency responsiveness without turning career officials into partisan appointees, I would update toward thinking the accountability problem is more tractable than this chapter suggests. I have not seen evidence that such a mechanism exists or has been tried successfully.

## Still Puzzling

The Hatch Act prevents federal employees from engaging in political campaigns. This is meant to keep the bureaucracy from being captured by one party. But it also prevents bureaucrats from organizing politically to defend their agencies and missions. The meteorologists in 2025 could not campaign against the hiring freeze that was gutting their capacity to protect public safety. It is not clear to me whether the Hatch Act's restraint on political activity does more to protect the merit system than it does to disable the bureaucracy's ability to defend itself against political attack. I do not have a clean answer.

## LLM Exercises

**1. Weber's ideal type, stress-tested.**
Weber's model of the ideal bureaucracy assumes that written rules, hierarchy, and specialization produce both competence and fairness. Take one of those three features and argue that it can produce the opposite effect under realistic conditions. When does relying on written rules produce rigidity rather than fairness? When does hierarchy produce unaccountability rather than clear authority? When does specialization produce tunnel vision rather than expertise? Use a specific government agency or program as your example.

**2. Spoils versus merit, specific trade-offs.**
The spoils system made bureaucrats accountable to the winning party. The merit system made them accountable to professional standards and legal protections. Using the 2025 National Weather Service cuts as your case, explain what would have happened under a spoils system instead. Who would have been fired, who would have been hired, and what would the practical effect on weather forecasting have been? Then explain whether you think the spoils outcome would have been better or worse than what actually happened under the merit system. Be specific about the trade-offs.

**3. The fourth-branch problem.**
The Constitution creates three branches of government. The bureaucracy is not one of them, but it exercises powers that look like all three: it writes regulations (legislative), implements programs (executive), and adjudicates disputes (judicial). Using a specific agency — the EPA, the FCC, the FDA, or another of your choice — describe one example of each of these three types of power. Then argue: is this concentration of functions in a non-elected body a problem that needs to be fixed, or a practical necessity that the three-branch framework cannot handle alone? What would "fixing" it actually require?

**4. Congressional oversight, the limits.**
The text argues that congressional oversight is episodic and resource-limited. Using the IRS targeting scandal of 2013 as your case, trace what actually happened: what did Congress do, what did it accomplish, and what changed as a result? Then evaluate: did oversight work in this case? If not, what would have made it work? If yes, why is the standard of "a scandal explodes" an inadequate substitute for continuous oversight?

**5. Designing accountability.**
You are asked to design a single oversight mechanism for a federal agency of your choice that (a) does not politicize the bureaucracy by making career employees responsive to party loyalty, (b) does not rely on the episodic scandal-driven model of congressional oversight, and (c) is practically implementable given real resource constraints. What do you build? What specific problem does it solve that current mechanisms do not? What does it sacrifice or risk? Be precise about the mechanism — not just the goal.
