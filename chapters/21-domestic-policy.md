# Chapter 21 — Domestic Policy
*Why Most Problems Stay Broken*

---

Here is a thing that has been true since the 1980s.

Social Security, America's largest domestic program, will run out of money sometime around 2033 if nothing changes. Not speculation — the program's own actuaries publish this every year. The problem is understood. The solutions are understood. You can raise the payroll tax, raise the retirement age, cut benefits, or means-test so that wealthy retirees receive less. Any combination of these would fix the mathematics. None of them require new research or new legislation technology. The fix is sitting on the shelf.

And yet nothing has been done. No major Social Security reform has passed since 1983. Forty years of knowing exactly what is wrong and exactly how to fix it, and the system has not moved.

This is not incompetence. It is not corruption, exactly. It is the system working as it was designed to work — which produces outcomes that look, from the outside, like failure.

Understanding why requires a model of how policy actually moves.

---

## The Three Streams

In the 1980s, a policy theorist named John Kingdon noticed something that had always been true but that nobody had quite named. Policy does not happen because a problem gets identified, a solution gets designed, and the solution gets applied to the problem. That is not how it works. What actually happens is that three separate systems — Kingdon called them streams — operate mostly independently. Policy happens only when all three converge. Most of the time, they do not.

The first stream is **problems**: the set of things that people believe the government should fix. Not all real problems enter this stream. Disease was rampant in medieval Europe. Nobody thought of it as a problem requiring government action — they attributed it to God or bad air. The same disease, after germ theory, became a political problem demanding public health response. What changed was not the disease. What changed was how people understood it and who they thought was responsible for fixing it.

Problems enter the stream through crises (a pandemic forces public health onto the agenda), focusing events (a school shooting makes gun policy salient), new data (studies revealing lead in drinking water), or sustained advocacy (environmental groups hammering on climate for decades until it becomes impossible to ignore). The crucial point: not every real problem becomes a political problem. Only problems that gain enough visibility and champions get treated as things government is supposed to address.

The second stream is **policy**: the reservoir of proposals, designs, and solutions that specialists have developed, often with no knowledge of whether anyone will ever implement them. An economist proposes a carbon tax structure. A health policy researcher designs an insurance model. A civil engineer specifies a safer pipeline design. These proposals circulate in professional networks — journals, think tanks, universities, conferences. Most never move beyond specialists. But they are there. Waiting.

The crucial point about the policy stream: solutions are developed mostly independently of political opportunity. A researcher spends years on a proposal that may never see a congressional hearing. But when a problem suddenly becomes urgent, the policy stream can supply options immediately. The solutions for the Affordable Care Act were not invented in 2009. They had been sitting in the policy stream since the 1990s, some since the 1970s. The Massachusetts system that served as a model had been running since 2006. What happened in 2009 was not invention. It was selection from an existing shelf.

The third stream is **politics**: the shifting landscape of what is politically possible. Which party controls Congress? What does the public care about right now? What is the president willing to spend capital on? What have recent elections signaled about voter priorities? This stream moves on its own logic — the logic of elections, of party ideology, of who donated to whom, of what issue caught fire last week on social media.

Here is the mechanism: policy moves when all three streams align. The Affordable Care Act is the clearest modern example. The problem stream: 45 million uninsured Americans and rising costs. The policy stream: health insurance models that had been developed, tested, and refined for two decades. The political stream: Democrats controlled both chambers after 2008, and Obama had made health care a campaign priority. All three converged. Major legislation passed.

But notice what that sentence obscures. The solutions in the ACA had been waiting seventeen years since Hillary Clinton's failed 1993 effort. The political stream aligned in 2009, not because the problem was suddenly worse — it had been building for decades — but because an election produced unified Democratic control of government. The problem and the policy had been ready. The politics was the bottleneck.

Now consider climate change. The problem stream has been building for thirty years. The science is clear and increasingly severe. The policy stream is rich: carbon pricing, renewable energy subsidies, efficiency standards, international agreements. Tested versions exist in other countries. Every piece of the technical apparatus is ready.

The political stream has not aligned. Senators from coal-dependent states oppose carbon pricing. Republicans in Congress block major climate legislation consistently. Even when Democrats control Congress, the margins are narrow and the disagreements are real. The problem exists. The solutions exist. The politics hasn't moved enough to connect them.

This is not failure in the ordinary sense. It is the system producing its normal output.

## Why the Safety Net Doesn't Change

The three-streams model explains why policy is hard to start. A separate but related mechanism explains why it is even harder to change once it exists.

Social Security was created in 1935 as a response to old-age poverty during the Depression. The design was elegant: workers pay a payroll tax, accumulate credits, and receive a monthly pension upon retirement. Ida Mae Fuller, who became famous as the first Social Security beneficiary, paid into the system for three years and collected benefits for thirty-five. Over her lifetime she received roughly eight times what she paid in. This was not an accident — the program was designed to be generous to early enrollees, with the expectation that later generations of workers would fund the system.

That generosity was sustainable in 1940, when sixteen workers supported every retiree. It is not sustainable in 2024, when 2.8 workers support every retiree. The demographic math changed because birth rates fell and life expectancy rose. Both trends were predictable. Neither was accounted for in the original design.

Medicare, created in 1965, faces a similar problem compounded by a second one. Not only are more people reaching 65, but health care costs rise faster than general inflation. A treatment that cost $5,000 in 2000 costs $20,000 today. The program cannot control medical cost growth the way it can control the number of beneficiaries.

The solutions to both problems are straightforward to specify. For Social Security: raise the payroll tax, raise the retirement age, reduce benefits for higher earners, or some combination. For Medicare: raise premiums, increase the eligibility age, or aggressively negotiate drug and treatment prices. None of these require new technology. None require complex analysis. The actuaries have modeled all of them.

What each requires is harming a specific, powerful constituency. Raising the retirement age harms workers in physically demanding jobs who genuinely cannot work longer. Cutting benefits harms current and near-future retirees who have planned their finances around promised levels. Means-testing harms the middle class, who paid into the system for forty years and now find they don't qualify. The AARP, which represents 38 million members, opposes any benefit cuts. The labor movement opposes raising the retirement age. Every solution has organized opponents.

This creates what political scientists call a policy trap: the cost of inaction accumulates slowly (the trust funds won't be empty until 2033, then 2031), while the cost of action is immediate and politically concentrated. Politicians who support reform lose elections. Politicians who oppose it retain support. The individual incentive for every politician is to defer reform to a later Congress.

Medicaid is different in a revealing way. Unlike Social Security and Medicare, Medicaid is means-tested — you only qualify if your income is below a threshold. It serves the poorest Americans: children in poverty, people with disabilities, pregnant women. And unlike Social Security and Medicare, Medicaid has weak political protection. It is regularly cut in state budget crises. It was excluded from the expansion in twelve states after the ACA. Politicians who touch Social Security face electoral retribution from millions of middle-class voters who collect it or expect to. Politicians who cut Medicaid face less organized opposition from people who are politically disorganized by virtue of being poor.

The lesson cuts against comfortable assumptions. Americans often talk about welfare as if it means programs for the poor. But the most expensive, most politically protected programs — Social Security and Medicare — primarily serve the middle class and elderly, not the poor. The programs explicitly designed for the poor have the weakest protection. Universal entitlements ("you paid in, you deserve this") build constituencies that prevent cuts. Means-tested benefits ("you're poor so you need this") build constituencies that can't defend themselves.

## Economic Policy and Why It Moves on Ideology

When the 2008 financial crisis hit, unemployment rose from 5 percent to 10 percent. The question was what government should do.

Keynesian economists argued that recessions happen when consumers and businesses stop spending. Demand collapses. Businesses lay off workers. Workers have less income. Demand collapses further. You are in a negative feedback loop. The only way to break it: government injects spending. Stimulus checks to individuals, infrastructure projects, direct transfers — anything that replaces collapsed private demand with public demand. Workers get hired to build the bridge, spend their wages at restaurants, the restaurant hires more staff, the loop reverses.

Supply-side economists disagreed. The problem is not insufficient demand but insufficient productive capacity. The economy has accumulated distortions — over-regulation, high taxes on investment, capital misallocated to unproductive uses. The solution is to remove these distortions: cut taxes on business and investment, reduce regulation, let capital flow to productive uses. Don't try to prop up consumption; unleash production.

Both theories have genuine evidence behind them in particular contexts. After a major contraction with high unemployment and idle capacity, stimulus spending really does boost output and employment — the government is deploying resources that were otherwise sitting unused. In an economy already at full employment, more spending mostly causes inflation rather than output gains, because there's no idle capacity to deploy.

The problem is that which theory gets applied doesn't depend primarily on which fits current conditions. It depends on which party controls government. Republicans apply supply-side theory. Democrats apply Keynesian theory. Neither switches based on whether the economy is currently in recession or boom, in inflation or deflation. The political stream drives the choice. The evidence informs the rhetoric after the fact.

This produced one instructive case. In 2021, with the economy recovering from COVID shutdowns, Congress passed $1.9 trillion in stimulus. The political conditions were clear: COVID was a crisis, Democrats controlled Congress, public opinion supported relief. The stimulus passed quickly. But the economic conditions were different from 2008. The economy had already bottomed out and was recovering. Firms were hiring. Supply chains were disrupted. There was less idle capacity to deploy. Pumping $1.9 trillion into an economy with constrained supply pushed prices up rather than output up. Inflation rose to 7 percent.

The same tool, Keynesian stimulus, works when conditions match its assumptions and fails when they don't. The 2021 stimulus was calibrated to the crisis conditions of 2020, not the recovering conditions of 2021. But the political stream had aligned for stimulus, so stimulus it was.

The monetary system operates on a slightly different logic. The Federal Reserve sets interest rates. High rates make borrowing expensive, which reduces spending and investment, which slows the economy and tames inflation. Low rates make borrowing cheap, which increases spending, which stimulates the economy and risks inflation. The Fed is supposed to be politically independent — its governors serve long terms, cannot be fired for political reasons, and make decisions by committee.

In practice, independence is partial. The Fed Chair and governors are appointed by the president and confirmed by the Senate. The appointment process is explicitly political. A president who wants low interest rates appoints a chair who favors low rates. A president who prioritizes inflation control appoints a hawk. The technical apparatus insulates daily decisions from political command, but the people making those decisions were chosen through a political process.

The fiscal side — taxing and spending — faces the same ideological lock-in, made worse by the deficit. The federal government has run a deficit every year since 2001. Annual deficits accumulate into debt; total federal debt reached $34 trillion in 2024. This is mathematically unsustainable over long time horizons: at some point, debt service consumes enough of the budget that borrowing costs accelerate the problem.

Balancing the budget requires either cutting popular spending or raising taxes. Cutting popular spending means Social Security, Medicare, defense — which have the constituencies already discussed. Raising taxes means asking voters to pay more, which is the fastest way to lose an election. Both parties promise fiscal responsibility and both run deficits. The three-streams logic explains this exactly: the problem is clear, the solutions are clear, but the political stream blocks both. So deficits persist.

## What the Machinery Produces

Put these mechanisms together and you get a system with specific properties.

Policy moves rarely, but when it moves, it moves fast. The conditions for alignment are unusual, but when they arrive — crisis, political opportunity, ready solutions — legislation can pass within months. Social Security took six months from proposal to passage in 1935. The ACA took about a year of intense negotiation. The speed, when movement happens, comes from the fact that the solutions were already developed. The policy stream had been waiting.

Once policy passes, it calcifies. The constituency that benefits from the program acquires an interest in its continuation. Social Security has existed for ninety years. Medicare for sixty. Nobody talks seriously about abolishing them because tens of millions of people depend on them. The original problem that motivated them — old-age poverty in the Depression — has been largely solved by the programs themselves. But the programs continue because their beneficiaries are powerful, organized, and vote.

Economic policy oscillates with ideology rather than tracking with evidence. The party in power applies its preferred economic theory. When that theory fits conditions, policy works. When it doesn't fit, it creates new problems. The Fed provides some stabilizing correction at the margins, but its independence is partial and its tools limited.

The deepest truth in all of this is not cynical. The system produces what it was designed to produce: stability and inertia, with occasional bursts of change when unusual conditions align. The framers built friction into the system because they feared rapid change. The safety net was built to be durable because the people who depended on it needed to be able to count on it. The ideological lock-in of economic policy reflects the fact that voters elect parties with coherent philosophies, not technocratic committees.

The problem is that stability and inertia are fine for problems that can wait, and disastrous for problems that cannot. Climate change cannot wait. Social Security insolvency in 2033 cannot wait indefinitely. But the system's response time is calibrated to political alignment, not to the urgency of underlying problems. The streams move at their own pace. They don't accelerate because an actuary publishes a report.

## What Would Change My Mind

Evidence that the three streams are not independent — that problems automatically attract solutions, or that political conditions reliably respond to problem severity — would require revising the central argument here. Every policy case I have examined shows the streams as largely independent, with alignment driven more by accident and political timing than by rational response to need. But I have not examined all cases, and the model might overstate independence at the expense of feedback effects.

## Still Puzzling

I do not understand what causes the political stream to shift at specific moments. Why did health care reform happen in 2010 but not in 1993, despite the problem being roughly similar? The political conditions were also similar — Democratic president, Democratic Congress. Something was different. What is the mechanism by which a focusing event or a crisis actually changes the possibility set, rather than just the visibility of the problem?

## LLM Exercises

**1. Find the three streams.**
Pick a current policy debate: gun control, climate legislation, student loan forgiveness, immigration reform, or one of your own choosing. Map all three streams explicitly: what is the evidence that constitutes the problem, what specific policy proposals exist in the policy stream, and what is the current state of the political stream. Then assess: is policy movement likely in the next two years? Which stream is the bottleneck, and what would have to change for it to unblock?

**2. The Social Security trap.**
The chapter argues that Social Security reform is blocked despite clear mathematical urgency and clear solutions. Map the specific constituencies that oppose each solution: who loses from raising the retirement age, who loses from means-testing, who loses from raising the payroll tax cap, who loses from benefit cuts. Then argue: is there any solution that does not create a powerful opposing constituency? If not, what does that imply about whether the system can reform itself before insolvency?

**3. Keynesian vs. supply-side, context-dependent.**
The chapter argues that both economic theories have evidence supporting them in specific contexts. Identify the specific economic conditions under which Keynesian stimulus is likely to work (output increases without significant inflation) and the specific conditions under which it is likely to backfire (inflation without significant output gains). Then evaluate the 2021 American Rescue Plan: which conditions were present in early 2021? Does the subsequent inflation confirm or complicate the chapter's argument that policy was miscalibrated to the wrong conditions?

**4. Universal vs. means-tested.**
Social Security and Medicare are universal entitlements with strong political protection. Medicaid is means-tested and vulnerable to cuts. The chapter argues this pattern is not accidental — it reflects the different political constituencies each type of program builds. Test this argument against a counterexample: find a means-tested program that has strong political protection (SNAP, the Earned Income Tax Credit, or another of your choice) and explain whether its durability confirms or complicates the chapter's claim. What features of a means-tested program can generate political support comparable to a universal program?

**5. The alignment problem.**
The three-streams model implies that policy is largely reactive — it moves when conditions align, not when problems are most urgent. Argue the strongest case that this is actually a good design feature, not a bug. What would a system look like that responded to problems in proportion to their urgency? What dangers would such a system create? Then argue the countercase: what is lost when a system's response time is calibrated to political alignment rather than problem severity? Use Social Security solvency or climate change as your example.
