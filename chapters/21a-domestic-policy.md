# Chapter 21A — Domestic Policy: The Pipeline Question
*Why some problems get solved and most don't.*

---

August 2016. A 1,200-mile pipeline was under construction from North Dakota through four states to Illinois. The Standing Rock Sioux Tribe sued the Army Corps of Engineers, arguing the pipeline threatened tribal sacred sites and federally protected waters. Environmental lawsuits file constantly. This one did not go unnoticed.

Social media amplified the protests. National news covered the standoff. Environmental groups with resources and mainstream credibility joined. The Obama administration halted the pipeline pending further review. Months later, a new president reversed the decision. The pipeline proceeded.

The Standing Rock story is not about whether the pipeline was right or wrong. It is about something more structural: why did this particular problem, at this particular moment, produce this particular response? And why did the same underlying issues — pipeline safety, tribal sovereignty, water protection — fail to produce federal action for decades before 2016, and again after 2017?

The answer is that policy is not made by identifying problems and rationally selecting the best solutions. It is made by three separate systems that operate mostly independently — problem recognition, solution development, and political opportunity — and only occasionally align. When they do, policy moves. Most of the time, they don't.

---

## The Three Streams

The political scientist John Kingdon spent years studying how policy actually happens and arrived at a framework that remains the clearest description of the machinery: three streams, flowing mostly in parallel, converging rarely.

The first stream is problems. A problem, in the policy sense, is not just a condition that causes suffering. It is a condition that enough people believe *government should fix*. Disease was rampant in medieval Europe. Nobody framed it as a problem requiring government action — they attributed it to God, or bad air, or fate. Once germ theory arrived, the same disease became a political problem demanding public health infrastructure. The problem did not change. The frame changed.

Problems enter the stream through several mechanisms. A crisis makes something impossible to ignore — a pandemic kills millions, suddenly public health is urgent. A focusing event concentrates attention — a school shooting makes gun policy salient for weeks. New data reveals something previously hidden — studies showing lead in drinking water. An advocacy group makes noise consistently until people pay attention — environmental groups hammering on climate for decades. The mechanism matters because it determines how long the problem stays visible. Crises produce brief windows. Sustained advocacy produces slower but more durable openings.

The second stream is policies. In parallel — and largely disconnected from whether anyone in power is paying attention — specialists and researchers develop solutions. An economist proposes a new tax structure. A health policy researcher designs an insurance model. A civil engineer invents a safer pipeline system. These proposals live in professional networks: conferences, journals, think tanks, university research centers. Most never move beyond specialists. They exist as technically feasible solutions waiting for a problem to make them relevant.

This is the part that surprises people: solutions are usually developed before the problems they solve become politically visible. The Affordable Care Act was not invented in 2009. Multiple insurance models had been proposed, analyzed, and tested for decades. Massachusetts had implemented a version in 2006. When the political moment arrived, the policy stream had options sitting on the shelf. What aligned was not problem plus solution — it was problem plus solution plus politics.

The third stream is politics. Political conditions shift: elections change who holds power, ideology changes what politicians believe government should do, public opinion changes what voters will support. A new president takes office with a different philosophy. A party gains control of Congress. A major disaster shifts public attention and tolerance for government action. The political stream is largely beyond any individual's control. It responds to events, accidents, and election timing — not to the logical urgency of problems.

Now here is the mechanism that makes the whole system tick: policy moves only when all three streams align simultaneously. A problem without political will is a complaint. A solution without a visible problem is academic. Political will without a solution on the shelf gets you symbolic gestures that don't last. The Standing Rock protests aligned all three: a problem that had become nationally visible, policy frameworks that had existed for years, and an administration with political reasons to respond. When the administration changed, the political stream shifted, and the alignment dissolved.

This is not cynical. It is structural. Even thoughtful, honest politicians operate in a system where alignment is rare. A senator who cares deeply about climate change cannot unilaterally create political will. A public health researcher cannot force a problem into visibility if the public is not paying attention. The system is not designed to solve the most urgent problems. It is designed to solve problems when conditions happen to align.

## The Safety Net and Its Mathematics

Social Security was born from a problem stream so large it was impossible to ignore. In 1929, the Depression hit. Unemployment reached 25 percent. Families lost everything. Elderly people who had worked their whole lives lost their savings and their capacity to work. Suicides among the elderly spiked. The problem was undeniable.

The policy stream had been developing for years. Progressive reformers had designed old-age pension systems in the 1920s. European countries had implemented them. In 1934, Roosevelt assembled a committee to design a U.S. version. Within six months they had a framework: a payroll tax on workers and employers, with monthly payments to retirees. The system was designed without knowing if politicians would pass it — it was a policy waiting on the shelf.

The political stream: Roosevelt had won a landslide in 1932. Democrats controlled Congress. The Depression made government action not just acceptable but demanded. Congress passed Social Security in 1935, weeks after Roosevelt proposed it.

Notice the sequence: the problem did not create the solution. The solution existed. The problem made politicians willing to consider it. When all three streams aligned, policy moved fast.

Social Security's machinery is worth understanding precisely, because it explains why the program is both politically invincible and mathematically fragile. Workers pay 6.2 percent of wages into the program; employers pay a matching 6.2 percent. Upon reaching retirement age — currently 67 for most workers — recipients receive a monthly pension based on lifetime earnings. It is called an entitlement because anyone meeting the eligibility conditions is entitled to the benefit. No needs test. No approval process. You qualify, you get the check.

In 1950, there were 16 workers for every retiree. The mathematics were comfortable. In 2020, there were 2.8 workers per retiree. By 2030, closer to 2.3. Fewer workers supporting more retirees, for longer, because people live longer. The Social Security trust fund, by actuarial projection, runs out around 2033 — after which incoming payroll taxes cover only about 80 percent of promised benefits.

The solutions are not mysterious. Raise the retirement age. Remove the payroll tax cap, which currently exempts earnings above $168,000 — meaning a surgeon and a billionaire pay the same dollar amount. Cut benefits for wealthy retirees through means-testing. Each solution hurts a politically powerful constituency. Raise the retirement age and you tell people who have done physical labor for 40 years to keep working. Remove the cap and you raise taxes on high earners. Means-test and you tell the middle class they paid into a system that will not pay them back.

The political stream has never aligned for Social Security reform because every solution has opponents with enough power to block it. The problem stream is clear. The policy stream is rich. The political stream is frozen. The result: a program with clear fiscal problems and no political path to fixing them, because the pain of any solution is immediate and the crisis is a decade away.

Medicare, created in 1965 for people 65 and older, has the same structure and a worse fiscal trajectory. Workers pay 2.9 percent of wages into the program. Upon reaching 65, they receive health insurance. Unlike Social Security, Medicare faces two compounding crises: the demographic problem (more people reaching 65) and medical inflation (health care costs rise faster than general inflation). A treatment that cost $5,000 in 2000 might cost $20,000 today. The hospital insurance trust fund is projected insolvent in 2031 — before Social Security.

Medicaid is different in every important way. Not an entitlement but a means-tested program: you qualify only if your income falls below a threshold set by each state. The federal government covers roughly 60 percent of costs; states cover the rest. Because it is needs-based rather than universal, it has less political protection. Politicians denounce Medicaid as "welfare" while carefully defending Social Security and Medicare — programs that pay benefits to millionaires — as "insurance you paid into." The political logic is revealing: Americans support social insurance but resent means-tested welfare. Universal programs survive. Targeted programs stay vulnerable.

In 2005, George W. Bush had won reelection with a Republican Congress and proposed privatizing Social Security — letting workers invest their payroll taxes in personal accounts. The problem stream was clear. The policy stream had multiple privatization models ready. But the political stream collapsed. The AARP opposed it. Labor unions opposed it. Moderate Republicans in competitive districts worried about the risk. Bush's proposal went nowhere. No major Social Security reform has passed since 1983, despite 40 years of warning that the mathematics require adjustment.

## Economic Policy and the Ideology Problem

In fall 2008, Lehman Brothers collapsed. Credit markets froze. Unemployment began climbing toward 10 percent. Two economic philosophies clashed on what government should do.

Keynesian economists argued: recession happens because consumers and businesses stop spending. Demand collapses. Without demand, businesses lay off workers. Without workers' income, demand collapses further. You are in a negative feedback loop. The only way to break it is for government to spend — inject demand into the economy through stimulus payments, public works, infrastructure. Increased hiring leads to workers spending their wages, demand recovers, the recession ends.

Supply-side economists argued: the problem is not lack of demand but structural inefficiency preventing productive investment. Regulations burden businesses. Taxes discourage capital formation. The solution is to cut taxes, remove regulations, and let capital flow to productive uses. Stop propping up consumption and unleash production.

The Obama administration chose Keynesianism. Congress passed $787 billion in stimulus. Most economists who have studied it conclude it prevented a worse recession and created 2 to 3 million jobs. Critics argued the jobs were temporary and left unsustainable debt. The debate has not resolved.

Here is what the debate reveals: economic policy is not decided primarily by evidence. It is decided by which economic theory is politically ascendant at the moment alignment occurs. Republicans apply supply-side economics regardless of whether the economy is in recession or growth. Democrats apply Keynesian economics regardless of conditions. Both frameworks have evidence supporting them in some contexts and contradicting them in others. The political stream does not respond to which theory fits current conditions. It responds to ideology.

The mechanism for taxing illustrates this clearly. A progressive tax structure — higher rates on higher incomes — has a specific economic logic: poor and middle-class people spend most of their income, so tax cuts for them increase demand directly. Wealthy people save most of their income, so tax cuts for them are less stimulative. Progressive taxation funds spending on programs that boost consumption and finances it by taxing those least likely to spend anyway.

A regressive tax structure — the same rate regardless of income, or consumption taxes like sales taxes — has a different logic: if you want more of something, subsidize it; if you want less of something, tax it. If you want investment, tax consumption and reduce taxes on capital gains and corporate profits. This incentivizes investment, increases productive capacity, and grows the economy.

Both have internal logic. Both have empirical support in some conditions. What determines which one governs policy is not an economist's recommendation but the political stream: who holds Congress, what ideology is ascendant, which theory powerful donors prefer.

The Federal Reserve is supposed to be insulated from this dynamic. The Fed controls interest rates and the money supply, operating with legal independence from Congress and the president. Raise rates and you make borrowing expensive, reducing spending and inflation. Lower rates and you make borrowing cheap, increasing spending and employment. The Fed's dual mandate — maximum employment and stable prices — can pull in opposite directions, and the Fed has to choose which goal to prioritize.

But "independent" does not mean apolitical. The Fed chair is appointed by the president and confirmed by the Senate. The choice of chair is a political decision that determines the institution's direction for years. An inflation-hawk chair raises rates aggressively and accepts higher unemployment to control prices. An employment-focused chair holds rates low and accepts inflation risk to protect jobs. The decision of who to appoint — and when, in the electoral cycle — is intensely political even when the institution's operations are not.

## The Machine as a Whole

Three things now fit together.

Policy requires alignment of three streams that operate mostly independently. When all three converge — visible problem, available solution, political opening — policy moves fast. The rest of the time, urgent problems go unaddressed not because politicians are corrupt or stupid but because the system is designed to make alignment rare. The Standing Rock protests aligned briefly, then the alignment dissolved. Social Security reform has never aligned because every solution hurts a constituency with veto power. Climate policy has not aligned because the political stream has not opened despite decades of problem visibility and a rich policy stream.

Once policies are enacted, they entrench. Social Security and Medicare persist because they serve constituencies with political power. The programs have mathematical problems clear since the 1980s and have not been reformed in 40 years. Inertia is the default.

Economic policy follows ideology more than evidence. Both major economic frameworks have evidence supporting them in specific conditions. But the political stream does not respond to conditions — it responds to which philosophy is politically dominant. This produces policy mismatches: stimulus in a growing economy generates inflation, austerity in a recession deepens it. The 2021 COVID stimulus, applied to an economy that had already bottomed out and was recovering, contributed to the inflation surge of 2022. The same tool that worked in 2008 produced different results in different conditions, applied with the same ideology.

Understanding this machinery does not produce cynicism — it produces strategy. If you know that policy moves when three streams align, you can work to open them. If you know that entrenched programs are hard to reform, you can focus energy on windows when political alignment briefly occurs. If you know that economic policy follows ideology, you can predict what policies will emerge from any given political configuration without needing to wait and see.

The policy machine is not broken. It is doing exactly what it was designed to do: make large changes hard, protect existing arrangements, and require broad alignment before anything major moves. Whether that design serves the current moment is the question this chapter raises without answering.

## LLM Exercises

**Exercise 1 — Three streams in the wild**

Pick a current policy debate — climate, health care, housing costs, student debt, gun policy. Ask a language model: *"Map this policy issue onto the three-streams model. What is the current state of the problem stream — how visible is it and what is driving visibility? What is the policy stream — what solutions exist and how well tested are they? What is the political stream — who holds power and what are they willing to do? Are all three aligned right now? If not, which stream is blocked?"* Then push back: ask the model which stream, if it shifted, would most change the probability of policy action.

**Exercise 2 — Social Security arithmetic**

Ask the model: *"Walk me through the Social Security solvency problem precisely: what are the current worker-to-retiree ratios, when is the trust fund projected to run out, and what happens to benefits after that? Then give me every serious reform proposal that has been analyzed, with the estimated fiscal impact of each and the political constituency that opposes it."* Use the model's answer to map why reform has not happened despite 40 years of warning.

**Exercise 3 — Means-tested vs. universal**

Ask the model: *"Social Security and Medicare are universal entitlements. Medicaid is means-tested. Compare the political vulnerability of each program over the past 20 years — which has faced the deepest cuts, which has been most protected, and why? What does the pattern tell you about the political logic of universal vs. means-tested social programs in the United States?"*

**Exercise 4 — Ideology vs. evidence in economic policy**

Give the model this prompt: *"The 2009 stimulus was $787 billion applied to an economy in deep recession. The 2021 stimulus was $1.9 trillion applied to an economy that had bottomed out and was beginning to recover. Economists have argued the 2021 stimulus was too large for the conditions. If that's true, why did Congress pass it anyway? Use the three-streams model to explain the decision — specifically, what was the state of the political stream in early 2021 that made a large stimulus politically inevitable regardless of economic conditions?"*

**Exercise 5 — Designing an alignment**

Ask the model: *"Choose one major unsolved domestic policy problem — pick something where the problem stream is clear and the policy stream has developed solutions, but the political stream has not aligned. Design a political strategy for creating alignment. What kind of focusing event would open the political stream? What coalition would you need to build? What existing policy proposals would you pull off the shelf? Be specific about the sequence of steps."* The goal is to use the three-streams model as a practical tool for thinking about political strategy, not just description.

## What Would Change My Mind

The three-streams model assumes the streams are genuinely independent — that problems do not automatically generate solutions, and solutions do not automatically find political champions. If evidence showed systematic patterns connecting the streams — that certain kinds of problems reliably generate aligned political responses, or that certain solution types reliably find political homes — the model would need revision. I have seen individual cases where the streams seem more connected, but I have not seen evidence that the connection is systematic rather than occasional.

## Still Puzzling

What causes the political stream to open at specific moments remains genuinely unclear to me. Why did health care reform happen in 2010 but not in 1993 despite similar Democratic majorities and similar public support? Why did the political stream for climate policy not open during the 2009–2010 window when Democrats had large Congressional majorities and public concern about climate was high? The three-streams model names the phenomenon but does not fully explain the mechanism by which political windows open and close. That mechanism is what I most want to understand and do not.
