# Chapter 22 — American Foreign Policy: Who Decides and How the Decision Shapes the World
*The Constitution divided foreign power between the branch that moves fast and the branch that moves slow — and then the world stopped caring about the distinction.*

---

Here is the thing about the Cuban Missile Crisis that most accounts miss.

October 1962. A U-2 spy plane photographs Soviet nuclear missiles on the island of Cuba, ninety miles from Florida. The installations are clear enough that untrained eyes can read them. Within hours, Kennedy has military advisors urging air strikes, State Department diplomats urging restraint, and a world that could end in a week depending on what he decides next.

Legally, Kennedy could have ordered air strikes that morning. He is commander-in-chief. The military answers to him. The Constitution gives him that authority, and no one was going to stop him.

He didn't do it. He spent thirteen days consulting, deliberating, backchannel negotiating with the Soviet ambassador, briefing congressional leaders, holding the special committee meetings that became known as ExComm. When he finally ordered a naval blockade — neither war nor surrender, but pressure — it was a presidential act, but not a unilateral one. It had the weight of institutional counsel behind it.

Now here is the puzzle. Kennedy had enough authority to act alone and chose not to. The thirteen days worked — the Soviets withdrew the missiles, nuclear war was avoided. So was the consultation necessary? Did it make the outcome better, or did Kennedy get lucky despite the delay? And if he had been wrong — if the Soviets had called his bluff, if the blockade had failed, if a naval engagement had escalated — would anyone have said the thirteen days of deliberation were worth it?

This is not a rhetorical question. It is the actual design problem of American foreign policy: how do you give one person enough authority to respond to a crisis in hours while also ensuring that the response is wise, legitimate, and consistent with what the country actually wants? The Constitution's answer was to split the power — the president moves, Congress funds and ratifies — and hope that the tension produces good outcomes. Sometimes it does. Sometimes the tension produces paralysis. Sometimes the president moves so fast that the tension never activates at all.

Understanding how this works, and when it breaks, tells you more about American governance than almost anything else.

## How the Power is Split — and Why the Split Doesn't Work the Way It Looks

The Constitution divides foreign power in a way that seems balanced on paper and tilts heavily toward the executive in practice.

Article II makes the president commander-in-chief of the armed forces. It gives the president the power to negotiate treaties and to recognize foreign governments — to say, formally, whether another regime is legitimate. Article I gives Congress the power to declare war, to raise and support armies, and to regulate commerce with foreign nations. The Senate, acting alone, must ratify treaties by a two-thirds vote.

This looks like a real check. The president commands the military; Congress authorizes the wars. The president negotiates the treaties; the Senate ratifies them. Two branches, two roles, genuine shared power.

Here is the asymmetry. The president is one person who can decide in hours. Congress is 535 people who must deliberate, and deliberation takes days or weeks at minimum. If a crisis demands a response faster than Congress can deliberate — and most crises do — the president acts, and Congress decides afterward whether to fund, authorize, or condemn.

The War Powers Resolution of 1973, passed over Nixon's veto, was supposed to fix this. The impetus was Vietnam: fifty-eight thousand Americans had died in a war that Congress never formally declared, waged under an accumulating executive claim that Cold War strategy required presidential flexibility. The resolution imposed a rule: a president can use military force for sixty days without congressional approval. After sixty days, the president must get explicit authorization or withdraw.

What actually happened is that the resolution codified presidential power for exactly sixty days. Most military conflicts are decided within that window. The president acts, something happens, and by the time the sixty-day clock expires, troops are in the field and Congress faces the politically ruinous choice of defunding them. Nobody defunds troops in the field. The mechanism intended to check presidential power instead licensed it for exactly as long as needed to present Congress with a situation it couldn't practically reverse.

The treaty process has its own version of this problem. Presidents discovered early that they could bypass the two-thirds Senate ratification requirement by calling agreements "executive agreements" instead of treaties. The Iran Nuclear Agreement — a sweeping seven-country pact that would shape Middle Eastern affairs for a generation — went forward as an executive agreement. Obama chose that route precisely because a Senate two-thirds vote would have killed it. Republicans, who opposed the deal, wanted the treaty process so they could block it. The deal went forward, and when Trump took office, he reversed it unilaterally, because what one president signs as an executive agreement, the next can unsigned.

The constitutional design assumes that the tension between a fast-moving executive and a slow-moving legislature will produce the right balance. What it actually produces is a system where the president moves first and Congress decides later whether to fund, override, or quietly acquiesce. Most of the time, Congress acquiesces. Not because senators and representatives lack opinions about foreign policy. Because by the time Congress acts, the moment has passed — troops are deployed, the agreement is signed, the strike has happened — and opposing the president means opposing the action after the fact, which is both harder and costlier than opposing it before.

The deeper point: Congress is an institution designed for deliberation. The president is one person who can decide in hours. In a world where military technology moves at the speed of missiles, the institutional advantage lies with speed. Congress retains the theoretical veto but rarely uses it because by the time Congress acts, the moment is gone.

## Four Ways of Thinking About What "Right" Means

The constitutional question — who decides — is separate from the strategic question: what should we actually do? Four schools of thought have shaped American foreign policy, and understanding them explains why the U.S. does what it does, when it does it, and why the critics of any given policy are often also right.

**Isolationism** held American foreign policy for its first 125 years. George Washington's farewell address enshrined it: avoid entangling alliances. The reasoning was pragmatic — Europe was volatile, America was young and distant, the Atlantic was a moat. Stay out, trade freely, let them sort themselves out.

Isolationism collapsed after Pearl Harbor, and for good reason: the oceans turned out not to be moats. Global conflicts cross water. But the instinct never fully died. After the Soviet Union fell in 1991, a genuine question arose — why is the U.S. still the world's policeman? In 2016, it surfaced again as a political force. The honest appeal of isolationism is that it is cheaper. The honest cost is that in a connected world, you cannot wish yourself uninvolved. Other nations' crises arrive at your borders whether you want them to or not. You can choose not to shape them; you cannot choose whether they affect you.

**Liberal internationalism** is the opposite theory. Democracies tend not to fight each other. If you can spread democracy, build international institutions that facilitate dialogue, and establish norms of free trade, you reduce the likelihood of conflict. Woodrow Wilson was the canonical version: he believed that open diplomacy, freedom of the seas, free trade, and the League of Nations could remake international relations. The League failed — the U.S. Senate refused to join — but the idea survived. The United Nations, NATO, the World Trade Organization — all are expressions of liberal internationalism. The honest appeal: institutions and dialogue do reduce conflict when the major players share an interest in the status quo. The honest cost: liberal internationalism assumes everyone plays the same game. Iran, North Korea, and al-Qaeda don't accept the premises of the framework. If you're facing an opponent who rejects consultation and international law, being a liberal internationalist means you're slow while they act.

**Realism** says: assume the worst. Other nations act in their own interest. They will lie if it benefits them. There is no world government to enforce rules. The rational response is to maintain a strong military, negotiate from strength, and never rely on anyone else's goodwill. The Cuban Missile Crisis is a realist moment — Kennedy didn't trust Khrushchev to be reasonable out of kindness. He assumed Khrushchev would keep the missiles unless removing them cost less than keeping them. The blockade made keeping them more expensive. That's realism: honest about incentives, unsentimental about loyalty. The honest cost: realism is self-fulfilling. If you treat everyone as an enemy, everyone eventually acts accordingly. The Cold War was stable partly because both sides understood each other through a realist lens — but the constant military buildup, the proxy wars, and the nearness to nuclear catastrophe suggest that realism without restraint is exhausting and dangerous.

**Neoconservatism** emerged from a specific disillusionment: liberal internationalism had tried to convince South Vietnam to embrace democracy and capitalism. It failed. The communists won. The lesson neoconservatives drew was that you cannot simply persuade other nations to become democracies. You must be willing to impose it. Neoconservatism is realism with a missionary zeal: yes, other nations act in self-interest, but the U.S. is in a unique position to define what self-interest should be for others. Use overwhelming military power. Promote democracy not because nations will choose it willingly but because it is objectively better, and might makes right. The Iraq War was the definitive neoconservative venture. The honest appeal: clarity. You identify a problem, you have the tools, you use them. The honest cost: forcing your values on others is expensive and usually backfires. Iraq consumed American resources for twenty years, killed hundreds of thousands, and did not produce a stable democracy.

Here is what is worth noticing: every school captures something true. Isolationism is right that military overcommitment is costly. Liberal internationalism is right that institutions reduce conflict. Realism is right that strength matters and self-interest drives behavior. Neoconservatism is right that some threats cannot be negotiated away. And every school misses something. The practice of U.S. foreign policy has always been a hybrid — switching lenses as the problem changes — which makes it incoherent in the moment and comprehensible only in retrospect.

## When the Machinery Breaks

The foreign policy machine sits in a set of institutions: the president, the Secretary of State, the Secretary of Defense, the National Security Advisor, the CIA Director, Congress, and the international organizations — NATO, the UN — that provide forums for legitimacy. On paper, this is sensible. The executive branch proposes and implements. Congress approves money and major commitments. International organizations provide forums for diplomacy.

In practice, the machinery was built for a world that is changing faster than the machinery can adapt.

For two centuries, foreign policy meant relations between governments. France had interests, Britain had interests, the U.S. had interests. You negotiated, threatened, sometimes fought. The winner imposed terms. This model assumed that your enemy was a state — something with borders, a capital, a government, a military that wore uniforms and reported to a chain of command.

That model started breaking down in the late twentieth century. Al-Qaeda operated simultaneously from Afghanistan, Pakistan, Yemen, Somalia, and a dozen other countries. ISIS recruited fighters from around the world and held territory in Syria and Iraq while conducting attacks in Paris, Brussels, and San Bernardino. Terrorism is not a country you can invade and occupy. It is a tactic that spreads wherever failed states, extremist ideology, and access to weapons overlap. You cannot negotiate a surrender from a network. You cannot sign a peace treaty with an organization that doesn't recognize states as legitimate.

And terrorism is only the most visible of these transnational problems. Climate change is produced by every country on earth and threatens every country on earth — you cannot solve it by defeating one adversary, because the adversary is a physical process driven by collective behavior across a hundred nations. A pandemic spreads through the movement of people, ignoring borders and alliances alike. Trade flows are shaped by supply chains that touch dozens of countries; disrupting them with tariffs affects your own companies as much as your rivals'.

The machinery was built for the first kind of problem. It is poorly suited for the second.

When the U.S. pursued al-Qaeda after September 11, it used a patchwork approach: a military coalition (legitimacy-seeking) to invade Afghanistan, unilateral drone strikes (speed-seeking) in Pakistan and Yemen, special operations raids in countries where we operated without full permission from host governments, intelligence-sharing with allies, and financial pressure on terrorist networks. Each piece made sense as a response to a specific tactical problem. The whole was not a strategy so much as an accumulation of tactical responses.

This is what the machinery does with transnational problems: it produces tactical responses that lack strategic coherence. The president can authorize a drone strike in hours. Building a multilateral agreement to address the underlying conditions that produce terrorism takes years and may produce nothing binding. The tools that move fast — military force, executive orders, unilateral sanctions — address symptoms. The tools that address causes — institution-building, diplomatic agreements, economic development — move slowly and require cooperation from actors with different interests.

## The Honest Limits of American Power

For the decade after the Soviet Union's collapse, the U.S. was the world's sole superpower by any measure that seemed to matter: military capability, economic output, cultural reach. It felt, briefly, like the combination of overwhelming power and democratic legitimacy would be decisive. If the U.S. wanted something, it could have it.

It couldn't. The U.S. won the war in Afghanistan in weeks and then spent twenty years unable to win the occupation. It won the war in Iraq in three weeks and then spent twenty years unable to stabilize the country. It killed Osama bin Laden and dismantled much of al-Qaeda's original leadership, and terrorism continued because it wasn't generated by a leadership structure — it was generated by conditions.

The lesson that military planners slowly absorbed is the one realists have always known: force is useful for breaking things, not for building them. You can invade a country. You cannot force its population to adopt your values. You cannot solve a civil war by killing the side you oppose. You cannot build a stable government with foreign troops; at some point, the troops leave, and what remains is what the local population actually wants — which is rarely what the foreign power spent blood and treasure trying to create.

This is not a counsel of despair. It is a description of what the tools can and cannot do. Military force can stop a genocide, destroy a terrorist training camp, deter a conventional invasion. It cannot create legitimacy, rebuild institutions, or make people trust a government imposed from outside. The confusion between the two — treating military victory as equivalent to political success — is what made the post-9/11 wars so costly and so inconclusive.

The broader implication: American foreign policy faces a fundamental mismatch between the problems it confronts and the tools it has built. The problems are increasingly transnational — terrorism, climate, pandemics, the flow of capital and technology — and the tools are national: military force, diplomacy between governments, trade policy between states. The machinery of nation-state foreign policy is being stress-tested by a world where the most serious threats don't arrive from other states and can't be resolved by defeating them.

## What It All Adds Up To

Pull back and look at the whole picture.

The constitutional division of foreign power gives the president the authority to act fast and Congress the theoretical ability to check that action through appropriations and ratification. In practice, the president acts first and Congress decides later, usually by acquiescing. The War Powers Resolution, intended to restore congressional authority, codified presidential power for sixty days. The treaty ratification process, intended to ensure Senate buy-in on major agreements, gets sidestepped by executive agreements that the next president can reverse.

The schools of thought — isolationism, liberalism, realism, neoconservatism — each capture a piece of the strategic truth and each fails when applied as a single governing theory. The actual practice of U.S. foreign policy is hybrid and situational, which is less elegant and more effective than any pure school.

The machinery was built for a world of competing sovereign states. That world still exists — Russia invades Ukraine; China pressures Taiwan; North Korea develops nuclear weapons. The machinery handles those problems reasonably well. But the world also presents problems that aren't about state competition: a virus that kills three million people in a year, a physical process that threatens to destabilize agriculture and coastal populations worldwide, a network of fighters that disperses across failed states and recruits through social media. The machinery has no good tools for those problems, because the machinery was built when they didn't exist as foreign policy challenges.

Hamilton described the judiciary as having neither force nor will, only judgment. The same could be said of American foreign policy at its best: it has force, but force is only useful for some problems; it has will, but will without legitimacy is just imperialism; what distinguishes effective foreign policy is the judgment about which tool fits which problem, and the honesty to acknowledge when none of your tools fit at all.

The Cuban Missile Crisis worked because Kennedy had the authority to act, the wisdom to consult, and the luck that Khrushchev was rational and wanted an off-ramp. Two of those three are controllable. One isn't. The machinery of American foreign policy can give future presidents the authority and the institutions for consultation. It cannot guarantee they will use them wisely, or that the adversary on the other end will be rational enough to take the off-ramp when it's offered.

That's not a design flaw. It's the condition of operating in a world that doesn't organize itself around the convenience of any single nation's constitutional architecture. The machinery is what we have. The judgment about how to use it is what we have to cultivate.
