## Figure 1: Water Allocation During Drought
**Placement:** Concept 1 cold open, after the Lake Mead scene (roughly 400 words in)

**Description:** A diagram showing the Colorado River system, Lake Mead, and three states' allocations before and after the 2017 negotiation. Before: allocation bars showing Nevada, Arizona, California at roughly equal heights. After: allocation bars showing reduced heights, with Nevada and Arizona taking steeper cuts. Include one small residential profile icon labeled "Las Vegas homeowner" to maintain human scale.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Visualizes the constraint (there is less water than before) and the mechanism (government negotiation allocated reduction, not market price). Shows scale (a regional problem that requires multi-state coordination).

**Style notes:** Use a color scheme that suggests scarcity (shift from blues to grays/browns). Include historical low-water mark on the lake diagram. Simple, not ornate.

## Figure 2: Four Types of Goods (Taxonomy)
**Placement:** Concept 1, after the mechanism explanation (roughly after 1,200 words)

**Description:** A 2×2 matrix. Rows: "Excludable" and "Non-excludable." Columns: "Rival" and "Non-rival." Fill four cells:
- Excludable + Rival = Private Good (sandwich, haircut, house)
- Excludable + Non-rival = Toll Good (bridge, toll road, Netflix subscription)
- Non-excludable + Rival = Common Good (fish, groundwater, air quality)
- Non-excludable + Non-rival = Public Good (national security, fire protection, public school)

**Pedagogical purpose:** Makes the taxonomy explicit and memorable. Helps student predict what market can/cannot provide and what government must do.

**Style notes:** Use icons in each cell (sandwich, bridge, fish, fire truck) to make abstract concepts concrete. Include one or two sentence definition in each cell.

## Figure 3: The Free-Rider Problem as Flow
**Placement:** Concept 1, while explaining free-rider problem (roughly 1,400 words in)

**Description:** A simple flowchart showing three paths:
- Path A: "I pay taxes" → "Fire truck comes when my house burns" → "I feel the cost" ✓
- Path B: "I don't pay taxes" → "Fire truck comes when my neighbor's house burns" → "I benefit from lower fire spread, but didn't pay" (free-riding)
- Path C: "Nobody pays taxes" → "No fire truck" → "All houses burn" (tragedy)

Show why everyone is tempted toward Path B (rational individually) and why government solves this by making Path B illegal (taxation compulsory).

**Pedagogical purpose:** Explains the logic that leads to free-riding and why only government can solve it.

**Style notes:** Use different colors for each path. Show the emotional/physical outcome (house burns or doesn't) not just the financial transaction.

## Figure 4: Ritchie Torres's Path Through the System
**Placement:** Concept 3, worked example (roughly 4,800 words in)

**Description:** A timeline showing Torres's progression:
- 2000: "Child with asthma from mold in public housing apartment"
- 2005: "Volunteers on city council campaign"
- 2008: "Hired as housing director"
- 2013: "Elected to New York City Council"
- 2020: "Elected to U.S. House of Representatives"

Annotate each step with: the action he took (learned system, volunteered, ran), what he gained (knowledge, experience, credibility, platform), and what problem he moved closer to solving (mold in public housing → oversight of housing authority → federal influence over HUD).

**Pedagogical purpose:** Visualizes that participation is not one act but a sequence. Shows how individual learning and action can accumulate into institutional power. Makes the abstract mechanism concrete.

**Style notes:** Use increasing icon size or increasing background brightness to show accumulation of power. Include one quote at each stage from his story.

## Figure 5: Voter Turnout by Age Group and Politician Response (1990–2024)
**Placement:** Concept 3, mechanism section (roughly 5,200 words in)

**Description:** A dual-axis graph. Left axis: voter turnout percentage (0–70%) for three age groups (18–29, 30–49, 50+). Right axis: number of campaign ads or policy mentions targeting youth (indexed from 0–100). Show that turnout and campaign attention track each other across elections.

Example data points:
- 2000: youth turnout 36%, youth campaign focus 40
- 2008: youth turnout 51%, youth campaign focus 78
- 2016: youth turnout 42%, youth campaign focus 45
- 2020: youth turnout 53%, youth campaign focus 82

**Pedagogical purpose:** Shows empirically that participation affects politician behavior. Demonstrates the feedback loop: turnout → politician attention → policy → more turnout or less.

**Style notes:** Use contrasting colors for turnout (solid line) and campaign focus (bars or dashed line) so the correlation is visually obvious.

## Figure 6: The Cod Fishery Collapse and Recovery (1980–2010)
**Placement:** Concept 1, worked example (roughly 1,800 words in)

**Description:** A line graph showing cod population (biomass in thousands of tons) from 1980 to 2010.
- 1980–1990: steep decline from 200,000 to 20,000 tons
- 1992: vertical drop (fishing ban imposed)
- 1992–2010: flatline near zero with very slight recovery

Annotate with:
- "Tragedy of the commons: each fisherman takes maximum, population collapses"
- "1992: Government imposes total fishing ban after population crashes"
- "2010: Population still recovering, ban gradually being relaxed"

**Pedagogical purpose:** Shows visually that tragedy of the commons is real (population did collapse), and that only government action (ban) could stop it, and that recovery is slow even after action.

**Style notes:** Use red for the decline, black for the ban/flatline, green for recovery. Keep simple — this is about the pattern, not fine detail.
