# Images & Figures: The Constitution and Its Origins

## Figure 1: Shays' Rebellion (Part 1)
**Placement:** After the opening scene, after "The national government has no troops to send."

**Description:** A contemporary engraving or sketch showing armed farmers facing court officials or militia. The image should convey: ordinary men, not soldiers; determination and desperation; the machinery of law (courthouse, officials) meeting popular force. The scene is tense but not yet violent—it is the moment of confrontation before the uprising escalates.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Make the problem concrete. This is not an abstract failure of the Articles. This is a man losing his farm because the government cannot pay him for service. The image should evoke empathy and urgency.

**Style notes:** Historical accuracy preferred, but artistic interpretation acceptable. The key is to show the *human cost* of institutional failure, not just the institutional machinery.

## Figure 2: The Articles of Confederation vs. The Constitution (Part 1–2)
**Placement:** After the section "Here is what the weakness looked like in practice."

**Description:** A two-column comparison table or infographic:

**Left column (Articles of Confederation):**
- National government can request taxes (states rarely pay)
- Cannot regulate interstate commerce
- Cannot impose tariffs
- Cannot raise an army
- Cannot declare war without state militias
- Unanimous consent required for amendments
- No executive, no judiciary

**Right column (Constitution):**
- Congress can impose taxes directly
- Can regulate interstate and foreign commerce
- Can impose tariffs
- Can raise and maintain army/navy
- President is commander-in-chief
- Amendment by 2/3 Congress + 3/4 states
- Executive branch (president) and federal judiciary created

**Pedagogical purpose:** This is a visual summary of the scale shift from Articles to Constitution. Students should see at a glance what powers were added and what this meant for the nation's capacity to act.

**Style notes:** Use color to distinguish (e.g., red for weakness, green for strength). Use icons or symbols to make the comparison scannable.

## Figure 3: The Great Compromise (Part 2)
**Placement:** In the section "The fear of majority tyranny vs. the fear of small-state irrelevance."

**Description:** A diagram showing how representation in Congress works:

- House of Representatives: shown as a chamber with seat counts for a few key states (Virginia with 10 seats, Pennsylvania with 8, Delaware with 1)
- Senate: shown as a chamber with exactly 2 seats per state, regardless of size
- A note explaining that this allowed large states to win population-based representation while small states won equal representation in the upper chamber

**Pedagogical purpose:** Students often struggle with the bicameral legislature. A visual showing how the two chambers differ in apportionment makes the compromise concrete.

**Style notes:** Use proportional sizing (e.g., larger state blocks in the House, equal-sized blocks in the Senate) to make the difference visible.

## Figure 4: The Three-Fifths Compromise (Part 2)
**Placement:** In the section "The question of slavery—and the compromise that mortgaged the future."

**Description:** A map of the United States circa 1787, showing:
- Northern states (with slavery abolished or being gradually abolished)
- Southern states (with large enslaved populations)
- A notation showing how the Three-Fifths Compromise affected representation. For example, South Carolina's representation would have been X seats if enslaved people were not counted, Y seats if fully counted, and Z seats (three-fifths) under the compromise.

**Pedagogical purpose:** The map makes clear that slavery was geographically concentrated. Students should see that this was not a nationwide institution but a southern one, and that the compromise gave southern states disproportionate power in the House.

**Style notes:** Consider showing enslaved population percentages by state (as a heat map or legend). The visual should make the moral asymmetry clear: these people are being counted to increase their enslavers' power, yet they have no representation.

## Figure 5: Separation of Powers & Checks and Balances (Part 2)
**Placement:** In the section "The fear of too much central power vs. the fear of too little."

**Description:** A diagram of the three branches, with arrows showing checks and balances:

**Legislative (Congress):**
- Makes laws
- Can override presidential veto (2/3 vote)
- Ratifies treaties
- Confirms judges
- Controls the purse
- Can impeach and remove president

**Executive (President):**
- Enforces laws
- Vetoes legislation
- Appoints judges and cabinet (with Senate confirmation)
- Negotiates treaties (with Senate ratification)
- Commands armed forces (but Congress declares war)

**Judicial (Supreme Court):**
- Interprets laws
- Can rule laws unconstitutional
- Tries impeachment cases (Chief Justice presides)

Arrows between the branches showing how each limits the others.

**Pedagogical purpose:** This is the core mechanism of the Constitution. Students must understand that the branches are not separate; they are interdependent. Checks and balances force cooperation.

**Style notes:** Use color coding (e.g., one color per branch) and clear arrows (solid lines for powers, dashed lines for checks). The diagram should be scannable and memorable.

## Figure 6: Federalist vs. Anti-Federalist Divide (Part 3)
**Placement:** In the section "The Federalists" and "The Anti-Federalists."

**Description:** A two-column comparison or a spectrum diagram showing:

**Federalists:**
- Merchants, manufacturers, military officers
- Coastal, commercial interests
- Want strong national government
- Believe union necessary for defense and commerce
- Fear mob rule, chaos
- Concentrated in New England and commercial centers

**Anti-Federalists:**
- Small farmers, artisans
- Inland, agricultural interests
- Want weak federal government, strong states
- Believe state governments closer to the people
- Fear concentrated power, distant government
- Concentrated in rural areas and the South

**Pedagogical purpose:** This makes clear that the ratification fight was about material interests and geography, not just abstract ideology. Students should see that Federalists and Anti-Federalists were not equally distributed across the country.

**Style notes:** Consider a map showing where each group had strength. Or a spectrum diagram showing the range of positions (Federalists on one end, Anti-Federalists on the other, with some figures and states labeled at their positions).

## Figure 7: The Ratification Process (Part 3)
**Placement:** In the section "The ratification sequence and what it revealed."

**Description:** A timeline or map showing which states ratified and when:

- Delaware, New Jersey, Connecticut: December 1787–January 1788 (quick)
- Georgia, Pennsylvania, Massachusetts: early 1788
- Virginia: June 1788 (9th state—Constitution takes effect)
- New York: July 1788 (swing state, close vote)
- North Carolina, Rhode Island: 1789–1790 (late)

A notation showing that Virginia and New York were the swing states. A note that if New York had rejected, the Constitution might have failed to gain legitimacy.

**Pedagogical purpose:** The timeline makes clear that ratification was not inevitable. It was a close call, especially in the large states. This undercuts the "framers' intent was unambiguous" narrative.

**Style notes:** Use color to show states that ratified quickly vs. slowly. Highlight Virginia and New York as swing states.

## Figure 8: The Bill of Rights (Part 3–4)
**Placement:** In the section "The Bill of Rights as compromise."

**Description:** A visual summary of the ten amendments:

| Amendment | Right Protected |
|-----------|-----------------|
| First     | Religion, speech, press, assembly, petition |
| Second    | Bear arms |
| Third     | No quartering of soldiers |
| Fourth    | No unreasonable search/seizure |
| Fifth     | Due process, no self-incrimination |
| Sixth     | Right to counsel, jury trial |
| Seventh   | Jury trial (civil cases) |
| Eighth    | No excessive bail/cruel punishment |
| Ninth     | Rights beyond those listed |
| Tenth     | Powers to states, not federal government |

**Pedagogical purpose:** Students often encounter the Bill of Rights in fragments. A visual summary of all ten makes clear what was protected (individual liberties) and what was reserved to the states.

**Style notes:** Consider using icons or symbols for each right (e.g., a newspaper for the First Amendment, a house for the Third). Make it scannable.

## Figure 9: Federal vs. State Power (Part 4)
**Placement:** In the section "Integration—The Constitution as Pragmatic Repair."

**Description:** A Venn diagram or concentric circles showing:

**Federal (Enumerated) Powers (inner circle):**
- Declare war
- Tax
- Regulate interstate commerce
- Coin money
- Make treaties
- Naturalize immigrants

**State (Reserved) Powers (outer circle):**
- Regulate intrastate commerce
- Marriage and divorce
- Education
- Public health and safety
- License professions

**Concurrent Powers (overlapping area, if using a Venn):**
- Levy taxes
- Establish courts
- Maintain armed forces (though federal is primary)

**Pedagogical purpose:** The federal system is hard for students to visualize. A diagram showing what power belongs where makes the division concrete.

**Style notes:** Use color coding (federal = blue, state = red, concurrent = purple). The diagram should emphasize that states did not disappear; they retained significant power.

## General Style Notes for All Figures

- **Attenborough × Feynman visual aesthetic:** Images should be clear, slightly naturalistic rather than cartoonish, and focused on showing *how things work* rather than illustrating emotions. The Shays' Rebellion figure is an exception; it should evoke the human cost.
- **Accessibility:** All figures should be accompanied by alt text and a caption explaining what is shown and why it matters.
- **No decoration:** Avoid ornamental borders, excessive colors, or clip art. The figures serve pedagogy, not aesthetics.
- **Consistency:** Use the same visual language across all figures (fonts, color palette, icon style).
