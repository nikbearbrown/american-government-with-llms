# Images and Figures: American Federalism

## [FIGURE 1: The Three Systems of Government]

**Placement:** Section 1 (Chapter Opening), after "Federalism is an institutional arrangement..."

**Description:** Three side-by-side diagrams showing the structure of unitary, federal, and confederal systems.

- **Unitary**: National government at top with arrows pointing down to subnational governments. Power flows in one direction.
- **Federal**: National government and state governments at equal level, each with arrows pointing down to citizens. Dual authority.
- **Confederal**: Subnational governments at top with arrows pointing up to weak central government. Power flows upward.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Clarifies the distinction between three systems often confused. Students can see structurally why federalism differs from unitary (which the UK and France use) and confederal (which the US briefly tried).

**Style Notes:** Keep labels minimal. Use consistent iconography (people icons for "citizens," building icons for "governments"). Color code: federal level in one color, state level in another, to show distinct authorities. Include a caption: "Federalism creates two distinct levels of elected authority. Unitary systems concentrate authority in the center. Confederal systems concentrate authority in the states."

## [FIGURE 2: Division of Powers in American Federalism]

**Placement:** Section 2 (Division of Powers), after introduction of enumerated, reserved, and concurrent powers.

**Description:** A Venn diagram or three-circle diagram.

- **Left circle** (Enumerated Powers): List major federal powers — defense, foreign trade, interstate commerce, coining money, post office, naturalization.
- **Right circle** (Reserved Powers): State powers — criminal law, education, marriage/family law, intrastate commerce, property regulation, professional licensing.
- **Center overlap** (Concurrent Powers): Taxing, borrowing, making laws, running courts, law enforcement.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Visualizes the theoretical division of powers. Students see what is exclusive to each level and what both do. The diagram also implicitly asks: what happens in the overlap? (Answer: Federal law prevails if it conflicts with state law.)

**Style Notes:** Use neutral colors for the circles. Label clearly. Add a legend if needed. Include note: "Federal law prevails in the overlap if it conflicts with state law." Size the circles to approximate how much authority each level holds in practice — the federal circle should be large (its enumerated and implied powers are vast), the reserved circle smaller, the overlap substantial.

## [FIGURE 3: Evolution of Federalism Over Time]

**Placement:** Section 3 (Evolution), after introduction of dual, cooperative, and new federalism.

**Description:** A timeline from 1790 to present showing the three (or four) phases of federalism.

- **1790–1870s: Founding period** — National government consolidates authority. *McCulloch v. Maryland* (1819), *Gibbons v. Ogden* (1824) shift power toward federal authority.
- **1870s–1930s: Dual Federalism** — States and federal government in separate spheres. *United States v. E.C. Knight*, *Lochner v. New York* enforce boundaries.
- **1930s–1970s: Cooperative Federalism** — New Deal, Great Society, expansion of federal programs. *NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin* (1937) enables federal authority. Federal-state collaboration increases.
- **1970s–present: New Federalism & Contemporary** — Attempts to restore state authority (Nixon, Reagan) but federal authority continues to grow through mandates and conditional grants. Recent Supreme Court decisions (*United States v. Lopez*) attempt to restrain federal power but effect is limited.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Shows that federalism is not static. Students see how the balance of power has shifted over two centuries, and that current debates have historical roots.

**Style Notes:** Use a horizontal timeline. Label each era with key legislation and Supreme Court cases. Color the timeline to show conceptually where authority lies (e.g., darker federal color during cooperative federalism, darker state color during new federalism attempts). Include brief annotation for each era: what changed, why it changed. Add note: "Note the trend: federal authority has increased over time, with brief countertrends during New Federalism."

- X-axis: Year (decade markers or 5-year intervals).

**Style Notes:** Use distinct colors for each grant type. Label the chart to show that categorical grants are the vast majority. Add annotation: "Medicaid spending within the categorical grants category has grown fastest." Include a note about the significance: "By 2020, federal grants accounted for 30% of state government revenue, making states dependent on federal funding."

## [FIGURE 5: Sources of Revenue — Federal, State, and Local]

**Placement:** Section 4 (The Distribution of Finances), in discussion of how governments fund operations.

**Description:** Three pie charts or a grouped bar chart showing revenue sources for federal, state, and local governments.

**Federal (2020 data):**
- Individual income taxes: 47%
- Payroll taxes (Social Security/Medicare): 38%
- Corporate income taxes: ~7%
- Excise and other taxes: ~8%

**State (2020 data):**
- Sales tax: ~47% of tax revenue
- Individual income tax: ~38% of tax revenue
- Corporate income tax: ~8%
- Federal grants: ~25% of total state revenue
- Service charges: ~15%

**Local (2018 data):**
- Property tax: ~72% of tax revenue
- Federal and state grants: ~30% of total local revenue
- Service charges: ~18%
- Other sources: remainder

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Shows the dramatic differences in how each level funds itself. Federal government relies heavily on income and payroll taxes (broadly based). States rely on sales and income taxes. Local governments depend heavily on property tax and federal/state grants. Students see why each level has different fiscal pressures and priorities.

**Style Notes:** Use pie charts for clarity (students can visually grasp the relative sizes). If using a single chart with multiple groups, use grouped bars. Label percentages clearly. Add annotations: "Federal government has access to broader tax base." "States depend heavily on sales tax, which is regressive." "Local governments depend on property tax, which is inelastic." "Federal grants are critical to state and local budgets."

## [FIGURE 6: Federal Mandates and Their Cost to States]

**Placement:** Section 4 (Unfunded Mandates), after discussion of Real ID Act and other examples.

**Description:** Bar chart or timeline showing major unfunded or partially-funded mandates and their estimated cost to states.

Examples:
- Real ID Act (2005): $11 billion estimated cost, federal reimbursement minimal.
- Civil Rights Act, Title VI compliance: Ongoing, costs vary by state.
- Clean Air Act implementation: Ongoing, costs vary by air quality baseline.
- Clery Act (campus crime reporting): Compliance costs vary by institution size.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Quantifies the burden of mandates on state budgets. Students see that mandates are not cost-free impositions; they shift spending from states' chosen priorities to federal requirements.

**Style Notes:** Use horizontal bars to show estimated cost vs. federal reimbursement (use distinct colors). Include a note: "The Unfunded Mandates Reform Act (1995) was intended to prevent this, but Congress has found ways around it." Highlight the Real ID Act as the most visible recent example. Include annotation: "Unfunded mandates let the federal government accomplish goals while passing costs to states."

## [FIGURE 7: Key Supreme Court Cases on Federalism — Timeline]

**Placement:** Section 3 & 5 (Evolution and Integration), as visual reference for major cases.

**Description:** A vertical or horizontal timeline of landmark federalism cases from 1819 to present.

- **McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)**: Implied powers, national supremacy.
- **Gibbons v. Ogden (1824)**: Commerce clause broadly interpreted.
- **United States v. E.C. Knight (1895)**: Manufacturing excepted from commerce clause.
- **Lochner v. New York (1905)**: States cannot regulate labor contracts (dual federalism).
- **NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel (1937)**: Manufacturing subject to federal regulation (cooperative federalism begins).
- **Wickard v. Filburn (1942)**: Commerce clause includes local production for local use.
- **United States v. Lopez (1995)**: Federal power has limits; Gun-Free School Zones Act struck down.
- **Arizona v. United States (2012)**: Federal supremacy in immigration, but some state role permitted.
- **United States v. Windsor (2013)**: Federal government cannot discriminate among state-recognized marriages.
- **Obergefell v. Hodges (2015)**: Marriage equality is a constitutional right, removing it from state authority.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Shows how courts have interpreted the Constitution's federalism provisions differently over time. Students see that the boundary between federal and state authority is not fixed but shaped by Supreme Court interpretation.

**Style Notes:** Use a clean timeline format. Group cases by era (dual, cooperative, etc.) with color coding or bracketing. Include brief holding for each case (1–2 sentences). Add note: "The Supreme Court is the ultimate arbiter of federalism disputes. Its interpretation of the Constitution changes over time."

## [FIGURE 8: Interest Groups and Venue Shopping]

**Placement:** Section 5 (Contemporary Tensions), after discussion of MADD, anti-abortion advocates, immigration advocates.

**Description:** Diagram showing how interest groups navigate state and federal venues.

Example 1 — MADD:
- State legislatures (resisted) → Congress (receptive) → National Minimum Drinking Age Act → All states complied.

Example 2 — Anti-abortion advocates:
- Congress (resisted) → State legislatures (receptive) → Parental involvement laws, funding restrictions, etc.

Example 3 — Immigration advocates:
- Federal government (opposed) ↔ State governments (some receptive, some resistant) → Competing state laws, federal intervention via courts.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Visualizes how federalism creates multiple access points for political advocacy. Interest groups can shop for the venue most likely to support their position. Students see that federalism is not neutral; it shapes which groups win and which lose.

**Style Notes:** Use arrows to show movement between levels. Use color to show receptiveness (green for receptive, red for resistant, yellow for contested). Include note: "Federalism allows interest groups multiple routes to power. This can be democratic (many access points) or dysfunctional (the same issue never gets settled)."

## Notes on Pedagogy

Each figure should include:
1. A clear, complete caption (3–4 sentences) explaining what the figure shows and why it matters.
2. A note inviting student analysis ("Why might the federal government prefer categorical grants?" or "What explains the shift from dual to cooperative federalism?").
3. Source attribution for data (Census Bureau, Congressional Budget Office, Supreme Court databases, etc.).
4. Consistency in color, font, and style across all figures in the chapter.

Figures should not duplicate the text. They should visualize relationships, timelines, and data the text discusses. Each figure should be referenced in the text with a clear lead-in: "As shown in [Figure X]..." or "The graph [Figure X] illustrates..."
