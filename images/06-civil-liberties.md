# Images: Civil Liberties Chapter

## Image Placement & Pedagogy

### [FIGURE: Incorporation Timeline – Selective Application of the Bill of Rights to States]

**Placement:** End of Concept 1 (Incorporation Doctrine), before Case Study.

**Visual:** Timeline showing:
- 1868: Fourteenth Amendment ratified
- 1920s–1930s: First Amendment freedoms incorporated
- 1950s–1960s: Criminal procedure rights incorporated (Gideon 1963)
- 1970s–1980s: Most remaining rights incorporated
- 2010: Second Amendment incorporated (McDonald v. Chicago)
- Blank: Third Amendment, Seventh Amendment (not incorporated)

**Description:** Each milestone shows the year, the amendment, the landmark case, and what right became binding on states. A visual way to show that incorporation is *not* automatic and *not* complete.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Students often assume the whole Bill of Rights applied to states from 1791 on. This timeline shows it is a 140+ year process, still incomplete, case-by-case. The gaps (Third, Seventh) matter because they show courts making distinctions, not just applying the amendment wholesale.

**Style notes:** Use color to distinguish incorporated from un-incorporated. Use different markers (checkmarks, X) to make the distinction clear. The timeline should feel like a *process*, not a list.

### [FIGURE: The Two Directions of First Amendment Protection – Establishment and Free Exercise]

**Placement:** Beginning of Concept 2, First Amendment section.

**Visual:** Two-way arrow or balance diagram showing:
- **Left side (Establishment Clause):** Government cannot establish religion. Cannot sponsor prayer in schools. Cannot fund churches preferentially. Cannot make religion official.
- **Right side (Free Exercise Clause):** Government cannot restrict religious practice. Cannot ban prayer in private schools. Cannot exclude religious providers from subsidy programs. Cannot burden sincere belief without compelling interest.

Show potential collision: Maine tuition subsidy program (Carson v. Makin) at the center. On left: establishment concern (avoiding state funding of religion). On right: free exercise concern (excluding religious schools from equal access to funds). Court ruled free exercise won.

**Description:** The two clauses protect religion in opposite directions. They can collide. The chart shows how courts balance them.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Students think of the First Amendment as protecting religion. It does, but in two ways that can conflict. This diagram makes the tension visible and concrete.

**Style notes:** Use arrows pointing in opposite directions to show the tension. Use the *Carson* case as a concrete example to show the balance point is not fixed.

### [FIGURE: The Three Tests for Speech – Where Courts Draw the Line]

**Placement:** Middle of Concept 2, Freedom of Expression section.

**Visual:** Pyramid or flow chart:
- **Bottom tier (No protection):** Incitement, true threats, fighting words, defamation, obscenity. Examples: "Rush the building now" to a mob, "I will kill you," lewd material with no redeeming value.
- **Middle tier (Content-neutral):** Regulation of *how* speech is delivered, not what is said. Example: "No amplified sound in parks after 10 PM" (applies to all speakers, all content).
- **Top tier (Content-based, requires strict scrutiny):** Regulation targeting the *message*. Example: "No speech criticizing the mayor" (fails strict scrutiny).

**Description:** Courts apply different tests depending on the category. Only content-based restrictions targeting message face the hardest test (strict scrutiny).

**Pedagogical purpose:** Students often think "free speech" is absolute. It is not. Some speech is unprotected; some regulation is allowed if content-neutral; only content-based restrictions face strict scrutiny. The chart shows where the lines are.

**Style notes:** Use the pyramid or flow chart to show hierarchy. Use color to show danger level: red (unprotected), yellow (allowed if content-neutral), green (highly protected). Label the tests clearly.

### [FIGURE: Miranda Rights – The Asymmetry of Custodial Interrogation]

**Placement:** Middle of Concept 3, Rights of the Accused section, after introducing Miranda.

**Visual:** Two-panel diagram:
- **Left panel (Before Miranda):** Police sitting across table from suspect. Suspect has no information about rights. Power asymmetry explicit. Question mark over suspect's head (does not know what rights exist).
- **Right panel (After Miranda):** Same scene, but a card showing the four warnings is visible. Suspect is informed. Information asymmetry reduced (though not eliminated—police still have power of the badge).

**Description:** Miranda warnings do not prevent all coercion. They reduce the information asymmetry that makes custodial interrogation inherently coercive. The suspect still faces pressure; now they at least know they have options.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Many students think Miranda rights prevent all coercion. They do not. They inform suspects of rights so they can actually *exercise* them. The diagram shows why the Court felt the warnings were necessary—the inherent power asymmetry.

**Style notes:** Use simple, clear icons. The suspect's expression might change between panels (confused → informed, or still anxious). The goal is to show that warnings level the field somewhat, not perfectly.
