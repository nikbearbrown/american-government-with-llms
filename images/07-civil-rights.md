# Images: 07—Civil Rights

Image placements, descriptions, and pedagogical purposes for chapter 07.

## Chart 1: The Three Tiers of Scrutiny

**Placement:** End of Concept 1, after the explanation of the three-tier framework.

**Type:** Comparative table/pyramid.

**Content:**
- Three rows or tiers, stacked
- **Tier 1 (Bottom/Widest):** Rational Basis Test
  - Applied to: Most classifications (age, wealth, occupation, etc.)
  - Burden of proof: On the challenger
  - Standard: Law is constitutional if rationally related to a legitimate governmental interest
  - Outcome: Government almost always wins
  
- **Tier 2 (Middle):** Intermediate Scrutiny
  - Applied to: Gender/sex discrimination
  - Burden of proof: On the government
  - Standard: Discrimination must be substantially related to an important governmental objective
  - Outcome: Government sometimes wins, but often loses
  
- **Tier 3 (Top/Narrowest):** Strict Scrutiny
  - Applied to: Race, ethnicity, religion, national origin discrimination
  - Burden of proof: On the government
  - Standard: Compelling governmental interest + narrowly tailored + least restrictive means
  - Outcome: Government almost never wins

**Visual style:** Pyramid or stacked bars showing increasing stringency. Color-code by outcome: green (government usually wins) → yellow (mixed) → red (government almost never wins).

**Pedagogical purpose:** Helps students understand that scrutiny is not binary (constitutional vs. unconstitutional) but a graduated framework with different standards applied based on what's being discriminated against. Reinforces that the burden of proof shifts as the stakes rise.

## Chart 2: Tools of Disenfranchisement (1870s-1960s)

**Placement:** Concept 2, after the explanation of literacy tests, poll taxes, grandfather clause, and white primary.

**Type:** Timeline or layered bar chart.

**Content:**
- Timeline from 1870 (Fifteenth Amendment) to 1965 (Voting Rights Act)
- Four bars representing the four tools, showing when each was introduced and when it was eliminated
- **Literacy Tests** (1890s-1965): Long bar showing they were used throughout the period and banned by the VRA
- **Poll Taxes** (1890s-1966): Bar showing similar span (note: some states kept them until the VRA, others until *Harper v. Virginia*)
- **Grandfather Clauses** (1898-1915): Shorter bar; Supreme Court struck them down in *Guinn v. United States* (1915)
- **White Primary** (1920s-1944): Bar showing they were used and then struck down progressively (white primary formally banned in *Smith v. Allwright*, 1944, but effects persisted)
- Overlay showing Black voter registration rates in the South: starts very low (3-7% in 1940), then rises sharply after 1965

**Visual style:** Stacked or parallel bars with color-coding. Each tool in a distinct color. Registration rates as a line graph on the same x-axis.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Shows that disenfranchisement was not accidental or one-off but a systematic, multi-layered approach lasting nearly a century. The lag between legal victories (literacy tests banned, grandfather clauses struck down) and actual voter registration shows that removing obstacles on paper doesn't create outcomes in practice. The sharp rise after 1965 shows what enforcement mechanisms can accomplish.

## Map 1: Preclearance Jurisdictions Under the Voting Rights Act

**Placement:** Concept 2, after the discussion of the Voting Rights Act of 1965.

**Type:** U.S. map with regions color-coded.

**Content:**
- Show states (or parts of states) that were subject to the preclearance requirement of Section 5 of the Voting Rights Act
- Color those jurisdictions distinctly (e.g., dark red)
- Indicate the other states in gray
- Include a legend explaining what preclearance meant: federal approval required before changing voting laws
- Optionally show a second map showing voting restrictions passed in preclearance jurisdictions in the years after *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013)

**Visual style:** Clear color distinction. State-by-state or county-by-county if showing the specificity of preclearance (it didn't always cover entire states).

**Pedagogical purpose:** Shows the geographic scope of federal oversight. Helps students see which states had the most egregious histories of voting discrimination and where the federal government had to step in. The contrast between the preclearance map and post-2013 restrictions shows how quickly states resumed discrimination once oversight was removed.

## Photograph/Historical Image 1: Lunch Counter Sit-In

**Placement:** Concept 3, in the section on direct action and grassroots organizing.

**Type:** Photograph from a sit-in campaign, likely from Greensboro (1960) or a subsequent campaign.

**Source:** Should be a historically accurate photograph, publicly available, perhaps from the Library of Congress or civil rights archive.

**Description:** Shows young Black students, usually men and women, sitting calmly at a lunch counter, dressed formally (jackets, dresses), refusing to leave when told they cannot be served. Often white patrons or staff are visible in the background, sometimes hostile.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Brings the abstract tactic of "sit-ins" into concrete form. Shows the dignity and composure required. Illustrates the non-violent discipline of the movement. The formal dress is important—it undermines stereotypes and shows the movement was deliberate and organized, not spontaneous anger.

## Photograph/Historical Image 2: Bloody Sunday / Selma March

**Placement:** Concept 2 or Concept 3, in the section on the Voting Rights Act or the Selma march.

**Type:** Photograph from the Selma-to-Montgomery march, March 7, 1965.

**Source:** Iconic photograph, widely available, from news archives.

**Description:** Should show the violence of state police attacking marchers. The "Tear Gas over Selma" photograph by Spider Martin or similar images showing the physical violence and the marchers' non-violent response are most powerful. John Lewis's fractured skull, the mounted police, the whips and clubs.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Shows what "massive resistance" looked like in practice. Not abstract violence but televised, photographed violence against peaceful citizens. This image shifted public opinion and moved Congress to act. It demonstrates that legal recognition of rights (the Fifteenth Amendment existed in 1965) is insufficient without enforcement and public pressure.

## Timeline 1: From Slavery to Civil Rights Act (1776-1964)

**Placement:** Concept 3, as a visual complement to the narrative arc.

**Type:** Simple timeline with key dates and events.

**Content:**
- 1776: Declaration of Independence ("all men are created equal")
- 1787: Constitutional Convention (Constitution protects slavery)
- 1857: *Dred Scott v. Sandford* (Black people are not citizens)
- 1865: Thirteenth Amendment (slavery abolished)
- 1868: Fourteenth Amendment (citizenship, equal protection)
- 1870: Fifteenth Amendment (voting rights regardless of race)
- 1877: Reconstruction ends; federal troops withdrawn
- 1896: *Plessy v. Ferguson* (separate but equal is constitutional)
- 1930s-1950: NAACP litigation campaign against segregation in education
- 1954: *Brown v. Board of Education* (segregation unconstitutional)
- 1955: *Brown II* (desegregate with "all deliberate speed")
- 1960: Greensboro sit-ins begin
- 1961: Freedom Rides
- 1963: March on Washington
- 1964: Civil Rights Act
- 1965: Voting Rights Act

**Visual style:** Horizontal timeline with dates on top/bottom and events labeled. Color-code events: green for civil rights victories, red for defeats or reversals, gray for systemic violence/oppression.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Shows the long arc from 1776 to 1965. Emphasizes that progress was not linear and that many promises were made and broken before being enforced. Helps students see the relationship between key cases and legislation. The 88-year gap between *Plessy* and *Brown* illustrates the stagnation.

## Chart 3: Black Voter Registration in the South (1940-1975)

**Placement:** Concept 2, after the discussion of the Voting Rights Act's effects.

**Type:** Line graph or bar chart.

**Content:**
- X-axis: Years from 1940 to 1975
- Y-axis: Percentage of eligible Black voters registered
- Line/bars showing registration rates in Southern states
- Key data points:
  - 1940: ~3% in the South overall
  - 1950: ~7-10%
  - 1960: ~25-30%
  - 1964: ~35-40%
  - 1965: *Voting Rights Act passes* (marked as vertical line or annotation)
  - 1968: ~60%
  - 1972: ~75%+
- Include lines for a few individual states (e.g., Mississippi, Alabama, Georgia) to show variation

**Visual style:** Clear line graph with shaded region or highlighted area showing the rapid increase post-1965. Annotate the VRA passage prominently.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Shows empirically what the chapter claims: the Voting Rights Act's preclearance and federal examiner provisions had an immediate, dramatic effect. Registration rates that had been stuck at ~3% for 80+ years jumped to 60%+ within a few years of the VRA. This is the mechanism of enforcement visible in data.

## Chart 4: Contemporary Voting Restrictions (2013-Present)

**Placement:** Integration section or "Ongoing Challenges" section.

**Type:** Map or data visualization.

**Content:**
- States that have passed voting restrictions since 2013 (*Shelby County v. Holder*)
- Types of restrictions: voter ID requirements, reduced early voting, fewer polling locations, stricter voter registration deadlines, purged voter rolls
- Show which states that were previously under preclearance have passed the most restrictions
- Optional: Data on how these restrictions have affected registration/turnout, particularly among Black voters

**Visual style:** Map with color-coding or a table comparing pre-2013 and post-2013 restrictions.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Shows that the problem of voting rights is not historical. Once federal oversight was removed, states resumed discrimination. This reinforces the chapter's argument that civil rights require constant maintenance and enforcement. The contrast between the rapid registration increases of 1965-1975 and the new restrictions of 2013+ shows that progress is reversible.

## Infographic: From Legal Barrier to Practical Outcome

**Placement:** Integration or synthesis section.

**Type:** Conceptual diagram.

**Content:**
- Start with "*Brown v. Board of Education* (1954): Segregation is unconstitutional"
- Arrow down to "Implementation: Schools must desegregate"
- Arrow down to "Resistance: States employ massive resistance, create private schools"
- Arrow down to "Result: Schools remain segregated by residence, not law"
- Parallel track: "*Civil Rights Act of 1964*: Employment discrimination is illegal"
- Arrow down to "Implementation: Companies must not discriminate"
- Arrow down to "Mechanism: EEOC investigates, sues discriminators"
- Arrow down to "Result: Some progress; structural discrimination persists"
- Key annotation: "Law removes obstacles. It does not create outcomes. Outcomes require implementation, resources, and enforcement."

**Visual style:** Flow diagram or parallel paths showing that legal victory is necessary but not sufficient.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Helps students understand the persistent gap between what the law says and what actually happens. Reinforces that enforcement matters and that removing legal barriers is only the first step.
