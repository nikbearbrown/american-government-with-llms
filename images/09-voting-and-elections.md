# Image Placements and Descriptions: Ch 09 Voting and Elections

## Image 1: Voter Registration Rules by State (Map)

**Placement**: End of Concept 1 (Registration and Who Can Vote)

**Description**: U.S. map color-coded by registration rule complexity. Green = automatic/same-day registration (easy). Yellow = online + 14-day requirement (medium). Orange = paper + 30-day requirement (harder). Red = strict photo ID + 45-day requirement (hardest). Show 3-4 state examples with specific rules.

**Pedagogical Purpose**: Visual proof that registration rules vary by state and create different barriers. Lets reader see at a glance which states make voting easier/harder. Supports argument that rules are not neutral.

**Style Notes**: Keep color logic intuitive (green = go/easy, red = stop/hard). Include brief legend. Show real state names and rules, not generic "State A/B/C."

## Image 2: Turnout by Age (Bar Chart)

**Placement**: Mid-Concept 2 (Turnout and What Gets People to the Polls)

**Description**: Horizontal bar chart showing turnout rates by age group. 18-24: 39%, 25-44: 50%, 45-64: 62%, 65-74: 68%, 75+: 63%. Data from 2016 Census Bureau.

**Pedagogical Purpose**: Visual proof that age is a strong predictor of voting. Reader can see the pattern immediately. Supports discussion of why campaigns target older voters.

**Style Notes**: Use consistent color (not rainbow). Label each bar with percentage. Note: 75+ drops slightly because of health/mobility issues at very high age.

## Image 3: Campaign Spending in Primary vs. General (Timeline)

**Placement**: End of Concept 3 (Campaigns, Money, and Reaching Voters)

**Description**: Stacked area chart showing campaign spending over time (January → November of election year). Primary season (Jan-June) shows multiple candidates' bars stacking. General election (July-Nov) shows two candidates' bars dominating. Y-axis = millions of dollars. Use 2020 Democratic primary as example data.

**Pedagogical Purpose**: Shows visually why early money matters (primary phase is crowded and expensive) and why general election is different (consolidation around two candidates). Supports argument that primary and general require different strategies.

**Style Notes**: Use two colors to distinguish primary-phase spending from general-phase. Label specific candidates (Biden, Sanders, Bloomberg, etc.) so reader can see who spent when.

## Image 4: Direct Democracy by State (Map)

**Placement**: Beginning of Direct Democracy section, or end of it

**Description**: U.S. map showing which states allow which forms of direct democracy. Darkest color = all three (initiative, referendum, recall). Medium = two of three. Light = one only. White = none. Show state names.

**Pedagogical Purpose**: Visual proof that direct democracy is not uniform across U.S. Western states more permissive, eastern/southern states less so. Supports argument about Progressive Era influence and state-by-state variation.

**Style Notes**: Keep color logic clear. Could show California, Colorado, Oregon in slightly different shade to highlight as examples in text.

## Image 5: Ballot Fatigue in Colorado (Line Graph)

**Placement**: Case Study "Why Ballot Measures Get Fewer Votes Than Top-of-Ticket Races"

**Description**: Line graph showing turnout declining as you move down the ballot. X-axis = ballot position (presidential → senate → house → local → ballot measure). Y-axis = % of registered voters who voted on that item. Drop visible from ~70% (presidential) to ~54% (state supreme court retention).

**Pedagogical Purpose**: Visualizes ballot fatigue concept. Concrete proof that ballot position affects participation. Supports argument that structure of ballot affects outcome.

**Style Notes**: Use 2012 Colorado data as presented in chapter. Make the drop visible but not exaggerated.

## Image 6: Registration Rules and Black Voter Registration Over Time (Line Graph)

**Placement**: Case Study in Concept 1, showing Voting Rights Act effect

**Description**: Line graph showing Black voter registration in Mississippi and Alabama before/after VRA (1965). Y-axis = % Black registered voters. Two lines: one for Mississippi, one for Alabama. Vertical dashed line marks 1965 (VRA passage). Sharp uptick visible after 1965.

**Pedagogical Purpose**: Concrete visualization of how registration law change affects actual participation. Shows massive effect of federal intervention. Supports argument that rules determine who votes.

**Style Notes**: Make pre/post VRA comparison visually obvious. Could add shaded region highlighting preclearance period (1965-2013) to show how long it lasted.

## Image 7: Colorado Mail-in Voting Participation (Bar Chart)

**Placement**: Case Study in Concept 2, "Early Voting and Mail-in Voting"

**Description**: Simple bar chart comparing Colorado's votes cast in presidential election years: 2010 (1.8M congressional), 2018 (2.6M congressional), 2020 (estimated based on mail-in expansion). Show the trend upward, especially noting the spike in 2020 under all-mail system.

**Pedagogical Purpose**: Concrete proof that removing logistical barriers increases participation. Supports argument that mail-in voting works. Appendix could note that some of increase may be population growth, but mail-in expansion is cited as significant factor.

**Style Notes**: Keep bars simple. Could use different colors for congressional vs. presidential election years, or note that comparison is "apples to oranges" (congressional in lower-turnout years, but still shows upward trend under mail-in).

## Style Notes for All Images

- **Color Palette**: Use the book's established colors. Greens/blues for positive (higher access, higher turnout). Reds/oranges for barriers/difficulty. Neutrals for explanatory charts.
- **Typography**: Label all axes clearly. Keep legend minimal. Use font size that works at both full-page and thumbnail.
- **Data Source**: Every image needs a citation line at bottom. "Data: U.S. Census Bureau, November 2016" or similar.
- **Simplicity**: Resist showing too many dimensions at once. One key insight per image.
- **Accessibility**: Ensure color-blindness friendly (not red-green only). Include values in bars/points so reader doesn't have to guess.
