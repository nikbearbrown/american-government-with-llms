# Images Guide: The Media (Chapter 11)

## Image Placement and Description

### [FIGURE 1: White House Briefing Room Seating Chart]
**Placement:** Chapter opening, after cold open scene

**Description:** Aerial view of the White House briefing room showing the 49 assigned seats. Front rows labeled "Associated Press," "Reuters," "Bloomberg." Back rows labeled "Digital outlets," "Local stations." Visible: physical gatekeeping—literal assigned positions that determine who gets asked, whose follow-ups are heard.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Makes abstract concept (gatekeeping) concrete and spatial. Reader sees gatekeeping is not just editorial but architectural.

**Style notes:** Simple, factual, not dramatized. Could be a photograph or clean diagram. The point is that gatekeeping is a physical reality, not just a metaphor.

### [FIGURE 2: Media Format Evolution Timeline]
**Placement:** Concept 2, after "The Three Economics of Media" section

**Description:** Timeline showing:
- 1704: First continuous newspaper (Boston)
- 1920s: Radio emerges
- 1950s: Television becomes dominant, three networks
- 1980s: Cable emerges (CNN, FOX News, MSNBC)
- 1990s: Internet begins
- 2015: Social media dominance
- 2025: Algorithm-driven feeds

Each point annotated with economic model (subscriptions, ads, narrowcasting, engagement).

**Pedagogical purpose:** Shows that media fragmentation is not accidental but follows from economic shifts. Helps reader see pattern across time.

**Style notes:** Clean, chronological. Could include visual metaphors for each era (printing press → broadcast tower → computer network → algorithm).

---

### [FIGURE 3: Gatekeeping at Scale]
**Placement:** Concept 2, "Scale Shifts" section

**Description:** Four-panel diagram showing:
- Panel 1: 1950s single editor at newspaper desk, deciding what 100,000 people see
- Panel 2: 1980s three network anchors, each reaching 30 million
- Panel 3: 2010s many cable outlets + newspapers, viewers fragmented by outlet choice
- Panel 4: 2025 algorithm, invisible, reaching billions, optimized for engagement

Each panel shows the gatekeeping decision point and scale of reach.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Illustrates that gatekeeping has not disappeared, it has changed form and scale. Invisible algorithm is more powerful than visible editor.

**Style notes:** Could use icons (person → group → network → machine) to show abstraction increasing.

---

### [FIGURE 4: Framing the Same Story Three Ways]
**Placement:** Concept 1, "Framing: The Context That Becomes the Story"

**Description:** Same street scene (homelessness) photographed three times:
- Wide shot: city skyline, dozens of tents, structural problem visible
- Close shot: individual person's face, emotional, human
- Overhead shot: abstract arrangement of tents and supplies

Captions: "Thematic framing (structural)," "Episodic framing (individual)," "Statistical framing (numbers)."

**Pedagogical purpose:** Shows that framing is about what you include and exclude, what gets magnified and what recedes. Same reality, three completely different stories.

**Style notes:** Should use real images (with permission) or composite images. Point is that frame is a *choice*, visible in what is shown and what is not.

---

### [FIGURE 5: Media Regulation Matrix]
**Placement:** Concept 3, "Three Mechanisms of Constraint"

**Description:** Table with columns: Medium | Oversight | Constraint | Rationale

- Print | Courts, libel law | Truth standard, public figure vs. private figure | Protects reputation, privileges public debate
- Broadcast | FCC | Equal time, indecency limits, public interest | Spectrum scarcity, children in homes
- Cable | Minimal | Content warnings only | Multiple channels, choice, not scarce
- Internet | Platforms | Algorithm, content moderation | Private business, not government

**Pedagogical purpose:** Shows that different media face different regulation because of different economics and distribution. Not arbitrary, but logical.

**Style notes:** Could color-code by level of regulation (green = light, red = heavy) to make the asymmetry visible.

---

### [FIGURE 6: Fairness Doctrine Paradox]
**Placement:** Concept 3, "The Fairness Doctrine: A Cautionary Tale"

**Description:** Two-panel before/after:
- Before (1949–1987): Broadcast stations, required balanced coverage of controversies. Shows one station with "pro" and "con" content mixed. Outcome: less controversial programming overall.
- After (1987–present): Broadcast stations free from fairness requirement. Shows separate FOX News (conservative) and MSNBC (liberal) logos. Outcome: more partisan content, clearer ideological divisions, but zero obligation to balance.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Illustrates the unintended consequence problem. Regulation intended to promote fairness led to less speech. Deregulation led to more speech but more polarization. Neither is obviously correct.

**Style notes:** Could include speech bubbles showing "what citizens saw" under each regime.

## Sourcing Notes

All images should avoid:
- Dated references or fashions that make chapter feel old
- Overly artistic framing that obscures the point
- Partisan imagery that favors one political viewpoint

All images should use:
- High contrast and legibility (for mobile reading)
- Captions that do pedagogical work, not just labels
- Scale to show comparative scope where relevant

Most images are **composite/conceptual** rather than documentary. The chapter is about the machinery of media, not specific current events. Images should illuminate mechanism, not report news.
