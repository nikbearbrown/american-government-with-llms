## Image 1: Duverger's Law Arithmetic

**Placement:** Concept 2, immediately after "The Mechanism" section explaining plurality voting

**Description:**
Three-panel visualization showing vote distribution and winner under different voting systems.

*Panel A (Plurality Voting, Single-Member District):*
- Candidate A: 48% (Blue)
- Candidate B: 47% (Red)
- Candidate C: 5% (Green)
- Winner: A (most votes, not majority)
- Seats: A gets 1, B gets 0, C gets 0

*Panel B (Same votes, Proportional Representation):*
- Same percentages
- Seats allocated by percentage: A gets ~48 seats (out of 100), B gets ~47, C gets ~5
- Coalition required to govern

*Panel C (Next Election Cycle, After Duverger's Law Effect):*
- Plurality voters avoid wasting vote on C
- Candidate A: 52%
- Candidate B: 48%
- Candidate C: 0% (defected to A or B)
- Same two parties solidify

**Pedagogical Purpose:**
Shows mechanism of two-party lock-in. Not about preference, but about incentive structure. Voters rationally abandon third party after experiencing wasted votes.

**Style Notes:**
Clean, labeled bars or pie charts. Show "repeat" arrow from C to A to indicate vote migration. Color-code: blue, red, green for consistency.

## Image 2: Party Structure (Three Components)

**Placement:** Concept 3, "The Mechanism: The Three Components of a Party" section

**Description:**
Concentric circles or three-column comparison showing V.O. Key's framework.

*Central visualization (Concentric Circles):*
- Innermost circle: Party-in-Government (elected officials, ~500 people)
- Middle circle: Party Organization (staff, operatives, ~50,000 people)
- Outermost circle: Party-in-the-Electorate (voters, ~70M people for major parties)

*Arrows showing interaction:*
- Down arrow (Organization → Government): "Discipline, resources, campaign support"
- Up arrow (Government → Organization): "Electoral victory, mandate, legitimacy"
- Voters around outer circle: "Cast ballots, identify with party, pay attention intermittently"

*Time annotation:*
- Electorate: thinks in 4-year cycles (presidential elections)
- Organization: thinks in 2-year cycles (House elections)
- Government: thinks in 1-week cycles (next vote, next reelection fight)

**Pedagogical Purpose:**
Shows that "the party" is not monolithic. Three groups with different logics, timescales, incentives. Explains internal conflict (e.g., McCarthy speakership fight: party-in-government factions fight while organization tries to coordinate).

**Style Notes:**
Color each circle differently. Use font size to indicate scale of people. Include timeline labels showing different decision timescales.

## Image 3: The Breadth-Coherence Trade-off (Historical Arc)

**Placement:** Concept 1, after "Trade-off: Breadth Versus Coherence" section

**Description:**
Two-axis graph with timeline showing party coalition dynamics 1960-2024.

*Axes:*
- Horizontal: Ideological Coherence (Low ← → High)
- Vertical: Electoral Breadth/Seats (Few ← → Many)

*Plotting the historical arc:*
- 1960: Both parties at high-breadth, low-coherence (Dems hold segregationist Southerners and New Deal liberals; GOP holds Midwest progressives and Southern conservatives)
- 1990: Moving toward low-breadth, high-coherence (sorting begins)
- 2024: Both parties at high-coherence, lower-breadth (clearer ideologies, fewer swing voters)

*Annotations:*
- 1960: "Southern Strategy era begins" (GOP pivot toward South)
- 1980s-1990s: "Sorting accelerates" (realignment completes)
- 2010s: "Coherence complete, no moderates" (primary voters dominant)

*Trajectory arrows:*
- Democratic arrow: moving up-right (coherence gained, breadth lost)
- Republican arrow: moving up-right (coherence gained, breadth lost)

**Pedagogical Purpose:**
Visualizes the trade-off. Shows that higher coherence is not inevitable — it's a choice made by voters and parties over decades. Explains why 1950s APSA commission wanted more coherence (thought it was democratic), but we got less-governable parties as side effect.

**Style Notes:**
Use contrasting colors for party trajectories. Label specific election cycles or events (1964 Goldwater, 1972 McGovern, 1994 Republican wave, 2008 Obama, 2016 Trump). Quadrants could be labeled: top-left = unstable (losses coming), bottom-right = impossible (no one votes for incoherent majority).

## Image 4: McCarthy Speakership Timeline (The Freedom Caucus Holds)

**Placement:** Concept 1, Case Study section

**Description:**
Horizontal timeline showing McCarthy speakership from January 2023 to September 2023 with key concessions and failure points.

*Timeline structure:*
- January 7, 2023: McCarthy elected after 4-day floor fight, accepts historic concessions
- Concessions labeled:
  1. "Freedom Caucus gets motion-to-vacate power" (ability to call speaker vote anytime)
  2. "Committee removals approved" (members they dislike removed)
  3. "Leadership weakened" (McCarthy loses tools to enforce discipline)

- February-August 2023: McCarthy tries to govern
  - Attempt 1: Move major spending bill → blocked (Freedom Caucus withholds votes)
  - Attempt 2: Pass rule allowing Ukraine aid → blocked
  - Attempt 3: Move appropriations → blocked
  - Each attempt shows same dynamic: McCarthy needs their votes, they block

- September 3, 2023: Motion to vacate triggered
  - McCarthy ousted, becomes first speaker in history to lose gavel
  - Why he lost: made concessions that prevented him from aggregating majority for anything else

*Visual element:*
- McCarthy's effective authority shown as declining bar chart (100% in Jan, declining to ~20% by Sept)
- Freedom Caucus leverage shown as increasing bar chart (opposing)
- Crossing point where McCarthy loses ability to govern = August

**Pedagogical Purpose:**
Makes concrete the abstract idea that "party loses ability to enforce discipline when a faction becomes pivotal." Shows how aggregation trade-off plays out in real time. McCarthy chose coherence (accept their demands, keep them inside party) over breadth (negotiate with Democrats), and lost the ability to aggregate majority for anything.

**Style Notes:**
Clean timeline, clear labels for concessions. Show votes (222-213 split initially) to make clear why 30 votes = power. Could add annotation: "In proportional system, Freedom Caucus would get 30 seats and negotiate post-election, not imperil speaker."

## Image 5: Geographic Sorting of Ideology (Senate 1960 vs. 2024)

**Placement:** Concept 2 or Integration section, showing the sorting effect

**Description:**
Two maps of U.S., side-by-side comparison: 1960 and 2024 Senate seats by party.

*1960 Map:*
- Southern states: mostly Democratic Senators (Texas, Oklahoma, Arkansas, Georgia, Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, Carolinas) — but conservative on race
- Northeastern states: mix (Massachusetts, Connecticut have Republicans; New York has both)
- Midwest: mix (Minnesota, Wisconsin have both parties)
- Western states: scattered

*Key annotation:* "Liberal Northern Democrats coexist with segregationist Southern Democrats in same party"

*2024 Map:*
- Southern states: all or nearly all Republican Senators
- Northeastern/coastal states: all or nearly all Democratic Senators
- Midwest: Republican-leaning (except Minnesota, Illinois)
- Western states: sorted by urbanization (CA, WA, OR blue; MT, ID, UT, AZ red)

*Key annotation:* "Ideology now predicts geography; geography now predicts party"

**Pedagogical Purpose:**
Visual proof of sorting. Makes clear that the two-party system is the same mathematically (still two parties) but politically very different because coalition composition has changed. Explains why compromise becomes harder: a Republican senator from South now shares ideology with a Republican senator from Midwest (both conservative); a Democratic senator from California shares ideology with a Democratic senator from Massachusetts (both progressive). No cross-party ideological pairing.

**Style Notes:**
Use solid colors: blue for Democratic seats, red for Republican seats, gray for split/unclear. Use inset for specific state examples (Texas: 2 Dem senators in 1960 → 2 Rep in 2024). Could add annotation showing where sorting accelerated most (1980s-2000s).

## Image 6: The Wasted Vote Problem (Voter Calculus)

**Placement:** Concept 2, immediately after introducing Duverger's Law

**Description:**
Decision tree showing voter's thought process under plurality voting.

*Starting point:* "I prefer Green Party candidate on climate"

*First branching:*
- "What % will Green get?" → 5% (based on previous election)
- "What % will Dem/Rep get?" → 48% and 47%

*Second branching:*
- "If I vote Green, I help my least-preferred party win" → Yes (vote splitting)
- "If I vote Dem (second choice), I guarantee Rep doesn't win" → Yes

*Conclusion path:*
- Vote Dem despite preferring Green
- Repeat: Green loses election
- Next cycle: Green donor pool shrinks
- Next cycle: Green infrastructure shrinks
- Next cycle: Green becomes unviable

*Circular arrow* showing feedback loop: low viability → wasted votes → lower support → lower viability

**Pedagogical Purpose:**
Makes tangible why rational voters abandon third parties. Not because third party is bad, but because the arithmetic of plurality voting makes the vote wasted. Shows that system incentive, not voter preference, locks in two parties.

**Style Notes:**
Use decision tree format (diamond nodes for decisions, rectangle nodes for outcomes). Color-code: green for preferred choice, blue/red for strategic alternatives. Use thickness of arrows to show likelihood/frequency.

## Image 7: Party Discipline Enforcement (Tools & Their Limits)

**Placement:** Concept 3, after explaining enforcement mechanisms

**Description:**
Matrix showing party leadership tools vs. politician vulnerability.

*Rows (Tools):*
1. Primary challenge funding
2. Committee assignments
3. Leadership positions
4. Campaign money/endorsement
5. Earmarks for home district
6. Media/messaging support

*Columns (Politician Vulnerability):*
- Safe district (won by 20+ points)
- Swing district (within 5 points)
- Swing state (close Senate race)
- First-term politician
- Established veteran

*Cells colored:*
- Green (tool very effective): primary challenge to swing-district politician, committee removal from first-term
- Yellow (tool moderately effective): committee assignment changes, campaign money withdrawal
- Red (tool ineffective): primary challenge to safe-district politician (primary loses = general election loss), earmarks threatened

*Annotation at bottom:* "Why McCarthy lost speakership: Freedom Caucus in safe districts were immune to most tools; their defection cost him votes but not their votes"

**Pedagogical Purpose:**
Shows why party discipline weakens as seats become safer. In a sorted system, safe-district politicians face primary threat from own party's wing, not from opposition. This inverts the leverage: party leaders cannot threaten them, but primary voters can. Explains why ideological extremes have power.

**Style Notes:**
Heat map format (red = weak, yellow = medium, green = strong). Include specific examples: McCarthy's concessions to Freedom Caucus in red cells.

## Image 8: Three-Part Party in Conflict (The 2024 Harris Campaign)

**Placement:** Concept 3, Case Study section

**Description:**
Three circles (party-in-government, organization, electorate) with conflicting arrows between them during 2024 campaign.

*Party-in-Government (sitting officials, donors):*
- Message: "Unify behind Harris, move to center to win moderates"
- Constituency: college-educated suburbs, wealthy donors, establishmentarians

*Party Organization:*
- Message: "Harris is our nominee, support the platform"
- Constituency: campaign staff, operatives, state party officials
- Tool available: messaging, get-out-vote

*Party-in-Electorate (voters):*
- Subset A (45%): "Harris move to center, losing us on Gaza/healthcare"
- Subset B (35%): "Harris too far left on identity issues, scary moderates"
- Subset C (20%): "We'll vote for her anyway because Trump is worse"

*Conflict arrows:*
- Electorate → Organization: "Your discipline not working, real disagreement"
- Organization → Electorate: "Trust us, stay on message"
- Electorate → Government: "You nominated her, she doesn't represent us"
- Government → Electorate: "Best option available, forward together"

*Outcome annotation:* "Electorate fragmented, organization unable to bind. Campaign discipline attempted, failed. Electorate chose defection (low turnout or third party) over compromise."

**Pedagogical Purpose:**
Shows real-time party fracture under voting-system stress. Demonstrates that loose party identification (traditional American advantage) becomes weakness when voters have clear ideological identity and can afford to defect. Shows why the three components cannot always be coordinated.

**Style Notes:**
Use separate boxes for each component, show communication arrows. Color code: green for alignment, red for conflict. Include polling numbers showing electorate split (made-up illustrative numbers fine with caveat).
