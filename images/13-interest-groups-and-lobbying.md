# Image Placements and Descriptions: Chapter 13

## Image 1: The Lobbying Landscape
**Placement**: Section 3 (Inside Lobbying and Outside Lobbying), after the explanation of inside lobbying

**Description**: A split-screen comparison. Left side: a congressional office, one lobbyist sitting across from a staffer, papers and documents on a table, conversation happening. Right side: a street rally or press conference, multiple people with signs, media present, public visible. The contrast is visual: inside (small, quiet, technical) vs. outside (large, public, emotional).

**Pedagogical purpose**: Make visible the two channels of influence that are central to the chapter. Students often have images of protests and rallies but not of inside lobbying (because it is private). This image shows that both exist and look completely different.

**Style notes**: Realistic but not stock-photo flat. Use real images if possible (congressional hallways, real lobbying offices, real protests). Avoid staged photos with too-perfect people. The point is that inside lobbying is mundane—just people in offices. Outside lobbying is visible—it happens in public.

## Image 2: The Iron Triangle
**Placement**: Section 4 (Money, Access, Whose Voice Counts), in the subsection on "The iron triangle"

**Description**: A geometric diagram: three points (congressional committee, executive agency, interest group) connected by arrows showing the flow of resources and information. Label each arrow with what flows: "budget authority, legislation" from committee to agency; "political support, votes" from interest group to committee; "information, political support" from interest group to agency; etc. Show the loop of dependencies that makes the triangle "iron."

**Pedagogical purpose**: Make concrete the abstract concept of iron triangle. The chapter explains it verbally; the diagram lets students see the structure. The diagram also shows why it is hard to break in from outside—if you are not part of this triangle, you lack the leverage to influence any point.

**Style notes**: Clean, minimal. Use different colors for each point. Make the arrows directional and labeled. Show that information, money, and political support are what flow through the triangle—not abstractions, but concrete things.

## Image 3: Campaign Spending Growth (Citizens United)
**Placement**: Section 4, in the subsection on "Citizens United and the money floodgates"

**Description**: A line graph showing campaign spending over time. X-axis: election years (2000, 2004, 2008, 2012, 2016, 2020). Y-axis: total spending in billions. Show a relatively flat line until 2010, then a sharp uptick starting in 2012 (first election after Citizens United). Include separate lines for different sources if space: PAC spending, super PAC spending, dark money, individual donations. The visual should make obvious: something changed in 2010.

**Pedagogical purpose**: Data visible. The chapter claims Citizens United opened the floodgates. Show the floodgates opening. Students often read numbers passively; a chart makes growth visceral.

**Style notes**: Simple two-axis chart. Label the 2010 Citizens United decision on the x-axis with a vertical annotation. Do not clutter with too many data series; 3-4 series maximum. Make the uptick obvious.

## Image 4: Interest Group Influence (Gilens & Page)
**Placement**: Section 4, after the Gilens & Page study is described

**Description**: A bar chart showing adoption rates of policies. Two bars: one for "wealthy voters support" (50%+ adoption), one for "poor voters support, wealthy oppose" (<10% adoption). The gap should be visually stark. Include a third bar showing "business groups vs. public interest groups" (2:1 ratio). The visual drives home: there is measurable inequality in representation.

**Pedagogical purpose**: Evidence made visible. The chapter claims representation is unequal. Show the inequality quantified. Let students see the numbers and draw conclusions.

**Style notes**: Clear, minimalist bars. Use consistent colors. Label the adoption rates directly on the bars. Do not add decoration that obscures the data.

## Image 5: Lobbying Firm Revenues (Top 10)
**Placement**: Section 4, in subsection on "Scale and sophistication"

**Description**: A horizontal bar chart ranking top 10 lobbying firms by annual revenue (from chapter: Akin Gump $49.9M, Brownstein Hyatt $48.4M, etc.). The revenue differences are small relative to the scale, so use a truncated y-axis if needed to show variation. The point is not just the top firm but the tier of firms. Every top firm makes $15M+.

**Pedagogical purpose**: Make concrete the claim that "wealthy interests can hire sophisticated lobbying firms with dozens of staff." Show what that costs—tens of millions annually. Let students see that this is a barrier—a grassroots organization cannot compete here.

**Style notes**: Horizontal bars make firm names readable. Use a light grid background. Do not overcomplicate. The point is: there is a tier of expensive lobbying capacity that only wealthy interests can access.

## Image 6: Dark Money Growth
**Placement**: Section 4, in the subsection on "Dark money"

**Description**: A stacked-area chart showing dark money spending over time (2012, 2016, 2020). Show the area growing from ~$300M (2012) to ~$800M+ (2016) to billions (2020). Optional: if space, also show disclosed spending in a different color for comparison. The visual drives home the exponential growth and the increasing invisibility of funding sources.

**Pedagogical purpose**: The chapter claims dark money has "exploded." Show the explosion. Students often hear "dark money" and understand it abstractly as "hidden." The chart makes it concrete: it is growing, and it is invisible.

**Style notes**: Use a simple two-color system: disclosed spending one color, dark money another. Let the area grow visibly. Label election years on x-axis. Let the visual speak—the growth is striking without much annotation.

## Design Consistency Notes

All images should:
- Use a consistent color palette (aligned with the book's house style)
- Include clear axis labels and legends where applicable
- Avoid stock photos; prefer diagrams, charts, and realistic images
- Be readable at book size (images should not be smaller than 3 inches × 3 inches)
- Have alt text for accessibility (description of what the image shows and why it matters)

Tone: Educational and clear, not sensationalist. The data is striking enough—do not add inflaming visual language.
