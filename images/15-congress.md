## [FIGURE: The Great Compromise — Two Chambers]

**Placement:** Chapter opening, after TL;DR  
**Description:** A two-panel diagram. Left panel shows Virginia Plan (large state): pyramid with population at base, few delegates at top. Right panel shows New Jersey Plan (small state): flat rectangle with equal delegates at top. Center panel shows Connecticut Compromise: House (pyramid, proportional to population) stacked on Senate (flat, equal representation).

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Illustrate the structural solution to the deadlock. Readers see why the compromise satisfied neither side completely but gave both something. The visual reinforces that bicameralism is a compromise, not a discovery.

**Style Notes:** Arrows showing the disagreement, then the solution. Use state size to reinforce the logic (large states get more House seats, small states get equal Senate seats).

## [FIGURE: Incumbency Advantage — 2020 Cycle]

**Placement:** Concept 2 section  
**Description:** Bar chart with four metrics. (1) Average House incumbent fundraising vs. average House challenger fundraising (show the 4:1 ratio). (2) House incumbent reelection rates (85–100%) vs. Senate incumbent reelection rates (roughly 80%). (3) Name recognition: share of voters who recognize incumbent's name vs. challenger's name. **Pedagogical Purpose:** Show that incumbency advantage is not one factor but four factors compounding. Each bar represents one force. Together, they create an nearly insurmountable obstacle for challengers.

**Style Notes:** Use consistent colors. Show the scale (4:1 fundraising ratio) clearly. The point is that individual factors are large; combined, they are huge.

## [FIGURE: The Legislative Process — Classic vs. Modern]

**Placement:** Concept 3 subsection on how a bill becomes law  
**Description:** Flowchart with two parallel tracks. Classic process (left): Bill introduced → Committee → Hearings and markup → Floor debate and vote → Other chamber (repeat) → Conference committee → President. Modern process (right): Bill introduced (or Budget resolution) → Leadership assignment → Floor consideration (possibly skipping committee) → Vote (51 or 60 votes depending on filibuster status) → President. Annotations show key differences: classic is longer; modern is faster but requires strong party leadership control.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Illustrate how Congress's actual procedure has changed. The classic process is described in civics textbooks. The modern process is how legislation actually passes. Readers see the difference between theory and practice.

**Style Notes:** Use different colors for classic vs. modern paths. Highlight the decision points where bills can die (committees, conference).

## [FIGURE: Congressional Approval vs. Incumbent Reelection]

**Placement:** Chapter integration section  
**Description:** Two-axis graph. X-axis is time (years 1990–2024). Y-axis is approval rating (0–100%). One line shows Congressional approval rating (generally 15–40%, spiking in 2001 after 9/11 and occasionally in 2017). Second line shows incumbency reelection rates (consistently 85–95% in House). The paradox: institutional approval is low, individual reelection is high.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Make the paradox visible. The contradiction between hating Congress and reelecting your representative is the core puzzle of modern representation.

**Style Notes:** Use contrasting colors. Annotations mark key events (9/11 spike, government shutdown, polarization).

## [FIGURE: The Filibuster and Cloture]

**Placement:** Concept 3 subsection on the filibuster  
**Description:** Timeline showing filibuster rules changes. (1) Original rule (no cloture available). (2) 1917: Cloture rule adopted (2/3 majority required). (3) 1975: Cloture threshold reduced to 3/5 (60 votes). (4) Modern era: Modern filibuster (threat of filibuster, requiring 60 votes to proceed) vs. traditional filibuster (senator takes floor and talks). Show visually that the threshold has remained at 60 for ~50 years.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Show that the filibuster has evolved. Most modern filibusters are not speeches but threats. The 60-vote threshold is not constitutional but created by Senate rules.

**Style Notes:** Use a timeline format. Show that rules changes have made filibusters easier to threaten and harder to overcome via cloture.
