# Images: Chapter 16 — The Presidency

## [FIGURE: Constitutional Convention, September 1787]
**Placement:** Chapter Opening, after first paragraph  
**Description:** Engraving or painting of the Convention floor. Delegates seated around tables. James Wilson and James Madison at the fore, in conversation. Edmund Randolph standing. One delegate (clearly Hamilton) gesturing expansively. Historical dress, period furniture.  
**Pedagogical Purpose:** Anchor the opening narrative in a specific place and moment. Make the framers' uncertainty visible—these are people *debating*, not people certain of the answer.  
**Style Notes:** Historical accuracy matters. Not a romanticized tableau. Show tension, disagreement. The machinery of disagreement is the point.

## [FIGURE: Radio Broadcasting Setup, 1930s]
**Placement:** Concept 2, after "FDR broadcast thirty fireside chats..."  
**Description:** FDR at a microphone in a formal room (the White House, probably). Microphone in foreground. FDR in profile, speaking clearly. Some technical equipment visible (recording, transmission apparatus). Caption: "Millions of Americans heard this voice in their homes."  
**Pedagogical Purpose:** Make the technology *visible*. Students often think "radio" abstractly. This image shows: a voice, a microphone, a man speaking directly to millions. Illustrate how technology is power.  
**Style Notes:** Photograph or high-quality reproduction. Period appropriate. Show the simplicity of the setup—a man, a voice, millions of listeners.

## [FIGURE: Television vs. Radio Timeline]
**Placement:** Concept 2, after discussing technology shifts  
**Description:** Simple timeline. Left side: radio era (1930s-50s). Middle: television era (1950s-80s). Right side: internet era (1990s-2026). For each era, show: (1) the technology, (2) the reach, (3) an example president, (4) the power mechanism.
- Radio: FDR's fireside chats. Millions heard his voice. Trust built through intimacy.
- Television: Kennedy's appearance in 1960 debate. Nixon sweat visible. Visual presence matters.
- Internet: Trump's tweets. Direct access. No editorial filter. Millions reach in seconds.
**Pedagogical Purpose:** Show how the *same president* with different technologies has different reach and power. The Constitution didn't change; the machinery did.  
**Style Notes:** Clean graphic. Information hierarchy clear. Avoid clutter.

## [FIGURE: Lincoln's Suspension of Habeas Corpus]
**Placement:** Concept 2, crisis section  
**Description:** Historical document (Lincoln's proclamation) or a formal engraving depicting Lincoln signing the suspension order. Somber tone. Cabinet members present or in background.  
**Pedagogical Purpose:** Make visible the moment when a president claims power the Constitution doesn't explicitly grant. This is not abstract constitutional law. This is a president deciding he has power he doesn't have, and acting anyway.  
**Style Notes:** Primary source image preferred. Shows the gravity of the decision.

## [FIGURE: FDR's Bank Holiday Proclamation]
**Placement:** Concept 2, crisis section  
**Description:** Image of the actual proclamation text, or a newspaper headline from March 6, 1933: "BANKS CLOSED BY PRESIDENT'S ORDER." Shows the speed and unilateral nature of the action.  
**Pedagogical Purpose:** Illustrate how crisis enables unilateral action. Congress did not authorize this. The president acted. Only later did Congress approve.  
**Style Notes:** Historical primary source or reproduction. Shows both the text and the public reaction (headline).

## [FIGURE: Japanese American Internment Camp]
**Placement:** Concept 2, case study on precedent  
**Description:** Photograph from internment camp (archival, many exist). Shows barracks, guard towers, or families in cramped conditions. Sobering image. Not sensationalized, but not whitewashed either.  
**Pedagogical Purpose:** Make the human cost visible. This is not abstract constitutional law. This is 110,000 people—many citizens—deprived of liberty based on executive order. Force students to confront what "emergency power" means in practice.  
**Style Notes:** Primary source photograph. Respectful but direct. Powerful image that will stay with students.

## [FIGURE: Executive Orders Over Time]
**Placement:** Concept 2, precedent section  
**Description:** Line graph showing number of executive orders issued per presidency, 1789-2026. Should show:
- Relatively low numbers in 19th century
- Spike under Theodore Roosevelt, FDR
- Increased use in 20th century
- Modern presidents: moderate (relative to Roosevelt/FDR) but higher than 19th century
- Trump spike visible in recent data
**Pedagogical Purpose:** Show the *accumulation* of unilateral presidential action over time. Not one crisis, but many crises adding to the baseline. Precedent is visible in the data.  
**Style Notes:** Clean graph. Y-axis labeled "Number of Executive Orders." X-axis labeled by presidency. Color coding by era (19th/20th/21st century) can help.

## [FIGURE: Bill Clinton & Hillary Clinton, Health Care Reform Task Force]
**Placement:** Concept 3, domestic policy constraint section  
**Description:** Photograph of the Clintons at a public event related to health care reform. Hillary Clinton in formal dress, speaking or listening. Show the engagement and effort. But caption should note: "Despite the presidency and congressional majorities, Clinton's health care reform failed."  
**Pedagogical Purpose:** Illustrate that even a powerful president with the bully pulpit cannot overcome congressional opposition in domestic policy. Make students see: power is not absolute; constraint is real.  
**Style Notes:** Archival photograph. Professional, formal. Shows the seriousness of the effort.

## [FIGURE: Gulf of Tonkin Incident Map]
**Placement:** Concept 3, foreign policy latitude section  
**Description:** Map of the Gulf of Tonkin, showing the location of the alleged attacks on U.S. destroyers (USS Maddox, USS Turner Joy). Show the dates (August 2 and August 4, 1964). Indicate the ambiguity—where exactly did the second attack occur? Show naval positions.  
**Pedagogical Purpose:** Ground the Gulf of Tonkin in geography. Students often treat it as an abstract event ("Congress passed a resolution"). Seeing the map makes the question concrete: Where did this attack happen? Was it real? This enables discussion of deception, fog of war, and how presidents can use ambiguity to justify action.  
**Style Notes:** Clear map. Legend for ship positions, dates, coastline. Minimal text.

## [FIGURE: War Powers Resolution: Congress vs. President]
**Placement:** Concept 3, trade-off section on war powers  
**Description:** Two-panel diagram:
- **Left panel (Congress's intent):** "War Powers Resolution (1973): President must notify Congress within 48 hours. Congress must authorize within 60 days."
- **Right panel (Practice):** "Presidents claim emergency exemption. Congress threatens to enforce but never actually defunds operations."
- Arrow between: "50+ years of standoff"
**Pedagogical Purpose:** Make visible the gap between what the law says and what actually happens. This is not a constitutional debate in the abstract. This is a real institutional failure/standoff.  
**Style Notes:** Two-column layout. Simple, clear language. Use icons or colors to distinguish Congress vs. President.

---

## [FIGURE: Formal vs. Informal Presidential Powers]
**Placement:** Integration section  
**Description:** Two concentric circles or a Venn diagram:
- **Inner circle (Formal Powers):** Enumerated in Constitution. Veto, commander-in-chief, pardon, appointments, etc. Fixed. Not easily expanded.
- **Outer circle (Informal Powers):** Not in Constitution but claimed/exercised. Executive orders, signing statements, executive privilege, foreign policy latitude, media/bully pulpit. Expand over time through precedent.
- **Area of overlap:** Powers that are both formal and informal (e.g., commander-in-chief—formal grant, but informal expansion through precedent).
**Pedagogical Purpose:** Show the student that the presidency operates in two registers: what the document says (formal) and what presidents *do* (informal). The informal has grown to rival the formal.  
**Style Notes:** Clean diagram. Color-code the circles. Use short, concrete examples in each section.

## Image Sourcing & Rights Notes

- Constitutional Convention: Multiple historical paintings exist (public domain, Library of Congress)
- FDR at microphone: Several photos available (public domain, FDR Library, NARA)
- Lincoln suspending habeas corpus: Historical prints (public domain)
- Japanese internment camps: Ansel Adams, others photographed camps (some public domain, some restricted). Check Densho.org, NARA for public domain versions.
- Gulf of Tonkin: Contemporary newspaper photos, maps (public domain, Library of Congress)
- Executive Orders graph: Create custom using historical data from Office of the President, HistoryLab at Columbia

- **Date (if historical)**
