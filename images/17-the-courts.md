# Image Placement Guide: The Courts

## [FIGURE: John Marshall portrait, circa 1810-1835]

**Placement:** Chapter opening, after the Marbury anecdote (end of section 1).

**Description:** Formal portrait. Marshall in robes, serious expression, holding papers. The image should convey: this is the man who seized power by declining to use it.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Anchor the historical narrative. Marshall isn't abstract; he's a person who made a specific choice in a specific moment. His face should make the story concrete.

**Style notes:** Grayscale or muted color. Formal but not stiff. The papers in his hand are important—they represent the law he's interpreting.

## [FIGURE: Three-tier diagram of the federal court system]

**Placement:** Concept 2, at the start of "The mechanism: three tiers, precedent as cement."

**Description:** Inverted pyramid or three-level chart. Bottom: 94 district courts (trial courts, judges alone or with jury). Middle: 13 circuit/appellate courts (three-judge panels). Top: Supreme Court (nine justices). Arrows show appeals flowing upward. Labels should indicate jurisdiction and role.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Make the structure visible and spatial. Students need to understand that most cases end at the bottom, few make it to the middle, fewer still reach the top. The shape should convey this graphically.

**Style notes:** Clear labels. Color coding optional (bottom = trial, middle = appellate, top = final) but not necessary. The key is showing that it's hierarchical and that movement upward is rare.

---

## [FIGURE: Timeline: Plessy (1896) to Brown (1954)]

**Placement:** Concept 2, in the trade-off section or case study.

**Description:** Two boxes, separated by 58 years. Left box: *Plessy v. Ferguson*, "separate but equal" is constitutional. Right box: *Brown v. Board of Education*, "separate but equal" is unconstitutional. Below or beside, note key changes: Court membership changed. Reasoning changed. Culture changed.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Show that precedent can be overruled when circumstances and membership warrant it. The timeline format makes clear that time and changing membership are factors courts consider.

**Style notes:** Simple. The contrast between the two rulings is the point. No need for elaborate graphics.

---

## [FIGURE: Supreme Court docket funnel]

**Placement:** Concept 3, in the "cert. and the Rule of Four" section.

**Description:** Funnel diagram. Top (wide): ~7,000 petitions per year. Middle (narrower): cert. pool, four-justice vote. Bottom (narrow mouth): ~80 cases granted. The narrowing should be dramatic and visual.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Make clear that the Supreme Court is supremely selective. Almost all petitions are denied. The justices choose which constitutional questions to answer. That choice is power.

**Style notes:** Label each stage with numbers. A single number or percentage isn't enough; show the funnel visually so the reader sees the diminishment.

---

## [FIGURE: Andrew Jackson, 1820s-1830s portrait]

**Placement:** Concept 1, in the Jackson anecdote (when discussing what happens when the executive defies the Court).

**Description:** Portrait or contemporary image. Jackson as president, stern, in military dress. Include a quote below: "John Marshall has made his decision; now let him enforce it."

**Pedagogical purpose:** Show that the Court's power depends entirely on voluntary compliance. This portrait should make Jackson's defiance concrete and memorable. A president could refuse. He did.

**Style notes:** Contemporary image from the period. The defiance is dramatic; let the image and quote speak.

---

## [FIGURE: Little Rock Central High School, 1957, integrating]

**Placement:** Integration section or connections forward, when discussing implementation of court orders.

**Description:** School building. National Guard troops (federalized by Eisenhower's order) standing to protect Black students entering the school. The contrast between the guards and the school makes visible what it took to enforce the Court's desegregation order.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Show that enforcement isn't automatic. Court orders require action. Sometimes military action. This image shows the friction between a Court's judgment and reality.

**Style notes:** Historical photograph if available. The mood should be serious, tense. The point is that implementing a court order can be difficult and contested.

---

## [FIGURE: Current Supreme Court justices, 2025]

**Placement:** Concept 3, when discussing who picks judges and ideological composition.

**Description:** Group photo or individual portraits of current justices. Could label as conservative (Roberts, Thomas, Alito, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, Barrett) and liberal (Breyer if still on bench, Sotomayor, Kagan) if the data are accurate. Or simply show the nine.

**Pedagogical purpose:** Make clear that the Court is made of people. These are the nine. They have faces, biographies, ideologies. Who they are shapes what the Court decides.

**Style notes:** Recent photograph. Official Supreme Court photos are available. Labeling ideology is optional; the reader can research it. The key is showing the Court is a group of individuals.

---

## [FIGURE: Oral argument diagram or photo]

**Placement:** Concept 3, in the cert. and decision-making section.

**Description:** Diagram showing the layout of the courtroom during oral argument. Justices on the bench above. Lawyers at podiums below. Audience behind. Or a photograph of oral argument in session if available (no photo of current arguments allowed, but historical photos might be).

**Pedagogical purpose:** Make the courtroom concrete. Students should visualize where people stand, how justices can interrupt, what oral argument looks like.

**Style notes:** If a diagram, keep it simple and to scale. The point is spatial understanding, not artistic rendering.

---

## Visual rhythm and balance

- **Chapter opening**: Marbury portrait (establishes person, stakes)
- **Concept 1**: Jackson portrait (establishes consequence of Court's power being conditional)
- **Concept 2**: Three-tier diagram (establishes structure), then Plessy-to-Brown timeline (establishes that precedent can change)
- **Concept 3**: Docket funnel (establishes that selectivity is power), Supreme Court photo (establishes that justices are people)
- **Integration/Connections**: Little Rock photo (establishes that implementation is hard), oral argument diagram (establishes courtroom reality)

No more than 5-6 images total. Each should serve a distinct pedagogical purpose. The chapter is prose-heavy; images should break it up and make key concepts spatial or visual rather than just textual.
