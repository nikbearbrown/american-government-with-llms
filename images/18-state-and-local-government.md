# Images: State and Local Government

## 1. State Political Cultures Map

**Placement:** Section 2 (after introducing Elazar's framework)

**Description:** Map of the United States shaded by political culture type: moralistic (upper Midwest, West Coast, New England) in one color, individualistic (mid-Atlantic, parts of Midwest) in another, traditionalistic (South, parts of Southwest) in a third. Should show the original Elazar distribution from the 1960s-1970s.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Visual anchor for the three political culture types. Helps readers see the geographic clustering and understand that culture has roots in settlement and migration patterns. The map is not meant to be accurate to 2026 (culture has changed with migration) but rather to show what Elazar observed and what persists as baseline.

**Style Notes:** Clean state boundaries, clear color differentiation, legend. No annotations cluttering the map. The purpose is visual pattern recognition, not detailed accuracy.

## 2. State Legislature Professionalism Index (Squire Index)

**Placement:** Section 2 (after discussing variation in state legislatures)

**Description:** Chart showing states ranked by legislative professionalism (Squire Index). Y-axis is professionalism score (0-1.0). X-axis is states, ordered left to right by score. California, New York, Pennsylvania at the high end. New Hampshire, North Dakota, Wyoming, South Dakota at the low end. Color code or annotate a few key states.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Shows that state institutional variation is real and measurable. Illustrates the trade-off: professional legislatures require more investment (salary, staff, year-round meeting) but produce stronger legislatures that can check governors. Part-time legislatures are cheaper but weaker.

**Style Notes:** Simple bar chart. Include a note explaining the Squire Index (measured by salary, length of session, staff size). Do not need to show all fifty states—focus on extremes and a few in the middle.

## 3. Three County Government Systems (Diagram)

**Placement:** Section 4 (introducing county structures)

**Description:** Three simple diagrams showing:
1. Commission system: voters elect commissioners; commissioners are both legislature and executive
2. Council-administrator system: voters elect council; council appoints administrator; administrator runs operations
3. Council-elected executive system: voters elect council and elected executive; executive is separate from council

Each diagram should use boxes and arrows to show the flow of authority.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Shows the three structural models clearly so readers can understand the trade-offs (concentration vs. separation of power). Helps clarify that "same title, different powers" is the key variable.

**Style Notes:** Keep minimal, schematic. The point is structure, not decoration.

## 4. Municipal Government: Mayor-Council vs. Council-Manager

**Placement:** Section 4 (introducing city structures)

**Description:** Two diagrams comparing:
1. Mayor-council system: voters elect mayor and council; mayor is executive; council is legislature; mayor may have strong or weak powers
2. Council-manager system: voters elect council (and sometimes mayor from council); council appoints manager; manager is chief administrative officer; council is policy-setting

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Shows the difference between political leadership (mayor or council) and professional administration (manager). Helps readers understand that "who makes policy" and "who runs the bureaucracy" are separate questions.

**Style Notes:** Parallel structure in the diagrams so readers can compare directly. Arrow from voters to elected positions, separate arrow to manager (showing manager is appointed, not elected).

## 5. Property Tax Revenue Stability Over Time

**Placement:** Section 5 (discussing property tax as revenue source)

**Description:** Line graph showing property tax revenue in a sample county or state over 20-30 years, showing the cliff during the 2008 housing crash and gradual recovery. Include a secondary line or annotation showing population growth or service demand, to illustrate the mismatch: population grew but revenue fell.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Makes concrete the fiscal squeeze: revenue is volatile, but service demands are steady or growing. This is the structural problem local governments face.

**Style Notes:** Clean line graph. Highlight the 2008-2010 period clearly. Optional: show what property tax revenue would have been without Prop 13 or similar caps, to illustrate the impact of tax limitation laws.

## 6. Sources of Local Government Revenue (Pie Chart or Stacked Bar)

**Placement:** Section 5 (discussing local fiscal constraints)

**Description:** Pie chart showing the breakdown of local government revenue: property tax (largest slice), sales tax, user fees, state grants, federal grants, other. Or use a stacked bar chart showing the same breakdown for cities, counties, and special districts separately, to show variation.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Shows that local governments are heavily dependent on property tax and that federal/state grants are significant but variable. Helps readers understand why local governments are vulnerable to property value fluctuations and state/federal funding cuts.

**Style Notes:** Clear labeling of each slice/segment. Include percentages. If using separate breakdown for cities vs. counties vs. special districts, make the comparison easy—parallel structure.

## 7. Federal, State, and Local Government Employment Over Time

**Placement:** Section 6 (discussing state/local capacity) or as alternative to revenue chart

**Description:** Line graph showing employment in federal government, state governments, and local governments from 1960 to 2020. Should show that local government employment grew much faster than federal or state, reflecting the shift of service delivery to the local level.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Illustrates the principle: the federal government sets policy, states implement it, local governments deliver it. Employment patterns reflect that hierarchy.

**Style Notes:** Three lines with clear legend. Scale the Y-axis to make growth patterns visible.

## 8. Voter Turnout by Election Type

**Placement:** Section 6 (discussing political culture and engagement) or as introduction to Section 4

**Description:** Bar chart comparing voter turnout for presidential elections, state elections, local elections, and special district elections. Should show that turnout drops dramatically from federal to local (presidential might be 50-60%, local might be 10-20%, special district might be 5%).

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Challenges the assumption that local government is "closer to voters so voters care more." In fact, voters participate far less in local elections despite local government affecting them daily. Raises questions about why and about the health of local democracy.

**Style Notes:** Four bars (or ranges if using decade-by-decade data). Color code to distinguish election types. Include a note: "Source: U.S. Census Bureau, state election offices" or similar.

## Notes on Image Strategy

**Avoid:** Abstract diagrams disconnected from real examples. All images should illustrate concrete points made in the text.

**Lean toward:** Maps, organizational charts, and time-series data that make structural patterns visible.

**Check:** Ensure all images use actual data (or are clearly labeled as illustrative). If using states as examples, name them. If using aggregate data, cite the source.

**Captions:** Keep captions brief (one sentence). Let the image speak; the text explains implications.
