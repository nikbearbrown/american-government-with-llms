# Images: The Bureaucracy

## [FIGURE: The Federal Bureaucratic Hierarchy]

**Placement:** Concept 1, after the section on "The first American bureaucracy."

**Visual:** A simplified organizational chart showing how the cabinet (and agencies) fit within the executive branch. Top: President. Below: OMB, Executive Office. Below: Cabinet departments (State, Treasury, Defense, etc.) and Independent Agencies. Below cabinet: Undersecretaries, bureaus, offices, down to individual bureaucrats. Include a note showing how this looks from George Washington's three departments to the modern structure.

**Description:** This shows the hierarchical structure Weber described. A clear chain of command flowing downward. Each position has a defined role. Authority is nested—a bureau chief answers to an undersecretary who answers to a secretary who answers to the president.

**Pedagogical purpose:** To make concrete what "hierarchical" means. To show how government is organized and how individual bureaucrats fit within it.

**Style notes:** Use a traditional org chart format. Gray boxes for political appointees (subject to change with administration), green boxes for career civil service (protected by merit). This visual distinction helps students understand the two-tier system.

## [FIGURE: The Spoils System vs. Merit System]

**Placement:** Concept 2, after the section on "The Pendleton Act: merit replaces loyalty."

**Visual:** A two-panel comparison. Left panel (spoils): An incoming president replaces most federal employees—arrows flowing out for old employees, new arrows flowing in for party supporters. Caption: "Under spoils, political power changed government personnel." Right panel (merit): An incoming president changes only cabinet secretaries and a few top appointees—most of the bureaucracy remains unchanged. Caption: "Under merit, elected leadership changed, bureaucracy remained largely stable."

**Description:** This visually contrasts the two systems. It shows why the spoils system was problematic (chaotic turnover, incompetence) and why the merit system created the accountability problem (bureaucrats who survive changes in administration).

**Pedagogical purpose:** To help students grasp the consequences of the shift from spoils to merit. To see that the two systems are trade-offs, not a simple upgrade.

**Style notes:** Use arrows to show movement. Bold, clear labels. Possibly use color to distinguish the groups (politicians in one color, career bureaucrats in another).

## [FIGURE: The Federal General Schedule: Grades and Pay]

**Placement:** Concept 2, in the section discussing modern civil service hiring.

**Visual:** A simplified version of the actual Federal General Schedule. Rows: GS-1 through GS-15. Columns: Steps 1–10 (representing seniority/time in grade). Color gradient showing salary increase as you go right (more steps) and down (higher grades). Include example: GS-12, Step 5 = $75,706–$98,422.

**Description:** This is a real table from the source material. It shows how federal pay is structured and how it creates incentives for career advancement within grade levels and across grades.

**Pedagogical purpose:** To make concrete how the merit system operates at the level of pay. To show that career advancement is possible and that expertise is rewarded.

**Style notes:** Use a color scale (light = lower pay, dark = higher pay) so students can see at a glance where the money is concentrated. Include a note explaining that the schedule changes annually and is updated for cost-of-living adjustments.

## [FIGURE: Congressional Oversight Mechanisms]

**Placement:** Concept 3, after the section on congressional tools.

**Visual:** A diagram showing Congress at the center. Radiating outward: Budget power (Congress controls agency funding), Appointment confirmation (Congress confirms cabinet secretaries and some agency heads), Hearings and investigations, GAO reports (Congress controls the GAO), Legislative authority (Congress passes laws that establish agencies and set their mandates).

**Description:** This shows the various levers Congress has to control the bureaucracy. The diagram illustrates why no single mechanism is sufficient—they are all important but each has limits.

**Pedagogical purpose:** To help students see the range of tools available and understand why Congress does not simply "lose control" of agencies—it is more complex than that.

**Style notes:** Use icons or colors to distinguish different types of power. Make it clear that these are formal powers, even if their effectiveness varies.

## [FIGURE: The Bureaucratic Paradox: Competence vs. Accountability]

**Placement:** Integration section.

**Visual:** A two-axis graph. X-axis: "Political Independence" (left = responsive to elected officials, right = insulated from politics). Y-axis: "Competence" (bottom = incompetent political hacks, top = expert career officials). Plot a point for spoils system (low independence, low competence) and for the merit system (high independence, high competence). Add a third point for "ideal" (high competence AND high accountability). The question: can you reach the ideal?

**Description:** This visualizes the core tension. You cannot maximize both axes simultaneously without also having a third axis for oversight capacity, which is what Congress and the president try to provide.

**Pedagogical purpose:** To help students see that this is not a problem with a "solution" in the traditional sense. It is a structural trade-off. The best you can do is balance.

**Style notes:** Use a quadrant diagram. Make the unreachable ideal (high on both axes) a dashed line or lighter color to indicate it is aspirational. Label the current U.S. system somewhere in the high-independence, high-competence region with a note about the oversight mechanisms that attempt to address the accountability gap.

## [FIGURE: FOIA Request Volume and Processing Times]

**Placement:** Concept 3, in the section on citizen oversight through FOIA.

**Visual:** A bar chart showing FOIA requests over time (or a snapshot from recent years). Include data on the number of requests received, the number processed, and the average processing time. Highlight the finding that 772,869 requests were received in fiscal 2020, but many took months or longer to process.

**Description:** This quantifies the citizen oversight tool and shows its limitations. The volume is large, but processing is slow. Agencies can slow-walk requests.

**Pedagogical purpose:** To show that while citizens have the right to request information, the actual effectiveness of this mechanism is limited by practical constraints (agency staffing, complexity of the request, etc.).

**Style notes:** Use bars or a line chart. Make it visually clear that there is a gap between the number of requests and the timeliness of responses. Consider including an inset showing the nine FOIA exemptions so students understand what agencies can refuse to release.

## [FIGURE: The National Weather Service Vacancy Crisis (2025)]

**Placement:** Concept 2, case study section.

**Visual:** A map of the continental U.S. with dots representing NWS field offices. Color the dots: green for normal staffing, yellow for 10–20 percent vacancy, orange for 20–40 percent vacancy, red for 40+ percent vacancy. Include a callout showing the Norman office (Oklahoma) and noting it as the example in the chapter.

**Description:** This visualizes the impact of budget cuts and hiring freezes on a specific agency. It shows that the problem is not uniform—some offices are hit harder than others.

**Pedagogical purpose:** To help students understand that the weather service example is not hypothetical but grounded in specific regional impacts.

**Style notes:** Use a map of the U.S. with GIS-style coloring. Include a title: "NWS Field Office Vacancies, May 2025." Add a note explaining that this is a consequence of presidential budget and hiring decisions constrained by civil service protections.
