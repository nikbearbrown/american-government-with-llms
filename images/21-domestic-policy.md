## [FIGURE: Standing Rock Pipeline Protest, August 2016]

**Placement:** Chapter opening (after cold-open text).

**Description:** Aerial view of Dakota Access Pipeline protest at Standing Rock. Hundreds of people gathered near the construction site, showing the scale of the movement. Teepees and tents visible, indicating long-term encampment. Some photographs show water from the Missouri River in the background (reinforcing the water protection argument). Alternative framing: close-up of protest signs with slogans about sovereignty, water protection, indigenous rights.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Grounds the policy discussion in a concrete, recent event. Shows that policy is not made in backrooms but involves visible, organized public action. The photo demonstrates scale of movement (hundreds became thousands nationally). Illustrates all three streams: the problem (pipeline threat to water), the policy (environmental and sovereignty frameworks), the politics (public pressure and election timing).

**Style Notes:** Use a photograph that shows people and landscape, not just police or conflict. The tone should be documentary, not propagandistic. The goal is to make the event visible, not to argue for or against the pipeline. Attribution required (photo credit, date, source).

## [FIGURE: Age Distribution of U.S. Population, 1950 vs. 2025]

**Placement:** Concept 2 (Social Security), after the Ida Mae Fuller example.

**Description:** Two population pyramids side by side. 1950 pyramid: wide at the bottom (many children), narrowing toward top (few elderly). 2025 pyramid: narrower at bottom (fewer children), thickening in the middle (many people now entering retirement). The visual should make clear the demographic shift: from many young people supporting few elderly to many elderly people supported by fewer young people. Bars can be color-coded (working-age vs. retirement-age) to make the ratio explicit.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Illustrates why Social Security's math has flipped. Early retirees (like Fuller) benefited from surplus workers. Modern retirees face deficit workers. The visual makes clear that this is not a policy failure but a demographic reality: people live longer, birth rates are lower, the ratio is unsustainable. Helps students understand why reform is technically necessary but politically impossible (everyone in the working-age portion of the pyramid understands they will not get what Fuller got).

**Style Notes:** Use neutral colors. Label both pyramids clearly. Include a caption noting the source (U.S. Census Bureau). Consider adding a third pyramid (2033 or 2050 projection) to show the trajectory.

## [FIGURE: The Three Streams Model (Kingdon)]

**Placement:** Concept 1 (The Three Streams), after the mechanism section.

**Description:** Diagram showing three parallel streams flowing from left to right. Each stream is labeled (Problem Stream, Policy Stream, Political Stream) and contains examples. Problem Stream: examples like "economic crisis," "environmental data," "public complaint." Policy Stream: examples like "research proposal," "regulation model," "tax structure." Political Stream: examples like "election," "ideology shift," "coalition change." A confluence point (where all three meet) is highlighted, with an arrow pointing to "Policy Window — Major Change Possible." The streams before and after the confluence flow parallel, with no policy output.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Illustrates the core argument that policy requires alignment. Makes visible that most of the time, streams flow parallel. Only at confluence points does major policy move. Students often think policy is about problems and solutions; this diagram shows that politics is equally important and often the bottleneck.

**Style Notes:** The diagram should be clean and simple. Do not overcomplicate with too many examples. The visual metaphor of streams converging should be self-explanatory. Consider animation (online version) showing streams occasionally converging, most of the time flowing parallel.

## [FIGURE: Federal Spending by Category, 2024]

**Placement:** Concept 2 (Safety Net) and Concept 3 (Economic Policy), showing how the budget is divided.

**Description:** Pie chart or stacked bar chart showing breakdown of federal spending. Categories: Social Security (~23%), Medicare (~16%), Medicaid (~10%), Defense (~12%), Other mandatory (~8%), Discretionary non-defense (~26%), Interest on debt (~5%). Alternatively, a simpler version with just three categories: Mandatory Entitlements (~48%), Defense (~12%), Everything Else (~40%). The chart should include total budget size (e.g., "FY 2024: $6.8 trillion").

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Shows that the U.S. budget is dominated by entitlements (Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid) and defense. Other priorities (education, infrastructure, research) compete for a much smaller discretionary portion. Explains why cutting the deficit is so hard: you would have to either cut popular entitlements, cut defense, or raise taxes. Illustrates why balancing the budget is politically impossible.

**Style Notes:** Use color-coding to distinguish mandatory vs. discretionary vs. defense. Include a caption explaining that mandatory spending is automatically paid (set by formula), discretionary is voted annually, and interest on debt is non-discretionary (owed to creditors). Link to recent Congressional Budget Office data for source.

## [FIGURE: Life Expectancy and Retirement Age Over Time]

**Placement:** Concept 2 (Social Security), showing the fundamental mismatch.

**Description:** Line graph with two lines: one showing life expectancy at age 65 (how many additional years), one showing retirement age (when benefits begin). 1935: retirement age = 65, life expectancy at 65 = 13 additional years (total lifespan ~78). 2024: retirement age = 67, life expectancy at 65 = 20 additional years (total lifespan ~85). The lines should show the divergence: people live longer, retirement age barely increased. Shaded area between lines shows the growing years of benefit collection.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Visualizes why the program is unsustainable. Workers pay in for 45 years, collect for 20+. The payout period has grown while the pay-in period has not. Life expectancy gain is a good thing (medicine works), but it breaks the program's math. Helps students understand why retirement age increases are proposed (to match increased lifespan) and why they are unpopular (people do not want to work longer).

**Style Notes:** Use clear labeling. Include source (Social Security Administration, CDC life expectancy data). Consider a note: "This chart shows why every reform proposal involves pain: rising the age means working longer, cutting benefits means less money, raising taxes means paying more."

## [FIGURE: Tax Progressivity: Three Models Compared]

**Placement:** Concept 3 (Economic Policy), showing the difference between tax structures.

**Description:** Three bar charts or visual comparisons showing how three tax structures affect someone making $30,000 and someone making $300,000. Progressive tax: person at $30K pays 10%, person at $300K pays 37%. Flat tax: both pay 20%. Regressive tax (sales tax example): person at $30K spends $25K (pays tax on that), person at $300K spends $100K (pays tax on that). Show the effective tax rate for each. Progressive: person at $30K pays $3,000, person at $300K pays $111,000 (both visible as dollar amounts and percentages).

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Makes concrete the difference between tax types. Students often confuse "progressive" (higher rate as income rises) with "regressivity" (lower rate as income rises). The chart shows that this is about who bears the burden, not about what the rates are called. Illustrates why Keynesians prefer progressive taxes (poor spend more, so tax them less) and supply-siders prefer regressive taxes (tax consumption, not investment).

**Style Notes:** Use consistent visuals (stacked bars, side-by-side bars) for easy comparison. Color-code to show tax paid vs. income remaining. Include the key insight: "With progressive taxes, the rich pay more dollars and more as a percentage. With regressive taxes, the poor pay less in dollars but more as a percentage of their income."

## [FIGURE: The Policy Cycle]

**Placement:** Concept 1 (The Three Streams), showing how evaluation feeds back.

**Description:** A circular diagram with four stages: Agenda Setting → Enactment → Implementation → Evaluation → (back to Agenda Setting). Arrows show forward progress and feedback loops. At each stage, examples: Agenda Setting = "problem identified, solution specified," Enactment = "Congress votes," Implementation = "agency carries out law," Evaluation = "does it work?" The feedback loop shows: if evaluation shows the policy worked, momentum builds for expansion. If evaluation shows failure, policy gets revised or ended. If evaluation is unclear, cycle stalls.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Shows that policy is not one-time but cyclical. A law passes, it gets implemented, results are measured, the results inform the next cycle. Many policies fail not because they are bad but because evaluation is unclear or ignored. Understanding the cycle helps students see that policy is revisable: bad policies can be fixed, good policies can be improved.

**Style Notes:** Make the feedback loop prominent. Include examples of policies that improved in second cycle (e.g., DARE program was evaluated, found ineffective in some contexts, redesigned) and policies that got cut after evaluation (e.g., abstinence-only education).

## [FIGURE: Employment and Inflation Trade-off (Phillips Curve)]

**Placement:** Concept 3 (Economic Policy), monetary policy section.

**Description:** A simplified Phillips Curve showing the inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation. X-axis: unemployment rate (0-10%). Y-axis: inflation rate (0-8%). A curve slopes downward from left to right, showing that as unemployment drops, inflation tends to rise. Multiple points on the curve show different policy choices: "low unemployment, high inflation" (full employment but prices rising), "high unemployment, low inflation" (price stability but people jobless), "sweet spot" (moderate unemployment, moderate inflation, but rarely achievable). Arrows show Fed choices: tightening (move up-left, raise unemployment to fight inflation), easing (move down-right, increase employment but risk inflation).

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Illustrates why the Fed's dual mandate is genuinely conflicting. You cannot optimize both unemployment and inflation simultaneously; you have to choose. Shows that economic policy involves trade-offs, not solutions that are good in all dimensions. Helps students understand why Fed chair decisions are controversial: hawks and doves disagree on which goal (inflation control vs. employment) should take priority.

**Style Notes:** Include a note that the Phillips Curve relationship is weaker than it was historically and has shifted over time. This reflects that economic theory is approximate, not exact, and that policy makers often apply models even when conditions have changed.

## [FIGURE: Federal Deficit Over Time, 1970-2025]

**Placement:** Concept 3 (Economic Policy), fiscal policy section.

**Description:** Line graph showing the federal deficit as a percentage of GDP from 1970 to 2025. Should show: (1) relative stability and small deficits in the 1970s-1990s, (2) brief surpluses in late 1990s (1998-2000, during Clinton administration), (3) large deficits after 9/11 and Iraq War (2001-2008), (4) massive deficit during 2008 recession and stimulus (2009-2010), (5) deficit reduction 2011-2015 (austerity), (6) rising deficits again 2017-2020 (tax cuts, spending increases), (7) pandemic spike 2020-2021, (8) continued large deficits 2022-2025. Include a horizontal line at "sustainable deficit" (roughly 2-3% of GDP) to show how far off we are.

**Pedagogical Purpose:** Shows that deficit is not a new problem but has grown worse over time. Also shows that it is responsive to policy choices (deficits spike after tax cuts, during wars, after stimulus). Illustrates that balancing the budget has been attempted (2011-2015 reduction) but abandoned when it became unpopular. Shows that no recent president, Democrat or Republican, has left office with the deficit lower than it was when they took office. Helps students understand why it persists: every group opposes the painful fixes.

**Style Notes:** Label key moments (recessions, wars, tax changes) to show that deficit responds to external events and policy choices. Include a note about GDP denominator (deficit as % of GDP is different from dollar amount; as % it can shrink even if dollars increase if GDP grows faster).
