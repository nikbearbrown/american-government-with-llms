## [FIGURE: Cuban Missile Crisis Photo - U-2 Aerial Reconnaissance, October 1962]

**Placement:** Chapter opening, after first paragraph setting the scene

**Description:** Black-and-white aerial photograph showing missile installations in Cuba. Clear enough that the reader can see the geometric arrangement of buildings, fuel tanks, and launch pads. The resolution is high enough to make the threat real—this is not a cartoon, it is evidence of a real system capable of delivering nuclear warheads.

**Pedagogical purpose:** 
- Makes abstract ("Soviet missiles in Cuba") concrete and visible
- Shows what Kennedy saw: not a report or a briefing, but photographic evidence
- Demonstrates how intelligence gathering works (U-2 spy plane, interpretation of images)
- Sets up the stakes: you can see the threat, now what?

**Style notes:** 
- Grayscale; documentary feel
- No labels needed; the form is distinctive enough
- Include source attribution: "U.S. Air Force reconnaissance photograph, October 1962"

## [FIGURE: Foreign Policy Machinery Flowchart]

**Placement:** Concept 3, "The Architecture of Modern Foreign Policy"

**Description:** A simplified diagram showing:
- The president (center, in larger box)
- Radiating outward: Secretary of State, Secretary of Defense, National Security Advisor, CIA Director, U.S. Trade Representative
- Below: Congress (House and Senate)
- To the side: NATO, UN, other international organizations
- Arrows showing:
  - President proposes, Congress appropriates
  - President negotiates, Senate ratifies (or not)
  - President orders, military executes
  - Dotted lines showing intended vs. actual influence

**Pedagogical purpose:** 
- Visualizes the institutional complexity without oversimplifying
- Shows why foreign policy is fragmented: multiple actors, competing authorities
- Illustrates presidential centrality and congressional leverage
- Demonstrates that formal organization does not predict actual power dynamics

**Style notes:**
- Simple geometric shapes
- Arrows distinguish between formal authority and actual influence (solid vs. dotted)
- No need for elaborate shading; clarity > decoration

## [FIGURE: Timeline - American Foreign Policy Schools of Thought]

**Placement:** Concept 2, after the overview of the four schools

**Description:** A timeline from 1776 to 2025 showing:
- 1776-1917: Isolationism era (U.S. stays out of European wars)
- 1917-1941: Isolationism returns (post-WWI, pre-WWII)
- 1941-1991: Realism and Liberal Internationalism (Cold War; NATO, UN, containment)
- 1973-: Neoconservatism emerges (Vietnam disillusionment, 1973 forward)
- 1991-2001: Post-Cold War uncertainty; neo-isolationism debate
- 2001-2011: Neoconservatism dominant (War on Terror, Iraq/Afghanistan)
- 2009-2017: Hybrid approach (Obama's mix of liberal internationalism and drone strikes)
- 2017-2021: Isolationism resurgence + unilateralism (Trump era)
- 2021-: Rebuild alliances while managing competition with China

**Pedagogical purpose:**
- Shows that schools of thought are not timeless; they emerge in response to historical moments
- Illustrates that the U.S. has moved between frameworks multiple times
- Demonstrates that pure adherence to one school is rare; hybrids are normal
- Helps students understand the present moment as one position in a long arc

**Style notes:**
- Color-coded by school (isolationism = one color, realism = another, etc.)
- Key events marked on timeline (Pearl Harbor, Cuban Missile Crisis, 9/11, etc.)
- Include major presidents to help orient students

## [FIGURE: Trade-off Matrix — Speed vs. Legitimacy in Foreign Policy Decision-making]

**Placement:** Concept 1, after discussion of trade-offs

**Description:** A 2x2 matrix:

|  | **High Speed** | **Low Speed** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **High Legitimacy** | UN coalition on direct threat (rare) | UN coalition on routine matter (common) |
| **Low Legitimacy** | Unilateral drone strike (fast, controversial) | Treaty negotiation that fails (slow, disappointing) |

Examples in each quadrant, with arrows showing how decisions tend to cluster.

**Pedagogical purpose:**
- Visualizes the core trade-off of the chapter
- Shows that you cannot have both maximum speed and maximum legitimacy
- Helps students understand why presidents make different choices in different situations
- Demonstrates that speed and legitimacy are not moral categories, just dimensions

**Style notes:**
- Clean grid, no unnecessary decoration
- Use axis labels as questions: "How quickly can we act?" and "How much buy-in do we have?"

## [FIGURE: Military Capability vs. Political Outcome in Iraq and Afghanistan]

**Placement:** Concept 3, "The Limits of Unilateral Power" section

**Description:** Two parallel timelines:
- **Military timeline:** weeks to months (regime falls, Taliban defeated)
- **Political timeline:** years to decades (no stable governance, occupation fails, return to previous state)
- Gap between them illustrated visually (widening distance as time goes on)

**Pedagogical purpose:**
- Makes visible the gap between military success and political success
- Shows why overwhelming military capability did not produce desired outcomes
- Illustrates the core misconception: "If we just used more force, it would work"
- Demonstrates that the machinery of statecraft conflates two different timelines

**Style notes:**
- Two parallel arrows, one rising then plateauing (military), one rising more slowly (political)
- Can include casualty figures or spending figures on the military timeline to emphasize the cost of the gap
- Must be sensitive to the human cost; numbers should be stark but respectful
