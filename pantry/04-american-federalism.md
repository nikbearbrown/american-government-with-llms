# Pantry: American Federalism

## Scenes & Cold Opens

**The Postcard Problem** — State borders have billboards with simple messages, but those billboards hide the complexity. The chapter opens with this: what makes a state border a legal line instead of just a geographic one? The mechanics of federalism make it so.

**1787, Philadelphia, The Stalemate** — Delegates to the Constitutional Convention had already experienced what happens without federal authority: states as separate nations, war debts unpaid, trade wars among themselves. The Articles of Confederation proved that subordinating all power to states was a recipe for paralysis. But handing power to a central government meant sacrificing the state autonomy they had just fought for. Federalism was the compromise — not written in a flash of insight but hammered out because it solved the immediate problem.

**Maryland's Tax on the Bank (1819)** — A concrete dispute: Maryland taxed all out-of-state chartered banks, including the Baltimore branch of the federal Second Bank. James McCulloch (the bank's agent) refused to pay. The case went to the Supreme Court. Chief Justice John Marshall had to answer two questions: *Did Congress have the authority to charter a bank?* (The Constitution did not say so explicitly.) *Could a state tax federal property?* The ruling in *McCulloch v. Maryland* established implied powers and national supremacy — principles that have shaped federalism ever since. But the case began with a real conflict over money, not theory.

**The Great Depression Forced the Hand** — By 1933, state and local governments had no money. Tax bases collapsed. Unemployment hit 25 percent. President Roosevelt's federal programs were not an ideological choice; they were the only institutions with resources. When the Supreme Court initially blocked them, the political tension became unbearable. The Court backed down. Necessity rewrote federalism.

**Edith Windsor's Tax Bill** — After her partner Thea Spyer died in 2009, Windsor faced a $363,000 federal tax bill on the inheritance. Spyer had left her the estate; normally surviving spouses get an exemption. But the federal government did not recognize their marriage (they were a same-sex couple). The Defense of Marriage Act made marriage a state choice, but federal law then punished states that chose differently. Windsor's lawsuit became *United States v. Windsor* (2013), which struck down DOMA and eventually led to marriage equality nationwide. A personal injustice at the intersection of state and federal authority.

**Arizona's SB 1070 (2010)** — Arizona passed a law trying to make life so difficult for undocumented immigrants that they would leave. Police could stop and demand papers. Employers faced penalties for hiring undocumented workers. The law divided Arizona society: 81 percent of Latino voters opposed it; advocates said the federal government was not enforcing immigration law, so states had to. The Supreme Court ruled most of it unconstitutional but let the "show me your papers" provision stand — a narrow gap showing where state and federal authority still collide.

**MADD's Detour to Congress** — Mothers Against Drunk Driving wanted states to raise the legal drinking age from eighteen to twenty-one. State legislators said no: lower drinking ages attracted youth from neighboring states and were small economic assets. MADD could not move state legislatures, so it went to Congress and asked for a federal condition on highway funding. Congress passed it in 1984. States complied. Venue shopping worked.

## Analogies & Metaphors

**The Layer Cake and the Marble Cake** — Dual federalism was layer cake: state authority on one level, federal authority on another, clearly separated. Cooperative federalism is marble cake: colors (authorities) swirled throughout, impossible to fully separate. The metaphor is vivid but has limits — actual federalism is more like a contract with constant renegotiation rather than either baked good.

**The Carrot and the Stick** — Grants are carrots (incentives); mandates are sticks (coercion). The metaphor captures the tone but obscures the mechanism. Grants come with conditions; mandates come without compensation. Both are tools of power, just operating differently. Both changed federalism — grants by making states dependent on federal money, mandates by imposing federal rules without funding.

**The Pendulum Swinging** — Federalism goes through phases: dual, then cooperative, then "new federalism" attempted a swing back to the states. But the image of a pendulum suggests equilibrium — that it will swing back and forth. In reality, centralization has only deepened over two centuries. The pendulum metaphor obscures that the baseline itself has shifted.

**The Supreme Court as Referee** — The Court settles disputes between federal and state authority by saying what the Constitution permits. But the image of neutral refereeing obscures that the Court's interpretation of the Constitution changes over time. In *Wickard v. Filburn* (1942), the Court let the federal government regulate a farmer growing wheat only for his own use, calling it interstate commerce. In *United States v. Lopez* (1995), the Court struck down a gun-free-school-zones law, saying it did not substantially affect interstate commerce. Both cases invoked the same clause but reached opposite conclusions. The Court is not a neutral referee; it is the rule-maker.

## Etymology & Specification

**Federalism** — From Latin *foedus*, meaning treaty or alliance. A federal system is one formed by a treaty or compact between distinct units (states) to create a new level (the national government). The term carries the idea of voluntary association and limited central authority, even though in practice the federal government's power has grown far beyond what the framers imagined.

**Enumerated Powers** — Powers listed (enumerated) explicitly. They are specific and bounded. Congress can tax, borrow, regulate interstate commerce, coin money, declare war. Article I, Section 8 names them. The Constitution does not say Congress can do anything else.

**Implied Powers** — Powers not explicitly enumerated but necessary to carry out enumerated powers. Congress is not authorized to charter a bank, but it is authorized to tax, borrow, and regulate commerce. Chartering a bank serves those purposes, so Congress can do it. The elastic clause ("necessary and proper") is the textual hook. But *implied* is doing a lot of work. Implied by whom? The Supreme Court decides.

**Reserved Powers** — Powers kept by the states. Not explicitly named until the Tenth Amendment. The idea is that the federal government has only the powers listed or implied from them; everything else belongs to the states. In practice, this is more of a principle than a boundary. The federal government reaches into nearly every area through its commerce power, its taxing power, and its power to condition grants.

**Concurrent Powers** — Powers exercised by both levels of government simultaneously. Both can tax, borrow, make laws, run courts. This is where federalism gets complicated: when both levels regulate the same activity, which one prevails? Federal law does, if it is constitutional. But a state can impose higher standards (stricter pollution controls, higher minimum wage) as long as it does not conflict with federal law. The boundary between federal and state authority is often the boundary between minimum and maximum.

**Supremacy Clause** — Article VI says the Constitution and federal law made in pursuance of the Constitution are the supreme law of the land. This means if a state law conflicts with a valid federal law, federal law wins. But federal law must be constitutional — it must rest on a federal power. A federal law that violates the Constitution is not supreme; it is invalid.

**Categorical Grants** — Federal money for a specific purpose with detailed rules about how to spend it. Medicaid is the canonical example. The federal government says: here is money for health insurance for the poor, but you must follow these rules about eligibility, covered services, and provider payments. Recipients get discretion within the rules but not outside them.

**Block Grants** — Federal money for a general purpose with fewer rules. The Workforce Investment Act gives money for job training but lets states decide which programs to fund, which workers to target. Block grants are more flexible but also less controlled — the federal government has a harder time ensuring outcomes align with national goals.

**Unfunded Mandates** — Federal requirements on states without federal funding. The Real ID Act requires states to verify identity on driver's licenses using specific technology. The federal government set the standard but states paid for implementation ($11 billion estimated, federal government reimbursed a fraction). The Unfunded Mandates Reform Act (1995) tried to prevent Congress from imposing new mandates without funding, but it has not worked — Congress keeps finding ways to impose requirements.

**Coercive Federalism** — Using mandates and conditional grants to force states toward federal goals. It is "coercive" because states have no real choice — refuse a grant and lose federal money; refuse a mandate and face penalties. The term suggests that federalism has become less about respecting state autonomy and more about federal power operating through state apparatus.

## Trade-Offs Explicitly Named

**Uniformity vs. Diversity** — Federalism allows policy diversity — states can have different tax systems, different education standards, different welfare rules. This accommodates regional preferences and lets states experiment. But it also means citizens in poor states may get worse services than citizens in rich states. Education spending varies from $33,400 per student (New York) to $9,390 (Idaho). Unemployment insurance benefits vary by state; Medicaid varies. Is diversity worth that inequality?

**Efficiency vs. Accountability** — The federal government is more efficient at raising revenue (income tax, which requires fewer exemptions across a unified market) and at coordinating national standards. But federal programs are harder to change, less responsive to local needs, and diffuse responsibility. When the federal government funds a state program but the state mismanages it, who is accountable? When a federal mandate requires costly compliance, who bears the cost? Cooperative federalism promises the best of both but often delivers diffused responsibility and cost-shifting.

**Decentralization vs. Capacity** — States have advantages: they are closer to citizens, more responsive, better able to tailor policy to regional needs. But they have less fiscal capacity, less borrowing power, and smaller tax bases. In a recession, state and local tax revenue collapses; the federal government can borrow. During the Great Recession (2007–2009), federal grants to states increased 33 percent to offset state revenue losses. Federal capacity solves state problems, but dependency follows.

**Federal Control vs. State Flexibility** — Categorical grants let the federal government specify outcomes (clean air, well-fed children, schools that perform) but constrain states in how to achieve them. Block grants let states innovate but reduce federal leverage over results. The federal government prefers categorical grants because it can claim credit for outcomes; Congress prefers them because they support favored constituencies. States prefer block grants for flexibility. But block grants are only 10 percent of federal grant outlay because they are politically less appealing to federal legislators.

**National Supremacy vs. State Autonomy** — The supremacy clause is clear: federal law prevails if it is constitutional. But that leaves enormous gray area about what is constitutional. The commerce clause has been interpreted so broadly that Congress can now regulate nearly any activity. Yet states retain de facto authority in many areas simply because the federal government has not acted or acts sporadically. The federal government does not fully enforce federal drug law in states that have legalized marijuana. The practical balance is negotiated constantly, case by case.

**Venue Shopping vs. Democratic Deliberation** — Federalism's multiple access points let interest groups find a receptive venue if they are blocked in one. MADD succeeded in Congress after failing in state legislatures. Anti-abortion advocates succeeded in states after failing in Congress. This is democratic — multiple routes for political change. But it also allows losing groups to keep fighting, moving the issue around, never settling it. Some issues (abortion, immigration, gun control, marriage equality) bounce between state and federal authorities for decades. Is that resilience or dysfunction?

## Worked Examples & Case Studies

**McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)** — *The Issue:* Does Congress have authority to charter a bank? Can a state tax it? *The Setup:* The Second Bank of the United States opened a branch in Baltimore. Maryland imposed a tax on all out-of-state banks. James McCulloch, the branch cashier, refused to pay. *The Ruling:* Chief Justice John Marshall said Congress could charter a bank under the necessary and proper clause. Congress has enumerated powers (tax, borrow, regulate commerce). Creating a bank is an instrument suited to carrying out those powers. Therefore it is "necessary and proper." The Court also said states could not tax federal property or institutions. The supremacy clause makes federal law supreme within its sphere. *The Implication:* This doctrine of implied powers gave Congress vast discretionary power. Nearly any federal action can be justified as necessary and proper to some enumerated power. The boundary between federal and state authority began to shift toward federal authority from this moment.

**Wickard v. Filburn (1942)** — *The Issue:* Can Congress regulate a farmer growing wheat only for his own consumption? *The Setup:* During World War II, Congress set quotas on wheat production to stabilize prices. Roscoe Filburn, an Ohio farmer, grew wheat beyond his quota, saying he grew it only for his own use, so it was not interstate commerce. *The Ruling:* The Supreme Court said even local production of wheat for local use substantially affects interstate commerce because it reduces the amount the farmer will buy in the market. Therefore Congress can regulate it. *The Implication:* The commerce clause became nearly limitless. If even a farmer's homegrown wheat affects interstate commerce, what does not? This case gave Congress broad authority to regulate agricultural, manufacturing, and labor practices — authority it still has.

**United States v. Lopez (1995)** — *The Issue:* Can Congress ban guns in school zones under the commerce clause? *The Setup:* Congress passed the Gun-Free School Zones Act, making it federal crime to possess a gun within 1,000 feet of a school. Alfonzo Lopez brought a gun to his high school. He was arrested and charged under the federal law. *The Ruling:* The Supreme Court struck down the law. Possession of a gun in a school zone does not substantially affect interstate commerce. The Court said Congress had gone too far; the link between the regulated activity and interstate commerce must be clear. *The Implication:* For the first time in sixty years, the Court used the commerce clause to limit federal authority. But Lopez has not stopped federal power; subsequent cases have allowed broad federal regulation of manufacturing, agriculture, labor, and environmental protection. Lopez might be an exception that proves the rule — the commerce clause is still the main vehicle for federal authority.

**Arizona v. United States (2012)** — *The Issue:* Can Arizona enforce its own immigration laws? *The Setup:* Arizona passed SB 1070, which made it a state crime to be in Arizona without valid immigration papers, to seek work without papers, and gave police authority to arrest undocumented immigrants without a warrant if they had probable cause to think they had committed a crime. *The Ruling:* The Supreme Court struck down three provisions but upheld one. The Court said immigration is a federal matter, and state laws that conflict with federal immigration law are invalid. But the "show me your papers" provision — allowing police to check status during a lawful stop — survived, though the Court warned Arizona that applying it based on race could trigger civil rights lawsuits. *The Implication:* Federal supremacy in immigration is clear. But the ruling was narrow: the Court left room for states to cooperate with federal immigration enforcement. States have taken this opening; some have passed laws that work with federal immigration authorities.

**United States v. Windsor (2013)** — *The Issue:* Can the federal government define marriage and deny benefits to same-sex married couples? *The Setup:* Edith Windsor and Thea Spyer were married in Canada in 2007. When Spyer died in 2009, Windsor inherited her estate but faced a $363,053 tax bill because federal law did not recognize their marriage. She sued, saying the Defense of Marriage Act violated equal protection. *The Ruling:* The Supreme Court agreed. The federal government has no authority to define marriage (traditionally a state matter) and cannot discriminate among marriages the states have recognized. *The Implication:* This shifted marriage equality from a state issue to a federal constitutional issue. It did not immediately legalize same-sex marriage nationwide; that took *Obergefell v. Hodges* (2015). But it changed the dynamic: federal law could no longer penalize same-sex couples, and federal courts could use equal protection reasoning to overturn state bans.

**The Minimum Drinking Age (1984)** — *The Issue:* Can Congress require states to raise the drinking age? *The Setup:* Mothers Against Drunk Driving lobbied states to raise the legal drinking age from eighteen to twenty-one. States refused — lower drinking ages attracted youth from neighboring states. So MADD went to Congress. Congress passed the National Minimum Drinking Age Act, a crosscutting mandate: states that did not raise the drinking age to twenty-one would lose federal highway funding. *The Ruling:* The Supreme Court upheld the law in *South Dakota v. Dole* (1987). Congress has the power to condition federal grants; states choose whether to accept the grant and the condition or decline both. *The Implication:* This was venue shopping in action. It also showed the power of conditional grants. States nominally chose to raise the drinking age, but the choice was coerced. Every state complied by 1988. This is how federalism works in practice: the federal government sets a condition on money, and states comply because they cannot afford to lose federal funding.

## Common Misconceptions to Address

**"States have no power once the federal government acts."** Not true. States retain power in areas the Constitution reserves to them. Even where the federal government acts, states often set higher standards. For example, federal law sets minimum wage ($7.25/hour); many states set higher minimums. Federal law sets environmental standards under the Clean Air Act; states can impose stricter rules. States can regulate within their domain as long as they do not conflict with federal law or violate the Constitution.

**"Federalism means the federal government and states act in separate spheres."** That was true under dual federalism (1870s–1930s), but it has not been true for ninety years. Cooperative federalism (1930s–present) involves both levels in most policy areas. Social Security, Medicaid, education, environmental protection, criminal justice — both levels are involved. The boundary is not between separate spheres but between federal minimums and state discretion to do more.

**"The Supreme Court enforces the Constitution neutrally."** The Court interprets the Constitution, but interpretation changes. The same commerce clause that permitted Congress to regulate a farmer's wheat for personal use (*Wickard*) was invoked to strike down a gun law (*Lopez*). The Court's reading of the Constitution reflects political judgments and changes with the composition of the Court. History is not neutral.

**"Grants are pure incentives; mandates are pure coercion."** In practice, the line is blurry. When a state depends on 30 percent of its revenue from federal grants, "accepting" grant conditions is not really voluntary. When a mandate comes with a small federal reimbursement, the state must choose between compliance with underfunding or non-compliance with penalties. The distinction matters rhetorically but not in lived experience.

**"Federalism protects individual liberty."** It can, but it does not always. States restricted voting, enforced segregation, and banned abortion — all within their constitutional authority. Federal action was needed to protect liberty (Fourteenth Amendment, Civil Rights Act, court decisions). Federalism is a structure; it does not determine whether liberty expands or contracts. That depends on who holds power.

**"If a state passes a law, it is legal."** Only if it is constitutional. State laws cannot violate the U.S. Constitution, the supremacy clause, or valid federal law. Federal courts strike down state laws regularly — on grounds of free speech, equal protection, due process, conflict with federal law. A state cannot legalize slavery, ban voting based on race, or tax interstate commerce, no matter what it wants.
