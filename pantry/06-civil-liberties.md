# Pantry: Civil Liberties

Scenes, analogies, trade-offs, scale shifts, case studies for expansion or calibration.

## Scenes / Cold Opens (Deployable)

**Family Separation** — El Paso, 2017. Three-year-old separated from mother at border. No court order. Government policy. Question: Does the Fifth Amendment's "liberty" protect family relationship? Used in Chapter Opening.

**Powell v. Alabama** — Ed Brown, Alabama, 1931. Three-day trial, no lawyer, murder conviction. Precedent for establishing Sixth Amendment incorporation. Demonstrates how slow incorporation is.

**Gideon's Handwritten Petition** — Clarence Gideon, Florida prison, writing petition from cell to Supreme Court. Human scale of a major constitutional case. Right to counsel becomes real when you see an indigent man representing himself.

**Pentagon Papers** — 1971, classified documents, government sued to stop publication. National security vs. press freedom. Shows how strict scrutiny works in practice (government bears burden of proof).

**Jacob Abrams and the Pamphlets** — 1919, young radical throws anti-war pamphlets from roof. Convicted under Espionage Act. Holmes's dissent invents "clear and present danger" test. Shows how individual cases reshape doctrine.

**Miranda Interrogation Room** — Danny Harris, hours of questioning, no recording, no warning, statement becomes "confession." Shows why Miranda warnings exist and the asymmetry of information in custody.

## Analogies (Tested, with Breakpoints Explicit)

**The Cage Metaphor** — Bill of Rights as cage around federal power, states left outside. Works for explaining early constitutional structure. Breaks when you try to apply it to modern federalism (states have their own constitutions; the cage is nested, not exclusive).

**Process as Fairness Theater** — Criminal procedure protections as guarantees of *process*, not outcomes. Like rules in a game: follow the rules, the result has legitimacy, even if an innocent player loses. Works for explaining why courts care about procedure even when evidence seems overwhelming. Breaks when procedure itself is corrupted or when the "game" has asymmetric stakes (wealthy defendants afford better counsel).

## Etymologies / Specifications

**Civil liberties vs. civil rights** — Often used interchangeably in conversation. Political scientists and lawyers distinguish: civil liberties are *limits on government power* (what government cannot do). Civil rights are *guarantees of equal treatment* (what government must do fairly). Both are in the Constitution; both are enforced by courts. The distinction clarifies the type of injury: a person whose speech is censored has had a civil liberty violated; a person denied a job because of race has had a civil right violated.

**Incorporation** — The process by which rights in the Bill of Rights become binding on states through the Fourteenth Amendment's "liberty" clause. Not automatic. Case-by-case. Two-pronged test: Is the right fundamental? Is it deeply rooted in the nation's history and tradition?

**Miranda warnings** — Not in the Constitution. A rule the Court invented to make the Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination meaningful in practice. Once in custody, the government has power; warnings attempt to level the information asymmetry.

## Trade-offs (Both Sides Visible)

**Incorporation vs. State Autonomy**
- Incorporation protects: Individual freedoms have a consistent constitutional baseline across all states.
- Incorporation costs: States lose ability to regulate rights differently, to experiment, to move at different speeds.

**First Amendment Protection vs. Public Safety**
- Protection of speech costs: Hateful speech, extreme political speech, dangerous conspiracy theories all get protection. Bad people use the right.
- Limiting speech costs: Government and majorities can suppress dissent, unpopular ideas, legitimate criticism. The cure may be worse than the disease.

**Right to Counsel vs. Prosecution Efficiency**
- Right to counsel protects: Innocent people are less likely to be wrongly convicted when they have competent defense; guilty people are convicted fairly, not because they were too poor to contest the charges.
- Right to counsel costs: Police and prosecutors have harder time solving cases; resources devoted to defense attorneys are resources not spent on other needs; some guilty people go free because evidence is challenged.

**Fifth Amendment Self-Incrimination Protection vs. Truth-Finding**
- Protection protects: People cannot be tortured or tricked into confessing; burden of proof stays on government; innocent people more likely to survive interrogation without false confessions.
- Protection costs: Guilty person who stays silent cannot be forced to reveal evidence only they know; trials become longer and more expensive.

## Scale Shifts (Zoom In / Zoom Out)

**Individual → National** — One person's arrest in one small town (Gideon, Brown, Abrams) becomes a rule for the entire country. By what logic does the personal become the universal? Supreme Court decides.

**One Right → Multiple Rights** — Incorporation happens one amendment at a time over a century, but once one is incorporated, courts move faster on the others. The logic becomes: if the Sixth Amendment right to counsel is fundamental, why isn't the Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination? Why isn't the Eighth Amendment protection against cruel punishment? The pieces fit together.

**Text → Practice** — A constitutional phrase ("due process") that is silent on specifics becomes dozens of rules: Miranda warnings, rules about evidence, rules about jury selection, rules about sentencing. The text is a starting point; practice is where meaning lives.

## Case Studies for Deeper Work

**Miranda v. Arizona (1966)** — Custodial interrogation, coercion, the invention of warnings to protect against invisible coercion. Shows how courts create procedural rules when constitutional language is too vague to apply directly.

**Gideon v. Wainwright (1963)** — Incorporation of right to counsel, but also the question of what counsel means. Right exists; implementation is incomplete.

**Carson v. Makin (2022)** — Religious schools and tuition subsidies. Balancing Establishment Clause (no establishment of religion) against Free Exercise Clause (cannot restrict religious practice). Shows how two clauses of the same amendment can pull in different directions.

**Ex parte Milligan (1866)** — Lincoln's suspension of habeas corpus during Civil War. Government can suspend the writ only if there is actual invasion or rebellion; mere concern about national security is not enough. Shows the limits of executive power even during crisis.

### Why deferred

The chapter as written (5,000 words) focuses on the *machinery* of incorporation and the *tests* courts apply to the major amendments. The source's detailed case law, especially on obscenity, search and seizure, and abortion, adds doctrinal depth but less pedagogical clarity at the introductory level. A student should first understand how incorporation works, what the First Amendment protects, and what due process means. Then they can drill into specific applications.

These deferral candidates are ideal for a follow-up chapter, a detailed case study unit, or a companion essay on one specific doctrine (e.g., "Privacy Rights from Roe to Dobbs," "The Evolution of Speech Protection").
