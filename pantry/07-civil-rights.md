# Pantry: 07—Civil Rights

Raw material—scenes, analogies, details, variations, cases for potential use.

## Scenes & Cold Opens

**Topeka Courtroom, May 17, 1954.** Used as chapter opening. Oliver Brown, his daughter Linda, the walk past the whites-only school seven blocks away, the bus ride to the Black school a mile distant. Earl Warren preparing to announce the Court's decision.

**Greensboro Lunch Counter, February 1, 1960.** Four Black college students sit at a whites-only lunch counter and wait to be served. The scene becomes iconic: calm, dressed in jackets and ties, refusing to leave when told they cannot be served. The sit-in campaign spreads. Hundreds of lunch counters segregated, then desegregated through this tactic.

**Selma Bridge, March 7, 1965, 3:00 PM.** "Bloody Sunday." Six hundred marchers walk across the Edmund Pettus Bridge toward Montgomery. State police and sheriff's deputies, mounted and in riot gear, attack them with clubs and whips. John Lewis has his skull fractured. Television broadcasts the violence into American living rooms. President Lyndon Johnson watches it. Congress watches it. The Voting Rights Act follows.

**Rosa Parks on the Bus, December 1, 1955.** The conventional story: she was tired. The real story: She was trained in nonviolent resistance. She had worked for the NAACP for twelve years. She sat in the "colored" section and was asked to move to the back. She refused. This sparked the Montgomery bus boycott, orchestrated by E. D. Nixon and carried out primarily by Black women who organized the routes and spread the word.

**Little Rock Central High, September 1957.** Governor Orval Faubus mobilizes the Arkansas National Guard to prevent integration. President Eisenhower, furious, federalizes the Guard and sends the 101st Airborne Division. Nine Black students are escorted to school by federal soldiers. Images broadcast worldwide. The U.S., fighting Cold War against the Soviet Union, confronts its own Jim Crow system.

## Analogies & Mechanisms

**The three gears metaphor.** Rational basis is low-effort—the government just has to show the law is rationally related to a legitimate interest. Intermediate scrutiny is medium—the government has to show it's substantially related to an important interest. Strict scrutiny is high-friction—the government must show a compelling interest and narrow tailoring. The metaphor captures that scrutiny is not a question of whether discrimination happens, but how much justification is required before a court accepts it.

**The literacy test trap.** Looks race-neutral on the page. Applied with complete discretion by registrars. A white applicant reads "The sky is blue." A Black applicant reads Article II of the Constitution. The mechanism is elegant: the same law, applied differently, produces opposite results. This is how facially neutral discrimination works.

**The grandfather clause as inverted logic.** States wanted poor white voters to have access even if they couldn't pass literacy tests. So they exempted people whose grandfathers could vote before the Civil War. This looks like a benefit to the poor, but it's actually a filter. No Black person's grandfather could vote. So it's a whites-only exemption hiding behind a family-history criterion. This pattern—facially neutral criteria that track historical discrimination—appears repeatedly in voting law.

**De facto vs. de jure segregation.** *De jure* is segregation by law—the state requires separation. *De facto* is segregation by choice—people live in segregated neighborhoods, attend segregated schools, worship in segregated churches, because of private decisions (or because public policy created conditions that incentivized those decisions). The distinction matters legally. *De jure* segregation is unconstitutional. *De facto* segregation is harder to attack in court because there's no explicit state action. But its effects can be just as damaging.

**The wealth gap as legacy.** Slavery extracted wealth from Black people for 246 years. Jim Crow extracted it for another 88 years. Redlining prevented Black families from buying homes in appreciating neighborhoods for decades after the Civil Rights Act. These policies created a wealth gap that compounds—descendants of Black families who were excluded from homeownership have less equity, less collateral, less inheritance. A law against discrimination in 1964 cannot undo 300 years of extraction. That's why civil rights sometimes requires repair, not just removal of obstacles.

## Etymology & Precision

**Civil liberties vs. civil rights.** Liberties are freedoms (from interference, from the state). Rights are entitlements (to equal treatment, to participate, to access). This distinction matters for understanding what the law can do. A civil liberty says "the state cannot censor you." A civil right says "the state must treat you equally." They're different problems.

**Scrutiny.** From Latin *scrutinium*, close examination. The three tiers of scrutiny are asking: How closely will courts examine this law? For age discrimination, courts barely look. For race discrimination, courts examine very closely indeed. The word captures that judicial review is not all-or-nothing but a question of degree.

**Disenfranchisement.** To remove someone's franchise—their voting right. The word appears repeatedly in civil rights history and is often overlooked now because voting rights seem secure (they're not). Understanding disenfranchisement as a tool helps explain how systematically the South worked to eliminate Black political power after Reconstruction.

**Preclearance.** The requirement in the Voting Rights Act that jurisdictions with a history of discrimination get federal approval before changing voting laws. It's a preventive mechanism—don't let the discrimination happen in the first place. The Supreme Court eliminated it in *Shelby County v. Holder*, saying the conditions that justified it no longer existed. What happened next: states immediately passed voting restrictions.

## Trade-offs & Tensions

**Democracy vs. protection of minorities.** Strict scrutiny limits what majorities can do, even if they want to do it. This protects minorities from tyranny of the majority, but it's also a profound limit on democratic decision-making. How do you balance these? When does protection of minorities require overriding majority preference?

**State autonomy vs. civil rights enforcement.** States historically controlled voting rules. Federalism suggests they should. But states used that autonomy to discriminate. The Voting Rights Act took the unprecedented step of federal oversight. Good—it worked. But it's also a huge expansion of federal power. The tension remains unresolved.

**Law vs. reality.** *Brown v. Board of Education* struck down segregation. Sixty years later, schools are more segregated than when *Brown* was decided. The law changed. The reality didn't. Why? Because law removes barriers; it doesn't create outcomes. Private choices, residential patterns, inherited inequality recreated segregation without the law's permission. This tension—between legal victory and practical failure—runs through all civil rights history.

**Individual vs. structural discrimination.** Early civil rights law focused on individual discrimination: one person refusing to serve another because of race. But much discrimination is structural—the system works in ways that disadvantage groups even when no individual is deliberately discriminating. Detecting and remedying structural discrimination is harder.

## Cases That Moved the Framework

**Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857).** The Supreme Court ruled that Black people, whether enslaved or free, could not be citizens and had no rights under the Constitution. This decision didn't create slavery; it explicitly protected it and prevented federal restrictions on its expansion. It's perhaps the worst Supreme Court decision in history and stands as a warning about what happens when courts fail to protect rights.

**Plessy v. Ferguson (1896).** The Supreme Court upheld Louisiana's requirement that trains provide separate cars for Black and white passengers, as long as the cars were "equal." The logic: separation doesn't harm because both races get equal facilities. This ignored the entire point of segregation—to mark one group as inferior. *Plessy* stood for 58 years. It was the legal foundation for Jim Crow.

**Brown v. Board of Education (1954).** The Supreme Court unanimously overturned *Plessy* as it applied to education. Chief Justice Earl Warren wrote that separation is inherently unequal because segregation itself causes harm. The decision invoked social science evidence about psychological damage but also named the reality: segregation exists to mark a group as inferior.

**Brown II (1955).** The Court ordered desegregation to proceed "with all deliberate speed"—a vague phrase that invited delay. Southern states used it to justify years of inaction, "massive resistance," and the eventual white flight to private schools.

**Missouri ex rel. Gaines v. Canada (1938).** The Court ruled that if a state excluded Black people from its law school, it had to either admit them to the white school or create an equivalent Black school. This bent *Plessy* without breaking it. The logic: separate but equal requires that the "equal" part be real. If you can't provide equal facilities in a separate school, you have to integrate.

**Craig v. Boren (1976).** Oklahoma allowed women to buy beer at 18 but required men to wait until 21. The state argued this was rational—traffic data showed more young male drunk drivers. The Supreme Court applied intermediate scrutiny and said statistical correlation is not enough. The law was struck down. This case established that discrimination based on sex/gender gets intermediate scrutiny.

**Loving v. Virginia (1967).** The Supreme Court struck down Virginia's law against interracial marriage. The law was race-based and applied strict scrutiny. The Court found no compelling governmental interest in preventing interracial marriage. The case is important because it extended civil rights logic beyond segregation to all forms of racial classification.

**Civil Rights Act of 1964.** Congress outlawed discrimination by private businesses in employment, public accommodations, and federally funded education. The justification: Congress's power to regulate interstate commerce. Restaurants that discriminate are regulating commerce in a discriminatory way. This was ingenious—it got around the problem that the Fourteenth Amendment applies only to state action.

**Voting Rights Act of 1965.** Congress banned literacy tests, provided federal examiners to register voters, and required preclearance—federal approval before jurisdictions with a history of discrimination could change voting laws. This moved enforcement from courts (which could only strike down laws after the fact) to the executive (which could prevent discrimination proactively).

**Shelby County v. Holder (2013).** The Supreme Court gutted the Voting Rights Act's preclearance provision, saying the conditions that justified federal oversight no longer existed. The decision was wrong. Within months, states that had been under preclearance passed new voting restrictions. The case shows that removing enforcement mechanisms allows discrimination to resume.

## Scale Shifts

**The long view: 1776 to now.** The Declaration says all men are created equal. The Constitution protects slavery. The Thirteenth Amendment abolishes slavery. The Fourteenth Amendment guarantees equal protection. Jim Crow violates the promise. Reconstruction tries to enforce it. Reconstruction fails. 80+ years of Jim Crow. Brown overturns *Plessy*. Brown is implemented glacially. The Civil Rights Act and Voting Rights Act provide enforcement. Enforcement is removed. Discrimination resumes. This is not a linear arc. It's a series of advances, retreats, and the eternal need to re-litigate.

**Zooming in: One case, one person.** Oliver Brown walks his daughter Linda past the all-white school. She has to ride a bus a mile away to an all-Black school. This is the concrete experience behind *Brown v. Board of Education*. The case is not abstract. It's about a specific father, a specific daughter, a specific walk, and the specific harm of being told "you cannot go there."

**The mechanics visible: Literacy test to voter suppression.** A literacy test looks like a neutral qualification. But it's administered with discretion. The same law produces different results depending on who's taking the test. Now zoom forward to 2013 and beyond: a voter ID law looks neutral. Voters must prove their identity. But its effects are racially disparate. States pass it after preclearance is removed. Same pattern, new form. The mechanism is updated, but the purpose is consistent.

## Deferred Material

**Women's civil rights movement in detail.** The source material covers women's rights extensively: Seneca Falls Convention (1848), Susan B. Anthony and Elizabeth Cady Stanton, the drive for the Nineteenth Amendment (ratified 1920), the second-wave feminism of the 1960s, the attempted Equal Rights Amendment, Title IX of the Education Amendments (1972), continuing challenges around equal pay, reproductive rights, violence against women, and underrepresentation in elected office. This chapter mentions women's rights but focuses on African American civil rights. A full exploration would require another chapter or, better, a dedicated treatment of gender discrimination using the same scrutiny framework.

**Other groups: LGBTQ+, disabled, immigrants, religious minorities.** The source material hints that the frameworks developed for African American and women's civil rights extend to other groups. Courts have applied strict scrutiny to religious discrimination and national-origin discrimination. Intermediate scrutiny has been applied to disability discrimination in some contexts. Sexual orientation and gender identity discrimination are newer areas. Immigration status creates complicated questions about what groups deserve protection. This chapter establishes the framework. Application to other groups is left for subsequent material.

**Affirmative action.** The source material includes a section on affirmative action and the Supreme Court cases that have shaped it (*Bakke v. California*, *Grutter v. Bollinger*, *Fisher v. University of Texas*, *Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard*). Affirmative action is intentional discrimination in favor of groups that have historically faced discrimination, meant to remedy past and present discrimination. It's controversial because it uses race-based classifications to remedy race-based harm. Understanding the full debate requires deep engagement with the scrutiny framework and the question of whether strict scrutiny is the right standard for remedial race-based action. Deferred.

**Shelby County and voting rights in the 2010s-2020s.** The chapter mentions the 2013 *Shelby County* decision and the voting restrictions that followed. A full exploration would include the specific restrictions (voter ID laws, reduced early voting, fewer polling locations, purged voter rolls) and the litigation challenging them. The pattern is clear: without federal oversight, states resumed voting discrimination. But the details of which states, which restrictions, and which courts struck down which laws requires more space than this chapter provides.

**De facto segregation and what remedies exist.** The chapter names de facto segregation as the main barrier to integration now. But what can be done about it? Housing discrimination law can reach some private discrimination, but not all. Can courts order housing desegregation or busing to achieve it? The Supreme Court has been skeptical of race-conscious remedies (see *Parents Involved in Community Schools v. Seattle School District*). If de jure segregation is unconstitutional but de facto segregation persists, and if courts won't order race-conscious remedies, what is the path forward? This is a live question in American civil rights and merits extended treatment.

**The role of grassroots power in civil rights.** The chapter emphasizes that laws came because people organized. But understanding exactly *how* organizing creates political pressure—the mechanisms of grassroots power—is complex. How do sit-ins work? How do marches change minds? What was it about the televised violence at Selma that shifted Congress? Why did some campaigns succeed and others fail? This is crucial material but sits at the intersection of civil rights history, political science, and social movement theory.
