# Pantry: Voting and Elections

## Scenes and Case Studies (Reusable)

**Beshear's Kentucky Win (2019)**: Cold open anchor for chapter. Close election in deep red state shows how narrow margins are. Use: demonstrates weight of individual vote, setup for "machinery matters" framing.

**Mississippi 1965 Registration Surge**: Before/after of Voting Rights Act preclearance. 6.7% to 60% Black registration in two years. Use: shows concrete effect of registration rule changes, historical weight.

**San Francisco 2016 Ballot (42 Measures)**: Chaos of direct democracy overload. 30-page sample ballot, voter fatigue visible. Use: illustrates cost of direct democracy without scaffolding.

**Iowa/New Hampshire Momentum Dynamic (2020 Biden Bounce)**: Biden finishes 4th, 5th, but story shifts after Nevada/South Carolina. Narrative beats votes. Use: shows primary machinery independent of voter preference.

**Texas Early Voting 1988**: Initial bump, then faded. Spread voting over weeks reduces social pressure. Use: shows unintended consequences of convenience.

**Colorado Mail-in System**: 1.8M votes (2010) to 2.6M (2018). Removes barriers, increases participation. Use: evidence that access changes outcomes.

**Georgia 2020 Wait Times**: 10-hour waits in some precincts. Logistics as voter suppression. Use: concrete example of how structure affects who votes.

**Access Hollywood Tape (October 2016) vs. Comey Letter (October 28, 2016)**: Two bombshells in final week, competing narratives. Unknown what impact because mail-in voters already cast. Use: shows cost of early voting for campaigns.

## Analogies and Metaphors

**Registration as a Filter**: Not just procedural—it's a sieve. Different mesh sizes let different particles through. Tight mesh = only sturdy particles (organized, stable, older). Loose mesh = captures particles of all types. The mesh is supposedly neutral, but it isn't.

**Campaign Money as Amplification**: You have a message. Without money, you're shouting in an empty room. With money, you're broadcasting. Early money is the amplifier that other amplifiers (media, momentum) then magnify. Cheap to start, expensive to scale.

**Turnout as a Map**: You can draw it as demographic strata—older more likely to vote, younger less likely, etc. But don't read it as destiny. Read it as a prediction that campaigns build into strategy. What's predicted becomes reality when campaigns ignore unlikely voters.

**Primary Process as a Sorting Hat**: Not sorting ideas but narratives. The machinery (Iowa first, delegates, Super Tuesday timing) sorts candidates through story-logic, not voter-logic. The candidate who wins the narrative wins the nomination, even if the voters are ambivalent.

**Ballot Fatigue as a Gradient**: Energy decreases as you move down the ballot. Presidential race = full engagement. Local judge retention = exhaustion. The ballot structure literally predicts what you'll see energy for.

**Preclearance as a Check Valve**: One-way mechanism. States had to prove new rules wouldn't discriminate. After Shelby struck it down, no valve. Rule changes flow in whatever direction.

## Trade-Offs (Both Sides Held)

**Access vs. Certainty**: Make it easy, risk fraud (however rare). Make it certain, burden some people disproportionately. No solution that doesn't trade one thing for another. States choose different points on the spectrum.

**Turnout vs. Selection**: Higher participation = less informed voters mixed with informed ones. Lower participation = more ideologically intense electorate. Different democracies choose different places on this spectrum.

**Individual Campaigns vs. Party Machinery**: Decentralized (individual candidate campaigns) requires more money but allows insurgents. Centralized (party machinery) is cheaper but gatekeeps. Post-1960s shift toward decentralization changed the cost structure.

**Early Voting Convenience vs. Final-Days Persuasion**: If you vote early, you miss last-minute news. If you vote on Election Day, you juggle work/childcare. Can't optimize both.

**Direct Democracy Power vs. Voter Competence**: Citizens can make law directly, but most don't have time to research all measures. Special interests can use the form for pet projects. Participation is powerful but not necessarily well-informed.

## Etymology and Language Precision

**"Voter Registration"**: Not "getting voters" but "marking down who is permitted." A limiting act, not an enabling one. Frames the question as *who is allowed* not *who wants to*.

**"Turnout"**: Not "engagement" or "participation." *Out* suggests something that emerges. Passive voice hides the machinery that produces it.

**"Likely Voter"**: Jargon that means "person our model predicts will vote based on past behavior." Becomes self-fulfilling prophecy when campaigns only reach them.

**"Preclearance"**: Federal approval before a voting rule change could go into effect. "Pre-" means before. After Shelby, states changed rules and *then* defended them in court. Different machinery entirely.

**"Shadow Campaign"**: Super PACs and groups "not officially connected" to candidates. But they coordinate messaging. "Shadow" implies hidden; better word might be "parallel."

**"Ballot Fatigue"**: Not tiredness (physical) but decision fatigue (cognitive). You have depleted your decision-making energy. Comes after multiple choices on one ballot.

**"Retrospective Voting"**: Looking backward at what the person did. (Vs. "prospective voting" = looking forward at what they might do.) The distinction matters—you're voting based on different information.

## Scale Shifts

**From Precinct to State to Nation**: A precinct can have a 70% turnout but the state 60% if precincts with different demographics have different participation. What looks like low turnout statewide might be normal-to-high in high-turnout precincts. The scale you choose changes what you see.

**From Individual Voter to Population Segment**: One 18-year-old can vote. But 18-year-olds as a group have 39% turnout. Campaigns use the population-level pattern to decide whether to reach individuals in that group.

**From Single Election to Structural Pattern**: One primary can be noisy. The pattern across decades shows age, education, income, race all predict turnout. The signal at scale.

**From Registration to Government**: A person registers (individual action). Millions register (turnout patterns). Millions vote (election). Electoral college translates votes to power. Majority in Congress makes law. The machinery at each scale looks different.
