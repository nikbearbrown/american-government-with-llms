# Pantry: Interest Groups and Lobbying

## Scenes and Analogies

**Cold open scene (Chapter Opening)**
- A young mother calling Congress about the ACA (3 minutes, no prior relationship)
- A lobbyist walking down the hallway with months of access (lunches, briefings, draft language, relationships)
- The asymmetry made visible: both trying to influence the same law, not equal chances of being heard

*Why it works*: The scene names the mechanism without naming it. Concrete details (the decorated office, the hunting photos, the first names) show what access looks like. The mother's three minutes vs. the lobbyist's months is the whole chapter compressed into one moment.

---

**Analogy: The construction worker and the union wage (Free rider problem)**

A construction worker benefits when the carpenters' union negotiates higher wages. Everyone gets the raise. So why would he pay dues or show up to union meetings if he gets the benefit either way?

*Test*: Does removing this analogy lose understanding? Yes. It viscerally explains why people have rational incentive not to contribute. The analogy works because it is immediate—people have been in situations where they wondered whether their contribution mattered.

*Where it breaks down*: The analogy assumes the wage increase actually depends on the union existing. In some cases, an employer might raise wages anyway (for competitive reasons). Then the free rider problem becomes even worse. The analogy needs the prior: the union is what created the bargaining power in the first place.

---

**Etymology: "Lobbying"**

The word comes from the lobbies of Parliament in 19th-century Britain, where citizens could wait in the hallway to intercept politicians. "Lobbying" meant literally standing in the lobby. Over time, it came to mean any effort to influence elected officials. The word carries the original geometry: you are trying to get access, to be in the room where the decision happens. Inside lobbying is literally trying to get into the lobby.

## Trade-offs (Core Tensions)

### 1. Small, Cohesive Groups vs. Large, Diffuse Ones

**Small group advantage on collective action**: A 50,000-person union can enforce membership and discipline free riders through social pressure. A 30-million-person "environmental movement" cannot. Small groups are better at overcoming free riding.

**Large group advantage on political power**: A 30-million-person constituency has more voters than a 50,000-person union. Per capita, the union has more power. But aggregate, the large group should dominate elections. Yet it often does not, because the large group is not organized—members are anonymous, dispersed, lacking clear leadership.

**The tension**: The machinery of interest groups favors the small, the organized, the concentrated. But large, diffuse interests (low-income voters, ordinary citizens concerned about environment) have potentially more political power if they could coordinate. They cannot easily coordinate, so they lose.

### 2. Inside Lobbying (Precision) vs. Outside Lobbying (Public Pressure)

**Inside lobbying**: Efficient for specific language. Requires ongoing relationships. Happens in back rooms. Advantage: you can get exactly what you want written into law. Disadvantage: only works if you have access; if public opinion is against you, inside access does not save you.

**Outside lobbying**: Noisier. Happens in public. Requires that an issue be "newsworthy." Advantage: creates political pressure that no inside conversation can. Disadvantage: less precise; public campaigns are blunt instruments. Hard to use outside lobbying to change the word "annual" to "biennial" in a regulation.

**The implication**: Sophisticated interests use both. But not all issues are equally visible. Gun rights are visible; budget amendments are not. Outside lobbying works better for visible issues.

### 3. Free Speech vs. Anti-Corruption in Campaign Finance

**Free speech side**: Restricting political spending restricts political expression. If you cannot spend money to amplify your message, your speech is constrained. The First Amendment protects political speech. Political speech includes spending money to communicate.

**Anti-corruption side**: Money in politics corrupts—it creates the appearance of bribery, tilts policy toward wealthy interests, and gives some citizens' voices more weight than others. The government has legitimate interest in preventing corruption and in ensuring political equality.

**The empirical fact**: The Supreme Court has sided with the free speech argument (Citizens United, McCutcheon). The result is more money in politics, more inequality in representation, and less regulation.

**What would resolve it**: Either evidence that money in politics does not actually tilt policy outcomes (would weaken anti-corruption argument) or a constitutional amendment (would change the free speech answer). Neither is likely soon.

## Scale Shifts

**Micro → Macro**: The individual worker benefits from the union wage increase but faces disincentive to contribute individually → 50,000-person union faces different disincentive (easier to enforce) → 30-million-person environmental movement faces different disincentive (harder to enforce) → What does interest group representation look like when we scale to the whole political system?

**Macro → Micro**: In 2009, Congress debated the ACA. Macro scale: 535 legislators, thousands of interests, years of negotiation. Zoom in: one committee room. One lobbyist meeting with one staffer for 30 minutes. That conversation shapes whether a regulation gets written one way or another. That regulation affects millions of people.

**The point**: The machinery works at all scales. Individual incentives shape group formation. Group incentives shape representation. Representation shapes who gets represented. Seeing the scale shifts shows that the problem (inequality) is not fixable at one level; it propagates through all levels.

## Case Studies

### AARP's Scale Solution
- Started as health insurance distributor for people over 50
- Material incentive: discounts
- Growth: membership became large
- Result: political power in size + organization
- Mechanism: hooked people on discounts; kept them engaged via purposive incentive (advocacy)
- This is the business model that works at scale: give people non-political reason to join; use resulting organization as political force

### NRA's Two-Track Strategy
- Inside: drafting bills, technical briefings, relationships with sympathetic lawmakers
- Outside: grading politicians, mobilizing voters against politicians with poor grades
- Result: operates on both dimensions at once
- Why powerful: legislator faces technical consequence (NRA will propose alternatives) AND political consequence (NRA will campaign against legislator)

### Healthcare Lobbying and the ACA
- Interests: insurance industry ($100M+ lobbying), pharma ($100M+ lobbying), hospital associations, doctors' groups, employers, patient advocates
- Inside: industry had access, provided language, negotiated side deals (pharma: price controls in exchange for not opposing)
- Outside: patient groups mobilized, testified, ran campaigns
- Result: legislation that reflected both influences, but language shaped largely by industry lobbyists in back rooms
- This is the system working: wealthy interests participate more in technical drafting; poorer interests' priorities are included if politically risky to ignore, but language favors those with lobbyists in the room

## Common Misconceptions (Antidotes)

### 1. Larger groups are always more powerful
Counter: NRA (6M members) vs. gun owners (40M). NRA punches above its weight because membership = engagement. Compare hypothetical "Americans Who Like Nice Parks" with 30M members who never interact. Smaller, activated group wins.

### 2. Interest groups exist mainly to elect candidates
Counter: Primary work is policy influence—drafting language, shaping regulations, allocating budgets. Elections matter because of what elected officials can then do, not because electoral competition itself matters.

### 3. Free rider problem affects all groups equally
Counter: Wealthy interests can hire staff, offer material incentives, sustain groups through outside funding. Poor people cannot. The problem is worse for the poor.

### 4. Lobbyists change lawmakers' minds
Counter: Lobbyists support lawmakers who already agree with them. They provide ammunition for existing positions. Change happens at margins (undecided votes). Core work is servicing allies.

### 5. Outside lobbying is always more democratic
Counter: Outside lobbying requires media coverage. Media coverage requires "newsworthiness." Some issues are invisible (budget amendments). A sophisticated outside campaign on visible issue can lose to quiet inside campaign on invisible one.

### 6. Money directly buys votes
Counter: Correlation ≠ causation. Interests support politicians with voting records aligned to interest. Causation: politician's vote → interest's support. Money buys incumbent advantage, not converts. NRA gets little support from anti-gun politicians no matter how much money NRA has.

### 7. Wealthy always win
Counter: Public opinion matters. If public cares about issue, politicians must account for it. Social Security very popular; proposal to eliminate it would fail even with well-funded opposition. Technical tax provisions unpopular but invisible; can pass even with constituency opposition because public not paying attention.

### 8. Citizens United created the problem
Counter: Campaign finance was tilted toward wealthy before 2010. Money could go to campaigns (with lower limits), to "issue advocacy," to lobbying. Citizens United amplified existing advantages but did not create them. The problem—money → political access—predates the decision.

## Evidence and Data Points

**Gilens & Page (2014, 1,800 policy decisions over 20 years)**:
- Wealthy voters' preferences: 50%+ adoption rate
- Poor voters' preferences (opposed by wealthy): <10% adoption rate
- Influence gap: 15:1
- Business groups 2x influence of public interest groups

**Lobbying spending (2020)**:
- Top firm (Akin Gump): $49.9M annually
- Top 20 firms: $400M+ annually
- This is compensation for staff, offices, research, outreach—money that enables information and access

**Dark money (growth)**:
- 2012: $300M from dark money groups
- 2016: $800M
- 2020: Billions
- Trend: exponential growth since Citizens United (2010)

**Revolving door**:
- House members: 1-year cooling off before lobbying
- Senators: 2-year cooling off
- Cabinet secretaries: 2-year cooling off (before lobbying previous department)
- Reality: many former lawmakers do become lobbyists (Trent Lott, Richard Gephardt examples)
