# Pantry: The Presidency

## Scenes & Cold Opens

**September 1787, Constitutional Convention (Chapter Opening)**
- The delegates circling around what a president is *allowed to do*
- Edmund Randolph's vague outline vs. James Wilson's direct election vs. Alexander Hamilton's elected king vs. William Paterson's weak committee
- The mechanical question: minimum power to govern + maximum constraint against tyranny
- Washington unopposed, four-person cabinet, two vetoes total—the presidency as modest office

**1798: The Quasi-War with France**
- Merchant ships seized. American sailors killed. No formal declaration of war.
- John Adams orders the Navy to protect American shipping by force
- Acting beyond explicit constitutional grant, but Congress does not stop him
- The precedent is set: when Congress's machinery is too slow, the president acts
- Later presidents invoke Adams to justify their own unilateral moves

**1933: FDR's Bank Holiday**
- Banks failing. Unemployment at 25%. 
- Roosevelt declares a bank holiday—orders all banks closed—without congressional authority
- Flurry of executive orders creating new agencies: CCC, WPA, NLRA
- Some post-hoc congressional approval. Some Supreme Court strikes. But the programs stick.
- The precedent: in extremis, the president can act, and the action becomes normal

**1943-44: Japanese American Internment (Case Study)**
- Pearl Harbor attack, December 1941. Fear of "fifth column" on West Coast.
- FDR signs Executive Order 9066. Military declares entire West Coast a military area.
- 110,000 Japanese Americans (many citizens) moved to relocation centers for 2.5 years.
- Fred Korematsu refuses to go. Supreme Court rules 6-3 against him in 1944.
- 44 years later, Reagan apologizes. In 2011, Justice Department admits error.
- What does this reveal about presidential war powers? About the weakness of constitutional checks during crisis?

## Analogies & Etymologies

**The Bully Pulpit (Etymology)**
- Theodore Roosevelt's phrase, early 1900s
- "Bully" originally meant "excellent" or "wonderful" (not bullying/aggressive)
- Roosevelt: "The presidency is a bully pulpit"—a platform from which to push an agenda
- The metaphor: the presidency as a raised stage, the audience as the nation
- The mechanism: speak + public moves + constituents pressure Congress = presidential power

**The Two Presidencies Asymmetry**
- Domestic policy: Congress constrains. President needs money, votes, legislative authority.
- Foreign policy: President decides. Congress too slow. National security too urgent.
- The difference is not formal (Constitution is same for both). The difference is practical.
- But practical differences accumulate into constitutional change over time.

**Technology as Power Multiplier**
- Each new medium transforms what the president can do
- Newsreels (1920s): Roosevelt could appear to millions
- Radio (1930s-40s): FDR's voice in every home. Fireside chats create intimacy + authority.
- Television (1950s-70s): Kennedy benefits from looks. Reagan from training. Image matters.
- Internet/social media (2000s+): Trump bypasses all intermediaries. Tweets reach millions directly.
- The constitutional powers don't change. The *reach* changes. The reach becomes power.

## Trade-Offs Across Concepts

| Concept | Benefit | Cost |
|---------|---------|------|
| **Constitutional Constraint** | Prevents tyranny. Requires negotiation. | Inefficient. Slow. Hampers crisis response. |
| **Precedent Accumulation** | Allows adaptation. Enables action when needed. | Irreversible. Shifts baseline permanently. Empowers future tyrants. |
| **Technology/Public Pressure** | President can persuade. Direct reach. | Congress eroded. Public manipulated. Media distortion possible. |
| **Foreign Policy Latitude** | Speed in crisis. Decisive action possible. | Danger of abuse. Undeclared wars. Public kept in dark. |
| **Domestic Policy Constraint** | Congress's power of purse protected. Legislation requires consensus. | Popular presidents blocked. Reform stalled. Government can't act. |

## Scale Shifts

**From Modest to Vast**
- 1787: Four-cabinet department. Two presidential vetoes. Single executive, weak by design.
- 1861-65: Lincoln suspends habeas corpus. Claims war powers beyond Constitution.
- 1933-45: FDR creates regulatory state by executive order. Hundreds of new agencies.
- 1964: Gulf of Tonkin gives president blank check for war.
- 2026: President controls vast security apparatus, commands media, wage war, issue binding agreements.

**From Congress-Centered to President-Centered**
- Framers assumed Congress would be the powerful branch
- By 1900, president could claim command over foreign policy
- By 1950, president was dominant in military matters
- By 2000, president was expected to *lead* on domestic policy (though Congress still controls spending)
- 2026: President is the visible face of government. Congress is legislative traffic.

**From Direct Legislative Action to Administrative Implementation**
- 19th century: President asked Congress for law. Congress debated. Law was written.
- 20th century: President issued executive order. Bureaucracy implemented. Congress ratified or objected later.
- 21st century: President sets tone. Bureaucracy interprets. Law is what the administration does.

## Case Studies Beyond the Chapter

**Clinton's Health Care Reform Failure (1993-94)**
- Clinton has presidency + strong party majorities in Congress
- Still cannot pass comprehensive reform
- Why? Opposition from insurers, pharma, Republicans + media narrative control
- Lesson: Even a powerful president loses when Congress opposes + public persuasion fails
- This is domestic policy constraint at work

**Gulf of Tonkin (1964)**
- Alleged attacks on U.S. destroyers by North Vietnamese
- Second attack probably didn't happen. First was ambiguous.
- Congress passes resolution giving president blank check
- President escalates from advisors to 500,000+ troops
- Decades later, historians reveal the deception
- Lesson: In foreign policy, president acts + Congress ratifies. By then, reversing is political suicide.

**Iran Nuclear Deal (2015-2017)**
- Obama negotiates as *executive agreement*, not treaty (avoids Senate)
- Trump withdraws by *executive order*
- Neither president had Senate approval to make or unmake the deal
- Treaty power is bypassed entirely via executive agreements
- Lesson: Senate's treaty power is a formality if presidents use executive agreements

**Japanese Internment (1942-44)**
- Shows what presidents can do in wartime with minimal constraint
- 110,000 citizens deprived of liberty, due process, property
- Supreme Court upholds. Decades later: apology, not reversal of precedent.
- Lesson: Emergency power + racism + fear = Supreme Court deference to president

## Deferred/Contentious Material

- **The War Powers Resolution (1973)**: Requires president to notify Congress within 48 hours, limits ops to 60 days without authorization. Presidents ignore it. Congress never enforces. Why? Would require political courage to defund ongoing military operations.

- **Executive Privilege**: Right to withhold info from Congress/courts. Asserted by Washington. Never explicitly in Constitution. What's the scope? When can courts override? Presidents claim broad privilege; courts have sometimes limited it. Contested space.

- **Signing Statements**: Presidents now sign bills with statements declaring how executive branch will interpret them. This is informal reinterpretation of law. Congress does not stop this. Is it constitutional? Largely unlitigated.

- **Removal Power**: President can fire cabinet officers and staff without Senate approval (settled 1926). But can president fire independent agency heads (Federal Reserve Chair, FTC members)? Courts have said *some* constraints apply, but the line is unclear.

- **Recess Appointments**: President can fill vacancies while Senate is in recess (appointments expire when Congress returns). Senate responds with "pro forma sessions" (empty meetings, Senate not really in recess). Supreme Court (2014) ruled Senate decides when it's in recess. Standoff continues.

## Misconceptions Worth Flagging

1. **"The president is all-powerful."** False. Congress controls budget, legislation, can impeach. President is powerful in foreign policy, limited in domestic.

2. **"Executive orders are laws."** No. They're rules issued under existing authority or emergency. Congress can override. Courts can strike. Successor can reverse.

3. **"The president enforces the law."** Partly true. But much enforcement is delegated to independent agencies (EPA, SEC, NLRB). President directs, but cannot micromanage.

4. **"The Constitution is stable."** Not really. Practice changes the Constitution. Precedent, crisis, technology all rewrite it informally.

5. **"Congress is a check on the president."** Mostly true in domestic policy. In foreign policy, Congress often rubber-stamps or arrives too late.

## Etymology Cluster: Power Words

- **"Executive power"**: Vested in president by Article II. But what does it mean? Framers left vague. Courts and presidents have been filling it in ever since.

- **"Commander-in-chief"**: Control of armed forces. Not power to declare war. But does it include power to wage undeclared war? Deploy troops? Presidents say yes.

- **"Take care that the laws be faithfully executed"**: Broad grant. President must enforce law. But who interprets what "faithfully" means? President or courts?

- **"Signing statement"**: When president signs bill into law, can issue statement about interpretation. Is this constitutional? Or is it presidential reinterpretation of Congress's work? Largely unresolved.
