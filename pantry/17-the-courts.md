# Pantry: The Courts

## Scenes (cold opens, present tense)

**Marbury's waiting room**
1803. William Marbury sits in an anteroom of the Supreme Court, which meets in a cramped committee room beneath the House chamber. He's waiting to hear if the Court will force James Madison to hand over his commission. The room is cramped, the stakes are personal, but something larger is being decided: whether a court with no power at all can tell the president what to do.

**A case at oral argument**
The justices sit in their robes. Lawyers stand to argue. The chief justice interrupts with a question. The lawyer pauses, restarts. Another justice asks. The logic of the case isn't being stated; it's being excavated, questioned, probed. The outcome isn't predetermined. It's being reasoned toward, in real time, in conversation.

**Jackson's defiance**
President Andrew Jackson hears the Court has ruled against Georgia's seizure of Cherokee land. He says (allegedly): "John Marshall has made his decision; now let him enforce it." He does nothing. The ruling hangs in the air. A court's decision that no one implements isn't law; it's a suggestion. This is the moment that reveals what courts actually depend on.

## Analogies and etymologies

**The Constitution as a written instruction that doesn't quite say what to do**
The Constitution names three branches. It gives Congress power to make law. It gives the president power to execute. It gives the Court power over "the judicial power of the United States." What does that mean? It doesn't say the courts can strike down laws. It doesn't say they can't. That silence is what Marshall had to fill.

**Precedent as a ratchet**
A ratchet lets you tighten but resists loosening. Precedent works similarly. A court can follow past rulings (easy, mechanical). A court can extend past rulings to new situations (possible, but requires reasoning). A court can overrule past rulings (very hard; requires showing why the past was wrong). This isn't perfectly symmetric. It creates a bias toward stability while allowing evolution.

**The Court as an interpreter, not an executioner**
A theater director interprets a script. She doesn't rewrite it or perform it herself; she tells the actors what the words mean. A judge interprets law. She doesn't execute it or make it; she says what it means in the case at hand. The limit of power is real: interpretation ends; someone else has to act on it.

## Trade-offs (both sides)

**Judicial review: Power and constraint**
- *For*: Courts can protect the Constitution from being amended in practice by the other branches. If Congress could just do whatever it wanted and courts couldn't say no, the Constitution would be whatever the legislature said it was. Judicial review is a check on power.
- *Against*: Judicial review gives unelected judges the power to stop laws made by elected branches. That's hard to square with democracy. If voters don't like a law, they should be able to vote it out, not have judges do it.

**Lifetime tenure: Independence and drift**
- *For*: If judges had to run for election or face reappointment, they'd cave to political pressure. They'd care about donors and voters, not law. Lifetime tenure gives them freedom.
- *Against*: A judge appointed at 50 might sit for 30 years. A president can't correct a bad appointment. The Court's ideology is locked in by the luck of when retirements happen. It's not democratic to be bound by judges you didn't elect.

**Precedent: Stability and error**
- *For*: If every case was decided fresh, the law would shift with each judge. Precedent means law is predictable. It means you can plan your life around what the law says.
- *Against*: If a past ruling was wrong—and some were (slavery was constitutional law, segregation was constitutional law)—precedent keeps the error in place. Stability can mean being stably wrong.

**Selective jurisdiction: Shaping the agenda and leaving others unheard**
- *For*: The Supreme Court can't hear everything. It has to choose. By choosing carefully, it can address the most important constitutional questions. This shapes the law in a direction the Court thinks is right.
- *Against*: Because the Court chooses which cases to hear, it can avoid hard questions or favor certain kinds of litigants. Interest groups with resources can get the Court's attention; the poor often can't. The agenda is shaped by power, not by the most important questions.

## Scale oscillations

**From Marbury's personal commission to the structure of government**
A man wants his job. That's personal. But the case he brings exposes a fundamental question: who decides what the Constitution means? That's structural. Marshall handled the personal case in a way that answered the structural question.

**From one guilty man's confession to national criminal procedure**
Ernesto Miranda confessed to a crime. The Court could have just said whether his confession was admissible in his case. Instead, it used his case to establish that police everywhere must warn suspects of their rights. One man's trial created national law.

**From state law to federal constitutional interpretation**
A woman wears a headscarf. That's particular. But her case raised the question: what does Title VII mean when religion and dress code clash? That's general. Courts take particular cases and derive general principles from them.

**From the Court's power to the Court's dependence**
At the top level: courts are coequal with Congress and the president. They can strike down laws. But zoom in: courts depend entirely on those other branches to care enough to obey. A president can ignore a ruling. Congress can rewrite a law and fund the Court or not. The Court's power is real and fragile at once.

## Case studies (mechanics at work)

**Marbury v. Madison (1803)**
- The trap: if Marshall orders Madison to comply, Madison ignores him and the Court looks weak. If Marshall refuses the petition, Marbury looks wronged and the Court defers to the executive.
- The solution: Marshall rules Marbury is right but the law authorizing the Court to issue the writ is unconstitutional, so the Court can't do it.
- The hidden move: In ruling the Court lacks power, Marshall asserted that the Court has the power to judge what the Constitution permits. He gave up the immediate battle to win the larger war.
- The timing: This happened when John Jay (first Chief Justice) had quit, when the Court met in a closet, when no one took it seriously. Marshall seized the moment when the Court had nothing to lose.

**National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius (2012)**
- The case: Is the individual mandate constitutional? The opposition brief argued it wasn't. The government's brief argued it was.
- The question beneath: If the mandate is a regulation of interstate commerce, does it exceed Congress's commerce power? The oral arguments suggested the conservative justices thought it did.
- Roberts's move: He found a different constitutional basis for the mandate—the taxing power. This let him uphold the law without accepting the government's Commerce Clause argument.
- What it reveals: Roberts cared more about the Court's legitimacy than about his ideological preference. If the Court looked like it was warring with Congress, it would be weakened. Restraint served the Court's interest in surviving as an institution.

**Miranda v. Arizona (1966)**
- The fact: Miranda confessed after police questioning. The confession was used as evidence. He was convicted.
- The constitutional question: Does the coercive nature of police custody require that suspects be warned of their rights before questioning?
- The holding: Yes. From now on, police must warn suspects of the right to silence and the right to counsel.
- The trace: This is judicial activism in the pure sense—the Constitution doesn't explicitly require Miranda warnings. The Court inferred it from the Fifth Amendment's protection against self-incrimination.
- What happened after: Miranda was retried, convicted again on other evidence. The rule stuck. Today it's embedded in every police procedural. We forget it was controversial.

## Misconceptions to surface and reverse

**"Judges just interpret law objectively"**
No. Judges are people. They read the law through the lens of their own values, priorities, and political views. A judge who thinks the Constitution should protect religious freedom will interpret it that way. A judge skeptical of executive power will interpret it narrowly. This isn't sin; it's how reasoning works. The difference between good and bad judges is whether they're willing to be constrained by the text, precedent, and facts when those pull against their views.

**"The Supreme Court always wins its cases"**
No. Courts have vast power but also vast constraints. Congress can rewrite a law the Court strikes down. The president can refuse to enforce a ruling (rare). The Court can't enforce its own orders. If it loses legitimacy, it loses everything. That's why courts are often cautious—they know their power is conditional on being believed to be fair.

**"Precedent means the law never changes"**
No. Precedent is strong but not absolute. Courts change their minds when circumstances and reasoning demand it. *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896) said segregation was constitutional. *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954) said it wasn't. The Constitution didn't change; the Court changed. This doesn't happen often, but it happens.

**"Federal courts hear most cases in America"**
No. About 90 percent of cases are resolved at the state level. The federal courts are smaller but more visible. The Supreme Court hears about 80 cases a year out of roughly 7,000 petitions. The real work of the judiciary happens below the headlines.

**"Judges have to rule according to what the law says, period"**
Not quite. Judges have to apply the law. But what the law "says" is often ambiguous. The law says "regulate interstate commerce." What does that let Congress do? Different judges answer differently. The Constitution says "cruel and unusual punishment." Does that permit capital punishment? Different courts answer differently. Law is real and binding, but it's interpreted, not just applied.
