# Pantry: State and Local Government

## Scenes & Cold Opens

**Denton, Texas fracking ban (2015)** — A city council votes to ban fracking. State legislature overrides it. Pure hierarchy: the state owns mineral rights, the state legislature controls energy law, the city's home rule does not extend to saying no to the state's resources. Dillon's Rule made concrete.

**California Prop 13 (1978)** — Seniors on fixed incomes face rising property taxes. Proposition 13 caps tax at 1% of assessed value, frozen until sale. Tax bills plummet. Two people with identical houses on the same block pay radically different taxes because one house sold recently and one did not. Equity problem hiding inside a populist solution.

**Detroit bankruptcy (2013)** — A city with a shrinking tax base, unfunded pension obligations, and decades of mismanagement files for Chapter 9. Four years of restructuring. The city emerges smaller but functional. Illustration of how local fiscal stress cascades from federal and state policy (suburban flight, globalization of manufacturing) and then hits local governments with no escape valve.

**New Hampshire legislature** — Four hundred members meet for four months every other year at $100 per year. Cannot compete with the governor. Illustration of how institutional design shapes power: part-time legislatures cannot muster the capacity to check governors.

**California legislature** — Professional body, year-round, well-staffed members. The legislature negotiates on equal footing with the governor. Illustration of the opposite: professionalized legislatures can balance executive power.

**Wisconsin's "Vanna White" and "Frankenstein" vetoes** — A governor can delete letters from words to create new words, or delete words and string the rest together to change bill meaning. Eventually voters ban the practice. The absurdity of concentrated veto power.

## Analogies

**States as parents, cities as children** — A parent can grant a child autonomy within the home (home rule) but can still ground them, take away privileges, or move the family. The child's autonomy is real but conditional.

**Dillon's Rule as hierarchy versus federalism as contract** — Federalism says both levels agreed to the Constitution; neither can unilaterally revoke the other's power. Dillon's Rule says one level (the state) owns the grant; it can take it back. Hierarchy versus partnership.

**Property tax as a visible bill** — You see it once or twice a year, all at once. You do not see sales tax (spread across many transactions) or income tax (withheld from paychecks). Visibility breeds resistance.

**Marble cake versus layer cake** — Dual federalism was layer cake (separate spheres). Cooperative federalism is marble cake (mixed throughout). But local government in a single-state system is more like a pyramid: state at top, locals at bottom, clearly ranked.

## Trade-offs

**Home rule versus state control** — Home rule grants cities autonomy but lets states override it. Cities want more; states want control. Trade-off: cities get real but limited autonomy; states keep ultimate authority.

**Professional legislatures versus citizen legislatures** — Professional legislatures can check governors and produce sophisticated law. Cost: higher salaries, permanent bureaucracy, professionalization. Citizen legislatures are cheaper, closer to voters, part-time. Cost: weak check on governors, simple law, high turnover.

**Property tax versus other taxes** — Property tax is stable, local, visible. Problem: it does not grow with inflation, it is unpopular, it is unequal (depends on property values, which vary by neighborhood). Sales tax and income tax are less visible, more stable. Problem: harder to tie to local services, less sense of local control.

**Categorical grants versus block grants** — Categorical grants let federal government set standards and take credit. Cost: bureaucratic burden, less local flexibility. Block grants let states innovate and reduce federal bureaucracy. Cost: federal government loses control, credit is unclear.

**Special districts versus consolidated local government** — Special districts let governments serve areas that cross city lines (water, transit). Cost: fragmentation, voter confusion, no one accountable for overall service quality. Consolidated governments are simpler. Cost: one-size-fits-all, less efficiency for some services.

**Fiscal squeeze as structural problem** — States want local services but do not want to pay for them. Mandate services, do not fund them. Local governments face rising demands, flat revenue (property tax caps), and unfunded mandates. The trade-off is baked in: either states fund locals (higher state taxes) or locals go broke.

## Scale Oscillation

**Federal → State → Local → Citizen**

Start at federal: Congress passes environmental law, water quality standards. Scale down to state: state legislatures implement standards, set rules for how local governments will meet them, do or do not provide funding. Scale down further: city government struggles with unfunded mandate, raises property taxes, cuts other services, or asks federal government for grant money. Citizen receives water bill, faces property tax hike, votes against school levy.

OR

**Citizen → Local → State → Federal**

Citizen unhappy with local zoning, tries to change city council, fails. Goes to state legislature, gets state preemption of local zoning authority. State removes local control. Citizen then goes to federal court on constitutional grounds (free speech, due process). Court says state had the authority under Dillon's Rule.

## Etymologies

**Dillon's Rule** — John F. Dillon, Iowa Supreme Court justice, formalized the principle that local governments are creatures of the state. Not named after anyone revolutionary. Named after a mid-nineteenth-century jurist who crystallized existing hierarchy.

**Home rule** — The word "home" connotes domestic sphere, the place you control. "Rule" is authority. Home rule is the authority to govern your home—your local jurisdiction. The phrase suggests a return to what locals should naturally have (self-governance of home) but in fact it is a grant the state makes.

**Charter** — From Latin "charta" (paper, document). A charter is a written document granting authority. Royal charters granted authority to cities from the king. In the U.S., home rule charters grant authority from the state to local governments.

## Misconceptions & Corrections

**"Cities are part of the federal system."** No. Cities are not mentioned in the Constitution. They are creatures of states. Federalism is federal-state. Local government is state-local (hierarchical, not federal).

**"Home rule means cities are sovereign."** No. Home rule means cities have some autonomy in areas the state allows. The state can revoke home rule, override home rule decisions, or restrict home rule scope. It is delegation, not sovereignty.

**"Governors are presidents of their states."** Close but not quite. Presidents are limited by Congress and courts on equal footing (checks and balances). Governors are often more powerful relative to their legislatures because legislatures are part-time, have fewer staff, meet briefly, and depend on the governor for information. President-as-individual > governor-as-individual, but governor's relative power within state > president's relative power within federal system.

**"Local government is closer to voters so it is more democratic."** Yes and no. Local government is close (more accessible, more influence per voter). But voter turnout in local elections is very low (10-20 percent in many places). Special district elections have turnout near 5 percent. Proximity does not guarantee engagement.

**"Property tax is good because it is stable."** It is stable but not on the upside. Property values fluctuate. Tax assessment practices lag market changes. Tax caps (like Prop 13) prevent growth in revenue even as population grows. It is stable but often insufficient.

## Case Studies

**Oregon's automatic voter registration (2015)** — State law made voter registration automatic for anyone with a driver's license. This is a state decision that affects local elections. Illustrates how state legislatures shape local democratic participation. Also illustrates political culture: moralistic states tend to expand voting access; traditionalistic states add barriers.

**Indiana's Religious Freedom Restoration Act (2015)** — State law said businesses could refuse service on religious grounds. Business groups said this would hurt their state, city officials from surrounding states poached companies. State then "clarified" the law to forbid discrimination. Illustrates: (1) states making policy that affects business location, (2) inter-jurisdictional competition (venue shopping in reverse), (3) state override of problematic law through pressure, not courts.

**San Francisco school district budget crisis (2023)** — Declining enrollment, expensive teacher pensions, federal COVID relief ending, property tax revenue not growing fast enough. District faces structural deficit (expenses > revenue by design). Illustrates: (1) how federal grants can temporarily mask local fiscal problems, (2) how pension obligations constrain local discretion, (3) how property tax limitations make growth impossible.

**New Jersey and Chris Christie's tax breaks for businesses in Camden** — State offers hundreds of millions in tax breaks to lure businesses to economically depressed areas. Illustrates individualistic political culture (use tax incentives to stimulate private initiative) and inter-jurisdictional competition (states compete to attract business). Also shows limits: businesses often extract incentives without delivering promised job growth.

## Questions for Deeper Inquiry

- Why do states impose unfunded mandates if it weakens local government capacity they depend on for service delivery?
- If Dillon's Rule is the legal principle, why do cities have any power at all? What is the political basis for home rule?
- Why is voter turnout so much lower in local elections than federal elections if local government is more powerful in people's daily lives?
- Property tax is unpopular. Why do states persist in relying on it instead of shifting to other revenue sources?
- Why do some states have professional legislatures and some have citizen legislatures? Is it a choice or a historical accident?
