# Pantry: The Bureaucracy

## Scenes

### Charles Guiteau and the Assassination of President Garfield (1881)

**Cold open:** A man walks into a train station and shoots the president because he was not given a government job.

**Scene setup:** The Baltimore and Potomac Railroad Station, July 1881. Garfield is waiting to board a train to New Jersey. Guiteau, who had worked for the campaign, believed he had been promised a position as a diplomat. The position never materialized. Guiteau decides that killing the president will make him famous and will force the government to acknowledge his grievance.

**What it teaches:** The spoils system made government jobs so valuable that a person would kill the president over one. This illustrates how patronage corrupted governance by tying government positions to personal loyalty and electoral reward rather than competence. The assassination became the catalyst for civil service reform.

**Use in chapter:** Cold open to Concept 2. Anchors the reader in a specific, consequential moment that motivates the Pendleton Act.

---

### The National Weather Service in May 2025

**Cold open:** Meteorologists cannot launch weather balloons because of budget cuts.

**Scene setup:** The Trump administration implements hiring freezes and dismissals. The National Weather Service, understaffed, loses the ability to gather high-altitude data critical to forecasting. A 19 percent vacancy rate across 119 field offices. Some offices at 40 percent vacancy. The margin between a forecast that saves lives and one that does not narrows.

**What it teaches:** The merit system protects career officials from arbitrary removal, but it also prevents them from mobilizing politically to defend their work. Bureaucrats are caught between independence (protected by merit) and powerlessness (unable to campaign or organize politically).

**Use in chapter:** Case study in Concept 2. Illustrates the trade-off of the merit system in real time. Also used to open Concept 3's discussion of presidential control mechanisms.

---

### The IRS Tax-Exempt Status Scandal (October 2013)

**Cold open:** An agency admits it gave extra scrutiny to conservative groups.

**Scene setup:** Congressional hearings. Angry representatives questioning IRS officials. The implication: if the IRS can weaponize tax policy against groups it disfavors, what other agencies might do the same? 

**What it teaches:** Congressional oversight can be visible and dramatic but may lack teeth. Agencies can face investigations and public pressure but continue operating with limited consequences. Also illustrates how partisan polarization affects bureaucratic oversight.

**Use in chapter:** Cold open to Concept 3. Shows both that oversight mechanisms exist and that their effectiveness is limited.

## Analogies

### The Fourth Branch Without a Ballot

**Analogy:** Bureaucracy is a branch of government that no one votes for, like a fourth branch running parallel to the executive, legislative, and judicial branches.

**How it works:** The judiciary was not directly elected for most of American history, but judges are appointed by elected officials and can be reviewed. Bureaucrats are not elected, not appointed through the same confirmation process, and are harder to remove. They are in some ways even further from democratic accountability than judges.

**Where it breaks down:** Bureaucrats do serve under appointed cabinet secretaries, and the president can set broad policy direction. Judges, by contrast, cannot be removed by the president for disagreeing with him. The analogy works better for career civil servants than for political appointees.

---

### Tenure as a Two-Edged Sword

**Analogy:** Job security in the merit system is like tenure in academia—it protects professors from being fired for unpopular research, but it also makes them harder to remove when they are genuinely not performing well.

**How it works:** Tenure exists to protect academic freedom. A professor cannot be fired because his research challenges prevailing dogma or upsets donors. Similarly, civil service protections exist to protect bureaucrats from arbitrary removal. But in both cases, the protection can make it harder to get people to do what the institution needs.

**Where it breaks down:** Academic tenure is explicitly about freedom. Civil service protections are about competence and insulation from partisan pressure. The motivations are different, though the mechanisms are similar.

## Trade-offs (Detailed)

### Spoils System vs. Merit System

**Spoils System Advantages:**
- Bureaucracy is responsive to elected leadership
- Election outcomes directly change government policy
- Turnover prevents entrenched power

**Spoils System Disadvantages:**
- Incompetent people get jobs based on loyalty
- Government effectiveness becomes unpredictable
- Corruption becomes routine
- Officials focus on party politics rather than public service

**Merit System Advantages:**
- Jobs go to qualified people
- Government becomes more competent
- Professional bureaucrats develop expertise
- Reduced corruption

**Merit System Disadvantages:**
- Bureaucrats insulated from democratic pressure
- Career officials can pursue their own agendas
- It is hard for elected leaders to change agency direction
- Officials can become unresponsive to public wishes

**Modern solution:** Presidential appointees at the top (responsive to the president) oversee career bureaucrats below (protected by merit). But the balance is always contested.

---

### Expertise vs. Accountability

**Why the tension exists:** Complex government requires expert knowledge. But democracy requires accountability. You cannot have both fully. The more you insulate experts from political pressure to let them do their best work, the less you can hold them accountable. The more you make them responsive to elected officials, the more you politicize the work.

**Example trade-off:** An epidemiologist at CDC wants to recommend certain public health measures based on data. A president wants to minimize economic disruption. Each is responding to legitimate pressures. But they cannot both be fully accommodated.

**No perfect solution:** Different democracies have tried different balances. The U.S. has chosen to protect career bureaucrats fairly substantially. Other systems rotate officials more frequently or give elected leaders more direct control. Each approach has downsides.

---

### Congressional Oversight Time Budget

Congress has 535 members, hundreds of agencies, thousands of possible targets for oversight. If Congress spends all its time overseeing, it cannot legislate. If it does not oversee, bureaucracies drift. The actual result is episodic oversight—intense when a scandal breaks, minimal otherwise. This creates a gap in which agency officials have room to act independently.

## Etymologies

### Bureaucracy

From French *bureau* (office/desk) and Greek *-kratia* (rule/power). Literally "rule by desks." The term carries a pejorative connotation in English (rules from behind desks, disconnected from reality) but was originally descriptive. Max Weber used it neutrally—a system of administration.

### Spoils

From the Latin *spolium*, meaning the stripped armor or goods taken from a defeated enemy. The phrase "to the victor go the spoils" is ancient. In the U.S. context, it refers to government jobs as the prize of electoral victory. The spoils system is also called the patronage system.

### Pendleton

The Pendleton Act is named for Senator George Pendleton of Ohio, who championed civil service reform. The act is formally the Civil Service Reform Act of 1883. It became known as the Pendleton Act because of Pendleton's leadership.

### Hatch

The Hatch Act (1939) is named for Senator Carl Hatch of New Mexico. The full name is the Political Activities Act. It prohibits federal employees from engaging in partisan political activities. The logic: if bureaucrats could campaign and use their official authority, the separation between politics and administration would collapse.

## Scale Shifts

### From Small Republic to Administrative State

**1789–1820s:** Bureaucracy small enough to fit in one building. Departments: State, Treasury, War. A few hundred employees. Personal relationships—the president might know department heads personally.

**1829–1883:** Expansion under spoils system. Political parties use government jobs to reward supporters. Patronage appointments reach into thousands of positions. But this creates chaos and corruption.

**1883–1930s:** Gradual expansion of merit system. Coverage starts at 10 percent, slowly expands. Bureaucracy professionalizes.

**1930s–1960s:** Massive growth under FDR and Johnson. Federal employment reaches 700,000 under FDR, 2.2 million under Johnson. New agencies: Social Security, SEC, SEC, NLRB. Bureaucracy becomes a major policy force.

**1970s–present:** Size stabilizes but complexity increases. Technology, regulation, entitlements mean fewer people are managing more complex systems. Shift from patronage to expertise is now nearly complete, but the problem of control remains.

---

### From Personal Loyalty to Institutional Loyalty

The spoils system relied on personal loyalty—a man worked for the party, the party rewarded him. The merit system replaces this with institutional loyalty—a man works for his agency, the agency provides security. This is safer from corruption but creates new problems: agencies develop their own agendas, unions form, bureaucrats identify with their missions over elected leadership.

## Case Studies (Expanded)

### CFPB: A Bureau Designed to Oversee, Itself Overseen

Created in 2011 after the financial crisis to regulate predatory lending. Republicans opposed it as growing government; Democrats saw it as necessary regulation after banks had run amok. The controversy: Was the bureau capturing the market, or was it a cop on a chaotic street? The agency became a proxy for broader debates about government size and regulation. Congressional action against it intensified, then declined as the recession receded. The bureau survived but hobbled. Lesson: even an agency designed to police bad behavior becomes politically contested.

---

### The Weather Service and the Presidential Bottleneck

Budget cuts cannot directly fire career meteorologists. But they can prevent the agency from functioning by eliminating funding for basic operations. This illustrates how presidential control can work around merit protections: the president cannot order the firing of bureaucrats, but can make their jobs harder through budgetary pressure. The trade-off: the merit system prevents direct removal, but allows indirect pressure.

## Misconceptions & Corrections

### Misconception: Bureaucracy is uniquely governmental

**Reality:** Nearly all large organizations—corporations, universities, hospitals, charities—are bureaucratic. They have hierarchies, specialized roles, written procedures. What is unique about government bureaucracy is the accountability structure (answer to elected officials, ultimately the public) and the impossibility of "exit" (unlike private companies where you can choose not to use their service).

---

### Misconception: The merit system is complete

**Reality:** The merit system covers roughly 85 percent of federal positions. Fifteen percent are still filled through appointment. Moreover, at the top of every agency, political appointees control direction and set agendas. Career bureaucrats operate under their supervision.

---

### Misconception: Whistleblowers will catch major wrongdoing

**Reality:** Whistleblower protections exist, but they are partial. An employee who reports wrongdoing may face subtle retaliation. Even protected whistleblowers often suffer in their careers. The machinery of response is slow.

---

### Misconception: Congress can directly control the bureaucracy

**Reality:** Congress has tools (budgets, hearings, GAO reports) but is resource-constrained and attention-limited. There are 535 congresspeople and hundreds of agencies. Most oversight is episodic, occurring when scandals erupt or political actors decide to target an agency.

3. Is it possible to design an oversight system that balances responsiveness to elected leaders with protection for expert judgment?
