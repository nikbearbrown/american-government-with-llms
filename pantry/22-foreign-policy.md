# Pantry — Chapter 22 Foreign Policy

## Scenes / Cold Opens

**The Cuban Missile Crisis moment (October 1962)**
- Spy plane photographs, clear evidence
- Kennedy convenes advisors with competing views
- Decision point: bomb? blockade? negotiate?
- The choice reveals the paradox: Kennedy has authority to act alone but chooses consultation
- Teaches: speed vs. legitimacy, presidential power vs. institutional restraint

**The Iran Nuclear Agreement negotiation (2015)**
- Seven countries negotiating nuclear limits
- Obama's choice: treaty (needs Senate, likely fails) or executive agreement (president acts alone)
- Republican opposition would block treaty
- Lesson: framework choice is itself a power move
- Sets up later reversal under Trump

**Vietnam War endgame (1973)**
- 58,000 American deaths in undeclared war
- Congress had not formally authorized conflict
- War Powers Resolution: attempt to restore congressional power
- Unintended consequence: it licensed presidential power for sixty days
- Teaches: reforms can have opposite effects

**Afghanistan withdrawal (2021)**
- Twenty-year occupation, trillions spent
- Pullout produces Taliban victory, refugee crisis
- Misconception revealed: military power ≠ ability to reshape societies
- Demonstrates the limit of neoconservative approach
- Shows that even vast resources cannot force political outcomes

## Analogies

**The chess game vs. the weather**
- Traditional foreign policy: two rational actors (U.S. and USSR) in zero-sum competition (chess)
- Modern foreign policy: transnational problems (terrorism, climate, pandemics) that cannot be won against, only lived with (weather)
- Cold War fit the chess model; transnational threats do not

**The moat and the highway**
- Isolationism assumes the oceans are moats that protect you
- Reality: modern oceans are highways—trade, information, disease flow across them
- You cannot isolate yourself; you can only choose whether to shape the flow or react to it

**The hammer and the house**
- Military power is a hammer: great for breaking things, poor for building them
- You can hammer a house into rubble in hours
- You cannot hammer rubble into a stable home
- This is why military victory does not equal foreign policy success

**The doctor's dilemma**
- A doctor has perfect information about a patient and can make decisions alone and fast
- A nation has imperfect information about other nations and cannot move alone at all
- Democratic deliberation is like consulting other doctors: slower but more legitimate

## Etymologies / Technical terms

**Soft power:** Non-coercive influence (diplomacy, cultural appeal, economic incentive). Coined by Joseph Nye to distinguish from hard power (military force).

**Hard power:** Military or economic coercion. The threat or use of force to change behavior.

**Realism (international relations):** Framework assuming states are rational actors pursuing self-interest in an anarchic international system. No world authority enforces rules.

**Liberal internationalism:** Framework assuming democracies can cooperate, institutions reduce conflict, and shared rules are preferable to power competition.

**Neoconservatism:** Political movement that emerged in 1970s, emphasizing unilateral use of military power to promote democracy and U.S. values globally.

**Isolationism:** Foreign policy stance prioritizing non-entanglement in other nations' conflicts and avoiding permanent military commitments.

**Containment:** Cold War strategy of preventing Soviet expansion through military presence, alliances, and support for anti-communist regimes.

**Transnational:** Crossing borders; not limited to any single nation. Used for threats (terrorism, pandemic), institutions (multinational corporations), and movements (migration).

## Trade-offs (explicit)

| Speed vs. Legitimacy | Fast action (president alone) loses buy-in from Congress and allies. Slow action (multilateral) loses ability to respond to crisis. |
| Hard power vs. Soft power | Military force gets immediate results but creates resentment and sets condition for blowback. Diplomacy is slower but more durable. |
| Unilateral vs. Multilateral | Acting alone means you decide but others resent it. Acting with others means consensus but also means slowness and diluted goals. |
| Military goals vs. Political goals | Winning a war (military victory) is possible. Winning a political outcome (democracy, stability) is much harder. The machinery of statecraft conflates them. |
| Institutions vs. Speed | International organizations provide legitimacy but move slowly. Presidential action is fast but lacks legitimacy when unilateral. |
| Democratic debate vs. Crisis response | Waiting for democratic deliberation ensures legitimacy but can mean missing the moment. Acting first and deliberating later preserves speed but risks overreach. |

## Scale Shifts

- From European stability (19th century) to global balance of power (Cold War) to transnational threats (21st century)
- From two superpowers (Soviet-American competition) to multipolar world (U.S., China, rising powers)
- From state-to-state diplomacy to non-state actors (al-Qaeda, ISIS, criminal networks, hacktivists)
- From military solutions (winning wars) to non-military problems (pandemic, climate, refugee flows)
- From permanent alliances (NATO, Cold War blocs) to ad hoc coalitions (war on terror, climate)

## Case Studies (short)

**Pearl Harbor (1941)**
- Surprise attack pulls U.S. into World War II
- Congress formally declares war
- Ends isolationism definitively
- Shows when isolationism breaks: when attacked directly

**Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)**
- Nuclear weapons ninety miles from U.S. shore
- Kennedy's choice between military strike, blockade, negotiation
- Result: blockade works; Soviets remove missiles
- Teaches: realism (understand incentives), consultation (legitimacy), restraint (could have escalated to nuclear war)

**Vietnam War (1955-1975)**
- Cold War proxy conflict: U.S. supports South Vietnam democracy against Soviet-backed North Vietnam
- U.S. military methods fail in jungle guerrilla warfare
- No formal congressional declaration of war; presidents wage war on executive authority
- Result: 58,000 American deaths, Vietnam becomes communist anyway
- Produces neoconservatism (those who conclude liberalism is naive) and distrust of presidential war powers

**Iran Nuclear Agreement (2015)**
- Seven-country pact limits Iran's nuclear program
- Choice: make it a treaty (Senate ratifies, likely fails) or executive agreement (president alone approves)
- Obama chooses executive agreement; Republicans oppose but cannot block
- Trump becomes president, reverses it unilaterally
- Teaches: framework choice (treaty vs. executive agreement) determines who has power; unilateral action is reversible

**Iraq War (2003-2011)**
- Neoconservative approach: use military power to overthrow dictator, implant democracy
- Quick military victory (regime falls in weeks)
- Two-decade occupation with no stable outcome
- Hundreds of thousands killed, trillions spent
- Demonstrates the gap between military capability and political goals

**Afghanistan withdrawal (2021)**
- After twenty years, U.S. withdraws; Taliban returns to power
- Refugee crisis; perception of American retreat
- Shows both the cost of overcommitment and the limits of military power
- Demonstrates that resources alone do not achieve political outcomes

## Misconceptions (expanded)

**"The president can do whatever he wants in foreign policy."**
Reality: The president has more power in foreign policy than domestic, but Congress controls the purse. Without appropriations, even a unilateral presidential decision cannot be implemented for long. Congress uses this leverage rarely, but it exists.

**"Congress can stop the president if it really wants to."**
Reality: Congress has the legal power but using it is politically costly. Defunding troops means defunding troops; impeachment requires two-thirds vote. Opposition must be overwhelming, not merely principled.

**"Military victory equals foreign policy success."**
Reality: You can win a war and lose the peace. The U.S. defeated the Iraqi military in 2003 but could not defeat the insurgency or establish stable governance. Military power answers military questions. It does not answer political ones.

**"If we just elected the right president, foreign policy would work better."**
Reality: The machinery is institutional, not personal. A president is constrained by Congress, the military bureaucracy, international law, and the intractability of problems. Individual judgment matters at the margins, but structure shapes outcomes more than any individual's views.

**"There is one right school of thought for foreign policy."**
Reality: Each school (isolationism, liberalism, realism, neoconservatism) works in some contexts and fails in others. Effective policy uses different lenses for different problems. Ideology insists on one lens always.

**"Sanctions and diplomacy can always solve problems if we're patient."**
Reality: These tools work when both sides benefit from a deal. They fail when one side's goals are fundamentally incompatible with yours (e.g., a totalitarian regime that values control over prosperity).

**"The U.S. can reshape other societies through military power."**
Reality: Military power can break a regime. It cannot install values, culture, or governance. Once the occupying force leaves, what remains depends on what the local population wants, not what foreign soldiers want.

## Connected ideas (deeper study)

- **Constructivism:** International relations school arguing that state interests are not fixed but constructed through interaction and discourse. Challenges realism.
- **Feminist international relations:** Examines how gender shapes foreign policy, often excluded from traditional analyses.
- **Structural realism:** Waltz's framework; states act as they do because of the structure of the international system (anarchy), not their internal ideology.
- **Democratic peace theory:** Empirical claim that democracies rarely go to war with each other. Undergirds liberal internationalism.
- **Hegemonic stability theory:** Argues that stable international order requires a dominant power to enforce rules. Relevant to U.S. role post-Cold War.
- **Securitization theory:** How threats are constructed (e.g., how "terrorism" becomes a security issue that justifies emergency powers).
